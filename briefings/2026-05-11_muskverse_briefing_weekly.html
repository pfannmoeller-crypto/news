Tesla May 9 2026

Last Model S and Model X roll off Fremont — line converts to Optimus, targeting 1M units/year

On Saturday May 9, the final Model S and final Model X rolled off the Fremont assembly line that built every example of both cars. The Model S ran 14 years (since 2012); the Model X ran 11. Tesla is now converting the entire Fremont S/X assembly area into a production line for the Optimus humanoid robot, with Musk targeting one million Optimus units per year at this site alone. Production begins "late July or August" — just four months after the last S/X — though Musk warned initial output will be "quite slow," calling it "literally impossible to predict" given Optimus has roughly 10,000 unique parts on an entirely new line. Tesla's Q1 2026 filing additionally confirms a 5.2 million sq ft Optimus factory expansion at Gigafactory Texas targeting up to 10 million robots per year at full ramp. Public sales targeted for end of 2027 at $20,000–$30,000. (EVXL, Teslarati, Electrek)

So what: This is the most consequential physical pivot in Tesla's history since the 2012 Model S launch. Killing the flagship sedan and SUV to retool for humanoid robots is a thesis statement: management believes Optimus is a larger TAM (Total Addressable Market) than the entire luxury-EV segment. Whether the line can actually produce robots at scale by Q4 is unproven — "10,000 unique parts" is not a confidence-building number for a brand-new line.

Tesla May 5–9 2026

FSD subscribers reach 1.3M; robotaxi expands to Dallas and Houston; TSLA back above $400

Tesla disclosed 1.3 million FSD (Full Self-Driving) subscribers in Q1 2026, anchoring the recurring-software thesis that increasingly underwrites the bull case. The robotaxi service is expanding cautiously: unsupervised rides launched in Dallas and Houston in April, and Tesla expanded its unsupervised operation area in Austin. Total fleet remains modest at 26 unsupervised vehicles. Musk reiterated on the Q1 call that robotaxi revenue will not be material in 2026, with meaningful contribution pushed into 2027, and that "a large-scale ramp-up in unsupervised FSD/robotaxis won't occur without major architectural improvements to the software." TSLA returned above $400 this week on the Optimus narrative and the Dutch RDW (Rijksdienst voor het Wegverkeer) supervised-FSD approval announced last week. Tesla also staged an "Autonomy Pop-Up" at the F1 Miami Grand Prix Fan Fest (Apr 29–May 3), displaying a Cybertruck towing a Cybercab inside a glass case. (TradingKey, Digitimes, HeyGoTrade)

So what: 1.3M FSD subs is the first hard datapoint that recurring software revenue is becoming material at Tesla — the long-promised "Apple-like" software-attach narrative now has a denominator. Robotaxi at 26 vehicles after a year of soft launches makes Musk's caveat ("not material in 2026") credible rather than evasive. The architectural-rewrite admission is what to watch: V15 is the gate for meaningful 2027 scaling.

(SocialBee X updates, FelloAI pricing, Releasebot xAI)

So what: The week's xAI news is execution, not strategy — shipping features that monetize the Grok 4.3 platform. The interesting datapoint is the STT API: 25-language coverage matches OpenAI Whisper's reach and is a B2B-developer play, not a consumer flourish. xAI is quietly building API-revenue surface area ahead of the SpaceX-xAI IPO disclosure cycle.

SpaceX May 7 2026 · static fire

V3 Super Heavy completes first full static fire; Flight 12 cleared for tomorrow (May 12)

On May 7, SpaceX completed the first successful full-up static fire of a V3 Super Heavy booster — the 33-Raptor-3-engine first stage that will fly Flight 12. The test cleared the major remaining hardware gate. Flight 12, the maiden flight of the redesigned V3 architecture, remains targeted for tomorrow May 12, 22:30 UTC (with backup windows extending through May 15), pairing Booster 19 + Ship 39. V3 is designed for 100+ tons to LEO (Low Earth Orbit), roughly triple V2's ~35-ton capacity. The mission profile is deliberately conservative: suborbital arc, both vehicles target water splashdown rather than tower catch. This will also be the first launch from Orbital Launch Pad 2 at Starbase. SpaceX has now spent over $15 billion on the Starship program since 2019. (Space.com, KeepTrack, BNN Bloomberg)

So what: The static fire is the largest single hardware milestone for V3 to date. Flight 12 tomorrow becomes the year's most important launch — success would de-risk the upcoming SpaceX IPO narrative (see next card), failure resets the V3 timeline by months but does not threaten the program. Watch the deliberate splashdown profile: SpaceX is treating this as a vehicle-shakedown, not a milestone catch.

SpaceX IPO May 5–8 2026

SpaceX IPO roadshow targets week of June 8; SEC filing requirements due May 18–22; $1.75T target cap

The S-1 timeline crystallized this week. SpaceX is targeting the week of June 8 to kick off its IPO roadshow, with the week of May 18–22 as the expected window to satisfy remaining SEC filing requirements. Target market capitalization: $1.75 trillion, with a structured offering valuation of approximately $2 trillion — what would be the largest IPO in U.S. financial history by a factor of more than 10× over Alibaba's 2014 debut. Starlink fundamentals continue to anchor the deal: ~10 million subscribers and $18.7 billion projected 2026 revenue, accounting for roughly two-thirds of SpaceX revenue. The structural caveat the bears flag: the IPO takes the entire combined entity public, including loss-making X and xAI from the February merger — their losses may materially compress the consolidated valuation that the Starlink/launch businesses alone would command. (Motley Fool, Space Explored, IndexBox)

So what: Two timelines now run in parallel and feed each other: the V3 launch (May 12) and the S-1 filing window (May 18–22). A clean Flight 12 going into the SEC window is the optimal IPO setup; a failure forces underwriters to recalibrate. The "take everything public" structure is the most contested architectural choice — sophisticated buyers will price X and xAI losses against Starlink cash generation, and that math is unfriendly relative to a pure-play satellite/launch IPO.

Politics May 2026 · rulings

Federal judge rules DOGE's NEH grant cancellations unconstitutional; EFF lawsuit cleared to proceed

A pair of significant legal rulings this week sharpened the operational and constitutional risk around DOGE. U.S. District Judge Colleen McMahon ruled, in a 143-page decision, that DOGE's cancellation of roughly $100 million in grants at the NEH (National Endowment for the Humanities) was illegal — finding DOGE and the Trump administration had "no constitutional authority to block, amend, subvert or delay spending appropriations based on the president's own policy preferences." Separately, the EFF (Electronic Frontier Foundation) lawsuit against OPM (Office of Personnel Management), DOGE, and Musk personally over access to federal employee records was cleared to proceed after the government's motion to dismiss was rejected. The core legal theory across the >200 DOGE-related filings remains consistent: DOGE acted without statutory authority, and its operators — including Musk — were never Senate-confirmed for the powers they exercised. Discovery in the central agency-dismantling lawsuit remains paused for more than a year after the DOJ successfully appealed an order requiring Musk to answer written questions. (Gephardt Daily, EFF, CNN)

So what: McMahon's 143-page ruling is the most substantive constitutional finding against DOGE to date and will be cited in dozens of parallel cases. The EFF ruling is procedurally more important — it keeps Musk personally on the hook as a defendant, surviving the dismissal motion. The DOJ-appealed discovery pause is what's protecting Musk from sworn testimony; if that pause lifts before the July 4 sunset, the optics shift sharply.

Trial May 5–6 2026 · Brockman testimony

Brockman rebuts Musk's origin story; testifies he feared Musk might physically hit him

OpenAI President Greg Brockman took the stand for two days, concluding Tuesday May 6. He systematically rebutted Musk's account of OpenAI's founding and the for-profit conversion. Brockman's core counter-narrative: Musk himself pushed for OpenAI to create a for-profit arm and "fought a bitter battle to have absolute control" over it. Brockman testified he never made commitments to Musk about the company's permanent corporate structure, "and never heard anyone else make them" — directly undermining Musk's "you can't just steal a charity" framing. He emphasized OpenAI remains governed by a nonprofit. The most dramatic moment: Brockman testified he "feared Musk might hit him" during an early power struggle for the company. Brockman also revealed secret work he did for Tesla during his OpenAI tenure — a disclosure with potential implications for both companies' historical governance. (CNBC, ABC7, Local News Matters)

So what: Brockman's testimony is the most credible direct rebuttal Musk's narrative has faced. The "absolute control" claim repositions the entire case: if the jury believes it, the for-profit conversion was not a betrayal of Musk's vision but a continuation of one Musk himself initiated, only differently structured than he wanted. The "secret Tesla work" disclosure is the wildcard — it could surface OpenAI/Tesla overlap that no prior filing addressed.

Trial May 7–8 2026 · Zilis testimony

Shivon Zilis testifies: Musk tried to poach Altman to a Tesla-internal AI lab

Shivon Zilis — former OpenAI board member, longtime Musk ally, and mother of his children — took the stand later in the week and delivered the second-most-consequential reveal of the trial. Under questioning by OpenAI counsel Sarah Eddy, Zilis acknowledged she acted as a conduit for Musk while he tried to poach Sam Altman and other OpenAI co-founders to work at a new AI lab inside Tesla — a parallel structure that, if true and contemporaneous with the OpenAI founding, materially complicates Musk's framing that he was a disinterested philanthropist concerned only with the nonprofit mission. Eddy framed OpenAI's broader thesis bluntly: "Musk is suing OpenAI only to undermine a competitor in the AI race" — positioning the entire lawsuit as commercial sabotage dressed as governance grievance. The trial is at its midpoint. The liability phase remains on track to conclude by May 21; remedies phase only if wrongdoing is found. (MIT Tech Review, ABC7 live, Fortune)

So what: The Zilis testimony is more strategically dangerous to Musk than the distillation admission from Week 1. Brockman's framing was "Musk wanted control" — that's interpretable. Zilis's testimony is concrete: Musk used a private channel to poach OpenAI talent into a Tesla AI lab while publicly criticizing OpenAI's commercial drift. That's the cleanest evidence yet for the "competitor sabotage" thesis. With liability verdict 10 days out, the trial is now Musk's narrative versus a coherent counter-narrative — not just denial.

Neuralink May 2 2026 update

Third patient implant (Brad Smith, ALS); Arbaugh at two years; thread-retraction story re-surfaces

Two years after Noland Arbaugh became the first human to receive Neuralink's N1 implant in January 2024, an extended progress feature this week revisited the trial cohort. Brad Smith, a paralyzed man with ALS (Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis), became the third person to receive a Neuralink implant in April and is now able to type with his brain — the most clinically significant patient milestone since Arbaugh. Arbaugh himself continues to use the device for video games, online chess, and assistive control, though the feature re-surfaced the well-known thread-retraction issue: some of the hair-thin connecting wires laced into the brain tissue stopped reporting data in the weeks after his surgery, requiring software-side compensation. Neuralink's broader 2026 plan — high-volume BCI production, automated surgery, and threads through the dura mater (Hirnhaut) without removing it — remains on track for later this year. (The Week, Fierce Biotech, Fox News)

So what: The third patient (Smith, ALS) extends Neuralink's clinical surface from spinal-injury patients into degenerative neurological disease — a much larger addressable population. The thread-retraction re-surface is a reminder that the device is still iterating on hardware-tissue interface problems; the dura-through threads in the 2026 production design are explicitly aimed at this failure mode.

Boring Co May 4 2026 · Dubai

Parsons named Dubai Loop program manager; 48 permits sought; tunnelling targeted H2 2026

Parsons Corporation was awarded a 9-month contract to serve as the Boring Company's delegated program manager for the Dubai Loop, supporting design-build activities. The Boring Company will seek approval for approximately 48 permits and No Objection Certificates across roughly 10 different entities, with a goal of beginning tunnelling in the second half of 2026. The first phase is estimated at ~$154 million with an anticipated delivery period of around one year following design completion. This makes Dubai the Boring Company's first internationally executed project and its first using a major external program manager — a shift from the lean, in-house Vegas Loop model. Separately, the Nashville Music City Loop continues to face the local-resistance pattern previously documented (state legislature override of local opposition). (GlobeNewswire, The National)

So what: Bringing in Parsons is a meaningful operational change — the Boring Company traditionally insists on doing tunnelling without traditional civil-engineering partners. A program manager of Parsons' scale suggests Dubai's regulatory complexity (48 permits across 10 entities) forced a more conventional structure. That's pragmatic, but it also dilutes the cost-disruption narrative that justifies Boring's existence.

Six takeaways from the week of May 11, 2026

  • 1
    Fremont's pivot from cars to robots is the most physically committal Muskverse bet of 2026. The four-month conversion timeline is aggressive; the "10,000 unique parts" admission is honest. Expect the first credible Optimus production rate datapoint in October.
  • 2
    Two trial bombshells reshape the OpenAI case at its midpoint. Last week the headline was Musk's xAI-distillation admission. This week, OpenAI's counter-narrative landed: Brockman saying Musk wanted absolute control of a for-profit OpenAI, and Zilis confirming Musk used her as a conduit to poach Altman and co-founders into a Tesla-internal AI lab. Together these convert the case from "Musk vs. an unfaithful nonprofit" to "two competing accounts of who wanted what" — with the jury now needing to choose, not just believe Musk. The May 21 liability verdict is now genuinely uncertain.
  • 3
    The V3 static fire + IPO calendar is the cleanest tactical setup of the year. A successful Flight 12 tomorrow (May 12) lands six days before SpaceX's expected May 18–22 SEC filing window, with the roadshow targeted for the week of June 8. Optimal sequencing for the underwriters. A failure tomorrow would not kill the IPO but would force SpaceX to either delay the S-1 or absorb a valuation discount tied to V3-timeline risk. Watch the launch and the filing as a paired event.
  • 4
    The McMahon ruling against DOGE is the most substantive constitutional finding to date. A 143-page opinion calling DOGE's spending interventions unconstitutional becomes the citation backbone for the >200 parallel DOGE lawsuits. Combined with the EFF case surviving dismissal — which keeps Musk on the hook personally — the legal cost of DOGE participation keeps rising. The DOJ-appealed discovery pause that's shielding Musk from sworn testimony becomes the single most important procedural lever to watch in June.
  • 5
    Tesla's software thesis got its first hard number. 1.3M FSD subscribers is the denominator the bull case has needed for years. At even modest ARPU (Average Revenue Per User), that's a real recurring revenue line. But the architectural-rewrite admission ("won't ramp without major software improvements") is the counterweight: V15 is the gate. Until V15 ships, 1.3M is a present good but not a 2027-scaling commitment.
  • 6
    The SpaceX IPO's structural risk is now visible. "Take everything public" means Starlink's cash generation (~$18.7B 2026 revenue) gets blended with X and xAI's losses. The bear case writes itself: $1.75T target market cap implies a multiple that prices in Starlink's growth plus monetized synergy from xAI — not just Starlink alone. Sophisticated buyers will discount that. Watch whether the S-1 leans into the combined-entity narrative or carves out segmented disclosures that let pure-play satellite investors get comfortable.
