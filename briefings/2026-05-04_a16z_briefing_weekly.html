4 Policy May 2 CFTC · 18-page filing

a16z files 18-page CFTC letter backing federal prediction-market oversight

In the week's highest-profile policy move, a16z filed an 18-page comment letter with the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) supporting federal authority over prediction markets and opposing state-level crackdowns. The letter argues that state regulators labeling prediction markets as "gaming" creates a "serious barrier to impartial access" and damages market liquidity. a16z describes prediction markets as "information machines" whose pricing mechanisms represent a distinct form of price discovery surfacing crowd intelligence on uncertain outcomes. (The Block)

Key policy proposals in the letter: prohibited-trader lists covering "Event Controllers" (those who can influence outcomes), Determinations Committees staffed by subject-matter experts, and cross-market surveillance between CFTC and SEC to prevent manipulation across correlated financial platforms. The firm also endorsed blockchain-based platforms, arguing that on-chain auditability makes regulatory oversight more effective. (a16z Crypto blog)

5 Crypto May 1 Robert Hackett · a16z Crypto

"Retire the word stablecoin" — a16z essay argues the label has outlived its purpose

Robert Hackett, a16z's editorial lead, published an essay on May 1 arguing that the term "stablecoin" should be retired in favor of "digital dollars," "digital euros," and "onchain assets." The core argument: stability is now table stakes, not a distinguishing feature. "The question is no longer 'will it hold its value?' but 'what else can we build with it?'" Hackett uses the "horsepower" analogy — a term James Watt coined in the 1770s to explain steam engines in familiar terms that long outlasted its usefulness. (CoinReporter)

The essay was widely covered across crypto media. Notably, Hackett argues for "digital dollar" and "digital euro" framing explicitly not as CBDCs (central bank digital currencies) in the ECB/Fed sense, but as privately issued, blockchain-native instruments. This is a rhetorical move — normalizing stablecoins by shedding crypto jargon while maintaining distance from central-bank-controlled alternatives. (Cryptopolitan)
