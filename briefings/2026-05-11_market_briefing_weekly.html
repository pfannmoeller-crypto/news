Jobs

April NFP: +115k vs. consensus +62k — first back-to-back gain in nearly a year; wages cooler

The April nonfarm payrolls report (released Fri 8 May) printed +115k versus a 62k consensus, with unemployment unchanged at 4.3%. Crucially, average hourly earnings (AHE) came in at +0.2% month-on-month and +3.6% year-on-year — both below the +0.3% / +3.8% consensus. This is the "warm but cooling" combination that lets the Fed hold without breaking the soft-landing thesis. Job gains concentrated in health care (+37k), transport/warehousing (+30k), retail (+22k). Federal government employment continued to decline (−9k). March revised up to +185k. Labor force participation dropped to 61.8%, lowest since Oct 2021 — the one yellow flag.

Fed

Warsh Senate confirmation vote teed up for this week — Powell term ends Fri 15 May

Kevin Warsh cleared the Senate Banking Committee on a 13–11 party-line vote on 29 Apr, the first fully partisan committee vote in Fed-chair confirmation history. The full Senate vote is scheduled for the week of 11 May, with confirmation expected ahead of Powell's term expiry on 15 May. Warsh needs a simple majority; Republicans hold 53 seats. The market's read so far is hawkish-on-balance: Warsh has signaled discomfort with "premature" rate cuts. Watch for his first post-confirmation public remarks for any tone shift on the 3.50–3.75% policy range.

Oil Geopolitics

Brent: $114 → $101 — Hormuz risk premium burns off despite continued US-Iran exchanges of fire

Brent crude fell roughly 7% across the week, closing $101.29 Friday after Monday's intraday $114.44 peak. The collapse came on Trump's assertion that the US–Iran ceasefire "remains in place" despite a Thursday exchange of fire in the Strait of Hormuz and an Iranian missile launch at the UAE Friday. The US also fired on two empty Iranian tankers attempting to evade the blockade. Critically, the International Energy Agency (IEA) is now warning the broader Iran-war supply disruption is removing roughly 14 million barrels per day from global supply — i.e., the market is pricing through a structural deficit on the bet that the negotiation works. If that bet breaks, this is the same setup as the Monday spike, only worse.

Energy supply

IEA flags ~14 mbpd off global market — structural deficit risk if ceasefire breaks

The International Energy Agency's warning that approximately 14 million barrels per day (mbpd) is currently removed from global supply because of the Iran war is the most important commodity number of the week. For context, global daily oil demand is ~102 mbpd, so this represents roughly 14% of total demand. The market is functioning because the Strait has been "largely closed since late February" and the world has absorbed it through inventory draws, SPR releases (US Strategic Petroleum Reserve), and demand destruction. That absorption capacity is finite. If Hormuz negotiations stall and inventories continue to drain, Brent can re-test the $114 peak quickly.

Editor's view

5 takeaways
  1. Last week's worst-case priced out — but the structural supply gap didn't go away. Brent fell from $114 to $101 on negotiation hopes, even as the US and Iran continued to exchange fire and the IEA flagged ~14 mbpd of supply still off the market. The tape is paying for the negotiation outcome, not the underlying balance sheet. That's an asymmetric position — limited upside if a deal is reached (it's largely priced), unlimited downside if Hormuz re-escalates. The right read here is that the floor moved from $100 to "wherever sentiment lets it move next."
  2. AMD is the clean validation of last week's AI-monetization screening rule. Five days after Meta got punished for $125–145B capex without a clean revenue line and Alphabet got rewarded for +63% Cloud growth, AMD delivered the third datapoint: MI300X traction with a Q2 guide above Street, +17.77% one-day. The market is now applying a consistent rule — show me near-term return on AI spend or you don't get the multiple. This means Nvidia's August quarter is now load-bearing, not optional.
  3. NFP +115k with wages at 3.6% is the goldilocks print central banks pray for. This is the rare combination that lets the Fed defer cuts indefinitely without breaking the soft-landing. Pair that with falling oil and the back-end of the curve rolling over below 5%, and you have the technical conditions for the equity record-high regime to continue. The risk is hidden in the labor force participation drop to 61.8% — if the unemployment rate is stable only because workers are leaving the labor force, the read on "warm" labor markets is partially illusory.
  4. Pet-food portfolio read. The Brent unwind from $114 to $101 mechanically reverses some of last week's diesel-and-freight pressure into DACH/EU cost-of-goods. Dutch TTF gas should track in sympathy — see the latest pet-food commodity briefing for the Verfügbarkeit and named-supplier detail on rendered proteins, packaging, and wet-line steam costs. Treat this week's relief as a window, not a trend: the same Hormuz negotiation that gave the relief is what can take it back.
  5. Warsh's Senate vote this week is the biggest underpriced event. The market is treating the leadership change as continuity because Warsh has hawkish credentials and the policy range is unchanged. But the framing of the Powell-to-Warsh handover under partisan conditions is unprecedented for a Fed chair and the first post-confirmation public remarks will matter disproportionately. Watch for any signal on (a) the size of the Fed balance sheet, (b) tolerance for above-target inflation, (c) communication about cuts in 2H. Each is a multi-point S&P move in either direction.
