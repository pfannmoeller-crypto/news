Week in Review · 27 Apr – 4 May 2026

Two parallel ceasefires, neither real. Iran war off the boil, Hormuz still shut.

Putin floated a 8–10 May Victory Day truce in a 90-minute call with Trump; Zelensky countered with a 30-day ceasefire offer and accused Moscow of "utter cynicism" after overnight strikes on Ukrainian energy. The US-Iran ceasefire from early April is holding loosely but the Strait of Hormuz remains closed since 4 May after Trump paused Operation Project Freedom. Russia's economy contracted 1.8% in Jan–Feb; the Economy Ministry conceded reserves "have largely been used up." A drip of dissent — internet curbs, Memorial banned, Vesna activists sentenced — is building, not breaking. Sanctions front: EU's 20th package and UK's 544-vessel shadow-fleet list are now the binding constraint on Russian oil revenue, with US OFAC expanding the Iran "Economic Fury" net.

Ukraine · Russia war and diplomacy
Ukraine 29 Apr – 4 May

Putin–Trump call produces dueling ceasefire announcements; strikes continue overnight

Putin and Trump spoke for over 90 minutes on 29 April. Putin proposed a 72-hour truce around the 9 May Victory Day parade. Zelensky — not on the call — countered on 30 April with a proposal for a 30-day, "long-term, reliable, guaranteed" ceasefire, and instructed his team to clarify the Russian offer with Washington. On 4–5 May Russia struck Ukrainian energy infrastructure overnight; Zelensky publicly accused Moscow of "utter cynicism." Ukraine then announced its own unilateral ceasefire from midnight 5 May; Russia said it had received no notice and that its 8–10 May truce stands. Net read: this is a Victory Day optics play, not a diplomatic breakthrough. The Geneva trilateral track has been on a "situational pause" since the Iran war pulled US bandwidth.

Ukraine 28 Apr – 5 May

Frontline net negative for Russia — 21 sq miles lost in the week, Kupyansk and Lyman pressure unchanged

ISW's weekly accounting: Russia lost a net 21 square miles of occupied territory during 28 April – 5 May (46 sq miles since 7 April). Russian forces continued to push in the Kupyansk and Borova directions in Kharkiv, and ran an infiltration mission toward Slovyansk in Donetsk, but no confirmed advances of operational consequence. Stated Russian objectives remain the same three lines: clear Belgorod border, take all of Luhansk and push west into Kharkiv Oblast, and finish Donetsk. The picture is grinding attrition with marginal Ukrainian gains, not a Russian breakthrough — which is what makes the Victory Day truce framing politically useful for Putin and operationally cheap.

Iran · regional conflict and proxies
Iran 4–6 May

Hormuz still closed; Trump launched then paused "Project Freedom" naval escort in 48 hours

Iran has kept the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed to commercial transit since 28 February — the IRGC (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps) has issued passage warnings, boarded ships, and laid sea mines. On 4 May Trump launched Operation Project Freedom, a US Navy mission to escort merchant ships out of the Gulf. By 6 May he paused it, citing "great progress" toward a wider settlement. As of the latest reports the strait is closed, with no transits recorded since 4 May. For pet food cost-of-goods this matters less directly than for energy markets, but the spillover into Brent and shipping insurance premia is the live transmission channel. The April US–Iran ceasefire is loosely holding; Tehran continues to insist no talks while "zero enrichment" remains a US demand.

Iran Ongoing

Nuclear program: enrichment infrastructure largely inoperable, large HEU stockpile unaccounted

The Twelve-Day War (US–Israel air campaign Feb–Mar 2026) rendered most of Iran's known enrichment infrastructure inoperable. But Iran has effectively curtailed IAEA (International Atomic Energy Agency) access since the strikes, and large stockpiles of previously enriched uranium — including HEU (highly enriched uranium) — remain in the country and unverified. Carnegie's framing: even after two wars, the nuclear question is unresolved, not closed. The US is pushing "zero enrichment"; Tehran rejects this and refuses to resume talks under that condition. The structural risk: a renewed Israel/US strike cycle, or an Iran dash to weaponization with the residual stockpile, would re-open Hormuz risk and the energy transmission channel that flows into transport, packaging-resin and feed-import costs.

Iran Late Apr – early May

Proxies fragmented: Hezbollah still firing into Israel, Houthis open Bab el-Mandeb front

Foreign Policy's read on the proxy network — Hezbollah, the Iraqi militias, and the Houthis — is that they are increasingly operating for themselves rather than as a coordinated Iranian arm. Hezbollah continues exchanging fire with Israel; the unresolved Lebanon front is the single biggest blocker to a wider US–Iran deal because Tehran refuses any peace package that doesn't include a halt to Israel–Hezbollah hostilities. The Houthis, on Iran's nudge, have opened a Bab el-Mandeb missile-and-drone campaign aimed at Israel — second maritime chokepoint live alongside Hormuz. For maritime insurance premia and Suez routing this is the more durable shipping risk.

Russia · Iran cooperation
Russia–Iran Late Feb – Apr (cumulative)

EUR 500m secret arms deal to rebuild Iranian air defenses, plus 500 Verba MANPADS and 2,500 missiles

Per FT and Iran International, Iran signed a secret EUR 500m (~USD 590m) deal with Russia in December to acquire thousands of advanced shoulder-fired air-defense missiles, explicitly to rebuild what Israel destroyed in last year's war. Separately, Russia is supplying 500 Verba MANPADS (man-portable air-defense systems) plus 2,500 missiles. This is the live operational consequence of the January 2025 Comprehensive Strategic Partnership treaty (no mutual-defense clause, but expansive military-technical cooperation in a 20-year framework). The two foreign ministries also signed a 2026–2028 consultations program in late April. Translation: Iran is buying back its own deterrence from Russia while continuing to feed Shahed-136 drone know-how into Russia's Alabuga production. This bidirectional dependency is now structural, not transactional.

Sanctions · US, EU, UK
Sanctions 23 Apr

EU's 20th sanctions package: 632 shadow-fleet vessels listed, future maritime services ban on Russian crude

The EU adopted its 20th sanctions package on 23 April 2026 (the 18th from June 2025 reworked the oil price cap; subsequent packages have escalated). The cumulative tally now stands at 632 shadow-fleet vessels under EU port-access ban and services ban. The 20th package introduces the legal basis for a future prohibition on transporting Russian crude and petroleum products entirely — coordinated with the G7 / price-cap coalition. Combined with the UK's 544 listed vessels (covering ~75% of Russian crude exports by tonnage, per HM Government), the squeeze on Russia's discount-priced oil revenue is now the most binding external constraint on the Russian economy. Lloyd's List flags a real risk: tighter EU/UK rules may simply migrate more tonnage into a more opaque shadow fleet.

Sanctions 15–29 Apr

OFAC "Economic Fury" net widens — Iran oil, Hezbollah gold, Lukoil GL extended 30 days

OFAC (US Treasury Office of Foreign Assets Control) on 15 April designated 24+ individuals, entities and vessels under the Economic Fury campaign. The centerpiece is the Mohammad Hossein Shamkhani-led multi-billion-dollar Iran/Russia petroleum sales network, plus a Hezbollah gold-financing scheme tied to Iranian oil and gold smuggling (joint with Homeland Security Investigations). Russia track: GL (general licence) 134B issued 17 April authorized wind-down sales of Russian crude already loaded by 17 April through 16 May; GL 131E on 29 April extended Lukoil International GmbH negotiation/sale-contingency authorization 30 days to 30 May. Read: US is keeping Lukoil divestiture optionality alive while tightening the Iran/Russia oil-marketing nexus. The Iran and Russia sanctions buckets are increasingly the same network operationally.

Russia · internal — economy and dissent
Russia internal 25 Apr

Economy Ministry: reserves "largely been used up"; Q1 GDP contracted 1.8%

The Economy Development Minister (Reshetnikov) publicly conceded that "somewhere in the economy there were reserves" but they "have largely been used up" — and called the current macro situation "substantially more difficult." The economy contracted 1.8% in January–February 2026, a sharp reversal from the 1.3% growth forecast for the full year, which officials are signaling will be downgraded. Inflation slowed from 9.5% to ~6% in 2025, but Nabiullina's 4% target for 2026 looks aspirational. The ruble has appreciated more than the Ministry would like; rates are still high despite a series of cuts. A communist Duma deputy went so far as to invoke "1917-style" risk — that's posturing, but the fact it lands publicly is the signal. Reserve drawdown plus binding sanctions plus fading wartime fiscal stimulus equals an austerity track now.

Russia internal 8–24 Apr

Internet curbs trigger rare public dissent; Memorial designated "extremist", Vesna activists get 8–12 years

Material shift in domestic mood. The Kremlin's intensified internet crackdown — recurring mobile-internet shutdowns, blocked messengers, thousands of websites and digital services dark — has produced rare public pushback. Several dozen people queued at the presidential administration in central Moscow to lodge formal complaints. Even pro-Kremlin voices have started criticizing the disruption to daily life and to small business. On 9 April the Justice Ministry asked the Supreme Court to designate Memorial (Nobel Peace Prize laureate, already liquidated as a legal entity) an "extremist organisation" — escalating criminal exposure for anyone formerly associated. On 8 April six Vesna youth-movement activists got 8–12 years for "extremist organisation" participation tied to anti-war activism. Direction of travel: deeper repression, but also the first signals — economic distress + internet pain + sentencing severity — that the political cost is starting to be visible at street level.

💡 Editor's view

What this week actually moves

  1. The Victory Day "ceasefire" is theater, not a turning point. Two unilateral, unsynchronized truces, energy strikes overnight, and a stalled Geneva track. Trump wants the photo; Putin wants the parade; Zelensky won't accept short-term optics. Real diplomacy is gated on US bandwidth currently absorbed by Iran.
  2. The binding constraint on Russia is now the joint EU/UK shadow-fleet squeeze plus reserve depletion — not the battlefield. 632 EU + 544 UK vessel listings cover most of the discount-oil channel. Combine with Reshetnikov's "reserves used up" and Q1 contraction, and the Kremlin's leverage clock is shorter than the front-line picture suggests. Watch whether the EU 20th-package transport prohibition gets concrete dates.
  3. Iran is the dominant tail risk for any energy-linked input cost. Hormuz still closed, Project Freedom paused after 48 hours, Hezbollah unresolved, large unaccounted HEU stockpile. A re-escalation re-opens Brent, shipping insurance and packaging-resin transmission. The April ceasefire is loose — treat it as a pause, not a settlement.
  4. Russia–Iran is now structural, not opportunistic. EUR 500m air-defense rebuild deal + Verba/MANPADS package + Shahed-136 production loop at Alabuga + 2026–2028 ministerial consultations program. Sanctions design is catching up — OFAC is merging the Iran-Russia oil-marketing networks into one enforcement target.
  5. Russia internal is the genuine "watch this" item. Internet-curb dissent is the first economic-reality-meets-repression friction visible at street level. Don't over-read it — Putin tightens before he loosens — but the combination of (a) reserves drawn, (b) public Economy Ministry pessimism, (c) sentencing severity, and (d) public queueing to complain is a sequence that historically precedes inflection points, not stable equilibria.

Wildcard: if Hormuz reopens cleanly inside two weeks under a US–Iran framework, Brent gives back the war premium fast and the Russia oil-revenue picture worsens further — accelerating, not relieving, the Kremlin's fiscal squeeze. The two theaters are coupled in a way that's underpriced.
