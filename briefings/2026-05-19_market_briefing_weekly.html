Records on Thursday, rout on Friday: Trump–Xi underwhelmed, Iran patience snapped, and the tape is now staring at Nvidia

The week pivoted on a single day. The S&P 500 closed above 7,500 Thu 14 May and the Dow reclaimed 50,000 — both fresh records. Then the Trump–Xi summit produced soybeans and Boeings but no semiconductor or AI breakthrough, and Trump publicly said he was "running out of patience" with Iran. Brent crude rocketed +3.4% to ~$109 Fri, the 10-year US Treasury yield hit a one-year high, and every major index lost roughly 1.2% Friday. The selling continued into Monday on a UAE nuclear-facility strike over the weekend; Brent briefly touched $111 before easing to $102 on a US-floated temporary sanctions waiver. Nvidia reports Wed 20 May after the close — the most important single print of the quarter, with $78bn guidance already on the table.

Calendar note: this is a normal 7-day pull, but the entire equity story sits in two sessions — Thu 14 May's records and Fri 15 May's reversal — bracketed by Mon 18 May's continuation. Nvidia (Wed 20 May post-close) and Walmart (Thu 21 May) land after this briefing.

Geopolitics Trade

Trump–Xi summit: agriculture and Boeings, rare-earths concession, no semiconductor deal — Friday rout

The two-day Beijing meeting ended Fri 15 May with a "three-year framework for strategic stability" — language drafters' victory, not a market catalyst. The concrete deliverables: China to buy US oil, 200 Boeing aircraft, more US agricultural product, and "effectively eliminate" rare-earth export controls; in exchange the US cleared Nvidia H200 AI-chip sales to several Chinese tech firms. The miss was bigger than the hits: no agreement on broader chip export controls, no AI guardrails, no tariff reset. Markets priced the gap immediately — the S&P 500, Nasdaq and Dow each lost more than 1% Friday, the worst single-day drop since the records were set 48 hours earlier. The market is now in the habit of needing the summit-grade catalyst to keep moving up; without one, Friday is the default direction.

Fed

Warsh's "family fight" — markets now price zero 2026 cuts; the December hike probability holds near 30%

CNBC's framing on Sat 16 May — "Warsh comes into the Fed facing a big family fight over cutting interest rates" — captured the new equilibrium. Despite the political nomination logic that pointed toward cuts, under 3% of investor expectations now put a 2026 cut on the table, and the December rate-HIKE probability has held near 30% since the hot CPI/PPI. The 10-year Treasury yield hit a one-year high Mon 18 May, which is the bond market saying the same thing in a different language. Warsh's first FOMC is 16–17 June; the market will read every public remark between now and then for whether he defends the data (hold or hike bias) or the political mandate (cut bias). A dovish lean against a 3.8% CPI and a 6% PPI would be the genuinely destabilising surprise.

Consumer

April retail sales +0.5% MoM — in line, but the mix is "gas up, goods down"

Retail and food-service sales rose 0.5% month-on-month and 4.9% year-on-year to $757.1bn in April (released Thu 14 May), in line with expectations after a downwardly revised March. The composition is the message. Gasoline stations led with +2.8% — that is the Iran oil pulse showing up in the wallet, not a sign of healthy consumer demand. Food and beverage stores fell 1%, furniture fell 1%, both still under pressure from rates. Core retail sales (excluding food services, autos, building materials and gas) rose 0.5%, modestly above the 0.3% forecast. Read together with the CPI energy contribution and the PPI surge from last week, this is the consumer absorbing the energy pulse but starting to ration discretionary goods.

Rates

10-year Treasury yield hits a one-year high Mon 18 May — bond market doing the Fed's work

The benchmark 10-year US Treasury yield punched to its highest level in twelve months Mon 18 May, the proximate trigger for the Nasdaq and S&P leg lower into Tuesday. With the hot CPI and PPI still in the system and the Iran energy pulse re-loading, the long end is repricing a higher-for-longer Fed and a heavier US deficit at the same time. The simplest read: the bond market is doing the tightening the new Fed chair has not yet had a chance to deliver verbally, which lowers the urgency for Warsh to lean hawkish in his first public remarks — but also means equity multiples are getting pressured by yields, not by Fed talk, and that pressure does not pause for FOMC dates.

Earnings AI

Nvidia reports Wed 20 May after the close — $78bn guide, ~$73bn data-center expected; the single most important print of Q1 reporting

Nvidia delivers fiscal Q1 FY27 results Wed 20 May after the close, with management's own guide at $78bn revenue ±2%. Consensus has crept higher to ~$78.9bn revenue and ~$1.77 adjusted EPS, with data-center revenue at roughly $73.3bn — roughly 87% year-on-year growth. The set-up is the asymmetry: every other AI-revenue print this cycle (AMD, Cisco, Nebius) was rewarded violently to the upside. Nvidia is the scoreboard, so the bar is on guidance and on three specific risks — supply constraints, custom-silicon competition, and the China envelope (the H200 clearance from the summit is a positive read for that piece). A guide that simply re-confirms acceleration would extend the AI-earnings cushion under the indices into Q2. A guide that hedges on Q2/Q3 would remove the only force currently holding the tape above its inflation-and-rates math.

Brent: $109 Fri → $111 Mon intraday → $102 Mon close — the highest-volatility 72 hours of the war

Friday: Brent +3.4% to ~$109/bbl on Trump's "running out of patience with Iran" and the Xi-agreed Hormuz reopening demand. Weekend: drone strikes on Persian Gulf energy infrastructure including a UAE nuclear facility. Monday intraday: Brent punched above $111. Monday close: Brent eased back to ~$102 after Iranian media reported the US had floated a temporary oil-sanctions waiver as a pre-deal sweetener. This is the third leg of the now-familiar pattern — every "ceasefire holds" headline gets faded by a "ceasefire wobbles" headline within days. Since the war began 28 February, both Brent and WTI are up more than 45%, and the $101–$111 trading band of the last 10 days is the new working range until either a real deal or a real escalation takes a side out. The inflation pulse is not abating; the IEA's "undersupplied until October" call from last week still applies.

Energy supply

UAE nuclear-facility strike over the weekend — supply premium now includes a Gulf-infrastructure tail risk

Persian Gulf energy infrastructure came under attack over the weekend, including a reported strike on a nuclear facility in the United Arab Emirates. The market read it as a step-change in the conflict's geography — for most of the war the supply premium was about the Strait of Hormuz transit chokepoint and Iranian export disruption. A Gulf-wide infrastructure-attack premium is broader and harder to price out on a single ceasefire headline. Concretely: the Strait remains largely closed, the US administration continues to blockade Iranian ports, and the new variable is whether OPEC+ producers' own facilities are now part of the threat surface. For the inflation transmission Stefan's tape cares about — diesel, jet fuel, European wholesale natural gas — this widens the base case for "energy stays elevated through autumn" beyond what the IEA already laid out.

Editor's view

5 takeaways
  1. Thursday's records were the top of the move that started in April. S&P 500 above 7,500 and Dow 50,000 on Thu 14 May were the cleanest "macro doesn't matter while AI prints" signal of the cycle — and they lasted exactly one session. Friday's ~1.2% rout across all three indices came from two unrelated catalysts colliding (summit underdelivery + Iran patience snap), which is what tops usually look like in retrospect. This does not call a bear market, but it does say the records were earned by the absence of a negative catalyst, not by the presence of a positive one. That asymmetry is now broken.
  2. Nvidia's Wednesday print is the only thing standing between the current tape and the inflation math. The mechanical logic of the last six weeks has been: macro is ugly, but the AI-revenue prints are bigger than the macro. Cisco, AMD and Nebius all kept that trade alive. Nvidia at $78bn guidance is the load-bearing one. A re-acceleration guide reopens the AI-earnings cushion under the indices through Q2. A merely-in-line guide that hedges on Q2/Q3 supply or China removes the cushion, and the tape has nothing else to lean on — 10-year yields at one-year highs, no Fed cuts priced, oil bid, Iran unresolved. Treat Thursday's open as a real binary.
  3. The Trump–Xi summit was the cleanest "expectations vs. delivery" lesson of the year so far. The market was paying for a chip-export breakthrough, an AI-guardrail framework or a tariff reset. It got soybeans, Boeings, rare-earth concessions and one specific Nvidia chip (H200) for China. Those are real, but they are smaller than what was in the price. The lesson is structural: this administration is willing to call narrow trade wins "historic agreements," and markets will keep mis-pricing the gap until they recalibrate the headline-to-substance ratio. Expect more Friday-15-May-style reversals when high-profile diplomatic events underdeliver.
  4. The bond market is now leading the Fed, and Warsh is being handed both the data and the curve. The 10-year hitting a one-year high Mon 18 May while the December rate-HIKE probability sits near 30% means investors have already priced a tighter stance than any Fed communication. That gives Warsh cover to lean hawkish at 16–17 June without owning the political fallout — the curve has done the work for him. The destabilising surprise here is no longer a hawkish lean; it is a dovish lean against this data, which would split the Fed publicly and force a yield re-rating higher anyway.
  5. Pet-food portfolio read. The $109 → $111 → $102 Brent rollercoaster plus the UAE-facility strike over the weekend mechanically widen the diesel-and-freight surcharge layered into DACH/EU cost-of-goods, and they bias Dutch TTF gas (the European wholesale natural-gas benchmark) higher into the autumn restocking window. The April retail sales mix — gasoline +2.8%, food and beverage stores −1% — also says the German/EU pet-food consumer will feel the same energy pass-through into shelf prices, with the same trade-down pressure on premium categories. Combine with last week's IEA "undersupplied through October" call: the input-hedge horizon should be at least two quarters, not one, and named-supplier coverage on rendered proteins, packaging films and wet-line steam costs is more valuable than spot-price chasing right now.
