Iran de-escalation

Geopolitics · 5 underlying markets · Ceasefire is now an open Hormuz war
trending_downDeteriorating hard — US-Iran traded strikes July 8–13, Hormuz tankers hit, Brent ~$79

Last week's “the memorandum is over” was the ceiling of the pessimism, not the floor. What Monday framed as a diplomatic break has, in six days, become a shooting war centred on the Strait of Hormuz. The US struck 80-plus Iranian targets July 7–8 after Iran attacked three tankers (Al Rekayyat, Wedyan, Cyprus Prosperity), hit again for a second night July 9, and by July 13 CENTCOM was running dozens more strikes while Iran launched missile and drone barrages against the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman and Bahrain and attacked the container ship MV GFS Galaxy. Iran has redirected all shipping to a coastal route it controls, marked restricted zones, and insists Hormuz will “open only with Iranian arrangements” — the Article 5 dispute the June 17 memorandum never resolved is now being settled by force. Neither side has formally torn up the MOU, but Trump has called negotiations “a waste of time” and the memorandum is functionally dead. For the board, this is the week the tail became the trunk. The “US-Iran nuclear deal by July 31” leg that news-mediated captures still showed near ~100% Yes six days ago is now facially indefensible — there is no deal path with tankers burning. The successor markets (“US-Iran final nuclear deal by…?”, “US-Iran diplomatic meeting by…?”) are the only live instruments, and any permanent-peace leg that news reports still carry in the 70s reflects a pre-war world. The single most important number this cluster now generates is not a probability at all — it is Brent at ~$79.26 (July 13), the highest since June 22 and ~9% above pre-war levels, with Hormuz throughput (a fifth of seaborne oil) cut to six vessels a day. That price is the transmission belt into every macro cluster below. Treat the diplomacy-market prints as maximally stale (1/5); treat the oil mark as the live signal.
