Mid-week refresh · 11 – 14 May 2026

Both ceasefires expired. The Russia–Ukraine truce ran its 3 days then collapsed; Trump put the Iran ceasefire "on life support." The diplomatic envelope is closing again.

This is a short-cadence refresh — only three days since the 11 May edition — and the delta is genuine but narrow. The headline is symmetrical disappointment on both negotiation tracks. The Trump-brokered Russia–Ukraine ceasefire ran its full 9–11 May window, delivered the 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner swap, then expired at midnight 12 May with both sides accusing each other of violations; Russia resumed its offensive within hours, firing 200+ drones at Ukrainian energy and residential targets. On the Iran file, Trump formally rejected Tehran's counterproposal on 10–11 May — calling the ceasefire on "massive life support" and the proposal "garbage" — after Iran demanded war reparations, sanctions relief and an end to the US naval blockade before any nuclear discussion. Some Trump aides now say he is more seriously weighing a resumption of major combat operations than at any point in recent weeks. The one hard new data point: Russia officially slashed its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 0.4% from 1.3% on 12 May — the government formally bracing for prolonged stagnation. Net read: no new escalation, but the brief opening both files showed last week did not widen — it narrowed.

Ukraine · Russia war and diplomacy
DEVELOPMENT Ukraine 12 May

The 3-day ceasefire expired on schedule; Russia resumed its offensive within hours with 200+ drones

The Trump-brokered truce ran its full 9–11 May window and expired at midnight 12 May. It was not extended. Russia ended it by launching more than 200 attack drones overnight that damaged energy facilities and apartment buildings and killed at least one person, per Ukrainian officials. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov confirmed the resumption directly: "the humanitarian ceasefire ended" and "the special military operation is continuing." Both sides spent the three days accusing each other of violations — Russia's Defence Ministry claimed Ukraine committed more than 1,000 breaches; Zelensky said Russia had avoided large-scale air strikes but front-line Russian forces "were not observing any quiet." The substantive read for Stefan: the ceasefire functioned exactly as designed — a narrow humanitarian window for the prisoner swap and a diplomatic signal — and nothing more. It was never a step toward a settlement, and its clean expiry confirms that. The diplomatic process exists; the terms still do not intersect.

Ukraine 12–13 May

Russia Matters / ISW: Russian forces posted a net loss of ~45 sq mi over the April 14 – May 12 window

The 13 May Russia Matters war report card quantified the trajectory shift the 11 May edition flagged qualitatively. Over the four-week window from 14 April to 12 May, Russian forces recorded a net loss of roughly 45 square miles (~116 sq km) of Ukrainian territory. The pace context is stark: Russia seized an average of 2.9 sq km/day in the first third of 2026 versus 9.76 sq km/day over the same period in 2025 — advances down roughly 70% year-on-year, then turning net-negative in April. This is consistent with, not new beyond, the ISW "year-long offensive stalled" assessment from last week, but the fresh four-week net-loss figure is a harder number. It also reframes the Kremlin's restated maximalist demand — Ukrainian withdrawal from all of Donetsk Oblast — as even less backable by force than it looked three days ago. With the ceasefire expired, the front returns as the live variable, but it is moving against Moscow, not for it.

Diplomacy 10–13 May

Putin "hints" at ending the war but rules out a Zelensky meeting until a settlement is "almost finalized"

In the post-ceasefire window, US and European officials were openly weighing how to steer both sides into further talks — but the structural positions did not move. Per Al Jazeera's 10 May explainer, Putin signalled readiness for direct talks with Zelensky in Moscow or a neutral country, yet simultaneously ruled out a face-to-face meeting until a negotiated settlement is "almost finalized" — a sequencing demand that makes the meeting a reward for concessions rather than a venue to negotiate them. Zelensky's standing offer (ceasefire plus a direct meeting) goes the other way. This is the same gap as last week, restated after the ceasefire's expiry rather than narrowed by it. No new round, no venue, no date. The honest read on a 3-day delta: the diplomatic track is alive but inert — the prisoner swap was the deliverable, and there is no visible follow-on mechanism yet.

Russia · internal — economy and dissent
DEVELOPMENT Russia internal 12 May

Russia officially cuts 2026 GDP growth forecast to 0.4% from 1.3% — government formally bracing for prolonged stagnation

The single hardest new data point of this refresh. On 12 May, Deputy Prime Minister Alexander Novak told Vedomosti that the Economy Ministry has cut its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 0.4% from 1.3% — and the 2027 projection to 1.4% from 2.8%. This is the official number catching down toward the reality the Fortune bond-default piece and the –0.5% Q1 print already described last week: the government itself is now formally signalling a prolonged slowdown driven by weaker oil revenues, high inflation and heavy wartime spending. The budget's oil "cut-off" price assumption was held at USD 59/barrel. Bloomberg framed the cut as coming "despite Putin's push to rally the economy." Read for Stefan: this is not a surprise development — it is the state catching up to the trajectory — but a formal downgrade of that magnitude (growth cut by roughly two-thirds) is the kind of admission a regime avoids unless the alternative is less credible. It compresses the Kremlin's room to claim it can outlast the front-line and sanctions pressure.

Iran · nuclear, regional, Hormuz
DEVELOPMENT Iran 10–12 May

Trump formally rejects Iran's counterproposal — ceasefire on "massive life support," proposal "garbage"; aides say combat resumption back on the table

The 11 May edition reported Iran's response as delivered and Trump's initial "totally unacceptable." This refresh captures the formal rejection and its escalation language. Speaking at the White House on 10–11 May, Trump said the ceasefire is on "massive life support" and called Iran's latest proposal "garbage." The detail that matters: Tehran's counterproposal demanded compensation for war damage, reaffirmed sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz, and asked the US to end its naval blockade, guarantee no further attacks, lift sanctions and remove the ban on Iranian oil sales — with nuclear discussion deferred to later phases entirely. That is a maximalist re-anchoring, not a narrowing. Per CNN and NBC reporting, some Trump aides now say he is more seriously considering a resumption of major combat operations than at any point in recent weeks. Iran's top negotiator, Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, responded that Iran is "prepared for every option" and its armed forces ready to "deliver a lesson-giving response to any aggression." The diplomatic gap did not just fail to close — both sides moved their public posture outward.

Iran 11–14 May

Hormuz still closed; no transits recorded — Iran's new Persian Gulf Strait Authority now collecting tolls on approved passage

No reopening in this window. Per Lloyd's List and the Hormuz crisis trackers, the strait remains closed with no transits recorded as of mid-May; roughly 2,000 vessels remain stranded in the Gulf. The newer structural detail crystallizing this week: Iran has stood up a "Persian Gulf Strait Authority" to approve ship transits and collect tolls — an institutionalization of control that signals Tehran is treating the closure as a durable lever, not a temporary crisis posture. That matters because it changes the reopening calculus: a body built to administer and monetize passage is harder to dismantle than an ad-hoc IRGC announcement, and its existence is itself a bargaining chip Iran put on the table (the counterproposal explicitly demanded the US end its naval blockade). With Project Freedom still paused and Trump's rejection of the Iran proposal hardening, there is no near-term clean-reopening path. The 11 May edition's read holds: expect the Brent war premium and shipping-insurance surcharges to stay embedded through Q2.

💡 Editor's view

What actually moved in three days

  1. Both negotiation tracks closed slightly rather than opened. This is the core finding of a thin 3-day delta. The Russia–Ukraine ceasefire expired exactly on schedule with no extension and no follow-on mechanism — it delivered the prisoner swap and nothing else. On Iran, Trump formally rejected the counterproposal ("garbage," ceasefire on "life support") and Tehran re-anchored maximalist (reparations, blockade end, nuclear talks deferred entirely). Last week the envelope opened; this week it narrowed. Neither file escalated, but neither advanced.
  2. The hardest new number is Russia's official GDP cut to 0.4% from 1.3%. This is the state formally catching down to the trajectory the bond-default and –0.5% Q1 data already showed. A two-thirds downgrade is not something a regime concedes lightly. It tightens the operational read from the 11 May edition: the Kremlin's claim that it can simply outlast Ukraine and Western sanctions pressure is getting harder for Moscow itself to sustain on paper.
  3. The front is back as the live variable — and it is moving against Moscow. With the ceasefire expired, the territorial picture matters again. The fresh Russia Matters four-week net-loss figure (~45 sq mi / ~116 sq km lost 14 Apr – 12 May) makes the Kremlin's "all of Donetsk" precondition look even less enforceable by force than three days ago. Russia resumed with 200+ drones on energy infrastructure — pressure on civilian morale and the grid, not territorial gain.
  4. Iran's new Strait Authority is the quiet structural item. Institutionalizing Hormuz control into a toll-collecting body signals Tehran is treating the closure as a durable bargaining asset, not a temporary crisis. That raises the bar for any clean reopening and keeps the energy tail risk to Stefan's cost-of-goods picture firmly in place through Q2. Watch for whether Trump's "combat resumption back on the table" rhetoric converts into actual force posture changes.
  5. Honest call on cadence: this refresh confirms direction, it does not change it. Three days produced one hard data point (the GDP cut), two scheduled expiries (both ceasefires), and one structural crystallization (the Strait Authority). No new escalation, no new opening, no named-elite defection beyond last week's. The 11 May edition's framing — coupled negotiation tracks, both with unresolved central gaps, Russia internal degrading faster than the front — all still holds. Next full weekly edition should reassess whether the post-ceasefire diplomatic vacuum produces a new track or just a return to attritional stalemate.

Wildcard: Trump aides floating a resumption of major combat operations against Iran is the highest-variance item in this refresh. If that converts from briefing-room language into a force posture change before the next weekly edition, the energy and shipping-insurance picture re-rates sharply within days — and it would land on top of a Russia file that has no active mechanism to absorb a second simultaneous escalation.
