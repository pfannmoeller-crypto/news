Week in Review · 4 – 11 May 2026

A real ceasefire — but only for three days. Iran rejects the memo. Russia's bond market starts cracking.

The week turned on three things. First, Trump brokered an actual 9–11 May Russia–Ukraine ceasefire with a 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner exchange — the largest since the war began — after both unilateral truces from last week collapsed inside 24 hours. Putin held the most pared-back Victory Day parade in two decades, no heavy armour on Red Square. Second, Iran sent its response to Trump's 14-point one-page memo via Pakistan on 10 May, offering to ship some highly enriched uranium to a third country but refusing to dismantle facilities; Trump called it "totally unacceptable." Hormuz remains effectively closed, with 22,500 mariners stranded on 1,550+ vessels. Third, Russia's domestic picture is degrading on a faster track than the front line: ~25% of the corporate bond market now at default risk, Q1 GDP at –0.5% year-on-year, Putin approval down to 65.6 from 77.8 at start of year, and a Kremlin attack-lawyer publicly calling for Putin's resignation before being committed to a psychiatric hospital.

Status quo · what carried over from last week
  • Putin's 8–10 May "Victory Day truce" and Zelensky's 30-day counter were both unilateral, unsynchronized, and not real ceasefires.
  • Strait of Hormuz closed since 28 February; Trump's Operation Project Freedom paused after 48 hours on 6 May.
  • Russia economy contracted 1.8% Jan–Feb; Economy Ministry conceded reserves "have largely been used up."
  • EU 20th sanctions package (23 April) listed 632 shadow-fleet vessels; UK list at 544. Joint coverage ~75% of Russian crude exports.
  • Russia–Iran cooperation now structural: EUR 500m secret air-defence rebuild deal, Verba MANPADS pipeline, Shahed-136 production loop.
  • Internet curbs and Memorial designation triggered first visible street-level dissent inside Russia.
Ukraine · Russia war and diplomacy
DEVELOPMENT Ukraine 8–11 May

Trump brokers a real 3-day ceasefire after both unilateral truces collapse; 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner swap

The dueling Victory Day ceasefires from last week collapsed almost immediately — Russia hit Kharkiv overnight 5–6 May with 108 combat drones and three missiles. On 8 May Trump announced that Putin and Zelensky had agreed to a synchronized three-day ceasefire (9–11 May) plus an exchange of 1,000 prisoners each side, the largest swap since the war began. Both leaderships confirmed via channels (Putin's foreign affairs adviser Ushakov, Zelensky directly). Zelensky's framing: "Red Square is less important to us than the lives of Ukrainian prisoners who can be brought home." The ceasefire took effect for the Victory Day window; Al Jazeera reported continued localized fire on 8 May despite the announced halt. This is the first synchronized truce of the war — narrow in scope, but a structural shift from the unilateral theatre Stefan saw last week. The Geneva trilateral track remains paused; Trump is now positioning this as "the beginning of the end."

Russia 9 May

Putin's most pared-back Victory Day parade in two decades — no heavy military hardware on Red Square

Putin presided over a Red Square Victory Day parade that, for the first time in nearly twenty years, displayed zero heavy military equipment — no tanks, no missile launchers, no armoured columns. Only four parade units marched, including a contingent from North Korea per RIA Novosti. The optics are unmistakable: the symbolic stage Putin needed the truce window for is itself signalling depletion and asymmetric reliance on Pyongyang. CNN's framing reads as restraint of necessity, not choice — equipment is at the front, not in storage. This is the same week Russia's bond market is showing 25% default risk (see Russia Internal). The visible "victory" is shrinking in parallel with the fiscal base that funds it.

Ukraine 3–8 May

Russian forces lose Kupyansk positions; year-long offensive officially stalls per ISW

The Institute for the Study of War's 8 May assessment is materially the most negative weekly Russian readout in months. A milblogger reported on 7 May that the ~20 Russian soldiers encircled in Kupyansk City Hospital since December 2025 "fought their last battle" after a Ukrainian airstrike destroyed the hospital — implying Russia no longer holds positions in central Kupyansk. ISW's broader read: Russia's year-long offensive has stalled, and Ukraine regained more territory through winter and spring 2026 than Russia captured in April alone. Russian forces continued limited ground assaults in Kupyansk direction on 6 May without confirmed advances. Crucially, a senior Kremlin official restated the maximalist demand: Ukrainian withdrawal from the entirety of Donetsk Oblast as a precondition for any longer ceasefire — territory Russia has failed to take by force. That is the central settlement gap.

Russia · internal — economy and dissent
DEVELOPMENT Russia internal 9 May

~25% of Russian corporate bond market at default risk; 11 technical defaults already in Q1

Fortune's 9 May piece, drawing on Russian financial data, is the sharpest single signal of the week from inside Russia. There were 11 corporate technical defaults in all of 2024, 24 in 2025, and 11 already in just January–March 2026 — an annualized acceleration. Roughly 25% of the corporate bond market is now at default risk because firms that borrowed at low rates must refinance at much higher ones; the volume of debt to be rolled over this year is about double last year's. Nonpayments on commercial bills hit a record USD 109 billion in January. Q1 GDP contracted 0.5% year-on-year (down-revision from the 1.8% Jan–Feb contraction the Economy Ministry conceded last month). The US–Israel war on Iran has raised Russian logistics costs and stoked inflation — restricting Central Bank chief Nabiullina's room to cut rates. Russian officials reportedly told Putin in early 2026 that a financial crisis could hit by summer. Putin's response, per Fortune, is largely absentee — micromanaging the war from bunkers.

Russia internal 3–6 May

Putin approval down to 65.6 from 77.8; senior Kremlin lawyer commits to psych hospital after calling for resignation

Two data points, one direction. First, Putin's approval fell to 65.6% from 77.8% at the start of the year per multiple polling read-outs. A Russian official quoted by the Washington Post: "The overall mood is that's enough already; you've been fighting for long enough" — and tellingly, the source went on the record to say "we can't even take one region." Second, Ilya Remeslo — a longtime Kremlin attack lawyer and propagandist — publicly posted that Putin should resign and "be brought to justice as a war criminal and a thief," and was committed to a psychiatric hospital. The Washington Post separately reports Kremlin factions battling to sway Putin as the economy sinks. The repression apparatus is intensifying — new round of high-profile political arrests and raids — but the simultaneous emergence of named-elite dissent is the qualitative change. Combined with the bond-market signal in the prior card, this is the strongest week-on-week deterioration inside Russia since the war began.

Iran · nuclear, regional, Hormuz
DEVELOPMENT Iran 6–10 May

Iran's response to Trump's 14-point memo: HEU transfer offered, dismantlement refused — Trump calls it "totally unacceptable"

The week's most consequential Iran diplomacy. The Trump administration delivered a one-page, 14-point memorandum of understanding (MOU) to Iran via envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner the week of 5 May. The memo asks Iran to halt all uranium enrichment for at least 12 years, hand over an estimated 440 kg (970 lb) stock of HEU (highly enriched uranium, ~60% enrichment), and accept a 30-day talks-track to settle remaining terms. Iran transmitted its response via Pakistan on 10 May: it offered to ship some — not all — HEU to a third country, refused to dismantle nuclear facilities, and proposed that the current negotiation phase focus exclusively on a cessation of hostilities, leaving the wider nuclear architecture for later. Trump's reaction: "totally unacceptable" and Iran is "playing games." Per Bloomberg/Axios, this remains the closest both sides have been to an agreement since the war began, but the central gap — Iran's "inalienable right" position versus US "zero enrichment" — has not closed. UK Foreign Secretary and E3 (France, UK, Germany) continue separate track per Commons Library.

Iran 3–8 May

Strait of Hormuz still closed; 22,500 mariners stranded on 1,550+ vessels; April through-traffic just 191 ships

Hormuz remains effectively closed. Trump launched Operation Project Freedom (US Navy escort operation) on 4 May, paused it on 6 May after 48 hours citing "great progress" toward a deal. Joint Chiefs Chairman Gen. Dan Caine confirmed on 6 May that 22,500 mariners are trapped on more than 1,550 commercial vessels in and around the strait. Pre-war Hormuz baseline: ~3,000 vessels/month and ~15 million barrels per day of oil exports. April 2026 through-traffic: 191 vessels for the entire month — a ~94% drop. A brief reopening attempt on 21–22 April collapsed inside 24 hours. The structural constraint is unchanged: Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) controls passage via boarding parties, mines, and missile-warning announcements; reopening requires either a US strike sufficient to suppress IRGC infrastructure (which the Twelve-Day War did not durably achieve) or a negotiated framework — which the Iran response above has not yet delivered. Cross-reference: this is the dominant tail risk to the energy and packaging-resin transmission channel into Stefan's pet food cost-of-goods picture.

Lebanon / Hezbollah 6–10 May

Israel strikes Beirut southern suburbs for first time in a month; senior Radwan commander killed

On 6 May Israel struck Beirut's southern suburbs in the first such attack in nearly a month, killing a senior Hezbollah commander from the elite Radwan force. The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) ground deployment in southern Lebanon — five divisions deep since 16 March — continues; cumulative Lebanon civilian and combatant deaths from the air campaign are reported at 2,700+, with 1.2 million displaced. The April US–Iran ceasefire framework Hezbollah was theoretically a party to has, per Al Jazeera, been breached every day since it was announced. The 9 May IDF readout (Jerusalem Post live updates) noted three Israeli reservists injured by explosive drones during fresh strikes on Hezbollah in southern Lebanon. Hezbollah's continued ability to fight despite the cumulative pummeling is the strategic anomaly the CSMonitor piece tries to explain — distributed command, cached munitions, Iranian rebuild via Russia (see prior week's EUR 500m air-defence card). Operationally: Lebanon is the binding sub-issue blocking the wider Iran deal — Tehran insists any deal must halt Israeli–Hezbollah hostilities, Israel will not commit to that without Hezbollah disarmament, which Hezbollah won't accept.

Sanctions · US, EU enforcement updates
Sanctions 1–7 May

OFAC tightens Iran shadow banking net; Qingdao Haiye oil terminal blocked; Lukoil GL extended to 30 May

This week's US Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) actions continued the Economic Fury campaign. New blocking sanctions on 17 individuals (16 in Iran, 1 in UAE) and 18 entities under the Iran shadow-banking program — targeting the dollar-clearing and trade-finance plumbing that lets Iran monetize oil into hard currency. Qingdao Haiye Oil Terminal Co., Ltd. (a major China-based receiving terminal for Iranian crude) was blocked on 1 May; OFAC simultaneously issued Iran-related General License (GL) W authorizing wind-down of in-flight transactions until 31 May. OFAC also issued a financial-sector advisory on China-based "teapot" refineries — small/medium independent refiners that account for roughly 90% of Iran's oil exports — warning of secondary-sanctions risk to anyone in their finance chain. Russia track: GL 131E extended Lukoil International GmbH negotiation/wind-down authorization 30 days, now to 30 May. Read: the US is keeping divestiture optionality on the Lukoil side open while sharpening enforcement against the Iran/China oil-marketing arc. EU 20th-package transport-prohibition concrete dates have not yet been published.

Hybrid warfare · Europe
Hybrid Standing — early May

Russian hybrid tempo in Europe persists; GLOBSEC warns of 2026 escalation in cable, logistics, retail sabotage

No single high-profile sabotage incident this week, but the structural backdrop continues to harden. GLOBSEC, CEPA and the Dutch intelligence service AIVD all published or maintained warnings in the past week that Russia's hybrid-warfare campaign — undersea cable sabotage via shadow-fleet dual-use vessels, drone surveillance over critical infrastructure, attacks on logistics nodes, retail-chain arson, and defense-supply-chain disruption — is on an escalatory path through 2026. The cumulative count exceeds 150 suspected incidents across EU and NATO members since 2025. The strategic logic per GLOBSEC: Putin is using the current negotiation phase to buy time, not to seek peace, and hybrid pressure is the lower-rung escalation lever — below kinetic threshold but high enough to extract European concessions and erode support for Ukraine. Baltic Sea fibre cables remain the highest-frequency target. The point for Stefan: if a Ukraine settlement emerges from the 3-day-ceasefire baseline, hybrid activity is unlikely to wind down with it — historically Russian non-kinetic pressure persists or intensifies through diplomatic phases.

💡 Editor's view

What this week actually moves

  1. Russia internal is now the load-bearing variable, not the front line. 25% of corporate bonds at default risk, 11 Q1 technical defaults already matching 2024's full year, Q1 GDP at –0.5%, approval down ~12 points in four months, a Kremlin propagandist hospitalized for calling for Putin's resignation, named "we can't even take one region" quote in WaPo. The bond-market signal is the one to watch operationally — if the summer financial-crisis warning Russian officials reportedly gave Putin lands, the Kremlin's window to negotiate from strength compresses sharply. This is a faster degradation track than the territorial picture.
  2. The 3-day ceasefire is the first real synchronized truce of the war — but it's narrow and probably not extending without Donetsk concessions. 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner swap is the largest since 2022 and a structural breakthrough on humanitarian terms. The Kremlin's restated maximalist demand (Ukrainian withdrawal from all of Donetsk Oblast) is the gap. ISW's "year-long offensive stalled" assessment plus Kupyansk loss makes that demand harder to back with force. The Trump-brokered process is now real diplomacy, but the substantive terms still don't intersect.
  3. Iran said no to dismantlement — Hormuz stays the dominant energy tail risk. Trump's 14-point memo got an HEU-transfer-but-no-dismantlement counter, which he called "totally unacceptable." Hormuz at ~191 vessels in April vs. baseline 3,000 is a 94% closure, with 22,500 mariners stranded. Project Freedom paused. The structural read: no near-term clean reopening; expect Brent and shipping-insurance war premia to stay embedded into Q2. The April US–Iran ceasefire is degrading, not consolidating — Beirut suburbs strike on 6 May proves the Lebanon sub-issue is unresolved.
  4. Russia–Iran coupling is now visible in opposite directions. Iran's bottleneck is enrichment infrastructure and HEU stockpile management; Russia's is bond defaults and reserve depletion. Both leaderships are buying time. Sanctions design is catching up — OFAC's Qingdao block plus the China teapot-refinery advisory is exactly the right enforcement geometry for the Iran–Russia oil-marketing nexus that emerged last year. If the EU's 20th-package transport prohibition gets concrete dates this quarter, the joint EU/UK/US squeeze becomes operationally tighter than at any prior point in the war.
  5. Hybrid is the leverage Russia keeps regardless of front-line state. 150+ EU/NATO incidents since 2025, Baltic cable cuts via shadow-fleet vessels, AIVD-Dutch-intel warning, GLOBSEC explicit "buying time" framing. If a Ukraine settlement crystallizes, hybrid does not wind down with it — historically it persists or intensifies through diplomatic phases. Pet-food and consumer-supply-chain businesses with EU logistics exposure should treat this as a permanent operational backdrop, not a war-window phenomenon.
  6. The architecture has shifted: this is no longer two separate wars. Iran's response timing (10 May, same week as the Russia–Ukraine 3-day ceasefire) is not coincidental. Trump is running a coupled negotiation track where both files are leverage on each other — and both leaderships read it the same way. The "no enrichment / no dismantlement" gap on the Iran file and the "all of Donetsk" gap on the Russia file are the two substantive blockers. Neither closed this week. The diplomatic envelope opened, the content did not.

Wildcard: if Russia's bond-market cracks accelerate into June and a named SOE or large regional bank defaults publicly, the Kremlin's negotiating posture flips faster than the front-line picture would suggest. Watch the corporate refinancing calendar through end-Q2 — that's where the political clock and the fiscal clock cross.
