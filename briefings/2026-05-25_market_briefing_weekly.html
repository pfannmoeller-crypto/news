Geopolitics Risk

The weekend's main event: a draft US-Iran MOU with published terms — then Trump pumped the brakes

The negotiation that has driven the equity tape for a month took a concrete step over the weekend. On Sat 23 May Trump said a deal reopening the Strait of Hormuz was "largely negotiated" and would be announced soon. Details of a draft memorandum of understanding then emerged: a 60-day MOU (extendable by mutual consent) under which the Strait of Hormuz would reopen gradually with no tolls in parallel with the US ending its blockade of Iran's ports, the US granting sanctions waivers so Iran can sell oil, Iran clearing the mines it laid in the strait, and Iran committing never to pursue nuclear weapons and to negotiate a suspension of uranium enrichment. Then on Sun 24 May Trump deliberately cooled it — saying talks were "proceeding in an orderly and constructive manner" but that he had told negotiators "not to rush" because "time is on our side," and that the deal "isn't even fully negotiated yet." Iran has not confirmed: the semi-official Fars agency cited "wide disagreements," the foreign-ministry spokesperson called the draft a "framework agreement" needing 30–60 days of detail work, and Tasnim warned the draft could still collapse over frozen-asset and enrichment clauses. Rubio's framing — "significant progress, although not final progress" — is the honest read.

Fed

Nothing changed on rates — June is a near-certain hold, and PCE on Friday is the next real test

No fresh Fed news this period, but the picture set by last week's FOMC minutes is unchanged and worth restating before the data-heavy week ahead. Markets price roughly a 97.5% probability of no change at the 16–17 June meeting — Kevin Warsh's first as chair. Warsh has historically sounded dovish, but he is one vote on a committee whose April minutes showed a majority would hike if inflation stays elevated, with energy-driven inflation running near 4% against the 2% target. The key new input lands Fri 29 May: the core PCE price index, the Fed's preferred inflation gauge, alongside GDP and personal income/spending. A hot PCE would harden the hold-with-hike-bias regime; the Brent drop below $100 is the first data point in weeks pointing the other way on the energy-inflation pulse — but PCE measures April/May, before this weekend's oil move.

Oil

Brent breaks below $100 — down ~4.5% to near $99 — as oil prices in the draft deal

The cleanest market move of the period: Brent crude fell below the $100 mark for the first time this cycle, down roughly 4.5% from Friday's ~$104 close to near $99, with WTI sliding toward $92. The driver is straightforward — oil is pricing the higher probability that the US-Iran MOU holds and the Strait of Hormuz reopens with sanctions waivers letting Iranian barrels back onto the market. Two cautions keep this in proportion. First, this is optimism-priced, not delivery-priced: the MOU is unsigned, Trump is explicitly "not rushing," and the enriched-uranium stockpile and frozen-assets disputes are unresolved — a credible breakdown would snap Brent straight back through $104. Second, even sub-$100 Brent is still well above where it sat a year ago; the spring geopolitical premium has thinned, not disappeared. The blockade itself also stays in place until a deal is completed, so the supply that would actually push oil structurally lower has not yet arrived. For now, the break below $100 is the market's vote that the deal is more likely than not — a vote, not a settlement.

Editor's view

5 takeaways
  1. This was a genuinely thin period, and the honest thing is to say so. Wall Street and London were shut, the prior briefing already had Friday's close, and there is no new US equity tape. Padding a holiday bridge with restated content would be filler. The two things that actually happened — the weekend Iran MOU and Brent breaking below $100 — are real, and they are the whole briefing.
  2. The Iran deal got more concrete and less urgent in the same 48 hours — that combination is the signal. A published draft MOU with named terms is a real step up from "near a draft." But Trump then explicitly slowed the clock — "not to rush," "time is on our side" — and Iran is not confirming, with Fars citing "wide disagreements." The 23 May briefing warned the tape had "banked the good outcome." It now has a draft to point to, but also a US president signalling he will not be hurried and an Iranian side calling it only a framework. Markets are pricing a deal that the principals are deliberately not closing.
  3. Brent below $100 is the period's one clean datapoint — and it is a vote, not a settlement. Oil pricing through $100 says the market puts the deal at better-than-even odds. But the blockade stays until a deal completes, sanctions waivers have not been issued, and the enriched-uranium and frozen-asset clauses are unresolved. The barrels that would push oil structurally lower have not arrived. Treat sub-$100 Brent as a sentiment reading that reverses fast on a breakdown headline — not as a new floor.
  4. The real test is Tuesday-to-Friday, not this weekend. Markets reopen into Salesforce, Dell, Marvell and Snowflake — the AI-software-and-infrastructure prints that decide whether the earnings cushion extends past Nvidia — and into Friday's PCE. After eight up weeks into a quiet holiday, and with June the worst month historically in midterm years, the market finally has to let fundamentals catch up. The path of least resistance is no longer obviously up.
  5. Pet-food portfolio read. Brent below $100 is the most favourable diesel-and-freight datapoint for the DACH/EU cost base in months — but it is built on an unsigned MOU that the US side is explicitly "not rushing." Do not treat sub-$100 as durable: if the talks wobble, the chokepoint premium snaps straight back, and the structural Hormuz exposure is unresolved until a deal is completed and the blockade lifts. The hedge horizon set in the 23 May read still holds — at least two quarters of forward coverage on diesel-linked freight and named-supplier protein/packaging contracts. This week's oil move is a tactical tailwind to bank opportunistically, not a regime change to rebuild the cost model around.
