Grok V9-Medium is still pre-release in its mid-June window, Starship stays grounded, and Judge Sooknanan’s settlement ruling remains outstanding — the legal tail that never fired before the debut.

SpaceX · IPO book June 5–11 2026 · 3.5–4× covered

The book closed June 10 at 3.5–4 times oversubscribed — over $250 billion of demand for a $75 billion deal — and SPCX priced June 11 at the fixed $135

The fixed-price gamble paid off. Per Bloomberg, SpaceX’s initial public offering (IPO) drew more orders than shares available within a day of the roadshow opening, and by the time books closed on Wednesday, June 10, institutional demand ran at 3.5 to 4 times the $75 billion raise — over $250 billion in orders — with multiple institutions individually bidding $10 billion or more. Unusually, SpaceX reserved up to 30% of the 555.6-million-share offering for retail investors, an order of magnitude above the single-digit retail slice typical for deals this size. Pricing followed on June 11 at the fixed $135 per share, confirming the ~$1.75 trillion valuation. One sobering counter-signal amid the euphoria: TechTimes reported the gray-market premium slid into pricing — unofficial pre-debut trading suggested the first-print pop was shrinking, not growing, as the free money got arbitraged away. (Bloomberg books close, Yahoo $10B orders, TechTimes gray-market slide)

So what: Last week’s open question — does the book fill at $135 with the rocket grounded? — is answered, emphatically. A 3.5–4× covered book means the marginal buyer fully underwrote the AI-infrastructure option without needing Starship flying, which validates the read that SPCX is being bought as a compute-and-scarcity asset, not a launch company. The 30% retail allocation is the structural novelty: it dilutes the institutional-only float dynamics we flagged, but it also seeds a retail shareholder base that behaves very differently in drawdowns. The shrinking gray-market premium is the honest number in the noise — it says the $135 price captured most of the value, leaving less first-day pop than the hype implies.

The official start of trading was delayed roughly 30 minutes, to about 9:50 a.m. Eastern Time, as designated market-making worked through the order imbalance — Fast Company tied the frenzy partly to intense prediction-market activity. Where it opens is the live question at compile time: prediction markets on Binance and Polymarket clustered around a $150–200 opening range, a Benzinga reader poll had 87% expecting an open above $135, and perpetual futures on the decentralised exchange Hyperliquid quoted SpaceX near $168 — a ~25% premium. Goldman Sachs led the underwriting syndicate, with Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Citigroup and JPMorgan Chase behind it. This card is deliberately unfinished: the opening print and first-day close were not confirmed when this briefing was compiled mid-session. (CNBC live updates, Fast Company delayed start, TradingKey prediction markets)

So what: The thing to watch is not the first print but the first week. A modest pop (the gray market’s ~+25%, versus the +50% retail dream) would actually be the bullish-structure outcome — it would mean the bankers priced a $1.75 trillion company within a quarter-turn of clearing, on the biggest deal ever, which de-risks the inevitable follow-on raises Starship and the data-centre build will demand. The bear mechanics to monitor: a thin effective float plus a 30% retail tranche plus prediction-market leverage is a recipe for violent two-way swings before the shareholder base settles. And note the second-order event: SPCX listing makes the Muskverse partially markable for the first time — every Tesla “SpaceX halo” argument now has a daily price to check itself against.

SpaceX · Starship no movement · still grounded

Starship: no movement in the window — still grounded, Flight 13 still July–August, and the FAA “closes”-versus-open ambiguity carries over unresolved

Continuity item. Nothing new surfaced this week on the Flight 12 mishap file: the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) still requires SpaceX to complete the investigation into Booster 19’s premature engine shutdown and failed boostback ignitions (May 22), and to win approval of root cause and corrective actions before Flight 13 (Ship 40 + Booster 20) can fly — the reported window remains July–August 2026. The documentary ambiguity flagged last week is still live: an FAA newsroom page titled “FAA Closes SpaceX Starship Mishap Investigation” exists, while early-June trade reporting (MyRGV, June 2) described the probe as SpaceX’s third open mishap investigation since January 2025. No launch-license announcement either way during IPO week. The stake is unchanged: Starlink Version 3 satellites fly only on Starship, and the S-1’s second-half-2026 V3 deployment commitment compresses with every grounded week. (FAA newsroom page, SpaceNews requirement, MyRGV third probe)

So what: The market just told you it doesn’t care — for now. A 4×-covered book with the flagship vehicle grounded means Starship risk is priced as a timing question, not an existential one. That holds only while the July–August Flight 13 window holds. Now that SPCX trades daily, Starship news stops being a private-company footnote and becomes a same-day stock catalyst: the first post-IPO earnings frame, the corrective-action approval, and the Flight 13 license are the next three dates that move a public ticker. Anchor on the launch license, not the newsroom headline.

Tesla · robotaxi June 3–4 2026 · geofence unfrozen

The robotaxi freeze ended — unsupervised service now covers the entire ~4,300-square-mile Austin metro — but the active driverless fleet is ~20 cars, and it’s been shrinking

The expansion freeze we carried for a month broke, in the least expected dimension. On June 3–4, Tesla expanded its unsupervised robotaxi geofence to the entire Austin metropolitan area — roughly 4,286 square miles including Pflugerville, Manor, the I-35 corridor and Austin-Bergstrom International Airport. “Unsupervised” means no safety driver in the front seats, though remote operators still monitor feeds and can intervene; this is the fifth geofence expansion since the Austin pilot began. The catch is the denominator: Electrek reports only ~20 active unsupervised vehicles serving the whole metro — a number that has been shrinking, versus ~50 total Tesla robotaxis per city officials and Waymo’s 250+ vehicles in the same market. Early field tests showed the strain: a reporter logged wait times up to 30 minutes and intermittent inability to book a ride at all. Separately, Cathie Wood took a publicised driverless Tesla ride during the week — the demand-side narrative machine is running. (Electrek ~20 vehicles, CleanTechnica metro expansion, GuruFocus wait times)

So what: Read the asymmetry carefully: Tesla expanded the map, not the fleet. A 4,300-square-mile geofence with 20 cars is a software-confidence statement (the stack now handles highways, airport runs, suburban edge cases) wrapped around a hardware-deployment failure (the fleet stopped growing in May and hasn’t restarted). Thirty-minute waits are what happens when coverage outruns capacity by two orders of magnitude. The bull case is that geofence-first is the right order — prove the operating domain, then flood it with Cybercabs. The bear case is that the fleet number, not the map, is the binding constraint, and it is moving the wrong way while Waymo runs 12× the vehicles in Tesla’s showcase city. Until the vehicle count inflects, this is a demo at metro scale.

Barclays followed June 8, calling robotaxi the critical growth driver in Tesla’s artificial-intelligence (AI) strategy. The $25 billion 2026 capital-expenditure programme — robotaxi infrastructure, AI compute, the AI5 inference chip, Optimus — remains the cash-burn backdrop, with free cash flow guided negative for the rest of 2026. (Capital.com $396 & JPM upgrade, GuruFocus Barclays note, HeyGoTrade capex)

So what: The June 8 briefing called Tesla “the asset most exposed to a sell-the-SPCX-news unwind” — the ~8% slide into debut week is consistent with exactly that rotation: why hold the proxy when the real thing lists Friday? The J.P. Morgan move is the more interesting signal, though: a $145→$475 target isn’t an upgrade, it’s a capitulation to the autonomy-and-Optimus framing by the street’s most prominent Tesla bear — the narrative war over what Tesla is just lost its loudest dissenter. Watch whether SPCX’s first trading week pulls halo capital back out of TSLA or re-anchors the whole complex higher; and keep Optimus V3 (July–August reveal) as the next Tesla-native catalyst.

Grok June 2026 · pre-release

Grok V9-Medium holds its mid-June window — new detail: it was trained heavily on Cursor coding data, with Musk promising “much better at coding,” but still no benchmarks

A quiet week for the AI division by its standards — the model everyone is waiting on hasn’t shipped yet. Grok V9-Medium (1.5 trillion parameters, ~3× the ~500-billion-parameter V8-Small in production) remains in supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning, with the public release still expected mid-June — i.e., imminently. The week’s incremental detail is the training recipe: per TechTimes and builder analyses, a significant volume of Cursor data — real-world developer workflows from the AI code editor — was incorporated in supplementary training, and Musk’s positioning is explicitly a coding play (“much better at coding”). The competitive gap it must close is documented: Grok 4 scores ~75% on SWE-bench Verified (a benchmark of real software-engineering fixes) versus Claude Opus 4.7’s 87.6%, and Grok sits at roughly 6% enterprise adoption against OpenAI’s 55%, Anthropic’s 47% and Google’s 39%. No V9 benchmarks have been published. (TechTimes Cursor data, AI CERTs adoption gap, xAI release notes)

So what: The Cursor-data detail sharpens what V9 is for: not a general-assistant play but a direct run at the coding-agent market, which is where the enterprise money and the Anthropic moat both sit. The 6%-versus-47% adoption gap is the number that matters more than parameters — xAI’s problem has never been model scale, it’s distribution and trust in enterprise procurement. With SPCX now public, V9’s release lands differently: a strong benchmark print is no longer just lab PR, it is same-week support for the AI-revenue line in a listed company’s story. The re-rating date we set last week stands — judge Grok on the released model’s benchmarks, due within days.

So what: “Special” is the tell that the DOGE network is converting political capital into private-market positioning before the charter formally lapses — an AI-roll-up fund staffed by people who spent eighteen months inside federal cost structures is a genuinely novel asset, whatever one thinks of the provenance. For the portfolio ledger it confirms the soft-landing path: no July 4 reckoning, just diffusion of DOGE people and methods into OPM on the government side and ventures like Special on the private side. This remains a fading variable for the Muskverse proper; the July 4 charter expiry is now a calendar footnote, not a catalyst.

SEC · OpenAI June 5–12 2026 · still pending

No Sooknanan ruling — the SEC settlement tail-risk window closed around the IPO without firing, and the docket goes quiet into the post-debut period

Continuity item, and a meaningful non-event. U.S. District Judge Sparkle Sooknanan has still not ruled on the proposed $1.5 million settlement of the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) suit alleging Musk’s late disclosure of his 2022 Twitter stake saved him over $150 million at shareholders’ expense. The state of play is unchanged from the June 1–3 filings: both sides defended the deal — penalty paid by a Musk-linked trust, no admission of wrongdoing — as “fair, reasonable and appropriate” and free of collusion, after Sooknanan’s May statements that she would not “rubber stamp” an agreement whose structure raised “red flags.” The scenario we tracked for three weeks — a rejection landing inside the pricing-and-debut window — did not materialise: SPCX priced June 11 and listed June 12 with the matter simply pending. The Musk v. Altman / OpenAI thread stays parked on the 9th Circuit appeal docket; nothing moved this week. (Insurance Journal filings, Law.com no rubber stamp, CPA Practice Advisor)

So what: The calendar risk is spent but the legal risk is not. A post-debut rejection by Sooknanan would now hit a public SpaceX — reopened SEC enforcement against the controlling shareholder of a listed $1.75 trillion company is a materially different disclosure event than the same news against a private one, and SPCX’s risk factors will own it. The cynical read — that the judge sat on the ruling through the IPO window — is unsupported; judges take months on contested consent decrees routinely. Keep it on the watch list at lower urgency: the binary is unchanged (approve and close, or reject and reopen), only the blast radius has grown.

insightsEditor’s View

Six reads on the week the Muskverse went public

  1. 1 The book answered last week’s question: the market bought the option, not the rocket. 3.5–4× oversubscribed, $250B+ in demand, $10B single-institution orders — all with Starship grounded and no Flight 13 license. The marginal SPCX buyer underwrote the AI-infrastructure and orbital-compute thesis on the strength of the Google contract and the scarcity narrative alone. That is now an empirical fact about how the largest pools of capital price this story.
  2. 2 The shrinking gray-market premium is the most honest number of the week. Prediction markets dream of $150–200; the gray market drifted toward ~+25% and falling into pricing. If the debut pops modestly rather than violently, that’s the bankers’ win — a $1.75T company priced within a quarter-turn of clearing — and it preserves credibility for the follow-on raises the capex plan will inevitably demand. Judge the IPO on week one, not minute one.
  3. 3 Everything in the Muskverse is now marked to a daily price. SPCX listing converts Starship licenses, Grok benchmarks, even Sooknanan’s ruling from private-company footnotes into same-day stock catalysts. It also disciplines the Tesla “SpaceX halo” trade: the halo now has its own ticker, and TSLA’s ~8% slide into debut week looks like exactly that capital rotating to the source.
  4. 4 Tesla expanded the map, not the fleet — don’t confuse the two. A 4,300-square-mile unsupervised geofence is a real software milestone; ~20 active driverless cars (and shrinking) against Waymo’s 250+ in the same city is the operating reality, and 30-minute waits are the arithmetic. The freeze we tracked since May 10 broke in the dimension that costs nothing. The vehicle count is the number to watch — it, not the geofence, decides whether the J.P. Morgan capitulation ($145→$475, Underweight→Neutral) was early or late.
  5. 5 Grok V9 lands in days, into higher stakes than it was announced for. The Cursor-training detail says xAI is aiming squarely at the coding-agent market where the enterprise revenue lives — and where Grok’s 6% adoption versus Anthropic’s 47% and a 75-vs-87.6 SWE-bench gap define the climb. Post-listing, a strong V9 print is balance-sheet-relevant news for a public company, not lab PR. A weak one punctures the “frontier lab worth the clusters” half of the SPCX thesis in week one of trading.
  6. 6 The tails that didn’t fire still exist — they just got bigger consequences. Sooknanan’s ruling and the Starship corrective-action approval both slipped past the IPO window harmlessly, but each now detonates (or resolves) against a public ticker: a settlement rejection means reopened SEC enforcement against the controlling shareholder of a listed $1.75T company; a Flight 13 slip past August guts the H2 Starlink V3 commitment in the S-1. The wildcard for next week: the actual SPCX close — and whether Grok V9 ships inside it.
