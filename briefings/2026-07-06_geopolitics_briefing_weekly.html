Geopolitics · 10-Day Catch-Up

The Iran de-escalation held: Hormuz is reopening, Brent is back at ~$72 — and now Khamenei's funeral freezes diplomacy while the successor stays invisible.

Ten days that mostly confirmed the June framework rather than breaking it. Doha talks made "positive progress" on the Strait of Hormuz and frozen assets, tanker traffic through the strait climbed back above 10 million barrels per day, and oil analysts pivoted from shortage to glut warnings. The wild card is now internal to Iran: the public funeral of Ali Khamenei (4–9 July) pauses the negotiating clock, and new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei has not appeared in public since taking office. Ukraine went the other way — Russia's June offensive collapsed at staggering cost, and Moscow answered with the deadliest Kyiv strike in months while rejecting the frontline-freeze idea outright. The 30 June US deadline for a Ukraine deal passed without visible consequence.

De-escalation30 Jun – 2 Jul

Doha talks: "positive progress" on Hormuz and frozen assets, a standing communication channel — and a fight over who gets $12B when

US and Iranian negotiators spent two days in Doha meeting separately with Qatari and Pakistani mediators (no direct contact), focused on maritime traffic in the Strait of Hormuz and unfreezing Iranian funds. Both sides agreed to continue, and Iran said it would open a standing "communication channel" on implementing the MoU. Vice President JD Vance called the talks "going well" and said the nuclear file — enrichment and the enriched-uranium stockpile — would be taken up soon; it has not really been negotiated yet. The visible crack: Iranian parliament speaker Ghalibaf claims $12B of ~$24B in frozen assets will be made available now, while US officials insist nothing is released until Iran demonstrably complies. The next round is deferred until after Khamenei's funeral rites end (~9 July).

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The 17 June memorandum of understanding — Qatar's foreign ministry calls it the "Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding", building on the "Lake Lucerne Summit" — opened a 60-day negotiation window toward a permanent deal: toll-free Hormuz transit, a temporary US sanctions waiver, frozen-asset releases. The Doha round followed days of tit-for-tat military strikes: in late June the US hit Iranian targets for two nights, citing a drone attack on a commercial vessel, and both sides accused each other of violating the MoU. The mediation architecture itself tells the story — Qatar and Pakistan shuttle between delegations that never sit in the same room.

Die Details — Iran's technical team was led by Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi; the top negotiators, Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi and Speaker Ghalibaf, stayed home. On the US side, envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner met Qatar's Emir Sheikh Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani. Per Gharibabadi there were two working meetings: one on the US's "violations of its obligations", producing the standing dispute-resolution channel; one on frozen funds — specifically an initial $6 billion tranche administered via Qatar's Central Bank, under which "required goods would be purchased and made available to Iran" — procurement in kind, not cash transfers. That is a much narrower mechanism than Ghalibaf's public "$12B now" claim, a gap worth keeping in view (Iranian domestic messaging inflates; the deep architecture mirrors the 2023 Qatar-administered $6B arrangement). Data firm Kpler counted commercial transits through Hormuz up more than 50% week-on-week (22–28 June). The nuclear file remains untouched — and contested: Ghalibaf says inspectors of the International Atomic Energy Agency currently access only the Bushehr power plant and the Tehran research reactor, with a parliamentary law and a Supreme National Security Council resolution barring access to the bombed sites Fordow, Natanz and Isfahan; IAEA chief Rafael Grossi insists inspectors "have to have access" under the MoU.

Einordnung — Institutionalization without trust: separate rooms, but a permanent channel — that is how adversaries build ceasefires that survive incidents. The programmed collision is nuclear: IAEA access is (per the US reading) in the MoU but barred by Iranian law, and the US sanctions waiver expires 21 August, days after the 60-day clock runs out. Someone must blink within ~6 weeks, and the Iranian someone is currently invisible (next card).

Watch-Items — Scheduling of the next round after the 9 July burial; whether the communication channel survives its first shooting incident; any movement on IAEA access or the parliamentary law; who on the Iranian side actually signs.

Succession4–9 Jul

Khamenei's state funeral runs 4–9 July — and new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei has still not been seen in public

Iran is holding the delayed public funeral for Ali Khamenei, assassinated 28 February in the war's opening phase; his son Mojtaba Khamenei (56) was named Supreme Leader on 9 March by the Assembly of Experts. The striking fact four months on: the new leader has not appeared or spoken publicly since his ascension, despite posters across Tehran. US Defense Secretary Hegseth said in March he was "wounded and likely disfigured" in the strike that killed his father, mother and wife; Reuters reported severe facial and leg injuries, possibly an amputated leg. The funeral is staged as a show of regime strength, but an invisible leader during the most consequential negotiation in the Islamic Republic's history is a real governance question: it is genuinely unclear who signs off on a final deal. Health details are intelligence-grade reporting — de-rated.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The 28 February strike that opened the war killed not only Ali Khamenei after 36 years of rule but much of his household: his daughter, a son-in-law, a daughter-in-law, a 14-month-old granddaughter — and Mojtaba's wife, Zahra Haddad-Adel. The Assembly of Experts installed Mojtaba on 9 March in a compressed, wartime succession. The public funeral was then deferred for over four months on security grounds; holding it now, during the negotiation pause, is a deliberate choice.

Die Details — The state has mounted a six-day, multi-city choreography beginning 4 July: private lying-in-state with foreign dignitaries, then public display under a glass enclosure at Tehran's Grand Mosalla, processions planned through the Shia holy cities of Karbala and Najaf in Iraq and Qom in Iran, and burial in Mashhad on 9 July. Tehran's metro logged ~7 million passenger journeys in a single night-to-morning span; the government is providing transport, food and lodging to mass mourners. Three of the late leader's sons — Mostafa, Meysam and Masoud — prayed publicly behind the coffins; President Masoud Pezeshkian, Speaker Ghalibaf and Quds Force commander Esmail Qaani attended; Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif was among dozens of foreign delegations. The crowds' tone was not quiet grief: red flags of revenge, chants of "Death to America", and mourners telling the Associated Press they came "to shout and seek revenge". Officially, Mojtaba's absence — even from his own wife's and father's rites — is due to Israeli assassination threats; Reuters' sourcing describes severe facial injuries and major damage to one or both legs. None of the health reporting is independently verifiable.

Einordnung — Al Jazeera's summary of the official message — "continuity and revenge" — captures the tension: the regime needs the funeral to demonstrate mass loyalty AND needs the deal the crowds are chanting against. Meanwhile Trump publicly vowed calm during the funeral days, and Turkey's Erdogan warned that Israel must not be allowed to "dynamite" the US–Iran deal — outside powers are treating the mourning week as a fragile window, not a threat. The structural question stays open: a Supreme Leader who cannot appear cannot visibly arbitrate between the IRGC (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps) faction toggling Hormuz and the government faction negotiating in Doha. Four months of invisibility looks less like recuperation and more like a regency-by-committee that no one has announced.

Watch-Items — Any appearance, audio message or signed decree from Mojtaba by/after the 9 July burial; who visibly chairs Supreme National Security Council decisions; whether funeral-week revenge rhetoric translates into IRGC action in the strait; Netanyahu's floated Washington visit (Trump: possibly "as early as next week") as an Israeli-pressure marker.

Reopening26 Jun – 5 Jul

Hormuz reopens faster than expected — flows back above 10M barrels/day — but IRGC radio warnings still trigger tanker U-turns

Shipping through the strait is recovering faster than analysts expected: the UAE restored exports above 3.9 million barrels per day, Saudi Arabia ramped exports to Asia, and total daily flows through Hormuz crossed 10 million barrels. But the recovery is uneven and jumpy. On 4 July a batch of tankers performed unexplained U-turns in the US-protected corridor after a reported VHF-radio (ship-to-ship voice) warning from the IRGC Navy (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps — Iran's parallel military, which runs the fast-boat fleet); patrols were confirmed operating in the strait, traffic resumed a day later along the Oman-side route. Western navies still rate the threat "substantial" and say the center of the strait remains mined. Liquefied natural gas (LNG) and fertilizer shipments are effectively still absent — relevant to the pet-food cost briefing's energy and input-logistics threads.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The strait was effectively shut from 28 February: IRGC radio warnings, ship boardings and attacks (the seized Epaminondas, the burning Al Salmi off Dubai), and sea mines. At the peak, shipments were down >90% — roughly 10 million barrels per day off the market, the largest supply disruption since the 1970s; the International Maritime Organization counted ~2,000 ships and 20,000 mariners stranded in the Gulf; QatarEnergy, Kuwait Petroleum and Bahrain's Bapco declared force majeure (contractual suspension due to events beyond control). The 17 June MoU promises 60 days of toll-free transit — with Tehran reading the text as leaving it joint control with Oman.

Die Details — The recovery is real but the metrics don't line up neatly, so precision claims deserve suspicion: Kpler counted transits up >50% week-on-week (22–28 June); Morgan Stanley counted 35 oil-and-gas tankers exiting on 2 July — the first day back within the typical pre-war range for that tanker class; live trackers put total transits near ~35/day against a ~110/day all-vessel pre-war average. Our "10M barrels/day" headline figure is a flow estimate, not a transit count — directionally corroborated, but mixing metrics across sources. The friction is equally real: on 4 July at least eight ships U-turned on the Oman-coast route after a reported IRGC Navy VHF warning; a day later a five-vessel convoy — a laden crude tanker leading LPG (liquefied petroleum gas) and LNG carriers plus a container ship — threaded the same route. An IRGC statement re-declared the strait "closed", citing Israeli strikes in southern Lebanon as a "blatant breach" of the MoU. Iran says it will "definitely" charge transit fees when the 60 free days end, with "special treatment" for friendly nations, and has warned the UK and France. Western navies still rate the threat "substantial"; the strait's center remains mined — one report says Iran has lost track of some of its own mines. Under UNCLOS (the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea), transit passage through an international strait cannot be tolled — a legal collision already rejected flatly by Washington and London.

Einordnung — Iran has converted the strait from a closed valve into a dial: cheap, deniable signals (a VHF warning costs nothing) that move insurance premiums and tanker behavior instantly. That dial is the IRGC's institutional property — which makes strait behavior the best real-time indicator of Tehran's internal balance during the succession opacity. Crude is normalizing first; LNG and fertilizer flows lag badly (Qatar's force-majeure legacy), which keeps European gas and agricultural-input costs elevated — the direct feed into the pet-food cost briefing.

Watch-Items — Transit counts after 9 July; the toll law in Iran's parliament vs the US "no tolling" red line as the 60-day window closes (~mid-August); first large-scale Qatari LNG loadings; war-risk insurance premiums; mine-clearance progress.

Oil pivot2 Jul

From shortage to glut in three weeks: Brent back at ~$72, Morgan Stanley cuts forecasts twice, OPEC pushes back

The oil market has fully priced the de-escalation: Brent is trading around $72/barrel — roughly pre-war levels (it was near $87-equivalent Urals pricing at the June peak). Morgan Stanley cut its oil forecast twice in two weeks, warning that resumed Hormuz flows plus weak Chinese import demand could tip the market into oversupply; the monthly analyst consensus for 2026 average Brent dropped from $90.44 to $84.50. OPEC's secretary general publicly rejected the glut thesis. For the Russia file this cuts both ways: cheaper crude squeezes Moscow's already-collapsing oil revenues (see Russia internal), but it also weakens the EU's rationale for keeping its price cap frozen. Full detail in the market briefing — this is the same master variable from the other side.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The whiplash scale: in March, Dubai crude touched a record $166 and Brent traded above $126; the International Energy Agency's 32 members released 400 million barrels of strategic reserves; Gulf exporters' shipments collapsed from ~25M to ~10M barrels/day amid force majeures. The 17 June MoU plus the temporary US waiver on Iranian oil reversed the shock in under three weeks.

Die Details — On 2 July Brent settled at $70.78 and US benchmark WTI (West Texas Intermediate) at $67.74 — a third consecutive ~1% daily decline. The supply surge has three engines: resumed Gulf transit (35 tankers exiting daily); Iranian barrels returning — 20M+ barrels ready to sail (+18% week-on-week) and an estimated 58–68 million barrels already on the water (Vortexa/Bloomberg data), over 90% of it with no fixed destination because Chinese independent refiners, Iran's pre-war customers, have contracted elsewhere; and record output in the Americas — the US set an all-time monthly record of 13.934M barrels/day in April, with Canada, Brazil and Argentina also up. Demand-side: China slashed imports during the price spike, drew commercial stockpiles, and re-sourced from Russia, Kazakhstan, Brazil, Indonesia and Venezuela. The expert caveats are worth quoting: Marburg economist Mohammad Reza Farzanegan calls it "a temporary surplus risk under high political uncertainty, rather than a stable oil glut"; IEEFA (Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis) analyst Kevin Morrison notes full 20M barrels/day restoration is "unlikely until sometime next year" given war damage to Gulf production infrastructure.

Einordnung — Two deadlines make the glut thesis fragile: the US sanctions relief for Iran expires 21 August — if not extended, up to ~2M barrels/day of Iranian exports go dark again; and the US midterm elections in November cut both ways (pressure to avoid a pre-vote price shock argues for de-escalation, but a hawkish turn is not priced). For the Russia file the price slide is the quiet main event: every dollar off Brent compounds the −45% revenue collapse in the next card. China emerges with restored pricing leverage over every producer, including Moscow.

Watch-Items — The 21 August waiver decision; Chinese import volumes restarting (or not); OPEC+ quota response to returning Iranian barrels; Brent below $65 (glut confirmed) vs back above $80 (risk premium returns).

Lebanon26–30 Jun

Lebanon fault line: Rubio announces a framework deal (26 Jun), Hezbollah rejects it — then a trilateral wind-down committee anyway (30 Jun)

The fault line the last edition flagged produced both directions at once. On 26 June Secretary of State Marco Rubio announced a US-mediated Israel–Lebanon framework for "lasting peace and security" — ceasefire, Hezbollah ends hostilities and withdraws from southern Lebanon. Hezbollah rejected it. Yet on 30 June Iran announced that Iran, the US and Lebanon agreed to establish a committee to oversee the conclusion of the war — the deconfliction mechanism sketched in the 22 June road map, now formally stood up. Net read: the state-level architecture is being built over Hezbollah's head, which contains escalation for now but leaves the militia as the unbound variable. No repeat (in this window's reporting) of the near-retaliation spirals of early June — a genuine improvement.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — Lebanon has been the MoU's designated fault line since June: Iran demanded Israel stop striking Hezbollah as part of implementation; Israel vowed to intensify; a US-brokered truce of 19 June held only nominally, with the IRGC repeatedly citing Israeli strikes in southern Lebanon as MoU violations (including in this window, as justification for Hormuz warnings). Hezbollah has been a party to none of these documents.

Die Details — The 26 June framework Rubio announced goes far beyond a ceasefire on paper: Lebanon recognizes "the legitimacy of Israel as a state", commits to "formally conclude any state of war", and — the maximal clause — commits to full disarmament of Hezbollah and dismantling of its infrastructure. Washington attached a $100M aid pledge. Hezbollah, excluded from the negotiation, rejected it through MP Hassan Fadlallah as "unilateral, gratuitous concessions", and stated its own position: the US–Iran MoU is the only valid framework for ending hostilities and securing Israeli withdrawal. Then the second track: on 30 June Iran announced that Iran, the US and Lebanon agreed to a committee overseeing the war's conclusion — with named participants US Ambassador to Lebanon Michel Issa, Israeli Ambassador to the US Yechiel Leiter, and Lebanese Ambassador to the US Nada Hamadeh Moawad. Note the composition: Israel is in the room, making the "trilateral" committee de facto four-party — and it exists because Iran wants it, not despite Iran.

Einordnung — Washington is splitting the file: the Lebanese state gets a peace framework plus aid; Iran gets a channel that implicitly acknowledges its standing in Lebanon; Hezbollah gets bypassed by both. The disarmament clause is declaratory — the Lebanese state has never had the capacity to disarm Hezbollah by force, and no timeline or mechanism has been published. The two architectures could converge (Iran pressures Hezbollah to stand down as part of its own deal economics) or collide (Hezbollah acts autonomously to prove it cannot be bypassed — the classic spoiler play, which Fox-cited analysts expect hardline factions to attempt). The committee's real function may be exactly that: a place to contain the first spoiler incident.

Watch-Items — Israeli strike tempo in southern Lebanon (the IRGC's stated Hormuz trigger); the committee's first convening and whether Hezbollah engages indirectly; publication of the framework text; whether the $100M aid is conditioned on disarmament steps.

Escalation2 Jul

Deadliest Kyiv strike in months: at least 22 killed after Zelensky warned of a "massive" attack — Moscow's answer to battlefield failure

Hours after Zelensky publicly warned of an impending "massive" Russian strike, Russia hit Kyiv and other cities with a combined missile-and-drone attack that killed at least 22 people and injured dozens; the Russian Defence Ministry itself described it as a "massive attack" with long-range precision weapons. Context matters: this came in the same week Russia's ground offensive visibly failed (next card) and after the 30 June diplomatic deadline lapsed. The pattern — losing on the ground, escalating from the air — is Moscow's standard negotiation-season behavior, and it landed while Washington's attention was consumed by Iran.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The warning itself was unusual: Zelensky, at a joint press conference in Dublin with Irish Taoiseach Micheál Martin on 1 July, said Ukraine had "relevant intelligence data" of an imminent massive strike and told citizens to shelter — then flew straight home. Air-raid volumes had been climbing all week (a 29 June barrage killed at least eight), and the strike landed in the vacuum right after the lapsed 30 June deadline.

Die Details — The overnight barrage of 2 July combined more than 70 missiles — nearly half of them ballistic — with roughly 500 attack drones, including jet-powered Shahed variants (Fox News tallied nearly 600 projectiles total, calling it the deadliest strike since May). More than 20 sites were hit in Kyiv alone: residential apartment blocks (one nine-storey building partially collapsed with people trapped), a hotel on central Shevchenko Boulevard, a research institute and an ambulance station. The death toll is genuinely unsettled across sources: 17–18 (NPR/AP day-of), "at least 22" (our headline basis), up to "at least 25 killed, 80+ injured" per Ukraine's State Emergency Service in later counts — the range reflects reporting cutoffs, and we carry it as a range rather than pick a number. The shock propagated beyond Ukraine: Poland scrambled fighter jets and Finland restricted airspace during the attack. Mayor Klitschko declared a day of mourning. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov claimed the strike targeted "military or military-related facilities" and vowed Russia would "continue increasing pressure"; the Russian Defence Ministry framed it as retaliation for Ukraine's strikes inside Russia.

Einordnung — Analysis of why this strike was exceptionally deadly converges on saturation economics: mixing ballistic missiles (minutes of warning, only Patriot-class systems can intercept) into mass drone waves is designed to exhaust and out-multiply Kyiv's thinning interceptor stock — and interceptor resupply is precisely the request Washington has deprioritized while managing the Gulf. Strategically it is the mirror of the collapsed ground offensive (next card): unable to convert 39,000 monthly casualties into territory, Moscow escalates where it retains an advantage — deep-strike volume against cities — both as punishment and as negotiation pricing.

Watch-Items — US/European Patriot and interceptor deliveries; whether multi-hundred-drone strikes become weekly cadence; Ukrainian counter-escalation via the refinery campaign; NATO eastern-flank air posture after the Poland/Finland alarms.

Front3 Jul

Russia's June offensive collapses: ~39,500 casualties in one month for a half-year net gain of 97 km²

Ukraine's military estimates Russia took ~39,490 casualties in June alone — far above its estimated recruitment intake of 24,000–30,000 a month — against a net territorial gain of just 97 km² for all of H1 2026. Cumulative Russian casualties are put at ~1.4 million since 2022. Ukraine credits its "Logistical Lockdown" strategy — systematic mid- and long-range strikes on fuel and ammunition supply lines — and Zelensky has announced a 40-day strike campaign against Russia explicitly framed as compelling Moscow to end the war (consistent with the standing ~40%-of-refining-capacity claim from June). Caveat: these are Ukrainian figures with the usual incentive to overstate; the direction (offensive stalled at extreme cost, anxiety rising in Moscow) is corroborated, the precision is not.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — Russia's declared objective is the remaining ~20% of Donetsk oblast by year-end — after, per Kyiv's count, 14 missed deadlines for the same goal. The comparison year is brutal for Moscow: in H1 2025 Russian forces took 2,190 km² gross; in H1 2026, 622 km² gross (ISW — Institute for the Study of War, which builds estimates from geolocated open-source data). That is 1.03 km²/day vs 16.6 km²/day. Strip out unconsolidated infiltrations and add Ukrainian counter-advances, and the net is the headline 97 km². At the current rate, ISW math says finishing Donetsk alone would take ~5,150 days — 14 years.

Die Details — The cost side: ISW calculates 1,298 Russian casualties per km² taken in June 2026, vs 68 per km² in June 2025 — a twentyfold deterioration in exchange efficiency. CSIS (Center for Strategic and International Studies) put cumulative Russian casualties at ~1.4 million as of 1 July. The mechanism is "Logistical Lockdown", the doctrine named by Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov: mid-range strikes on warehouses, resupply convoys and bridges rose from 210 in May to 303 in June (ISW), while the long-range campaign in this window alone hit the Ufa, Nizhegorodsky, Slavyansk and Yaroslavl refineries, the cable-laying ships Volga and Vyatka under construction in the Kerch Strait, three satellite-communication centres near Moscow (Beloomut, Minyayevo, Dubna), the Titan-Barrikady ammunition plant in Volgograd, Saky airfield in Crimea, a missile-parts institute in Penza — plus 12 electricity substations in southern Crimea in two days. Unmanned Systems Forces commander Robert "Magyar" Brovdi claims a target struck every 52 seconds in June (50,147 targets). The blowback inside Russia is visible: fuel shortages in most regions, a diesel export ban extended on 26 June, emergency imports of refined products from India (60,000 tonnes, targeting 400,000 t/month), Deputy PM Novak blaming "hype" for 20–30% demand spikes, and Putin reassuring citizens about 1.7M tonnes of gasoline reserves.

Einordnung — Sourcing caveat first: the casualty and strike figures are Ukrainian or Ukraine-adjacent (ISW leans on Ukrainian reporting), and 39,490 casualties in a month would be extraordinary — treat the precision skeptically. But the corroboration is what Russia itself shows: fuel rationing videos, import dependence, export bans, and a state-media tone shift that Al Jazeera describes as triumphalism giving way to anxiety. The attrition equation — ~39k losses vs 24–30k monthly recruitment — is the strategic crux: if even directionally true, Russia must either mobilize again (politically radioactive before the Duma season) or watch its force shrink while holding a 1,000-km front.

Watch-Items — July loss/gain rates (one month is not a trend); any covert or overt mobilization signal; refinery repair speed vs strike tempo; the 40-day campaign's end (~early August) as a built-in diplomatic checkpoint.

Stalled26 Jun – 6 Jul

The 30 June deadline passed with no deal and no visible US consequence; Lavrov rejects the frontline freeze outright

The US-set 30 June window for Ukraine–Russia progress — the organizing date of the spring diplomacy — expired without an agreement and, so far, without any announced US response. That absence is itself the story: no new trilateral round, no penalty, no extension has been reported in this window. Meanwhile Foreign Minister Lavrov, speaking at the Primakov Readings forum in late June, rejected a freeze along the current frontline as a basis for talks — directly dismissing the recalibrated offer Kyiv floated on 22 June — and restated the maximal demand for all of Donetsk oblast, including territory Russia has never taken. Witkoff's team points to prisoner exchanges as proof the track "delivers results." Honest read: the formal track is dormant; both sides are betting on the battlefield and on oil economics through the summer.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The 30 June deadline was set by Washington in February as the organizing pressure point of the spring track: Geneva meetings, prisoner exchanges, and Zelensky's 4 June open letter offering Putin a direct meeting (Putin called it "pointless" at the St Petersburg forum). On 22 June Kyiv floated its recalibration — a ceasefire along the de facto frontline instead of full withdrawal demands. The week under review answered that offer.

Die Details — Lavrov's rejection came at the Primakov Readings forum (24 June): no halt along the current line as a precondition, because "we will not take anyone at their word" — invoking the 2022 Istanbul talks and Russia's Kyiv withdrawal: "Then we got Johnson and Bucha", repeating Moscow's claim that the documented Bucha massacre was a "staged provocation". He demanded "sensible proposals" and "reasonable people" on the Ukrainian side. Putin's stated terms remain surrender of the entire Donetsk region, including areas never captured — a demand grafted onto the Istanbul framework after the August 2025 Alaska summit with Trump. The revealing asymmetry: two days later Lavrov told TASS "US proposals were discussed and accepted by the Russian side in Alaska" and Peskov said Moscow "would welcome" US mediation — while Putin separately boasted of rejecting two concrete Ukrainian offers (a mutual halt to long-range strikes, and a regional ceasefire covering Sumy, Kharkiv, Mykolaiv and Dnipropetrovsk), because Russia's own deep strikes are "far more powerful… destructive". NATO Secretary-General Rutte said (24 June) Trump aides are working on a peace plan; nothing surfaced by 6 July. The deadline lapsed with no announced US penalty, extension or new round.

Einordnung — Moscow's messaging is aimed at Washington, not Kyiv: reject everything Ukrainian, advertise openness to Trump-mediated terms from Alaska. The US silence on its own deadline is best read as leverage exhausted by the Iran file — the administration spent its coercive bandwidth in the Gulf and has, for now, no appetite to enforce a second ultimatum. That leaves the war to be decided by the two curves in the adjacent cards: Russian attrition and Russian revenue. Kyiv's parallel political track continues — EU accession negotiators expect five clusters opened by mid-July.

Watch-Items — Any retroactive US framing of the lapsed deadline (sanction, waiver, or silence); Witkoff/Vance re-engagement on Ukraine after the Iran funeral pause; prisoner-exchange cadence as the track's pulse; whether the rumored Trump-aide peace plan surfaces before autumn.

War economyJun 2026

The war economy hits its limits: wealth fund down to 1.8% of GDP, oil-and-gas revenue −45% year-on-year, Putin passing the hat to oligarchs

The June analytical consensus (Kiel Institute, EU Institute for Security Studies, others) is that Russia's wartime boom is over. Liquid assets of the National Wealth Fund (Russia's sovereign rainy-day fund) have fallen from 6.5% of GDP at the war's start to 1.8% as of April; oil and gas budget revenues collapsed ~45% year-on-year in Q1 — and that was before Brent's July slide back to $72 and with the price cap still frozen. The Kremlin's visible responses are small: freezing a planned tax hike on small businesses, and — notably — Putin personally requesting "donations" to the budget from oligarchs. Even pro-Kremlin nationalist commentators show barely concealed irritation. This is the material backdrop to both the collapsed offensive and the refusal to negotiate: Moscow needs either a battlefield win or an oil-price rescue, and in this window got neither.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — For three years Russia out-performed sanction forecasts on the back of oil windfalls and fiscal stimulus. The EU's 21st sanctions package (announced 9 June, being finalised through late June) contains two escalations: the first-ever direct restrictions on Russian liquefied natural gas (re-export through EU ports and long-term supply contracts — resisted by member states for over a year), and 20+ additional shadow-fleet tankers blacklisted, taking the total past 300 ships barred from EU ports, insurance and maritime services (France's 1 June seizure of a vessel off Brittany set the enforcement tone). Flag: the cap's level is reported contradictorily — the EU Commission's own January mechanism and Ukrainska Pravda (and our 22 June edition) say $44.10/barrel; EU Insider and European Business Magazine report a freeze at $60 vs a floated $65. The policy logic (freeze = deny Moscow a windfall) is identical in both versions; the number is not resolved here. De-rated accordingly.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — Commission President von der Leyen and High Representative Kaja Kallas presented the package on 9 June ("Brick by brick, we are collapsing the foundations of Russia's war economy" — Kallas), explicitly designed so that the Hormuz price spike would not become a Russian windfall. Since then it has been in the member-state approval pipeline, where unanimity is required. The measures with real structural bite are the LNG restrictions (first-ever, resisted by member states for over a year) and the bunkering-services criterion, which attacks the shadow fleet's logistics chain rather than playing whack-a-mole with hulls.

Counter-sanctions26 Jun

Putin extends Russia's counter-decree through 2027: no oil sales under any contract that references the price cap

On 26 June Putin extended through 2027 the decree banning Russian oil and petroleum-product sales to any buyer whose contract directly or indirectly invokes the G7/EU price-cap mechanism (G7 — the Group of Seven large advanced economies). It is the mirror image of Brussels' freeze: both sides are locking their positions in for another 18 months rather than adjusting to the post-Hormuz price slide. Practical effect is limited — the cap is enforced through shipping insurance, not contracts — but as a signal it says Moscow expects the sanctions war to outlast any Ukraine settlement window currently under discussion.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The counter-decree dates to February 2023, Moscow's original answer to the G7/EU cap: no Russian oil or petroleum products may be supplied to foreign legal entities or individuals if the contract "directly or indirectly" stipulates use of the price-cap mechanism. It was due to expire on 30 June 2026; Putin signed the extension on 26 June — four days before expiry and about two weeks after Brussels proposed freezing its own cap mechanism.

Die Details — S&P Global's primary reporting adds the market frame: the EU cap is dynamic (15% below Urals market rate, semi-annual review, currently $44.10/barrel), and Platts assessed Urals FOB Primorsk (free on board — price at the loading port) at $44.3/barrel on 26 June, meaning Russian crude currently trades at the cap level anyway — down from $109.7 on 2 April, which had been the highest since June 2014. A structural note from the same reporting: the G7 (the US, UK, Canada, France, Germany, Italy and Japan) no longer acts as one on this — Washington's approach has visibly diverged, including the temporary waiver of Russian-oil sanctions during the Gulf war (it expired 17 June). The decree's enforcement reality is unchanged: the cap regime bites through Western shipping insurance and maritime services, not through contract clauses, so the ban is largely declaratory.

Einordnung — Three signals in one document. First, horizon: Moscow is planning its sanctions posture through end-2027, past every settlement window under discussion. Second, pre-commitment: by outlawing cap-referencing contracts, the Kremlin makes it legally awkward for its own exporters to drift back into a Western-legalized trade even if a Ukraine deal offered one — a hand-tying device that raises the price of its own future concessions. Third, audience: with Urals at $44, no rational buyer needs cap-referencing contracts anyway; the decree costs nothing today, which is exactly why signing it was easy. The G7 fragmentation note is the more consequential fact for 2027.

Watch-Items — US policy on Russian-oil enforcement post-Iran-deal (waiver redux or re-tightening); the Urals–Brent spread as the real revenue variable; Indian and Chinese refiner behavior at cap-level prices; whether the EU's January 2027 cap un-freeze actually happens.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The escalation curve is multi-year: intelligence services documented a roughly fourfold increase in Russian sabotage operations across Europe in 2024 vs 2023, accelerating through 2025 into 2026. The operational signature shifted from professional intelligence officers (largely expelled after 2022) to disposable criminal proxies recruited online by Russian military intelligence — cheap, deniable, and often incompetent, which is partly why so many incidents are detected.

Einordnung — The shadow fleet has become a triple-purpose instrument — sanctions evasion, cable sabotage, drone launch platform — which is why the EU's vessel blacklisting (Sanctions section) is no longer just trade policy but security policy, and why the new bunkering-services criterion matters: it attacks the fleet's logistics rather than individual hulls. Strategically, the campaign's aim remains cohesion, not destruction: keep every incident small enough that no Article 5 (NATO's collective-defence clause) debate can crystallize, while normalizing permanent low-grade pressure. Iran normalizes, Ukraine hardens — and the oil price quietly switched sides

1. The Iran framework survived its first ten days without a mediator — that's the real news. Two rounds of Hormuz re-closures and strike threats in mid-June had made the MoU look like a coin flip; this window delivered Doha progress, a standing communication channel, a Lebanon wind-down committee, and 10M+ barrels/day through the strait. 2. The biggest Iran risk is now succession opacity, not Lebanon. A Supreme Leader who has not been seen in four months, reportedly gravely wounded, presiding invisibly over his father's funeral — that is the entity that must ratify enrichment concessions by ~mid-August. If Mojtaba cannot or does not rule visibly, factional actors (IRGC first among them) fill the vacuum, and the IRGC is the faction toggling Hormuz. Watch whether he appears before the funeral ends 9 July.

3. The oil price switched sides: it was Iran's leverage, now it's Russia's problem. In June, $87 Urals forced the EU to freeze its price cap defensively. At $72 Brent and glut forecasts, the same frozen cap plus a −45% collapse in oil-and-gas revenue and a wealth fund at 1.8% of GDP make Russia the party bleeding out. But 39,000 casualties a month against 24,000–30,000 recruits is an equation with an expiry date. Kyiv's bet — Logistical Lockdown plus the 40-day strike campaign — is that economics settles what Geneva couldn't. The autumn Duma-adjacent season is when Moscow's "small and symbolic concessions" stop being enough.

5. Both sanctions regimes just locked in through 2027. Brussels froze its cap and touched LNG for the first time; Putin extended his counter-decree through 2027 within the same fortnight. Whatever happens on the battlefield or in Doha, the economic war has been pre-committed past any currently discussed settlement horizon.
