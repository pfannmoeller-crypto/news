Coverage · 5 — 12 June 2026

Whiplash week: Trump threatens new Iran strikes, then cancels them and declares the war over — markets round-trip a 2% drawdown, the ECB hikes for the first time since 2023, and SpaceX prices the biggest IPO in history at $1.77 trillion

If last week was the breakdown, this was the whipsaw. Markets came in still bleeding from the $1 trillion chip wipeout and the broken Iran ceasefire, bounced modestly Monday, then sold off hard into Wednesday — the Dow lost 953 points — as Trump pledged to attack Iran "very hard" and May CPI printed 4.2%, the hottest headline in three years. Then the entire tape reversed: Thursday Trump cancelled the planned strikes, said negotiations were progressing, and by Friday claimed the war had ended with a deal to gradually reopen Strait of Hormuz energy trade "nearly finalized." The Nasdaq surged 2.54% Thursday in its best session of the quarter, chips ripped (Micron +12%, Intel +10%), oil slid back toward $90, and Friday opened higher again with SpaceX — $75bn raised, four-times oversubscribed — beginning life as a $1.77tn public company. Beneath the headlines, both central banks moved hawkish: the ECB hiked 25bp, its first increase since 2023, and US futures now price the Fed's next move as a hike. The week ends roughly flat on the indices — but that flatness hides a violent round-trip driven almost entirely by one man's Iran statements.

Earnings & movers

IPO SpaceX

SpaceX raises $75bn in the largest IPO in history — $135/share, $1.77tn valuation, four-times oversubscribed, trading begins today

The headline event of the week, and arguably of the year in capital markets. SpaceX priced its IPO Thursday at $135 per share, raising $75 billion — the biggest public offering ever — at a valuation of roughly $1.77 trillion, which makes Elon Musk's rocket company larger than Tesla on day one. The book was reportedly more than four-times oversubscribed, and shadow-market trading ahead of Friday's Nasdaq debut (ticker SPCX) indicated a pop of at least 35%. The first public trade was still working through the Nasdaq opening auction at compile time, with price indications starting at 9:50am ET. The read-through matters more than the spectacle: Goldman Sachs president John Waldron framed the deal as proof "the capital markets are demonstrating a willingness to finance this AI infrastructure build and this build in space." One week after a $1tn chip wipeout signalled fear that AI capex is decelerating, public investors absorbed the largest equity raise ever for an AI-and-space infrastructure story without blinking. Both things are true at once — and that tension is the market's defining feature right now.

Earnings AI capex

Oracle: record Q4, cloud infrastructure +93% — and the stock still fell 7% on a $40bn capital-raise plan

The AI-infrastructure demand test the prior briefing flagged came back split. Oracle's fiscal Q4 (reported Wed 10 June after the close) was a record: total revenue $19.2bn (+21% YoY, beating the ~$19.1bn consensus), non-GAAP EPS $2.11 (+24%, well above the ~$1.96 expected), total cloud revenue $9.9bn (+47%), and the key number — Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI, its data-centre rental business) — at $5.8bn, up 93% YoY. Demand for AI compute is plainly intact. The market sold it anyway: shares fell more than 7% after hours because Oracle disclosed plans to raise another $40bn through debt and equity (including a $20bn share sale) on top of the $43bn debt and $5bn equity it raised in fiscal 2026, with capex hitting $55.7bn. The verdict mirrors Broadcom last week, but with a different mechanism: there the worry was decelerating demand, here it is the cost of meeting demand. The market is no longer paying for AI growth at any price — it is now auditing how that growth is financed.

Earnings

Adobe beats with AI-first ARR tripling past $500m — but the CFO's exit days after announcement knocks the stock 5.5%

Adobe's fiscal Q2 (reported Thu 11 June after the close) delivered record revenue of $6.62bn (vs $6.58bn expected) and EPS of $5.96 (vs $5.94), and the metric every analyst watched — "AI-first" annual recurring revenue, Adobe's measure of subscription revenue from customers specifically paying for Firefly-based generative AI features in Photoshop, Acrobat and Creative Cloud — crossed $500m, tripling year over year. For a stock down roughly 30% over the past year on "AI eats creative software" fears, that should have been the rebuttal. Instead shares fell about 5.5% after hours: CFO Dan Durn's departure, effective 15 June, lands on top of an unresolved CEO search, leaving both top jobs in flux at once, and the beats were modest against expectations already priced for an AI-momentum story. The structural question — whether generative AI feeds Adobe or replaces it — got a genuinely encouraging data point this week, but governance noise is denying the stock credit for it.

The chip complex bounced hard Thursday — Micron +12%, Sandisk +14%, Intel +10% — but the Broadcom question is still open

Inflation · Fed

May CPI: 4.2% — hottest headline in three years, but in line; core soft; market now prices the Fed's next move as a December HIKE

The print the whole calendar pointed at landed Wednesday 10 June and resolved ambiguously-hawkish. Headline CPI (Consumer Price Index) rose 0.5% on the month and 4.2% year over year — the fastest annual pace since April 2023, almost entirely energy: energy prices jumped 3.9% in May alone and are up 23.5% over twelve months, the Iran war's direct fingerprint. The relief was underneath: core CPI (excluding food and energy) rose only 0.2% on the month, below the 0.3% estimate, holding at 2.9% annually. Because both annual figures matched expectations, the print didn't deepen the selloff on its own — Wednesday's 953-point Dow drop was driven by Trump's strike threats, not the data. The rate-path consequence is still material: CME FedWatch puts hold odds at the 16–17 June FOMC (Kevin Warsh's first full meeting as chair) at ~96%, traders now price zero cuts in all of 2026, and futures indicate the next move is most likely a hike in December. The cut era is formally dead; the only live question is whether the hike comes.

ECB Europe

The ECB hikes for the first time since 2023 — 25bp to 2.25% — and admits it is fighting war-driven stagflation

The week's most under-traded big event. On Thursday 11 June the European Central Bank raised its key rate by a quarter point to 2.25%, its first increase in three years, explicitly because the Iran war and the Strait of Hormuz disruption keep blowing eurozone inflation off target: headline inflation hit 3.2% in May (highest since 2023) and core climbed from 2.2% to 2.5%. The new staff forecasts are a stagflation confession — inflation revised up to 3.0% for 2026 (from 2.6%) while GDP growth was cut to 0.8% (from 0.9%), and markets now price two or more further hikes this year. For the DAX this is a direct headwind: tightening into a sub-1%-growth economy, with the rate path hostage to the same oil price the equity market is celebrating might fall. If the Hormuz deal sticks and energy retraces, the ECB's hiking cycle could be one-and-done; if not, Europe gets the worst combination — war-price inflation, rising rates, and stagnant growth.

Commodities & energy

Oil

Brent round-trips from $97 to ~$90 as the week ends with Trump claiming the war is over and a Hormuz-reopening deal "nearly finalized"

Oil traced the Iran headlines tick for tick. Monday's $97.49 panic high (after the weekend ceasefire collapse) faded fast: by Tuesday 9 June Brent settled at $91.45, down ~3%, after US Energy Secretary comments that ship traffic through the Strait of Hormuz is increasing "and will continue to rise." Wednesday reversed higher on Trump's pledge to attack Iran "very hard," then Thursday broke it back down — WTI fell below $90 — when the strikes were cancelled and negotiations resumed. By Friday, Trump claimed the war had ended and said the US is close to a deal that would gradually restore energy trade through Hormuz, with Iran confirming an agreement is nearly finalized. Keep the frame: this conflict has run since the February 28 strikes ("Operation Epic Fury"), Iran declared Hormuz closed in March, and the International Energy Agency calls it the largest supply disruption in the history of the oil market — prices are still up ~30% since February. A signed, implemented reopening deal would be the single most disinflationary event available to markets this year. An "almost" deal has already died once, two weeks ago.

Editor's view

6 takeaways
  1. The entire week was one trade: long or short a Trump statement. Wednesday's 953-point Dow drop ("we're going to be attacking them very hard"), Thursday's 2.5% Nasdaq surge (strikes cancelled), Friday's gap higher (war "ended," deal "nearly finalized") — every leg maps to a single man's pronouncements about Iran. That is not a market with a thesis; it is a market with a news feed. Position sizing, not directional conviction, is the only edge in that regime — and the late-May precedent (a published draft deal that collapsed into missile exchanges within two weeks) says treat "nearly finalized" as a probability, not a fact.
  2. The AI trade's fault line moved from demand to financing — and that is actually progress. Last week's fear was decelerating demand (Broadcom's guide). This week Oracle showed demand is fine — OCI +93% — and got sold 7% anyway for needing $40bn of fresh capital on top of $48bn last year, while SpaceX absorbed the largest equity raise in history four-times oversubscribed. The market is differentiating: it will fund AI infrastructure lavishly through equity for the right story, but it now charges for balance-sheet leverage. That is a healthier discrimination than the indiscriminate everything-rally of April-May.
  3. Both major central banks are now on the tightening side — the first time this cycle. The ECB's 25bp hike to 2.25% (with two more priced) plus a US curve where December's modal move is a hike means the global rate regime has flipped hawkish in unison, all driven by the same war-energy channel. Equities rallying on peace hopes are making a precise implicit bet: that the oil-inflation impulse reverses fast enough to abort both hiking cycles. If the Hormuz deal signs and implements, that bet pays. If it stalls like May's did, stocks face war risk AND a double-barrelled rate headwind.
  4. The CPI detail worth keeping: core at +0.2% m/m is the soft underbelly of a scary headline. 4.2% headline is energy arithmetic — 23.5% twelve-month energy inflation from a closed Hormuz. Core at 2.9% and decelerating monthly means the war premium has not yet broadly infected the price level. That is why a real reopening deal is so asymmetric: it could mechanically unwind most of the headline overshoot within months, flipping the macro narrative from "hike risk" to "disinflation windfall" faster than consensus can reposition. The inverse also holds — every month Hormuz stays shut, more of that energy cost leaks into core.
  5. Thursday's chip bounce was relief, not vindication. Micron +12%, Intel +10%, Sandisk +14% — on cancelled airstrikes and an IPO halo, not on any new AI-demand datapoint. Broadcom's light guide remains unanswered; Oracle's capex is demand evidence but also the very spending the market just said it distrusts. The complex is now a higher-volatility, two-way market. The eight-week melt-up regime of April-May is over; what replaced it is headline-driven chop around a still-unresolved valuation question.
  6. Pet-food portfolio read. Directionally better, not yet actionable: Brent back near $90 and a Hormuz-reopening deal "nearly finalized" is the scenario that would genuinely relieve the diesel-freight line of the DACH/EU cost base — but May taught exactly this lesson, when a published draft MOU died within two weeks and oil snapped back above $97. Hold the discipline: keep two quarters of forward coverage on diesel-linked freight, do not re-base the cost model below $90 Brent until tankers are actually transiting Hormuz at scale, and note the new EUR wrinkle — an ECB hiking cycle raises financing costs on inventory and any capex precisely while it strengthens the case that energy relief is coming. Bank the relief when it is physical, not rhetorical.
