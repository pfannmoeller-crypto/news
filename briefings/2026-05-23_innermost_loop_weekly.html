"The Singularity has had a price since March, but no major exchange to trade it on, until now."

A single long-form essay: compute finishes its journey from rate card to cleared, exchange-listed futures contract — the keystone of a $7 trillion buildout.

Compute lists on a major regulated exchange

Two months after Ornn began publishing the Ornn Compute Price Index (OCPI) on the Bloomberg Terminal — "the first compute benchmark that derivatives can reference and settle against" — Ornn will now list exchange-traded GPU compute futures on Intercontinental Exchange (ICE), parent of the NYSE. Contracts are USD-denominated, cash-settled, and reference the OCPI series across H100, H200, B200 and RTX 5090. "OCPI settles against cleared GPU prices, not rate cards or surveys. That was the credentialing step — but credentialing is not clearing." (Wissner-Gross discloses he advises and helped form Ornn.)

ICE's origin story is the exact template

Jeff Sprecher founded ICE in 2000, three years after buying the struggling Continental Power Exchange for $1,000 — the vision was to drag opaque OTC energy markets into transparent, electronically cleared trading. "The parallel to GPU compute today is exact." ICE inherited Brent crude futures via its 2001 London acquisition, grew Brent into the reference price for roughly three-quarters of traded oil, bought the NYSE itself in 2013, and now extends that same plumbing — which clears Brent, TTF gas and EU carbon — to compute.

What clearing unlocks: hedging the $7 trillion buildout

Once OCPI-referenced futures clear at ICE, lenders financing GPU buildouts can hedge exposure on the same infrastructure that clears oil and gas; insurers can underwrite residual-value risk against a regulated curve; hyperscaler treasury desks can lock forward compute costs "the way airlines have locked in jet fuel since the 1980s." Sovereign infrastructure funds and pension capital can finally participate in the $7 trillion compute buildout.

The historical pattern: builders always need bankers

Invoking Solve Everything (with Peter Diamandis): the Intelligence Revolution turns scarce domains abundant, "but only after the financial infrastructure catches up." Edison built the generators; Insull made them financeable. Carnegie made the steel; J.P. Morgan made it bankable. Oil took over a century from Drake's well to NYMEX futures; gas longer still. "Compute took only years because oil and gas had already built the machinery. Those years end now."
