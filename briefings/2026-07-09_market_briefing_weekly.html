US equities7–8 Jul

Dow gives back a record: 52,339 close, worst session in three weeks as chips wobble and oil spikes

Monday's celebration didn't survive Tuesday's tape. After the Dow closed above 53,000 on 6 July for the first time, Wednesday finished at 52,339 — down 586 points (−1.11%), the worst single-session drop since the mid-June Warsh selloff. The S&P 500 slipped ~0.28% to ~7,462; the Nasdaq Composite finished roughly flat (chipmakers held together on Samsung), while the Nasdaq 100 eked out a small gain on Apple's Broadcom deal. Two drivers hit at once: Brent surged 5% to $78 after President Trump declared the US–Iran ceasefire "over", and the June FOMC minutes released 2 p.m. ET confirmed nine of 18 policymakers still pencil at least one 2026 hike. Treasury yields firmed — the 10-year back toward 4.50% — and defensive rotation returned. The record configuration (Dow leading, S&P/Nasdaq lagging) inverted this week: cyclicals took the hit while mega-cap tech held.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Monday's edition closed on a genuinely bullish setup — Foxconn (Nvidia's assembler) had reported strong AI-server demand over the weekend, SMH (VanEck Semiconductor ETF, tracks 25 largest US-listed chip names) rallied +2.4% premarket Monday, and the Dow finished above 53,000 for the first time in history. The two-day roundtrip since then is a compressed lesson in what actually drives this market: the AI-hardware bounce needed corroboration (Samsung delivered it Tuesday), but the macro backdrop of an Iran flare-up and a hawkish Fed minutes release did not respect that.

Die Details: The Tuesday tape held up on Samsung's blowout preliminary Q2 print (167T won revenue, ~89T won operating profit — see the movers section), with the Nasdaq closing green even as the Dow slipped. Wednesday was the reversal: Brent traded from ~$73 into Wednesday's session to $78.17 by 8:30 a.m. ET (+$4.88 in a single session, ~5%), after Trump's overnight remark that "we hit them very hard last night" and would "probably hit them hard again tonight." Equities opened weak and stayed weak — the Dow closed −586.34 at 52,339, the S&P −0.28% to roughly 7,462, and the Nasdaq Composite roughly flat as Apple's Broadcom announcement (+$30B multiyear US chip deal) balanced defensive selling. Weekly initial jobless claims are due today (Thu 9 Jul); the CBOE Volatility Index (VIX) jumped from ~16 to ~19.

Einordnung: The clean reading is that the "bad news is good news" trade — which is what drove Friday's Dow record on a weak jobs print — only works when the negative catalyst is domestic labor data that lowers Fed hike odds. An Iran flare-up is a different animal: it simultaneously raises inflation risk (oil), lowers growth expectations (energy shock), and doesn't help the Fed decide anything faster. That's why the tape sold off across the curve: cyclicals on growth fear, tech on rate fear, and only the mega-cap defensives (Apple's Broadcom-driven stability, staples ahead of PepsiCo) held.

Watch-Items: Whether Brent settles above $78 for a second consecutive session (would signal the risk premium is being rebuilt structurally, not spiked); whether the Nasdaq Composite makes back Wednesday's losses on Thursday's PepsiCo/Delta setup; if 10-year yields cross 4.55% they carry rate-sensitive names lower even without Fed news.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Monday's briefing framed the region as the SK Hynix HBM-slowdown thesis's test bed: the 1 July report that Korean high-bandwidth memory expansion would slow triggered the entire semiconductor selloff late last week, and Samsung's preliminary Q2 print — released Tuesday 7 July — was going to be the tiebreaker for whether the AI-capex story was intact or cracking. The result: intact, and then some.

Die Details: Samsung's preliminary Q2 numbers: revenue 171T won (~$125B), operating profit 89.4T won (~$65B) — up 56% quarter-over-quarter and 1,810% year-over-year. That single quarter's operating profit reportedly exceeds NVIDIA's and Apple's on the same period, making it the largest quarterly operating profit ever posted by a technology company. HBM (high-bandwidth memory — the stacked-DRAM chips feeding AI accelerators) was the biggest driver, joined by conventional DRAM and NAND, which are also seeing significant price increases as AI workloads expand beyond training into inference. Samsung had started commercial shipments of its HBM4 chip earlier in the quarter, so Q2 2026 is the first period showing HBM4 revenue. Regional index response: Nikkei ran to 69,744 (+1.47% intraday early week), Hang Seng +1.28% to 23,350, CSI 300 +1.15%, Shanghai Composite to 4,043.64. Kospi rallied on Samsung, though tech-heavy Kosdaq was noisier. Taiwan's Taiex — the region's purest AI-hardware index — reversed prior weakness. The yen sat near multi-decade lows against the dollar, which is a Nikkei-earnings tailwind and simultaneously imports inflation.

Einordnung: Samsung's print kills the "SK Hynix is slowing HBM because AI demand is peaking" reading of last week's semi-selloff. The revenue and margin numbers imply both pricing power (memory spot prices firm) and volume (AI capex unbroken) — the opposite of the doubt-the-cycle narrative. The tell is that conventional DRAM and NAND are also rising, meaning the AI story is now bleeding into commodity memory pricing, which is the classic setup for a memory upcycle rather than a peak. For the tape: the marginal buyer in AI hardware has a fresh datapoint, and Micron's post-blowout selloff of 1 July now looks like positioning, not fundamentals. For the region: Korea won this week; the yen story keeps the Nikkei bid so long as the Bank of Japan does not intervene.

Watch-Items: Whether SK Hynix confirms or contradicts its own alleged HBM slowdown when it reports (later in July); the yen — if it crosses recent lows the Bank of Japan intervention risk rises; TSMC's Thursday 17 July earnings as the next AI-hardware verdict; whether Hang Seng's internet-name rotation extends past Tuesday's move.

The "family fight" at the Fed: nine hikers, no cutters, and a chair who declined to submit his own dot

The June FOMC minutes, released Wednesday, confirmed the hawkish framing without ambiguity: nine of 18 policymakers projected at least one 25-basis-point hike before year-end, six projected two hikes, and the committee unanimously held rates at 3.50–3.75%. The kicker: Chair Kevin Warsh withheld his own dot, the first sitting Fed chair to abstain from the Summary of Economic Projections, and disclosed a task force reviewing the dot plot and other forward guidance. That leaves the minutes as the only on-record document about the September rate-hike question. The minutes explicitly cite inflation risks — headline CPI still running 4.2% — and only lightly discuss labor cooling. Market reaction: 10-year yield firmed toward 4.50%, equities extended their intraday slide, and CME FedWatch reset hike odds up from the post-payrolls low.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Kevin Warsh took the Fed chair in April on Trump's nomination, killed forward guidance, shortened the policy statement, and — most consequentially — declined to participate in the dot plot in June. That combination puts abnormal weight on the minutes, because they are now the only vehicle by which the Fed telegraphs anything about the next meeting. Coming in, the market's live debate was whether the September meeting would deliver a hike, and RBC's post-June-meeting take was that the committee's hike camp needed some conditioning to soften — a soft July or August CPI would be the natural pivot excuse.

Die Details: The minutes revealed a divided committee — the "family fight" framing came from CNBC's Steve Liesman — with nine members projecting at least one 2026 hike and six projecting two 25-basis-point hikes. Inflation figured prominently: policymakers noted headline CPI still 4.2%, worried about sticky services inflation, and flagged tariff-driven price pressure (a step-up in the effective tariff rate had been factored into the June SEP already). Labor market conditions received less attention than in prior years' minutes. The 28–29 July FOMC meeting will not include a fresh SEP, so the minutes plus one more month of data (June CPI on 14 July, June PPI, retail sales, the July jobs report on 1 August) are all the market has to work with. Warsh's stated view: markets should price data, not Fed reaction functions. Practical consequence: higher data-day volatility by design.

Einordnung: The market read this as hawkish-hold with no cut in sight. But the setup is asymmetric: the hike camp doesn't need a written pivot to abandon September if June CPI prints below 4%, and the falling energy price of the past two weeks does the doves' work for them mechanically. The Iran flare-up complicates that: if Brent stays above $78, the energy relief that helped disinflate the June-July print is at risk of unwinding in July-August data. The market ended Wednesday reversing its post-payrolls dovishness for a reason.

Watch-Items: Fed speakers this week and next — any hint of what triggers or defers the September call; June CPI on 14 July as the primary conditioner; the July FOMC on 28–29 July (no SEP, so the statement and press conference carry the full signaling load); whether Warsh gives any indication of his personal position between now and then.

Three days changed the setup: Iran is back, memory is fine, and Warsh's Fed is still hunting a hike

1. The oil-disinflation trade paused, at minimum. Monday's edition organized the macro backdrop around Brent below $72 doing the doves' work for them. Trump's Wednesday remarks pushed Brent back to $78 in a single session. That's not enough to guarantee a September hike, but it is enough to unwind the mechanical channel by which the July and August CPI prints were going to soften. If Brent settles above $80 for two consecutive weeks, the hike camp gets its argument back regardless of what labor data does.

2. Samsung buried the HBM-slowdown thesis. The 1 July SK Hynix report was single-sourced and vague, and it took the entire memory complex down 10%+ in three days. Samsung's Tuesday preliminary Q2 print — 89 trillion won of operating profit, up 1,810% year-over-year, the largest tech quarter ever recorded — says the AI-memory cycle is not peaking. It's accelerating into conventional DRAM. That reprices the correction as positioning, not fundamentals. Micron's July 22 report is now the next tape-mover.

3. The FOMC minutes were exactly as hawkish as feared. Nine hikers, zero cutters, and a chair who won't tell you which side he's on. Warsh's abstention from the dot plot is not humility; it is a deliberate design choice to make every data print the market's own signaling mechanism. That means the 14 July CPI is now doing the work three Fed speeches would have done a year ago. Consequence: expect higher single-day volatility around data releases through the July 28–29 meeting.

4. Concentration risk quietly ratcheted higher. SpaceX joined the Nasdaq-100 Tuesday, sitting inside the same benchmark as Tesla. Alphabet joined the Dow. The Magnificent 7 (Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Tesla) lost ~$2.3T of market cap on the AI-capex jitters of the past month — and are still, together, the majority of index moves on data days.
