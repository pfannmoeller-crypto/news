Markets & Macro · Weekly

The cleanest CPI in six years lands in the middle of an oil shock — inflation fell 0.4% in June, the July hike is effectively dead, and the banks just posted the best quarter in Wall Street history.

Two forces fought to a near-standstill this week. On Tuesday the June CPI printed −0.4% month-over-month — the steepest monthly drop since April 2020 — dragging headline inflation to 3.5% (from 4.2%) and core to 2.6%, both well below forecast. That collapsed July rate-hike odds from ~42% to ~16% and lifted the S&P 500 to a fresh 7,548. But the disinflation was partly a mirage of falling gasoline that is now reversing: Trump's naval blockade of Iranian ports pushed Brent above $86 and WTI to ~$80, capping the rally and pinning the Dow flat at 52,463. Underneath it all, Q2 bank earnings were extraordinary — JPMorgan beat by $0.29 with profit +41%, Goldman posted its best quarter ever ($20.98 EPS) — as trading and IB revenue surged. Bad news for oil is still bad news for the September Fed. Next up: TSMC and Netflix Thursday.

The week split cleanly in two. Monday (13 Jul) opened risk-off: chip stocks tumbled on rate-hike speculation and profit-taking, SK Hynix slid after its disappointing US IPO, and Brent logged its biggest single-day jump as the Iran blockade headlines hit. The single question hanging over this week was binary: would the 14 July June CPI corroborate the soft-inflation thesis (bullish, kills the hike) or run hot (bearish, hands the Warsh Fed its September argument)? Every desk had positioned into it; the Monday chip sell-off was partly a de-risking ahead of the number.

Die Details: Monday 13 July was ugly — semiconductors led lower on a mix of rate-hike speculation, AI-capex spending worries and profit-taking, and SK Hynix dropped sharply after a weak US listing debut. Brent posted its single largest daily jump of the run as Trump confirmed a naval blockade of Iranian ports. Tuesday reversed hard on the 8:30 a.m. CPI: the S&P closed 7,548.20 (+0.39%), the Nasdaq Composite advanced more than 1% led by tech, and the Dow finished essentially flat at 52,463 (−0.11%). The split was mechanical — falling rate expectations lift long-duration tech multiples, while oil above $80 and IBM's miss weighed on the Dow's industrial/energy tilt. The VIX eased off its 8 July spike as the CPI removed the near-term hike tail. Note the level math: the S&P is now roughly 1.1% above where last week's edition left it, recovering the Iran-driven damage in a single session.

Einordnung: This is the "good news is good news" version of the trade finally working — a genuinely soft domestic inflation print, not a weak jobs number, doing the disinflation work cleanly. But the leadership tell matters: the rally was narrow and tech-led, not the broad cyclical/small-cap rotation last week's edition flagged. That means the market is treating the CPI as a rate story (buy duration) rather than a growth story (buy breadth). It also means the rally is fragile to exactly one thing — the oil impulse that the CPI itself was partly built on top of. If Brent holds above $86 into the July PPI and August's data, the tech-led bid is borrowing against an energy channel that is already reversing.

Watch-Items: Whether the Nasdaq holds its post-CPI gains through Thursday's TSMC/Netflix double-header; June PPI on 15 July and June retail sales on 16 July as the next conditioners; whether the Dow can reclaim 53,000 or stays capped by energy; the 10-year yield, which slid on the CPI but firmed on the oil move — a genuine tug-of-war on the curve.

If Brent stays above $86, German 2026 earnings estimates come down and the ~26,300 DAX target that circulated earlier this month stays on hold; but the index-level damage is capped as long as the ECB's hawkish-hold keeps the euro and Bund market orderly.

June CPITue 14 Jul, 8:30 ET

Inflation falls 0.4% in June — steepest monthly drop since April 2020; headline 3.5%, core 2.6%, both under forecast

The most important data print of the year delivered a decisive downside surprise. Headline CPI fell 0.4% month-over-month — the sharpest monthly decline since April 2020 — dragging the annual rate to 3.5% from May's 4.2%, below the 3.8% consensus. Core CPI was flat (0.0%) on the month, cutting the annual core rate to 2.6% from 2.9% (also under the 2.9% forecast). The driver was energy: the energy index fell 5.7% and gasoline dropped roughly 10%, the fourth-largest monthly decline in a decade. Market reaction was immediate — Treasury yields slid, equities rallied, and CME FedWatch odds of a July hike collapsed from ~42% to ~16%, the single largest one-session swing of the current tightening debate. The catch: the gasoline relief occurred during the June ceasefire, and Brent is now back above $86.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Under Chair Warsh's Fed, forward guidance is dead and the dot plot has an abstaining chair, which means individual data prints carry amplified market weight by design. June CPI was therefore the single most-watched number of the year — the July FOMC (28–29 July) will not include a fresh Summary of Economic Projections, so this print plus June PPI, retail sales and the August jobs report are essentially all the market has to price the next move. Coming in, July-hike odds had spiked to ~42% (from ~8% a month earlier) as the Iran flare-up and hawkish June minutes rebuilt the inflation-risk premium.

Die Details: The print was cool across the board: headline −0.4% m/m (steepest since April 2020), 3.5% y/y (from 4.2%, vs 3.8% expected); core flat m/m, 2.6% y/y (from 2.9%, vs 2.9% expected). The energy index fell 5.7% — its largest monthly drop since April 2020 — with gasoline down ~10%. Crucially, that gasoline decline reflected June pump prices, which fell during the interim US–Iran ceasefire that reopened Hormuz shipping through most of June. The reaction: CME FedWatch July-hike odds collapsed from ~42% to ~16% (largest one-session swing of the cycle), Treasury yields slid, and equities rallied (S&P +0.39% to 7,548). But Chair Warsh publicly pushed back — asked whether inflation was defeated, he said flatly "that is not my view" — signaling the hawkish core is unconvinced by a single energy-driven print. September-hike odds actually sit around 60% in some reads precisely because the July relief is seen as fragile.

Einordnung: The clean reading is that the July hike is dead and the disinflation thesis got real corroboration — core at 2.6% is genuinely encouraging and not all energy. The complicated reading is that the print is backward-looking into exactly the window (June) when oil was cheapest, and the forward path is now contaminated by the renewed Iran shock: with Brent above $86 and gasoline set to reverse, the July and August CPI prints face an energy headwind that could re-firm headline inflation. That is why the market simultaneously killed the July hike and left September live. Warsh's design — make every print the signal — means this asymmetry (soft now, risk of hot later) will drive outsized volatility around each summer data release.

Watch-Items: June PPI on 15 July and retail sales on 16 July as the next conditioners; the July FOMC on 28–29 July (statement/presser carry the full signaling load, no SEP); whether gasoline prices in the July data reverse the June relief; any Fed-speaker attempt to define what triggers or defers the September call; the 10-year yield's tug-of-war between disinflation (lower) and the oil premium (higher).

Die Details: Brent, which had eased to ~$78.31 on 13 July (−1.36% that day), reversed violently to above $86 — its highest in about a month and its largest single-session jump of the episode — on the blockade confirmation, with reports that Hormuz tanker traffic had "essentially stopped." The counterweight is real: OPEC+ is mid-cycle in monthly production adds, and OECD/Chinese/US inventories all drew down in June — a genuine supply cushion that has kept the risk premium contained in the mid-$80s rather than exploding toward March's ~$120.

Einordnung: Oil is again the master variable, and this week it works against the very CPI that lifted stocks. The energy channel that disinflated June is reversing in real time: if Brent holds above $86, July and August gasoline reverse higher, headline CPI re-firms, and the September hike the market just half-priced becomes live. That is the whole "Hormuz puts September back on the table" thesis. For equities, the offset is the tech-led rate-relief bid; for Europe, it caps the DAX and supports energy majors; for the Fed, it is the single reason a soft June print did not fully close the door on 2026 tightening. As always with Iran: watch the physical shipping data, not the headlines — the blockade's actual bite on tanker volumes is what sustains or deflates the premium.

Watch-Items: Hormuz tanker-transit volumes over the next 72 hours (the real gauge vs rhetoric); Brent's $86 hold and the $90/$100 escalation thresholds; whether OPEC+ signals faster production adds to offset; the read-through into July gasoline and the August CPI; the FTSE/STOXX energy majors and DAX input-cost sensitivity. A soft CPI killed the July hike — but it was built on gasoline that's already reversing under the Hormuz blockade

1. June inflation was genuinely soft, and genuinely backward-looking. Headline −0.4% m/m (steepest since April 2020), core at 2.6% y/y — the disinflation is real and it's not all energy. But the ~10% gasoline drop that powered the headline happened during June's ceasefire window, which closed on 8 July. With Brent now above $86, the exact channel that produced the soft print is unwinding. The market correctly killed the July hike (odds 42% → 16%) and correctly left September live (~60% in some reads). That asymmetry — soft now, risk of hot later — is the defining tension of the summer.

2. Wall Street just printed the best bank quarter on record. JPMorgan beat by $0.29 with profit +41%; Goldman posted its best quarter ever at $20.98 EPS; the five reporting banks approached $39B of combined trading revenue. It validates "higher-for-longer is good for banks" and a booming capital-markets cycle. But blow-out trading revenue is a late-cycle tell — desks feast on the same volatility (Iran, CPI, rate repricing) that stresses everyone else — and JPMorgan's AI-headcount commentary is the quieter structural story.

3. Oil is the master variable, and this week it fought the CPI to a draw. Trump's naval blockade of Iranian ports pushed Brent to a one-month high and stopped Hormuz traffic. The Strait carries 20–27% of seaborne crude; the $100 tail is now discussed openly. The only thing capping the move is OPEC+ still adding barrels into drawing inventories. If Brent holds above $86, it reverses the disinflation, re-firms August CPI, and hands the Warsh Fed its September argument. Watch the physical shipping data, not the headlines.

4. The rally's leadership narrowed back to tech. Last week's edition described a rotation into Europe, small caps and cyclicals. The CPI reversed that: Tuesday's bid was tech-led (Nasdaq +1%+) on falling rates, the Dow stalled at 52,463 on an IBM miss and oil, and the DAX churned flat at 25,147. The market is treating the print as a rate story (buy duration), not a growth story (buy breadth) — which makes the rally fragile to the one variable it's ignoring: the oil shock underneath it.

5. Thursday's TSMC print decides whether the tech bid has legs. With SK Hynix's soft US IPO reviving memory doubt and Samsung having buried it two weeks ago, TSMC (~$40B revenue, +33% y/y, record $52–56B capex, CoWoS "sold out through 2026") is the cleanest read on whether AI capex has a ceiling. A beat-and-raise confirms the early-July selloff was positioning; but with the stock up 97% in a year, even a solid print risks "sell the news." Netflix the same evening sets the consumer-media tone.
