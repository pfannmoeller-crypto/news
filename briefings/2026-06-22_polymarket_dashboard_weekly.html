Iran de-escalation

Geopolitics · 5 underlying markets · Near-dated prices 3/5 this week
trending_upImproving — the framework the cluster was waiting for arrived; MOU signed June 18, Hormuz reopened

The cleanest reversal the board has produced this year. Ten days ago this cluster's premise had failed — the ceasefire broke, strikes returned, Trump threatened the oil industry. This week the opposite happened: US and Iran reached a 14-point memorandum of understanding on June 15 to end more than 100 days of conflict, and signed it electronically on June 18 — the planned June 19 Geneva (Bürgenstock) ceremony was scrapped in favour of a quiet e-signature. The MOU formalises a 60-day ceasefire, reopens the Strait of Hormuz toll-free, lifts the US naval blockade, ends hostilities in Lebanon, and opens negotiation on enrichment, the highly-enriched-uranium stockpile, sanctions relief, and up to ~$25B in frozen Iranian assets (release conditional on compliance), with Pakistani and Omani mediation cited. The market response was a relief rally, not the whipsaw of last week: Brent fell ~5% to ~$83 as the war premium unwound.

The discipline this week is the inverse of last week's. The direction (de-escalation re-secured) is now the bankable fact — but the MOU is explicitly a framework, not a final nuclear deal. By its own terms it maintains Iran's existing nuclear program pending a subsequent agreement and defers stockpile disposition to a mechanism “to be mutually agreed” in that later deal — so it does not satisfy the resolution criteria for the “new US–Iran nuclear deal” markets, which is why the near-dated “deal by June 30” leg stays a minority bet even after the signing. The long-dated read firmed: the permanent-peace-deal market puts December 31 at ~68.5% ($259M cumulative volume — the deepest geopolitical market on the board), and US/Iranian interpretations still diverge on asset releases and long-term Hormuz management. The 60-day clock is the new master timeline: it runs into roughly mid-August, and that is when the “framework vs. real deal” question actually resolves. Fed monetary path

Macro · 5 underlying markets · June 17 FOMC delivered
trending_downHawkish realised — held 12–0, but the dot plot flipped to a hike; the cut case is now near-dead

The single highest-leverage event on the board resolved, and it resolved hawkish — not via the rate decision but via the projections. Kevin Warsh's first FOMC held the target range at 3.50–3.75% in a 12–0 vote on June 17, exactly as the ~98.7% no-change market expected. The information was in the dot plot: the median 2026 rate jumped from 3.4% (March) to 3.8%, nine of eighteen members now project at least one 25bp hike before year-end, and six project two. The statement leaned on “solid” growth, strong productivity and capital investment, a steady labour market, and inflation kept elevated by supply shocks — including energy. After the meeting and Warsh's remarks, traders pulled forward hike timing, with October cited as the live window. The hawkish break that began with two accelerating CPI prints (3.8% → 4.2%) is now institutionalised in the committee's own forecast.

Here is the week's structural twist: the inflation impulse that justified the hawkish dots is the one that just eased. The Iran MOU reopened Hormuz and dropped Brent ~5% to ~$83 — precisely the “signed deal that puts oil decisively lower” channel that last edition said had moved backward. So the Fed validated higher-for-longer on June 17 using prints (May CPI 4.2%, energy +23.5%) generated before the energy relief arrived on June 18. The next CPI prints will be the test of whether the dots are early or right: if falling crude pulls headline inflation down through the summer, Warsh's nine-member hike majority could prove a high-water mark; if domestic core inflation (~2.9%) stays sticky regardless of energy, the October hike becomes real. “0 cuts in 2026” is effectively settled near the high-70s/80s, and the live market has rotated entirely to the hike timing. Recession remains a minority read (~18%) — the crowd still prices a rates regime, not a growth scare.
