Geopolitics · Week of 12–18 July 2026

The Iran war stopped being about military targets. This week Washington started hitting bridges, railways, telecoms and power — and Tehran answered by firing on seven countries at once.

Seven days ago the frame was "three US strike rounds, oil at $85, EU sanctions jammed." All three intensified. CENTCOM ran a sixth straight night of strikes into 17 July, expanding from air defence and naval targets to civilian infrastructure in southern Iran — the Bandar-e Khamir bridge in Hormozgan province (at least seven killed), telecommunications networks, railways, a collapsed tower at a key port, and, for the first time acknowledged by Tehran's own Energy Ministry, power infrastructure. Trump's stated theory of victory is now explicit coercion of civilians: "Next week comes the power plants … unless they get to the table and negotiate," plus a named threat against the Pickaxe Mountain nuclear-linked tunnel complex near Natanz. Iran's answer on the night of 16–17 July was the widest regional barrage of the war: IRGC and army strikes claimed against US assets in Bahrain, Iraq, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Jordan and Syria, with a child injured by shrapnel in Doha and a power-and-desalination plant damaged in Kuwait. Brent closed +4.6% at $88.10; the IEA's Fatih Birol warned global energy security is at risk if Hormuz does not reopen "in the next few weeks," with roughly 12.8 million barrels per day of supply displaced and only a handful of tankers transiting daily. In Europe, the EU's 21st sanctions package failed a second time on 15 July after three days of ambassadorial talks; the oil price cap was frozen at $44.10 only until 23 July. And in Kyiv, Zelensky fired the war's most popular technocrat — Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov — triggering protests in at least 18 cities and a small wave of resignations, while Ukraine's long-range campaign quietly had its best week yet: 147 Russian vessels struck between 6 and 16 July.

Iran / Hormuz — the campaign turns against infrastructure

6 items
Threshold crossed12–17 Jul

Sixth straight night of US strikes, and the target set moves to bridges, railways, telecoms and power

Between 12 and 17 July CENTCOM ran continuous nightly strike waves — including a 90-minute round on Greater Tunb Island and a package Al Jazeera reported at roughly 140 military targets across Iran in a single night. The decisive change came at the end of the week: on 16–17 July the campaign hit civilian infrastructure in southern Iran, including telecommunications networks, railway systems and the Bandar-e Khamir bridge in Hormozgan province, where local media reported at least seven killed. On Friday 17 July US forces struck further bridges and energy sites and collapsed a tower at a key Iranian port. Iran's Energy Ministry then acknowledged "attacks on power infrastructure" for the first time, asking residents of southern provinces to reduce consumption during extreme heat. That is a materially different war than the one being fought on 8 July.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. The current phase descends from the 17 June memorandum of understanding (MoU) brokered by Qatar and Pakistan — a 60-day ceasefire, reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, and a timeframe for a final nuclear settlement. That memorandum survived three weeks. It broke on 8 July after IRGC attacks on tankers, and Trump declared it "over." The strike architecture since then has escalated in three distinct steps: (1) counter-force targets — coastal radar, air defence, missile and drone storage, naval infrastructure and small boats, including 30+ fishing vessels at Bandar Abbas; (2) blockade of Iranian ports, imposed, lifted, and reimposed on Tuesday 14 July; and (3) this week's move onto infrastructure that has no plausible naval-denial function at all. The stated justification remained constant throughout — protecting shipping in Hormuz — but the target set stopped matching the justification somewhere around Wednesday.

Die Details. Greater Tunb Island — one of three islands Iran seized from the United Arab Emirates in 1971 and which host IRGC Navy anti-ship missile and radar positions — took a 90-minute concentrated round. The Bandar-e Khamir bridge sits in Hormozgan province, the coastal governorate that fronts the strait and hosts Bandar Abbas; it is a road link, not a military asset, and local reporting put the death toll at seven or more. Telecommunications and railway targets in the south degrade Iran's ability to move materiel toward the coast but also its civilian economy indistinguishably. The port tower collapse (reported Friday) and the acknowledged power-grid damage complete the pattern. Iran's official cumulative toll since the 22 June Switzerland talks stands at 38 killed and more than 400 injured per AFP citing Tehran; a separate Iranian Health Ministry figure earlier in the week put "more than 260 injured," and government spokesperson Fatemeh Mohajerani cited "more than 30 civilians killed." These are Iranian-government numbers, they overlap inconsistently, and there is no independent verification. Treat them as directional.

Einordnung. Striking power plants and bridges to force a negotiation is a coercion strategy with a long and poor empirical record — it is the logic of Rolling Thunder and of the 1999 Serbian grid campaign, and it works best against governments that are both centralised and casualty-sensitive. Iran's current leadership is neither: Mojtaba Khamenei is invisible, decisions run through a security-elite consensus, and the IRGC's institutional interest points toward continued escalation rather than capitulation. The second-order risk is legal-diplomatic: attacks on electricity and water infrastructure in a heat emergency will be litigated in the Security Council and will cost Washington support among the Gulf states it is nominally defending — several of which spent this week being shot at because of US basing.

Watch-Items. (1) Whether power-plant strikes are executed as a named campaign or remain a threat. (2) Pickaxe Mountain — a strike there converts this from a shipping war into a counter-proliferation war. (3) Whether Gulf hosts begin restricting US launch and overflight rights. (4) Casualty reporting from any independent source — currently there is none.

Confidence: high (multi-outlet, wire-sourced)
Regional spillover12 & 17 Jul

Iran fires on seven countries in one night — Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman, Iraq, Jordan, Syria

On the morning of Friday 17 July, Bahrain, Iraq, Kuwait, Oman and Qatar — plus Jordan and Syria — all took defensive action against Iranian missiles and drones. In Doha, sirens sounded and explosions were heard; the IRGC claimed a strike on Al Udeid Air Base, and Qatar's Interior Ministry confirmed a child injured by falling shrapnel. In Kuwait, the IRGC claimed hits on a missile-defence radar, weapons depots and two HIMARS launchers; a power and water desalination plant was damaged. Iran's army claimed US helicopters and reconnaissance aircraft at Sakhir airbase, Bahrain; the IRGC claimed radars in Oman and a US special-operations command centre at al-Tanf, Syria. Jordan shot down three Iranian missiles transiting its airspace; US coalition forces downed eight drones over Erbil. An earlier wave on 12 July had already hit five Gulf states. Tehran's justification: these countries host the bases launching the strikes.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Iran's doctrine of holding US regional bases hostage predates this war — it is the whole point of the medium-range missile force built since the 1990s. What is new is the frequency and breadth. Through 2024–25 Iranian strikes on Gulf territory were single, telegraphed, largely symbolic events (Ain al-Asad 2020, Al Udeid June 2025). This week produced two multi-country barrages inside six days, against states that are formally neutral or actively mediating. Qatar is the most striking case: Doha co-brokered the 17 June MoU and hosted the Doha talks in early July, and was being shot at on 17 July while its foreign ministry was still working the diplomatic track.

Die Details. Damage claims are almost entirely IRGC-sourced via Tasnim, and none of the destroyed-radar or destroyed-HIMARS claims have been confirmed by CENTCOM or the host governments. What is independently confirmed is thinner and more revealing: a child injured in Doha, falling debris starting fires in Kuwait, damage to a Kuwaiti power-and-desalination plant, three missiles downed by Jordan, eight drones downed over Erbil by coalition forces. In other words, interception rates appear high and the physical military effect appears low — but the political effect is large, because Gulf populations are now experiencing air-raid alerts. Qatar separately and publicly rejected Israeli reports that it planned to join military action against Iran, which tells you how carefully Doha is managing its exposure. The IRGC's own framing was territorial rather than tactical: the strait "remains in the hands of the IRGC Navy's admirals."

Einordnung. Iran is deliberately converting a bilateral war into a regional one, because regionalisation is its only real escalation lever short of the nuclear threshold. Every Gulf state under fire has an incentive to press Washington for de-escalation, and Tehran knows it. The mediation response was immediate: China's Foreign Minister Wang Yi and Pakistan's Ishaq Dar jointly called on 17 July for an immediate ceasefire, with Wang calling the June agreement "hard-won" and warning that "peace is before our eyes, [we] cannot fall at the last hurdle." Note who that is — China and Pakistan, not the US or Europe, are the visible diplomatic actors on the Iran file. That is a durable shift in who owns Gulf crisis management.

Watch-Items. (1) Any Gulf state formally restricting US operations from its soil — the single most consequential possible development. (2) A civilian mass-casualty event in Qatar, Kuwait or Bahrain, which would force host-government positions. (3) Whether Iraq's government is dragged in via Erbil. (4) Whether the Wang–Dar channel produces an actual meeting.

Confidence: medium-high (events confirmed; damage claims IRGC-sourced)
Coercion ladder13–17 Jul

Trump names the next rungs: "power plants," "all their bridges," and Pickaxe Mountain

Trump spent the week publicising his escalation schedule rather than concealing it. He threatened to keep hitting Iran "very hard" unless it returns to talks — "Next week comes the power plants … We're gonna knock out all their power plants … We're going to knock out all their bridges unless they get to the table and negotiate." On the Hugh Hewitt Show on 13 July he named a specific target: "We're going to take out Pickaxe Mountain. Tell the Iranians to be ready" — the suspected underground nuclear complex (Kuh-e Kolang) buried up to 600 metres under granite, roughly 2 km from Natanz. He also abandoned his proposed transit fee on Hormuz shipping (reported as a 20% toll) on 14 July. Trump simultaneously claimed Iran "wants to meet."

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Pickaxe Mountain (Kuh-e Kolang Gaz La) has been the single most contested item in the nuclear file since satellite imagery revealed tunnelling in 2020. It sits ~220 km south of Tehran, about 2 km from the Natanz enrichment complex, with overburden estimated at up to 600 metres of granite — deeper than Fordow and, on most published assessments, deeper than the GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrator can reliably reach. Iran describes it as a centrifuge assembly plant. UN inspectors have never been granted access to verify that. The Institute for Science and International Security reported that satellite imagery from late June 2026 shows ongoing construction inside the tunnel complex and continued hardening of its entrances — i.e. Iran is still working on it during the war.

Die Details. The rhetorical structure of the week matters. Trump publicly named a target, a timeline ("next week"), and a condition ("unless they get to the table") — classic compellence, which requires the adversary to believe both the threat and the promise of relief. He then undercut the promise by continuing to strike during the mediation window, and undercut the threat's credibility on a different axis by withdrawing the Hormuz transit fee on 14 July after markets and allies reacted badly. Meanwhile CNN ran a widely circulated 17 July analysis arguing Trump "is edging towards another American 'forever war' in Iran" — the first sustained mainstream framing of this as an open-ended commitment rather than a punitive campaign. The domestic politics of that framing are the real constraint on the power-plant threat.

Einordnung. A strike on Pickaxe Mountain would be qualitatively different from everything so far. It converts a maritime-access dispute into an explicit counter-proliferation campaign, it would almost certainly fail to destroy the facility (which is the point of building it that deep), and a visible failure would strengthen the argument inside Tehran for weaponisation. The more likely function of the threat is bargaining: naming the crown-jewel target is how you establish that everything below it is available. The abandoned transit toll is the tell that market and allied feedback still binds this administration.

Watch-Items. (1) Movement of bunker-buster-capable B-2/B-21 assets to Diego Garcia or the region. (2) IAEA statements on Natanz-area activity. (3) Whether "next week" (i.e. the week of 20 July) actually produces power-plant strikes or quietly slips. (4) Any Iranian signal of willingness to meet that is not filtered through Trump.

Confidence: high (direct quotes, multiple outlets)
Naval14–17 Jul

Blockade reimposed: US disables a tanker heading for Kharg Island, traffic through Hormuz falls to seven vessels a day

The US military reimposed its blockade of vessels transiting "to and from Iranian ports and coastal areas" on the evening of Tuesday 14 July, and enforced it: CENTCOM said it disabled an empty oil tanker sailing toward Kharg Island, Iran's principal crude export terminal, and later struck a tanker attempting to skirt the blockade. The measurable effect on the strait was immediate — tanker transits fell to two-month lows, with only seven vessels passing on Wednesday, the first full day of the reimposed blockade, down from 13 the day before. For reference, Hormuz normally carries roughly one-fifth of world energy shipments.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Kharg Island handles the overwhelming majority of Iranian crude exports — historically around 90% — through a small number of jetties in the northern Gulf. Disabling an empty tanker inbound to Kharg is a deliberately calibrated act: it interdicts Iran's export capacity without creating a spill or a mass-casualty event, and it signals that the blockade is enforced against shipping rather than merely declared. The blockade itself has now been imposed, lifted (as part of the 17 June MoU) and reimposed within a month, which is itself informative about how little of the MoU survives.

Die Details. The traffic collapse is the number that matters, because it is externally observable rather than claimed by either belligerent. Seven transits in a day, against a normal baseline in the high dozens, is a functional closure regardless of whether anyone has formally declared one. Insurance is doing much of the work: war-risk premiums for Gulf transits have been the binding constraint on charterers all month, and a US blockade plus IRGC anti-ship activity makes the underwriting question nearly unanswerable. Note the symmetry that has developed — the US blockades Iranian ports, Iran effectively blockades everyone else's traffic through the strait, and both sides describe the other as the party closing the waterway.

Einordnung. This is the structural core of the crisis and the reason it is hard to end. The US has a stated war aim ("reopen and protect Hormuz") that requires Iran to stop doing the one thing that gives Iran leverage; Iran has an implicit war aim (make the strait ungovernable without Iranian consent) that requires the US to stop blockading Kharg. Neither can concede first without conceding the whole point. Absent a face-saving mechanism — which is what the Chinese and Pakistani mediation is trying to build — the equilibrium is a slow strangulation of both Iranian exports and global transit.

Watch-Items. (1) Daily transit counts — the single best real-time indicator, better than any statement. (2) A boarding or seizure of a third-country flagged vessel by either side. (3) Chinese-flagged or China-bound cargoes being interdicted, which would force Beijing's hand. (4) Whether Kharg itself is struck, which would take Iranian exports to roughly zero.

Confidence: high (CENTCOM statements + independent traffic data)
Diplomacy13–17 Jul

Nuclear talks technically resumed mid-July — and immediately disagreed about what had already been agreed

The negotiating track did not die this week; it just became irrelevant to events on the ground. Nuclear talks resumed in mid-July, but a RealClearDefense assessment on 13 July laid out "three headaches": major disputes over what has actually been agreed so far, plus two unresolved issues not yet even joined — country-wide intrusive inspections and tighter control of Iran's spent reactor fuel. Earlier Doha meetings (around 1 July) had produced "positive progress" and a promised "communication channel," with Vice President JD Vance saying talks were "going well." Frozen Iranian assets and inspector access remain the two structural blockers. On 17 July, China and Pakistan jointly called for an immediate ceasefire and resumption of dialogue.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. The negotiating architecture of 2026 is a stack of partial agreements that have each been partly repudiated. Sequence: Trump's letter to Ali Khamenei with a 60-day deadline; US-Israeli strikes from 28 February that killed Khamenei; fighting to 8 April; a Pakistan-announced two-week pause; talks in Switzerland from 22 June; the 17 June MoU brokered by Qatar and Pakistan (60-day ceasefire, Hormuz reopening, timeframe for a final deal); Doha meetings around 1 July; collapse on 8 July. Each layer left ambiguity that the next layer inherited — which is precisely why the parties now disagree about the content of their own prior agreements.

Die Details. The two named unresolved issues are the hard ones, not the easy ones. Country-wide intrusive inspections means an Additional-Protocol-plus regime with access to undeclared sites — the thing Iran has refused since 2006 and the thing Pickaxe Mountain exists to defeat. Spent reactor fuel control is the plutonium pathway, which the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action handled via the Arak redesign and which has been unmanaged since 2019. Neither is a bargaining chip that can be split; both are binary. The first-order dispute — what was already agreed — is more mundane but more corrosive: it means the mediators (Qatar, Pakistan, now China) are holding different texts or different verbal understandings, which is the classic failure mode of shuttle diplomacy without a written framework.

Einordnung. The honest read is that there is no live negotiation this week — there is a negotiation-shaped object that both sides keep alive for third-party audiences. Trump says Iran "wants to meet"; Iran has not said so in its own voice. The one genuinely new development is China's visible entry as a co-mediator with Pakistan, which is less about Beijing's leverage over Tehran (real but limited) than about Beijing's exposure: China is the largest buyer of Gulf crude and the party most damaged by a closed strait. If a deal happens in the next month, it will likely be Chinese-brokered, and that will be read in Washington as a strategic loss even if it is a substantive win.

Watch-Items. (1) Any Iranian statement in Farsi confirming willingness to meet. (2) Whether a written framework replaces verbal understandings. (3) IAEA access requests and Iranian responses. (4) A Wang Yi visit to Tehran or a Chinese-hosted venue.

Confidence: medium-high (analysis-heavy, few primary texts)
Iran internalongoing

Four and a half months in, Iran is fighting a war with a Supreme Leader nobody has seen

Mojtaba Khamenei was named Supreme Leader on 9 March 2026 after his father Ali Khamenei was killed in the 28 February strike. He has still not appeared publicly. He was reportedly injured in the same airstrike that killed his father, and his first message was issued in writing. Analysts describe a system that has adapted: he is "not quite supreme" and operates as one voice within a security-elite consensus, with the IRGC — which reportedly pressured Assembly of Experts members with "repeated contacts and psychological and political pressure" before his selection — carrying disproportionate weight. This week's decisions to fire on seven neighbouring states were therefore taken by an institution, not a person.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. The velayat-e faqih system is built around a single individual with religious and constitutional finality. It has survived one succession (1989, Khomeini to Ali Khamenei) and that one was managed over years with an explicit constitutional amendment to lower the clerical bar. The 2026 succession was executed in nine days, under bombardment, for a candidate whose religious credentials are thin and whose principal qualification is lineage — the precise dynastic outcome the 1979 revolution was rhetorically built against. The Assembly of Experts vote (3–8 March) was, by multiple accounts, coerced.

Die Details. Two facts drive everything else: he is unseen, and he was wounded. His condition is genuinely unknown outside a small circle — the absence of even a staged photograph after four and a half months is unusual enough to sustain serious speculation about incapacity. The system's workaround has been to distribute authority: the Supreme National Security Council, the IRGC command, and the presidency operate by consensus, with fatwa-level decisions apparently deferred or issued in writing. The 6–9 July funeral cycle for Ali Khamenei concluded with burial in Mashhad and, notably, without Mojtaba appearing at his own father's funeral — which is either a security decision of extraordinary caution or evidence he could not attend.

Einordnung. CNN's counter-intuitive reading — that invisibility "might be helping the regime survive" — is the right one, and it cuts both ways. A leader who cannot be seen cannot be decapitated a second time, cannot be personally blamed for defeats, and cannot be forced into a public climbdown. But he also cannot deliver one. Every negotiated settlement of this war ultimately requires someone in Tehran with the standing to accept humiliating terms and make them stick religiously. Right now nobody obviously has that standing. This is the most under-priced obstacle to any ceasefire: not Iranian unwillingness, but Iranian incapacity to bind itself.

Watch-Items. (1) Any verified image or live appearance. (2) A written fatwa or decree on the war, which would signal functioning authority. (3) Signs of IRGC-clergy friction over negotiating authority. (4) Any move to formalise a regency or council arrangement.

Confidence: medium-high (absence is verified; internals are inference)

Energy shock — the price of a closed strait

2 items
Markets17 Jul

Brent closes +4.6% at $88.10 — the highest of the war, and now a Russian revenue subsidy

Oil finished the week sharply higher: Brent crude, the international benchmark, advanced about 4.6% to close at $88.10 per barrel on Friday 17 July, driven by the sixth night of strikes, the reimposed blockade and the collapse in Hormuz transits. That is up from $84.91 a week earlier and roughly $78 two weeks ago — a ~13% move in a fortnight. The mechanical consequence is uncomfortable: every dollar of war premium partially offsets the revenue damage the EU price cap and Ukraine's refinery campaign are trying to inflict on Moscow. Russian crude exports had already climbed from 4.9 million barrels per day in February to six million by May.

CNBC · Brent $88.10 Trading Economics · Brent series TIME · windfall from elevated prices
Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. The oil narrative has now fully inverted twice in six weeks. Mid-June: Hormuz reopening hopes under the MoU drove prices down toward pre-war levels and the consensus view was a 2026 supply glut. 8 July: US strikes reversed that in a single session. 13 July: +9.6% in a day. 17 July: +4.6% to $88.10. Note that intraday and closing quotes diverge across sources this week — Trading Economics shows Brent at $85.95 on 17 July, +2.04%, while CNBC reports a $88.10 close. That gap is contract- and timestamp-dependent (front-month vs. settlement window) and both are plausible; the direction and magnitude are not in dispute, the precise level is.

Die Details. The supply arithmetic is unusually stark. The IEA puts roughly 12.8 million barrels per day of global supply as disrupted by the conflict — a number that dwarfs any single OPEC+ decision and is only survivable because it is partly reroutable (pipeline capacity to the Red Sea and across Saudi Arabia) and partly drawn from inventory. Asia takes 80–90% of its energy through Hormuz; Japan and South Korea have absorbed the shock with hedges and strategic stocks, while Pakistan, Bangladesh and India — the price-sensitive buyers — have been hit hardest. Meanwhile Russia's Urals discount narrows in a tight market, so the same barrel that earned Moscow a capped, discounted price in a $70 world earns materially more in an $88 world even with the cap nominally intact.

Einordnung. This is the week's central irony and the reason the geopolitical and market briefings converge. Washington is simultaneously running a maximum-pressure campaign against Iran that raises the oil price, and a maximum-pressure campaign against Russia that depends on the oil price being low. The two policies are in direct mechanical conflict, and Iran is currently winning that argument on Moscow's behalf. Every additional strike round transfers revenue to the Kremlin. Nobody in the EU sanctions room is unaware of this, which is part of why the 21st package is stuck.

Watch-Items. (1) Whether Brent holds above $85 into next week or fades on mediation headlines. (2) OPEC+ spare-capacity signals from Saudi Arabia and the UAE. (3) Strategic-reserve releases from the US, Japan or the IEA collectively. (4) The Urals-Brent differential — the cleanest measure of whether sanctions are still biting.

Confidence: medium-high (price level varies by source; direction certain)
Institutional warning16–17 Jul

IEA chief Fatih Birol: "We should be worried" — global energy security at risk if Hormuz stays shut for weeks

The head of the International Energy Agency issued the strongest institutional warning of the war on Thursday 16 July: global energy security is under serious threat unless US–Iran efforts improve oil flows through the strait. "Oil security is still a critical issue," Fatih Birol said. "We should be worried, and I am worried if the situation does not improve in the next few weeks." The IEA's framing: the strait normally carries about one-fifth of world energy shipments and has been mostly blocked since 28 February; roughly 12.8 million barrels per day of supply has been removed from the global balance; Asia sources 80–90% of its energy through it; developing importers — Pakistan, Bangladesh, India — are worst affected.

Al Jazeera · Birol warning Insurance Journal · full remarks Business Today · "we should be worried"
Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. The IEA was created in 1974 precisely for this scenario — a supply interruption large enough to require coordinated stock releases among consuming nations. Its executive director rarely uses the phrase "energy security is at risk" without a policy purpose behind it, and the explicit time horizon ("the next few weeks") reads as a signal that a coordinated release is being prepared or at least modelled. The agency has spent most of 2024–26 publishing glut forecasts; a public reversal of that posture is significant on its own.

Die Details. Two numbers deserve scrutiny. First, "mostly blocked since 28 February" is a much stronger claim than the week-to-week transit data implies — there have been periods of substantial flow, notably during the MoU window in late June. Read it as a statement about average degradation across the period, not continuous closure. Second, the 12.8 mb/d disruption figure is very large — roughly 12–13% of global supply — and almost certainly counts gross flows affected rather than barrels permanently lost, since a meaningful share reroutes via the East-West pipeline across Saudi Arabia to Yanbu and via the UAE's Fujairah line, which together bypass the strait with roughly 6–7 mb/d of nameplate capacity. The distinction matters enormously for how bad this actually is: gross exposure of 12.8 mb/d with 6 mb/d of bypass is a serious but survivable dislocation; 12.8 mb/d of genuinely lost supply would be the largest energy shock in history. The public reporting does not disambiguate, and that is a real gap.

Einordnung. The distributional point is the one that will have political consequences. Rich importers hedge and hold stocks; South Asia does neither at scale. Pakistan is simultaneously a co-mediator of this war and one of its principal economic victims, which explains the energy behind the Wang–Dar ceasefire call. Expect the Global South framing of this conflict — a US-driven war raising energy costs for countries with no stake in it — to harden considerably over the next month, with consequences for UN votes and for the sanctions coalition Washington needs against Russia.

Watch-Items. (1) An IEA coordinated stock release announcement. (2) Diesel and jet-fuel cracks, which move before crude in a genuine physical shortage. (3) Pakistani and Bangladeshi fuel rationing. (4) A clarified IEA breakdown of gross vs. net disruption.

Confidence: medium-high (quotes solid; 12.8 mb/d figure under-specified)

Ukraine / Russia — Kyiv's political crisis, Kyiv's best strike week

5 items
Domestic politics15–16 Jul

Zelensky fires Defence Minister Fedorov — protests erupt in at least 18 cities, and a wave of resignations follows

Ukraine's worst domestic political week of the war. On 15–16 July Zelensky removed Mykhailo Fedorov, the drone-first defence minister of six months and previously the digital transformation minister, in a wholesale cabinet reshuffle. Zelensky's stated reason: an unresolvable rift between Fedorov and armed forces commander Gen. Oleksandr Syrskyi — "I'm just showing that if the sides can't resolve an issue, I will have to resolve it." Fedorov, widely regarded by lawmakers, soldiers and civil-society figures as the most effective wartime official and the architect of drone scaling and procurement transparency reform, refused an offered role as presidential adviser. Thousands protested across at least 18 cities including Kyiv, Lviv and Kharkiv. Maj. Gen. Yevhen Khmara, acting head of the Security Service (SBU), was named interim defence minister; the Rada has not yet confirmed him.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Israel initiated this war jointly with the US on 28 February — the strikes that killed Ali Khamenei and wounded his successor — and Netanyahu subsequently framed the campaign as having "created an opening to topple Iran's rulers." Regime change was and remains the Israeli war aim; reopening a shipping lane is the American one. Those aims were compatible in February and are not compatible now, which is the source of the current friction. Trump's "I call the shots" remark and Netanyahu's response that Israel will strike "whenever necessary" mark the public surface of a real disagreement about who controls escalation.

Die Details. The most interesting fact is a negative one: Iran has not struck Israel in this round, despite striking seven other countries. Tehran is deliberately keeping Israel out, because Israeli entry would broaden the war in ways that serve Israeli objectives rather than Iranian ones, and because a second front invites the very decapitation campaign that killed the previous Supreme Leader. Washington's calculation runs parallel: Israeli participation would collapse whatever Gulf tolerance remains for US operations from Qatari, Kuwaiti and Bahraini soil, and would make Chinese-Pakistani mediation impossible. Qatar's public rejection of Israeli reports about its own participation shows how carefully every regional actor is policing the perception of Israeli involvement.

Einordnung. This is the war's most important tacit understanding, and it is fragile. Three actors — Washington, Tehran and Doha — all have an interest in keeping Israel out, and only Jerusalem has an interest in getting in. Netanyahu's stated trigger is an Iranian attack on Israeli territory, which Iran is scrupulously avoiding. The tail risk is therefore an accident or a proxy action attributed to Tehran, which would activate a commitment nobody in Washington currently wants activated. Watch also the domestic Israeli politics: the coalition has other fights running (the High Court and Ben Gvir dispute over police obedience surfaced again in early July), and a third Iran round has obvious domestic utility.

Watch-Items. (1) Any Iranian strike on Israeli territory or on Israeli-linked shipping. (2) Israeli independent action without US coordination. (3) Hezbollah activity on the northern border, currently quiet. (4) Whether Washington's restraint holds if the power-plant campaign begins.

Six things this week actually changed

1. The Iran war became a war on infrastructure, and that is a strategic downgrade disguised as an escalation. Hitting bridges, telecoms, railways and power plants is what you do when hitting military targets has not produced the concession you want. The US has run six-plus nights of strikes with total air superiority and Iran has not moved; the response is to widen the target set rather than change the theory. Coercion campaigns against infrastructure have a poor record generally, and a worse one against a regime with no visible leader who could accept terms even if it wanted to. Expect the "forever war" framing that appeared this week to become the dominant American domestic story within a month.

2. Regionalisation is Iran's actual strategy, and it is working better than its military strikes. Firing on seven countries in one night produced almost no verified military damage and enormous political effect: Gulf populations under air-raid alerts, a child injured in Doha, a Kuwaiti power-and-water plant hit, and a joint Chinese-Pakistani ceasefire call within hours. Iran cannot beat CENTCOM. It can make hosting CENTCOM unaffordable. The single most consequential thing that could happen next month is a Gulf state quietly restricting US launch rights — and every barrage makes that marginally more likely.

3. The two pressure campaigns are cannibalising each other, and Iran is winning that argument for Moscow. Brent at $88 is a direct transfer to the Russian budget. It also raises the domestic cost to Greece, Italy and Bulgaria of holding the EU sanctions line — which is why the 21st package failed twice in four days and the price cap now survives on a one-week technical extension to 23 July. Washington cannot run maximum pressure on Iran and maximum pressure on Russia simultaneously, because the first requires a high oil price and the second requires a low one. Nobody has publicly acknowledged this trade-off. Everybody is now living inside it.

4. Ukraine's deep-strike campaign has crossed from attrition into systemic effect — and this was the week you could see it in the front-line data. Russian artillery use down 60% and drone use down 30% in western Zaporizhia, assault intensity down 10%, mechanised assaults essentially abandoned in favour of dismounted infiltration — that is what fuel and ammunition starvation looks like at the sharp end. Upstream: 42.7% of refining capacity offline, 40% unrecoverable before mid-September even with a total ceasefire, 147 vessels struck in eleven days, and Russia buying gasoline from India through Comoros-flagged ship-to-ship transfers. A petro-state importing motor fuel is a category error that no amount of $88 crude fixes.

5. Ukraine's biggest vulnerability this week was domestic, not military. Firing the most popular official in the country, triggering protests in 18 cities and losing three senior drone and electronic-warfare figures within 48 hours is a self-inflicted wound at precisely the moment the strike campaign is delivering. Whether Zelensky was resolving a genuine command crisis or removing a political rival ahead of a settlement, the operational risk is the same: the drone ecosystem runs on informal networks that were loyal to Fedorov personally. Watch the strike tempo over the next fortnight — that is the only honest metric.

6. Russia is probing NATO's eastern flank at the exact moment Western attention is elsewhere. Baltic and Polish governments issued their most explicit joint warning of the year on 15 July; Polish jets intercepted Su-30SM2s from Kaliningrad the same day; ISW calls it Phase Zero. The realistic risk is not invasion — Russia's conventional forces are pinned — but a calibrated, deniable infrastructure attack designed to test whether Article 5 covers sabotage. The alliance has still not answered that question in public, and this is the quarter in which someone may ask it for real.

Two honest gaps this week. First, there is no independent verification of any casualty figure in Iran — every number in circulation is Iranian-government or IRGC-sourced, and the two Iranian figures in circulation do not reconcile with each other. Second, the Ukraine peace track is genuinely dark: nothing credible emerged between 12 and 18 July beyond the NATO Ankara afterglow, and I would rather say so than manufacture momentum from a February-dated Witkoff readout. The next hard dates are 22 July (EU ambassadors) and 23 July (price cap expiry).
