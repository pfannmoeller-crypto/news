SpaceX / IPO

2 cards
SpaceX May 27–28 2026 · FAA mishap

The FAA upgraded its Flight 12 review to a formal mishap investigation — Flight 13 now July–August, threatening Starlink Version 3 H2 deployment and the IPO marketing window

The single biggest move of the week, and the exact escalation the May 25 edition flagged as the wrinkle to watch. The FAA has formally declared Flight 12 a mishap — not merely an assessment — after the Super Heavy booster crash, citing Raptor 3 engine failures that sent the vehicle into an uncontrolled splashdown. NASASpaceflight and aviation trade press now report a likely July–August 2026 window for Flight 13, pending FAA mitigation requirements and testing — not an FAA statement, treat as indicative. The cleaner consequence: SpaceX’s S-1 commits to beginning Starlink Version 3 deployment in the second half of 2026, and Version 3 satellites are designed to fly exclusively on Starship. A grounded Starship into July is exactly the scenario that risks slipping that H2 commitment — the prospectus’s most concrete near-term revenue claim. Tech Times went so far as to headline the development as one that “threatens IPO timeline,” though the firm date set (roadshow June 4) has not moved. Musk in public continues to recast the upper stage’s success on five-of-six engines as the win to focus on. (Tech Times mishap upgrade, SEC Form S-1/A, TradingKey IPO timeline)

So what: The escalation from “assessment” to “mishap” is exactly the regulatory verb-shift the May 25 edition named as the thing to watch — and it happened. The real damage is not the Flight 12 vehicle (it is a sunk-cost loss of a V3 booster); it is the second-derivative risk to Starlink Version 3 deployment in H2 2026, which the prospectus relies on for the orbital data centre and revenue ramp narrative. A July–August Flight 13 leaves roughly four months to deploy V3 at any scale before year-end. The IPO can still price on June 11 with this in the disclosure — the risk factor language is the variable. Watch for the S-1/A subsequent-events update on the mishap status, and watch whether the underwriters request additional disclosure before the roadshow opens June 4.

SpaceX · IPO May 26 2026 · the $28.5T TAM line

The roadshow opens in 3 days: SpaceX puts a $28.5 trillion total addressable market on the SPCX story, anchored to the AI infrastructure pivot

The digested S-1/A this week added a number that will define the buy-side narrative for the roadshow: SpaceX is telling the market it is targeting a $28.5 trillion total addressable market (TAM) across its businesses, with the majority of that revenue tied to AI — not launch services, not Starlink connectivity. That line reframes SPCX from a launch-and-Starlink story into an AI-infrastructure platform whose terrestrial revenue is the bridge to orbital data centres. Pricing structure is unchanged: roadshow June 4, pricing June 11, debut June 12 on Nasdaq under SPCX, ~$75 billion raise at ~$1.75 trillion. Al Jazeera and others flagged the trillionaire-threshold framing — a successful pricing puts Musk on track to become the first individual ever credibly described as one. Musk’s 85.1% voting power via Class B super-voting shares stands; retail buyers underwrite the long-dated AI thesis with effectively no governance rights. (Al Jazeera $28.5T TAM, HeyGoTrade SPCX guide, ECIKS June 12 confirmation)

So what: The $28.5 trillion TAM is the line the buy-side will either swallow whole or sharpen its pencils against — and it is now the explicit pricing thesis. The honest read: a TAM of that magnitude exists only if you accept that AI compute migrates substantially off-planet within a decade and that SpaceX owns the launch monopoly that makes orbital data centres economic. Both can be true in 15 years; neither materially supports H2 2026 revenue, which is what the lock-up-period institutional buyer actually adjudicates on. The pitch is therefore that SPCX is a long-duration option masquerading as an IPO, and the Starship mishap above is the timing risk on the option. June 4 is the gauge: institutional take-up tells you whether the AI-infrastructure framing landed or whether the buy-side is treating it as a Starlink-and-launch business with a side bet attached.

SpaceXAI / Grok

2 cards
SpaceXAI May 6 2026 · structural shift

xAI is gone: Grok and X now sit inside SpaceXAI, a SpaceX division — and the consolidated S-1 puts a 2025 AI loss of $6.4 billion on the table

The structural shift the May 25 edition flagged as “financials consolidated into the SpaceX prospectus” this week became the rebrand itself. On May 6, 2026, Musk announced xAI ceases to exist as a standalone company; Grok and X are now part of a new SpaceXAI division inside SpaceX. The February 2026 all-stock merger that valued SpaceX at $1 trillion and xAI at $250 billion (combined $1.25 trillion — the largest merger of all time) now has its operational endpoint. The numbers it produced are now disclosable: per the S-1, the AI segment generated $3.2 billion in 2025 revenue against a $6.4 billion operating loss. SemiAnalysis separately confirmed that Anthropic has rented all compute capacity at Colossus 1 (a non-trivial competitive-economics signal — Anthropic is paying SpaceXAI for the older cluster while SpaceXAI keeps the larger Colossus 2 for its own work). The Grok team itself was further restructured: roughly 10 layoffs and an organisational reset under the new division. (SpaceXAI overview, HundredTabs dissolution, SemiAnalysis Colossus 2)

So what: Three reads, in order. (1) Governance: Anything that used to look like “xAI” for risk and capital purposes is now SpaceX. The S-1 disclosure of $6.4 billion in AI operating losses is the cleanest measure yet of what SPCX shareholders are actually being asked to fund — a real number, against a real $3.2 billion AI revenue base. (2) Competitive economics: Anthropic renting Colossus 1 means SpaceXAI is monetising older training capacity to a direct frontier-model competitor. That is a constructive sign for the SpaceXAI infrastructure thesis — it implies real third-party demand for the cluster — and a less constructive sign for the Grok competitive moat, which is now training on the strictly-larger Colossus 2 while a rival pays its electricity bill. (3) Narrative: The merger’s consolidated entity is the actual unit being underwritten on June 11. Cross-reference the AI briefing on the compute×power bottleneck for the broader industry frame.

SpaceXAI · compute continuity · ~2 GW path

Compute build-out: a third Memphis building added; the stated target is ~2 gigawatts of training capacity

The compute scaling story behind SpaceXAI also sharpened this week. Per Interesting Engineering and adjacent reporting, the December 30 2025 purchase of a third building in the Memphis Colossus footprint is part of an explicit target to push total training capacity to nearly 2 gigawatts (GW), combining the Memphis Colossus sites with the newer Colossus 2 build. That number is the practical ceiling SpaceXAI is currently underwriting against terrestrial power, before any orbital deployment. The May 23 TechCrunch “given up on solar” framing — covered in the May 25 edition — still describes how those gigawatts are being delivered: on-site natural-gas turbines, ahead of full permitting in Mississippi, with the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) injunction suit attached. Nothing on the Mississippi litigation moved this week. (Interesting Engineering third building, xAI Colossus page)

So what: The number to log is ~2 GW. That is the size of the bet on terrestrial power for SpaceXAI training before the orbital story has to start carrying any of the load. For frame: that is roughly the steady-state output of two large nuclear units, delivered on a build-out timeline measured in months, with gas turbines and contested permits. The orbital-data-centre pitch in the SPCX prospectus exists precisely because the next leg above 2 GW is what the Mississippi litigation and the political-environmental balance sheet cannot easily underwrite. The contradiction named last week stands; the size of the terrestrial ceiling is now legible.

Tesla May 25–31 2026 · stock + robotaxi

TSLA drifts to $434 even as the robotaxi scoreboard widens against Waymo — and 17 Austin incidents land in the press

The stock outperformed a flat hero takeaway this week: TSLA closed near $434.35, trading the $428–441 band, with retail sentiment turning “bearish” from “neutral” and chatter volume down ~32%. The market-cap line is still roughly $1.6 trillion. The substantive update is on the autonomy gap: a Texas Department of Public Safety disclosure showed 42 authorised driverless vehicles for Tesla in the state — against 577 for Waymo, 317 for Avride and 35 for Zoox. Separately, reporting placed 17 known incidents involving the Tesla Austin robotaxi fleet between July 2025 and April 2026, including two cases with minor injuries. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) investigation thread is unchanged: open inquiries into Autopilot- and Full Self-Driving (FSD)-related collisions with emergency vehicles, failure-to-stop incidents and reduced-visibility crashes are still on the docket. Tesla’s chief financial officer (CFO) reiterated negative free cash flow for the remaining three quarters of 2026 as the company funds AI compute, the AI5 in-house inference chip, Cybercab and Megapack 3 production ramps, and Optimus. (Stocktwits Waymo gap, Yahoo TSLA quote, HeyGoTrade Q1 recap)

So what: The price action and the news are pointing different directions. Stock up modestly; structural autonomy gap widening; 17 Austin incidents in print. The honest framing: the equity is pricing the Optimus and SPCX-halo halo trades more than the robotaxi data, because the robotaxi numbers do not yet support a clean trajectory. Tesla is now meaningfully behind Waymo on authorised vehicle count in its highest-visibility US deployment market, and the NHTSA file remains the binding regulatory variable rather than fleet supply. A 14× gap to Waymo on Texas authorisations is not a competitive nuance; it is a category fact. The reason the stock can still drift higher: investors are increasingly underwriting Tesla as an AI infrastructure / robotics platform with autonomy as one optionality among several. Watch whether NHTSA escalates or closes any of its open investigations — that, not the next vehicle delivery number, is the load-bearing variable.

Tesla · Optimus May 2026 · line conversion

Optimus V3 reveal officially in the late July / August window; Fremont Model S/X line is being physically converted now

Optimus moved from speculative to scheduled this week. Tesla’s Q1 2026 earnings call confirmed Musk’s commitment that Optimus production will begin at Fremont in late July or August 2026 — roughly four months after the final Model S and Model X rolled off the line in early May. As of late May, the Model S/X line at Fremont is being physically converted to Optimus manufacturing. A separate pilot Optimus line has been operational since January 21 2026 and provided the first video glimpse this month. Capacity targeted at full V3 ramp: 1 million units/year at Fremont, with Gigafactory Texas planned to add humanoid production aimed at 10 million units/year by 2027. Musk himself warned that initial output will be “quite slow,” calling it “literally impossible to predict” the production rate this year, given Optimus has ~10,000 unique parts across an entirely new production line. (Electrek line conversion, Basenor pilot line video, TradingKey V3 timeline)

So what: Optimus is now the calendarable Tesla event. The first set of marks: V3 reveal in late July/August, Fremont line live in the same window, initial throughput slow by Musk’s own admission, the 1 million×10 million targets standing as a 2027 multi-year promise rather than a 2026 deliverable. For portfolio purposes, this is the proof-point that meaningfully restocks Tesla’s catalyst pipeline after a quiet 60 days. The honest framing: a polished reveal is high-probability; meaningful production volumes in 2026 are not. The market will treat the reveal as the catalyst regardless; the actual investment thesis is what the pilot line throughput looks like in Q4 2026, not at the launch event. Politics May 25 – June 1 2026 · wind-down

DOGE in its final month: claimed savings revised toward $170–180 billion, OPM digital paperwork programme announced, charter still ends July 4

The Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) is heading into the final 33 days of its founding charter, and two pieces moved. First, the savings claim: external coverage this week placed DOGE’s self-reported figure at roughly $170–180 billion, down from the ~$215 billion cited in last week’s edition. The drop appears to be a method/dashboard revision rather than an explicit retraction; the public DOGE.gov widget is the source. The countervailing CBS News / USAFacts analysis estimating actions cost taxpayers roughly $135 billion in paid leave, mis-firing/re-hiring, and lost productivity has not been updated. Net: the savings claim’s headline figure is more contested than ever, days before the July 4 charter end. Second piece: on June 2, the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) announced it is replacing its 65+ year-old paper process with an all-digital alternative in collaboration with DOGE — small in dollar terms, large as a signal that DOGE is exiting via institutionalised process changes rather than headline cuts. Musk’s formal special-government-employee role ended in May 2025. (DOGE Wikipedia, CBS cost analysis, Britannica DOGE entry)

So what: Not market-moving in any direct sense, but worth carrying as a portfolio variable. With the charter expiry in 33 days, the savings figure’s slide toward $170 billion against a $135 billion cost estimate puts DOGE’s net-impact claim into a narrower — and contested — margin than it had a month ago. For the Muskverse, DOGE is now a slow drag on the political-capital ledger the defence-contracting and regulatory-approval parts of the portfolio quietly depend on. The OPM digital programme is more useful as a signal than as a number: it indicates DOGE is exiting via process embedding into the permanent civil service, not via fresh cuts. July 4 is the date when the scoreboard closes, and the post-mortem begins.

Musk Legal

1 card
SEC · OpenAI June 1 2026 · deadline today

Today is the SEC settlement’s written-answer deadline before Judge Sooknanan — three days before SPCX opens its roadshow

The single highest-stakes date on the calendar lands today. U.S. District Judge Sparkle Sooknanan’s June 1 deadline for written answers from the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and Musk on the Twitter-disclosure settlement is, by the time most readers will see this briefing, either passed or imminent. The settlement on the table: a $1.5 million civil penalty payable by a trust in Musk’s name, with no admission of wrongdoing, resolving the SEC’s claim that Musk waited too long to disclose his 5%-plus Twitter stake in April 2022 — allegedly saving him $150 million at other investors’ expense. Sooknanan has flagged three issues: the trust structure, the penalty’s size relative to the alleged $150 million benefit, and the removal of Musk personally as a defendant. She has also said she will evaluate fairness to ordinary investors and the possibility of collusion. Sooknanan rejected Musk’s earlier dismissal attempt in February 2026, which makes her not a rubber-stamp judge on this matter. The OpenAI/Musk v. Altman thread continues quietly on the 9th Circuit appeal docket; the May 18 statute-of-limitations verdict stands. (Globe and Mail settlement, Yahoo Finance hearing, Union Leader $1.5M fine)

So what: June 1 is the only legal date with a meaningful interaction with the IPO calendar — Sooknanan has a non-trivial demonstrated willingness to push back on this case (she rejected the dismissal attempt). The base case stays: she accepts the settlement, the SEC thread closes, the roadshow opens uneventfully on June 4. The tail case is what to watch for: if she rejects the settlement on her stated objections, the SEC enforcement thread re-opens just as the roadshow begins, and SPCX’s risk factor language has to address a live SEC action against the controlling shareholder during the marketing window. Outright rejection of SEC deals remains rare, but the dismissal-rejection precedent means it is not the standard rubber-stamp posture. The next 72 hours decide whether this stays a footnote or becomes the lead.

Neuralink high-volume production confirmed for 2026, Boring Co.’s Dubai Loop adds Parsons as contractor, X platform quiet

Three smaller updates collected for portfolio completeness. The Boring Company: on May 4 2026, Parsons Corporation was awarded the professional services contract for the Dubai Loop — the 6.4 km pilot route from Dubai International Financial Centre to Dubai Mall, AED 565 million (~USD 154 million), with construction starting late 2026 toward a mid-2026 operational target. The Vegas Loop transported ~82,000 passengers during CONEXPO/CON–AGG 2026 in early March; Prufrock machines now mine and build simultaneously, controlled remotely from Bastrop, Texas. X (formerly Twitter): no fresh material development in the window; Linda Yaccarino remains chief executive officer (CEO) of record under the new SpaceXAI structure. The advertiser-confidence question is the live one but produced no headline this week. (FierceBiotech Neuralink, The National Dubai Loop, Basenor Boring Company)

Dubai: Parsons as a named engineering contractor moves the Dubai Loop from announcement to procurement, the first credible operational endpoint outside Las Vegas. The X platform remains the loudest unrealised liability inside the consolidated SpaceXAI entity; ad-revenue trajectory is the next variable to surface, likely at the post-IPO disclosure cadence. None of this changes the Muskverse’s next-60-day catalyst stack — SPCX pricing, FAA Flight 13 timing, SEC settlement outcome, Optimus reveal — but all three sit in the second-order pipeline.
