Eighth straight week up: Nvidia's "garden-variety beat" got shrugged off, retailers held the consumer line, and a "near" Iran deal carried the tape to records

The week that was supposed to break on Nvidia instead broke higher on everything around it. After a soft start — the Nasdaq and S&P sank Mon 18 May with the 10-year Treasury yield at a one-year high — stocks rallied through the back half. Nvidia delivered a genuine blowout Wed 20 May (record $81.6bn revenue, +85%) and the stock barely moved; the market had already priced perfection. What actually drove the comeback was Middle East de-escalation: a Thu/Fri run of headlines that the US and Iran are "near" a draft resolution, even after one such report was flagged as bogus. The S&P 500 closed Fri 22 May at 7,469 and the Dow hit a record 50,580 — the S&P's eighth consecutive weekly gain, its longest winning streak since 2023. Brent fell back to ~$104, down more than 6% on the week. US markets are closed Mon 25 May for Memorial Day.

Calendar note: normal 7-day pull, but this was a two-half week — a yield-driven Mon/Tue dip, then a four-session grind to records on Iran-deal optimism plus solid retail earnings. Nvidia (Wed 20 May post-close) is now in the rearview; Salesforce, Dell and Costco report next week. US markets closed Mon 25 May (Memorial Day).

Earnings AI

Nvidia: record $81.6bn revenue, +85% YoY, $91bn Q2 guide — and the stock barely moved

Nvidia delivered fiscal Q1 FY27 Wed 20 May after the close: revenue $81.62bn (+85% YoY) against a ~$78.9bn Street consensus, adjusted EPS $1.87 versus $1.78 expected, net income $58.3bn versus a $42.9bn estimate. Data-center revenue nearly doubled. Management guided Q2 to ~$91bn, above the Street, and raised the dividend. By every conventional measure this was a blowout. The market response was the story: NVDA fell more than 1.5% in after-hours and traded down ~0.9% Thursday. Analysts called it "a garden-variety beat … well telegraphed" after the strong AI-hyperscaler prints earlier in the season — the bar had been set at perfection, and a beat that merely meets perfection clears nothing. The constructive read: Nvidia did its job, the AI-earnings cushion under the indices is intact for Q2, and the tape did not need the stock to rip to keep climbing. The cautionary read: the most important print of the cycle could not move its own stock, which is what late-cycle positioning looks like.

Earnings Retail

Walmart, Target, Home Depot: the consumer is holding — but every retailer hedged the back half

The retail trio gave the cleanest consumer read of the quarter, and the message was "resilient now, cautious ahead." Walmart (Thu 21 May) reported Q1 net income of $5.33bn, adjusted EPS $0.66 — matching consensus — and said low prices and fast delivery are pulling in shoppers across the income spectrum. But it guided the current quarter below Street expectations, citing economic uncertainty. Target (results 20 May) posted adjusted EPS of $1.71, up 32% on prior-year adjusted EPS though down 24% on a GAAP basis. Home Depot reported Tue 19 May with same-store sales modeled roughly flat after four quarters of modest positive comps. The pattern is consistent and matters for the macro: spending is being sustained by last year's tax cuts offsetting higher energy prices, but management teams are not willing to underwrite the second half. That hedge is the same caution the Fed minutes and the bond market are expressing — three different institutions reading the same data.

Sector

Leadership broadened: Dow record, Russell 2000 +2.7% on the week — the rally stopped being just the Mag-7

The most encouraging structural feature of the week was where the gains came from. The Dow hit two record closes and the Russell 2000 small-cap index ran up roughly 2.7% on the week — outpacing both the S&P (+0.9%) and the Nasdaq (+0.7%). With Nvidia's blowout failing to move megacap tech, the marginal buyer rotated into cyclicals and small caps on the Iran-deal optimism. The S&P 500 Equal Weight index also hit fresh all-time highs alongside the Dow. This is the opposite of the concentration risk the 14 and 19 May editor's views kept flagging — for one week, breadth led. Whether it holds depends on whether the Iran de-escalation is real; small-cap and cyclical leadership is the most rate- and geopolitics-sensitive part of the tape and would be the first to give it back.

Fed

April FOMC minutes: a "majority" would HIKE if inflation stays high — the rate-cut door is shut

The minutes of the 28–29 April Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meeting, released Wed 20 May, confirmed the new equilibrium in black and white: a majority of officials believe the Fed could RAISE rates if inflation stays elevated. The Fed held the federal funds rate at 3.50–3.75% in April, but the vote was an 8–4 hold — the most dissents since 1992 — and the minutes flag energy-driven inflation running near 4%, double the 2% target. Incoming chair Kevin Warsh, nominated on the expectation he would deliver cuts, inherits a committee with "little appetite" to ease while prices rise. His first FOMC is 16–17 June. The market read this as confirmation, not shock — equities still rallied — but it removes any remaining ambiguity: the base case is hold-with-a-hike-bias, and a dovish surprise is now the genuinely destabilising scenario, not a hawkish one.

Geopolitics Risk

Iran de-escalation became the week's main equity driver — even one "near a deal" report was bogus

The single biggest swing factor for stocks this week was not Nvidia or the Fed — it was a steady run of headlines that the US and Iran are closing in on a draft resolution. Secretary of State Marco Rubio cited "slight progress" in Pakistan-mediated talks; Tehran is reviewing a US proposal. The optimism carried the Dow to records and pushed Brent down more than 6% on the week. The caveat is sharp: TheStreet explicitly flagged that one Thu 21 May report of a draft resolution being "near" was bogus, yet markets still rose on it. That tells you positioning is leaning hard toward a deal — which means the asymmetry has flipped from a month ago. Then, any escalation headline was a selloff catalyst; now, a credible breakdown in talks would hit a tape that has already banked the good outcome. The Strait of Hormuz remains the unresolved structural issue: Trump rejected Iran's proposed toll framework, insisting the waterway stay open and free.

Oil

Brent back to ~$104, down 6%+ on the week — the volatility band is intact, the direction reversed

After last week's $109 → $111 → $102 rollercoaster, Brent crude spent this week grinding lower on Iran-deal optimism, settling near $103.94/bbl Fri 22 May (+1.3% on the day off a $106 intraday high). For the week, Brent fell more than 6% — the mirror image of the prior week's spike — as markets priced in a higher probability that the parties reach an agreement. Two things to keep in proportion. First, even after the 6% weekly drop, Brent is still roughly 60% above its level a year ago; "lower this week" is not "low." Second, the structural issue is unresolved — Trump rejected Iran's Hormuz toll proposal, so the chokepoint risk that drove the spring spike is still live. The energy pulse feeding the Fed's near-4% inflation reading has eased at the margin, not been removed. The $101–$111 band of the past three weeks remains the working range until a real deal or a real escalation takes a side out.

Editor's view

5 takeaways
  1. Nvidia's non-reaction is the most important signal of the week. The single most-anticipated print of the cycle came in as a genuine blowout — $81.6bn revenue, +85%, a $91bn Q2 guide above the Street, a dividend raise — and the stock fell. Last week's editor's view called Thursday's open "a real binary." It resolved, and the answer is uncomfortable: even perfection cannot move the most important AI stock when expectations have been set at perfection. The constructive read is that the AI-earnings cushion is confirmed and intact for Q2; the indices kept rising without needing NVDA to. The cautionary read is that when the load-bearing stock can't move on its own blowout, you are watching late-cycle positioning, not early-cycle momentum.
  2. The rally found new legs in breadth — and that is healthier, but more fragile. The Dow set two records, the Russell 2000 ran +2.7%, and the S&P Equal Weight hit all-time highs while megacap tech went sideways. For one week the concentration risk this briefing has flagged since 14 May actually reversed: cyclicals and small caps led. That is the rotation bulls want to see. But it is rate- and geopolitics-sensitive leadership — it climbed on Iran-deal optimism and a one-year-high yield that has not actually come down. If the deal narrative cracks, small caps and cyclicals are the first to hand it back. Breadth led, but on borrowed conviction.
  3. The Iran de-escalation trade has flipped the asymmetry — and the market is now long the good outcome. A month ago every escalation headline was a selloff. This week the tape rose on optimism reports, including one TheStreet explicitly flagged as bogus. Markets rallied on a false headline because positioning is leaning hard toward a deal. That is the definition of a priced-in outcome. The structural issue — Trump rejecting Iran's Hormuz toll framework — is unresolved, so the chokepoint risk is still live. If the talks break, this is no longer a tape with a cushion; it is a tape that has to give back what it borrowed.
  4. The Fed minutes shut the door, and three institutions are now saying the same thing. The April FOMC minutes confirmed a majority would HIKE if inflation stays high — an 8–4 hold, the most dissents since 1992, energy-driven inflation near 4%. Read it alongside the retailers' uniform refusal to underwrite the second half, and the bond market's one-year-high 10-year yield, and you have the Fed, corporate America and the curve all expressing the same caution. The market rallied anyway. That gap — between a hold-with-hike-bias regime and an eight-week winning streak — is the central tension. It does not have to resolve down, but it does have to resolve.
  5. Pet-food portfolio read. The 6%+ Brent drop to ~$104 is real relief at the margin for DACH/EU diesel and freight surcharges — but keep it in proportion: Brent is still ~60% above a year ago, and Trump's rejection of Iran's Hormuz toll framework means the structural chokepoint risk that drove the spring spike is unresolved. Treat this week's energy easing as a tactical window, not a regime change; the input-hedge horizon should still run at least two quarters. The retailers' message reinforces the demand-side caution from last week: Walmart and Target are seeing a consumer sustained by last year's tax cuts but are explicitly hedging the back half, which is the same trade-down pressure on premium categories that the German/EU pet-food shelf will feel. Named-supplier coverage on rendered proteins, packaging films and wet-line steam costs still beats spot-price chasing — see the latest pet-food commodity briefing for the Verfügbarkeit detail.
