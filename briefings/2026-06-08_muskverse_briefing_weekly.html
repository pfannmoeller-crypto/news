The IPO week, minus the rocket: SPCX priced its roadshow at a fixed $135 a share, Google agreed to pay $920 million a month for SpaceXAI compute, and Grok V9 finished training — all while Starship stays grounded and the SEC settlement sits unresolved on the judge’s desk.

The single largest IPO in market history is now four days from pricing, and the week handed it a near-perfect demand signal: Google agreed to pay SpaceX roughly $920 million per month for 32 months — about $29 billion total — to rent SpaceXAI compute capacity, the cleanest third-party validation yet of the AI-infrastructure thesis underwriting SPCX. SpaceX launched its roadshow June 4 at a fixed $135 per share, an all-primary $75 billion raise at a ~$1.75 trillion valuation; pricing lands after the close on June 11, debut June 12 on Nasdaq. xAI — now the SpaceXAI division — finished training Grok V9-Medium at 1.5 trillion parameters with a mid-June public release expected, and shipped an image-to-video model. The two overhangs from last week persist: Starship remains grounded under the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) mishap investigation with Flight 13 in a July–August window, and Judge Sparkle Sooknanan has not yet ruled on the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Twitter-disclosure settlement after publicly refusing to “rubber stamp” it. Tesla, meanwhile, drifted lower — ~$432, down 16% year-to-date — with its robotaxi fleet expansion still frozen since May 10.

SpaceX / IPO

2 cards
SpaceX · IPO June 3–4 2026 · fixed $135

The roadshow opened June 4 at a fixed $135 a share — an all-primary $75 billion raise that, at ~109–116× trailing revenue, makes SPCX the largest and richest IPO ever attempted

SpaceX dispensed with the usual price-range mechanics. Per CNBC, the company is marketing 556.6 million shares at a fixed $135 each — an all-primary $75 billion raise, more than triple Alibaba’s record — with the roadshow having launched June 4, ahead of the previously floated week-of-June-8 estimate, after a faster-than-expected SEC review. Pricing is set for after the close on June 11, first trade June 12 on Nasdaq under SPCX. The ~$1.75 trillion valuation implies roughly 109 to 116 times 2025 trailing revenue — a multiple that only coheres if the buy-side accepts the AI-infrastructure and orbital-data-centre story, not a launch-and-Starlink multiple. The fixed-price, all-primary structure is itself a tell: it signals enough demand certainty that the bankers see no need to discover a price through a range, and it means every dollar raised goes to the company rather than selling holders. (CNBC fixed $135 price, TECHi S-1/A and index risk, TradingKey June 12 timeline)

So what: A fixed price on the largest IPO in history is a confidence posture, not a neutral mechanic — it removes the downside-discovery that a range provides, and it works only because the order book is presumed oversubscribed. The 109–116× revenue multiple is the entire debate compressed into one number: institutions are not buying 2026 cash flows, they are buying a 10–15-year option on SpaceX owning the launch monopoly that makes off-planet compute economic. The Google deal below is the week’s strongest argument that the option has real near-term cash underneath it. Watch the June 11 pricing for whether the book fills at $135 or whether the underwriters have to flex — and watch the low free-float dynamic, since an all-primary raise with Musk’s 85%+ voting lock means thin tradeable supply and potentially violent early price action.

SpaceX · Starship May 27 – June 2 2026 · still grounded

Starship stays grounded under the FAA mishap investigation — Flight 13 hardware (Ship 40, Booster 20) is in flow, but no launch license, and the H2 Starlink V3 risk is unchanged

No relief for the rocket this week. The FAA requires SpaceX to complete a mishap investigation into Flight 12 — triggered by Booster 19’s premature engine shutdown and failed boostback ignitions on May 22 — and to win FAA approval of the root-cause report and corrective actions before Starship can fly again. SpaceNews and Spaceflight Now confirm the agency-overseen process; a July–August 2026 window for Flight 13 is the reported expectation, with Ship 40 and Booster 20 already in flow. One wrinkle for the record: an FAA newsroom page titled “closes” the Starship mishap investigation circulated this week, but June 2 trade reporting (MyRGV) still describes this as SpaceX’s third open mishap probe since January 2025 — the weight of evidence is that Flight 13 is not yet licensed. The substantive stake is unchanged from last week: a grounded Starship into July pressures the S-1’s commitment to begin Starlink Version 3 deployment in the second half of 2026, since V3 satellites fly exclusively on Starship. (SpaceNews mishap requirement, Spaceflight Now, MyRGV third probe)

So what: The IPO is being marketed with the rocket on the ground — which is the cleanest possible test of whether the buy-side cares about Starlink/launch cadence or has fully bought the AI-infrastructure framing. If the book fills at $135 with Starship grounded, that tells you the marginal SPCX buyer is underwriting the option, not the operating rocket. The binding date is not Flight 13 itself but whether the corrective-action report lands in time to keep V3 deployment inside the H2 2026 window the prospectus relies on. A slip into Q4 narrows V3’s at-scale runway to weeks. Conflicting FAA signals this week are a reminder to anchor on the launch license, not the press headline.

SpaceXAI / Grok

2 cards
SpaceXAI · compute June 5 2026 · the $29B tenant

Google agreed to pay SpaceX ~$920 million a month for 32 months — about $29 billion — to rent SpaceXAI compute capacity, the strongest third-party demand signal the IPO could ask for

The week’s most consequential development, and the one that arms the roadshow. Per CNBC, Google has agreed to pay SpaceX roughly $920 million per month for 32 months — about $29 billion in aggregate — for compute capacity at the SpaceXAI (former xAI) data centres. This rhymes with, and dwarfs, the earlier signal that Anthropic had rented all of Colossus 1: now a second hyperscaler-tier counterparty, and Google specifically, is paying SpaceXAI for compute. Two things matter. First, it converts the “AI infrastructure” line in the SPCX prospectus from a forward narrative into contracted, recurring third-party revenue — landing four days before pricing. Second, it is a notable competitive irony: Google, owner of its own tensor processing units and a frontier-model lab, is renting Musk’s clusters, implying the global compute shortage is acute enough that even a vertically integrated hyperscaler is buying capacity wherever it can find it. (CNBC Google–SpaceX compute deal, BTCC merger & free-float)

So what: Timing makes this almost too convenient — a ~$29 billion contracted-revenue headline dropping into the roadshow window is exactly what an all-primary, fixed-price book wants to see, and it materially de-risks the “majority of TAM tied to AI” pitch. The structural read is sharper: when Google — which builds its own silicon — is paying a competitor nearly a billion a month for compute, the binding constraint on the entire AI economy is power-and-capacity, not chips or models, and SpaceXAI is monetising that scarcity directly. The competitive cost: same as the Anthropic-Colossus-1 read, Grok now trains alongside paying rivals on shared infrastructure, so the moat is the cluster, not the model. Cross-reference the AI briefing’s compute×power bottleneck thread — this deal is the cleanest single data point in it.

Grok June 5 2026 · V9 + video model

Grok V9-Medium finished training at 1.5 trillion parameters with a mid-June release expected; an image-to-video model shipped in the API preview

The product cadence inside the division stayed brisk. Musk posted on June 5 that Grok V9-Medium completed training at 1.5 trillion parameters, with supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning underway and a public release expected mid-June 2026. Separately, xAI released grok-imagine-video-1.5-preview, an image-to-video model in the application programming interface (API) preview that turns stills into short cinematic clips up to 720p with natural-language motion control. Context from prior announcements: the larger Grok 5 is in training on the Colossus 2 supercluster in variants reaching 10 trillion parameters. Naming has gotten noisy across the V9 / Grok 4.x / Grok 5 lines — treat the consistent signal as “another frontier-scale model finishing training roughly monthly,” not the specific version label. (Releasebot xAI updates, xAI news, Basenor Grok updates)

So what: The release tempo is the point, and it is what the ~$29 billion of rented Google compute and the ~2 GW power build exist to sustain. For the SPCX thesis, frontier model output is the demand that fills the clusters; for the competitive picture, Grok shipping a 1.5T-parameter model and a video model in the same window keeps it credibly in the frontier conversation alongside the next Claude and GPT releases. The honest caveat: parameter counts are marketing, not capability — the load-bearing question is benchmark and real-world performance on release, which these announcements do not yet show. Treat mid-June as the date to re-rate Grok, not June 5.

Tesla

1 card
Tesla June 1–8 2026 · stock + robotaxi

TSLA at ~$432 and down 16% year-to-date as the robotaxi expansion stays frozen since May 10 — analysts sit at Hold, with the average target below spot

Tesla’s equity drifted the wrong way this week and the picture is now clearly two-track: a soft operating story and a stock partly carried by the SpaceX halo. TSLA traded around $431.65 as of June 1, down ~16% year-to-date, against the December 2024 all-time high near $498. The autonomy data is the drag: the unsupervised Full Self-Driving (FSD) robotaxi fleet grew from nine vehicles in Austin in early April to 39 across Austin, Dallas and Houston by early May — then the expansion abruptly stopped on May 10, and the share price stalled with it. Capital intensity is unchanged: 2026 capital expenditure guided above $25 billion, with free cash flow negative for the remaining three quarters as Tesla funds AI compute, the AI5 inference chip, Cybercab, Megapack 3 and Optimus. Wall Street is unconvinced near term: TipRanks shows a Hold consensus — 12 Buys, 12 Holds, 5 Sells — with an average 12-month target of $403.86, below the current price. The Motley Fool explicitly tied part of May’s strength to the SpaceX IPO read-through rather than Tesla fundamentals. (MEXC −16% YTD & robotaxi, HeyGoTrade Q1 recap, Motley Fool SpaceX read-through)

So what: The frozen robotaxi fleet is the tell — an expansion that halts at 39 vehicles and stays halted for a month is not a scaling curve, it is a pause, and the market read it as one. With the analyst average target ($403.86) sitting below spot, the sell-side is implicitly saying the stock is carrying valuation it has not yet earned operationally — consistent with the Motley Fool’s point that the SpaceX IPO halo, not Tesla’s own catalysts, drove May. For the portfolio, the live variables remain Optimus (V3 reveal still July–August) and any unfreezing of the robotaxi rollout; until one of those moves, Tesla is the part of the Muskverse most exposed to a “sell-the-SPCX-news” unwind after June 12.

DOGE / Politics

1 card
Politics June 2026 · wind-down dispute

Reports say DOGE has effectively wound down early, with functions absorbed into OPM — the administration insists the charter runs to July 4

The story this week is a dispute over whether DOGE (Department of Government Efficiency) still meaningfully exists. Fresh reporting (Decrypt, TIME, and earlier Reuters) says DOGE has effectively ceased operating, with most functions absorbed into the Office of Personnel Management (OPM); OPM Director Scott Kupor was quoted saying DOGE “doesn’t exist” as a centralised entity. The administration pushed back, stressing the charter remains active until July 4, 2026 and that DOGE’s principles continue through individual agencies even without central leadership. Musk’s own special-government-employee role ended back in May 2025. The savings-figure debate from prior weeks (self-reported ~$170–180 billion vs. an external cost estimate near $135 billion) is unchanged; no new dashboard revision this week. (Decrypt early wind-down, TIME disbanded, The Hill OPM pushback)

So what: Not directly market-moving, but the framing matters for the portfolio’s political-capital ledger. “DOGE quietly absorbed into OPM” is the soft-landing version — it lets Musk exit government without a hard scoreboard moment, which is the better outcome for the defence-contracting and regulatory-approval exposure across SpaceX and Tesla than a contested July 4 post-mortem would be. The substance hasn’t changed in months; what’s shifting is the narrative settling toward “wound down” rather than “triumphant.” Carry it as a fading variable, not an active one.

Musk Legal

1 card
SEC · OpenAI June 1–3 2026 · ruling pending

The SEC and Musk filed to defend the Twitter settlement after Judge Sooknanan’s “red flags” — she has not yet ruled, and the deadline passed without the IPO complication materialising

The June 1 deadline came and went without the tail risk firing. The SEC and Musk submitted written defences on June 1–3 arguing the $1.5 million settlement — payable by a trust in Musk’s name, no admission of wrongdoing — is “fair, reasonable and appropriate” and “not the result of any improper collusion.” The SEC stressed the seven-figure penalty was a defendant concession reached after nearly a year of negotiation. The backdrop: U.S. District Judge Sparkle Sooknanan publicly said in May she “will not” and “cannot rubber stamp this settlement,” flagging the trust structure, the penalty’s size relative to the alleged $150 million benefit from Musk’s late April-2022 Twitter-stake disclosure, and whether Musk received special treatment. She has not yet issued a ruling. The OpenAI / Musk v. Altman thread continues quietly on the 9th Circuit appeal docket; the May 18 statute-of-limitations dismissal stands. (Insurance Journal red flags, Benzinga SEC defence, WHTC compromises)

So what: The base case held: the deadline passed, both parties defended the deal, and no live SEC-action complication has surfaced to disrupt the roadshow that opened June 4. But the case is not closed — Sooknanan’s ruling is the open variable, and her demonstrated willingness to reject (she threw out Musk’s dismissal attempt in February) means rejection is a real, if minority, outcome. If she rejects the settlement in the next two weeks, the SEC enforcement thread reopens against the controlling shareholder precisely as SPCX prices and debuts — the one legal scenario that intersects the IPO calendar. Watch the docket through June 12.

Neuralink · X · Boring continuity · one correction

Neuralink’s Blindsight wins FDA Breakthrough Device status with first patient trial targeted in 2026; plus a correction on who runs X

Two ventures updates and one correction to last week’s briefing. Neuralink: the Blindsight visual-prosthesis implant — which restores a crude visual signal by stimulating the brain’s visual cortex from a head-worn camera — has received U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) Breakthrough Device Designation, accelerating its regulatory path, with recruitment underway in the US, Canada and UK and a first patient trial targeted in 2026. Musk has set expectations low: early output will be “low resolution, like Atari graphics,” though potentially enabling sight for the congenitally blind where the visual cortex is intact. Correction — X leadership: last week’s edition stated “Linda Yaccarino remains CEO of record.” That was wrong. Yaccarino resigned as X chief executive officer (CEO) in July 2025; X has had no permanent replacement CEO since, operating under Musk and the SpaceXAI structure. No fresh X platform news in the window. The Boring Company: no new development this week beyond the prior Dubai Loop (Parsons contractor) and Vegas Loop continuity items. (Neuralink Blindsight trial, pharmaphorum breakthrough tag, Yaccarino resignation)

So what: Neuralink’s FDA Breakthrough Designation is the meaningful item — it is a regulatory accelerant, not a clinical result, but it moves Blindsight materially closer to a first-in-human vision trial and keeps Neuralink’s optionality alive as the quietest high-upside piece of the portfolio. The X correction matters for hygiene: carrying “Yaccarino is CEO” for another week would have compounded a factual error — X has run without a permanent CEO since mid-2025, which is itself the relevant fact (advertiser-relationship continuity now sits with Musk directly, under SpaceXAI). Both are continuity-tier; neither changes the June 11–12 IPO calculus.
