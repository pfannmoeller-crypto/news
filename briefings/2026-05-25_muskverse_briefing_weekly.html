SpaceX / IPO

2 cards
SpaceX May 23–25 2026 · FAA assessment

FAA opens its assessment of the Flight 12 booster crash — a routine process, but one that now overlaps the IPO marketing window

The week's one substantive new development. Following the May 22 Starship Flight 12 launch, the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) Office of Commercial Space Transportation is assessing telemetry from the Super Heavy booster anomaly to determine whether a formal mishap investigation under 14 CFR Part 450 (the regulation governing commercial launch licensing) is required. The booster failed its boost-back burn — reporting indicates only one engine ignited for the landing burn — and crashed uncontrolled into the Gulf of Mexico at roughly 1,450 km/h. The FAA confirmed no public injuries or property damage, but the crash did disrupt civil aviation: six commercial departures were delayed and five aircraft were placed in holding patterns. Aviation-trade coverage indicates the FAA expects to conclude its mishap assessment by June 2026, with a launch licence for Flight 13 anticipated in the third quarter — though that timeline is aggregator-sourced, not an FAA statement, and should be treated as indicative. Musk, for his part, publicly recast the flight as a win, congratulating the team on an "epic" first V3 launch and posting "You scored a goal for humanity." (OmniFlights FAA review, SpacePolicyOnline mishap process, Spaceflight Now Musk reaction)

So what: An FAA review after a Starship anomaly is routine — it happened after most prior test flights and has historically resolved without long groundings, especially where SpaceX self-reports and proposes corrective actions. The honest read: the base case is a contained, by-the-book process. But the timing is the wrinkle. If the FAA elevates this to a formal mishap investigation, that becomes a live "subsequent event" the SPCX prospectus has to address in its risk-factor language precisely as the June 4 roadshow opens. The asymmetry the May 19 and 23 editions flagged still holds: the upper stage working is the part the Starlink revenue story needs, and it worked; the booster is a V3 debugging item, and the FAA process is the channel through which that item could — in a tail scenario — touch the IPO calendar. Watch for whether the assessment stays an "assessment" or becomes an "investigation."

SpaceX · IPO continuity · the digested S-1

The IPO countdown: 10 days to the roadshow, and the digested prospectus sharpens the picture — $1.75 trillion sought, 85% voting control retained

No new filing this week, but the post-S-1 digestion added granularity worth logging. The timeline is firm across multiple sources: roadshow June 4, pricing June 11, trading debut June 12 on Nasdaq under ticker SPCX, with a raise of roughly $75 billion at a $1.75 trillion valuation — reporting still spans $1.75–2 trillion. The financial detail now in clearer focus: 2025 revenue of $18.67 billion against a net loss of roughly $4.9 billion; around $13 billion in cumulative losses since the start of 2023; the Starlink connectivity segment crossing 10 million subscribers and posting a ~$1.19 billion quarterly profit; and a governance point that matters — through a super-voting Class B share class, Musk retains 85.1% of voting control while holding a far smaller economic stake. Public-market buyers of SPCX would have minimal say over governance. If priced as planned, this would be the largest IPO in history and the most valuable company ever to debut on a US exchange. (Motley Fool key dates, CoinDesk June 11 pricing, Washington Post losses disclosed)

So what: The roadshow is the next hard date, and the two numbers that will define the pricing test are now both visible: the loss profile (~$13 billion burned since 2023) and the control structure (85.1% Musk voting power). A buyer of SPCX is underwriting a pre-revenue orbital-AI thesis, on a company with a real cash-generative Starlink business inside it, with effectively no governance rights. That is a coherent package only if the institutional bid accepts the AI-infrastructure multiple — which is the same referendum every recent edition has named. Nothing this week changed the thesis; it sharpened the disclosure. June 4 is when the market starts voting.

xAI May 23 2026 · energy strategy

xAI's power bet draws fresh scrutiny: TechCrunch frames Musk as having "given up on solar power on Earth" while gas turbines run ahead of permits

The genuine xAI development this week is not a Grok product update — the product cadence (Grok Skills, the May 22 connector batch) is unchanged from the May 23 edition — but an energy story. On May 23, TechCrunch framed Musk as having effectively abandoned terrestrial solar power for AI: xAI is powering its Colossus supercomputer complex around Memphis with a large fleet of on-site natural-gas turbines, and reporting points to plans to spend a further $2.8 billion on turbine capacity. The underlying controversy has been building for weeks — an earlier TechCrunch report (May 13) described xAI running close to 50 turbines at its Mississippi site with permits for only about 15 of them, the units classified as "mobile" because they sit on flatbed trailers, a status that sidesteps air-pollution rules for roughly a year. The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) has sued on behalf of nearby residents and sought an injunction. Separately, Mississippi's Department of Environmental Quality (MDEQ) approved a permanent construction permit unlocking 1.2 gigawatts of self-generated power for a Colossus 2 site in Southaven. Turbine counts vary across reports — treat the precise figures as approximate. (TechCrunch "given up on solar", TechCrunch turbines unchecked, DCD Mississippi permit)

So what: There is a real contradiction here worth naming, because it is structural, not cosmetic. The SpaceX prospectus sells orbital data centers — AI compute powered by solar arrays in space, with the Federal Communications Commission asked to authorise up to a million satellites for the purpose. On the ground, xAI is doing the opposite: burning gas, ahead of permits, with litigation attached. Both can be rationalised — terrestrial solar cannot deliver gigawatts on xAI's timeline, and space solar is a decade-out bet — but together they tell you the actual near-term constraint on the whole AI side of the Muskverse is power, delivered fast, and the political-environmental cost of getting it. The NAACP suit and the permitting gap are a slow-burn reputational and regulatory liability that the consolidated SPCX entity now carries. SEC · OpenAI May 23–25 2026 · calendar holds

Both legal threads quiet: OpenAI appeal pending at the 9th Circuit, SEC settlement review still set for a June 1 deadline

No new development on either front — logged because the calendar matters into the IPO window. The Musk v. Altman matter is resolved at the district-court level: the May 18 verdict dismissed all of Musk's claims as time-barred under the three-year statute of limitations, and Musk has confirmed an appeal to the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals. Statute-of-limitations dismissals are rarely revived on appeal, so the realistic read remains that the district-court outcome stands; the dedicated trial briefing has closed its weekly cadence accordingly. The live thread is the SEC Twitter-disclosure settlement: the $1.5 million civil penalty — payable by a trust in Musk's name, with no admission of wrongdoing — remains pending approval before U.S. District Judge Sparkle Sooknanan, who flagged three objections (the trust structure, the penalty being roughly 1% of the ~$150 million Musk allegedly cost Twitter shareholders, and the removal of Musk personally as a defendant). Written answers from both sides are due June 1. (Moneywise appeal, CNBC SEC settlement)

So what: The single calendarable legal risk is the SEC matter, and the date has not moved: June 1 — the written-answer deadline — sits three days before the SpaceX roadshow opens on June 4. If Judge Sooknanan rejects the settlement after the June 1 filings, the SEC thread re-opens during the IPO marketing window: a specific, dated downside the SPCX prospectus must address in its risk factors. Courts almost always accept SEC settlements, so outright rejection stays a tail risk — but it is a tail risk with a date on it, inside the most sensitive window of the IPO cycle. June 1 remains the date to watch hardest.
