SpaceX May 20 2026 · public S-1 filed

SpaceX files its public S-1: ticker SPCX on Nasdaq, $1.75–2T sought, and a balance sheet recast as an AI-infrastructure company

The week's structural event. SpaceX filed its public Form S-1 with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) on May 20, converting the April 1 confidential filing, and confirmed it will list on Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX. The valuation sought is widely reported at $1.75–2 trillion, with a potential raise of $40–80 billion and a trading debut as early as June 12. The prospectus is the first hard look at the combined entity created when SpaceX absorbed xAI in February 2026: for the three months ended March 31, 2026, SpaceX reported consolidated revenue of $4.69 billion, a loss from operations of $1.94 billion, and adjusted EBITDA of $1.13 billion. The contrast with the standalone history is the headline: SpaceX posted $791 million net income in 2024, then a $4.94 billion net loss in 2025 after the xAI merger folded in xAI's burn. Coverage from DataCenterKnowledge and the VC Corner frames the filing bluntly: the prospectus "recasts the company as an AI infrastructure giant," with the speculative AI/orbital-data-center segment carrying the valuation while Starlink connectivity and launch carry the actual revenue. (Yahoo Finance S-1 filed, DataCenterKnowledge recast, SEC Form S-1)

So what: Every prior edition's thesis — "the whole Muskverse is being arranged for the S-1" — is now a filed legal document. The honest read of the numbers: this is no longer the profitable launch company of 2024. The merger that built the IPO narrative also built a $4.94B annual loss into the entity, and the $1.75T+ asking valuation rests on the orbital-data-center thesis, not on current cashflow. The four-week window to June 12 trading is now a referendum on whether institutional buyers accept an AI-infrastructure multiple on a company whose AI segment is pre-revenue. Watch how the roadshow handles the loss profile — that is the pricing test.

SpaceX May 22 2026 · Starship Flight 12

Starship V3 flies on the fourth attempt: upper stage reaches space, Super Heavy booster crashes in the Gulf

After scrubbing the May 21 attempt on last-minute holds, SpaceX launched Starship Flight 12 on Friday May 22 at 22:30 UTC from Starbase, Texas — the first flight of the next-generation V3 (Block 3) vehicle and the first launch from Starbase's second pad. The result was genuinely mixed. The upper stage reached space and deployed its batch of 22 mock Starlink satellites, two of them camera-equipped to image the heat shield, and made a controlled splashdown in the Indian Ocean. But only five of the ship's six engines ignited, which kept it off the intended path (the trajectory stayed "within bounds" for the suborbital profile) and prevented an in-space relight test. The Super Heavy booster failed its boost-back burn — the engines did not re-ignite for the sustained burn — and the booster tumbled and crashed uncontrolled into the Gulf of Mexico, where it likely exploded. Neither stage was slated for recovery, so the failure modes are test data rather than lost hardware, but a booster that cannot complete boost-back is a real V3 debugging item. (TechCrunch booster lost, CNN mixed success, Space.com V3 debut)

So what: The May 19 edition flagged the asymmetric risk — a failure with a fresh prospectus in the market. What landed is the in-between case: not a clean tailwind, not a fireball-on-the-pad disaster. The V3 ship working well enough to reach space and deploy payload on its maiden flight is the substantive positive; the uncontrolled booster crash is the headline negative. For the IPO, the framing matters: SpaceX can credibly call this "expected test-flight learning," and an upper stage that flew is the part the Starlink revenue story depends on. But the roadshow now opens with a "subsequent event" that is genuinely mixed, not a triumph — the prospectus risk-factor language on Starship development will be read more closely because of it.

SpaceX × xAI context · "SpaceX acquires xAI" framing

Clarifying the "SpaceX acquires xAI" story: the acquisition closed in February — the S-1 is what made the combined entity visible this week

A note to keep the timeline honest, because the "SpaceX acquires xAI" framing has resurfaced in coverage this week. The acquisition itself is not new: SpaceX absorbed xAI in a deal that closed February 2, 2026, with Space Exploration Technologies Corp. recorded as managing member of X.AI Holdings — at the time described as the largest merger ever, valuing SpaceX around $1T and xAI around $250B. What is new this week is the S-1 filing, which is the first time the combined entity's structure and finances have been laid out publicly. Musk's stated rationale stands: combining rockets, AI and the X platform into "the most ambitious, vertically-integrated innovation engine on (and off) Earth," with the central goal of building orbital data centers — SpaceX has asked the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) to authorize up to one million satellites as solar-powered, space-based compute. The vertically integrated entity is the thing the IPO sells; this week simply made it filable and measurable. (xAI: joins SpaceX, SpaceNews orbital data centers, CNBC biggest merger)

So what: Treat any "SpaceX just acquired xAI" headline this week as recycled framing, not fresh news — the deal is three months old. The genuine signal is the opposite direction: the S-1 is the document that converts a February press release into audited, disclosed financial reality, and that reality (a $4.94B 2025 net loss, see the S-1 card above) is the part the merger announcement glossed. The vertically integrated story is real and is the spine of the valuation; the question the prospectus forces is whether "orbital data centers" is a fundable business or a narrative carrying a launch company's multiple. That is the same question the AI briefing is tracking from the compute-supply side.

DOGE's self-reported savings tick up to ~$215B; the cost-vs-savings dispute persists; charter still expires July 4

Another quiet week on the Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) front. (DOGE Wikipedia, CBS $135B cost, USAFacts audit)

So what: Two competing figures — ~$215B claimed saved against ~$135B in estimated costs — will define the DOGE post-mortem when the charter expires July 4. The gap between them is the entire credibility argument, and it stays unresolved into the SpaceX roadshow. For portfolio purposes this is not market-moving, but it is a slow drag on the political-capital side of the ledger that the defense-contracting and regulatory-approval parts of the Muskverse quietly depend on. OpenAI Trial May 18–19 2026 · verdict digested

OpenAI verdict fully digested: a unanimous jury, the judge tossing the case, an appeal filed — the implication is the merits never got tested

The May 18 verdict in Musk v. Altman is now fully reported and its implications clearer. A nine-member advisory jury in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California returned a unanimous decision finding Musk had waited too long — his claims fell outside the three-year statute of limitations — and Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers agreed and tossed the case. Musk had accused OpenAI, Sam Altman and president Greg Brockman of converting the non-profit into a for-profit entity for personal enrichment, seeking disgorged "ill-gotten gains" reported variously at $134–150 billion. The decisive implication for Musk: the substantive question — whether OpenAI actually breached its founding charitable mission — was never adjudicated. The case died on timing. Musk has confirmed he will appeal (to the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals), and coverage notes the appeal "ensures the bitter feud is unlikely to end any time soon." Statute-of-limitations dismissals are rarely reopened on appeal, so the realistic read is that the district-court outcome stands. (NPR jury dismisses, Al Jazeera what to know, CNBC verdict)

So what: The implication for Musk is two-sided and worth stating plainly. Legally, this is an unambiguous loss — unanimous jury, judge adopting it, an appeal that is statistically unlikely to revive a time-barred claim. Reputationally, the outcome is softer: because the merits were never tested, Musk's core narrative — "OpenAI betrayed its non-profit mission" — survives politically intact, which is precisely the framing he leaned on calling the loss a "calendar technicality." The portfolio-relevant point: a major piece of legal overhang resolved at the district level before the SpaceX S-1 published, letting the prospectus describe the OpenAI matter as substantially concluded rather than actively at trial. One thread effectively closes; the SEC thread remains the live one.

SEC May 19–23 2026 · settlement pending

SEC Twitter-settlement review unchanged: June 1 written-answer deadline holds, three days before the SpaceX roadshow begins

No new development this week on the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Twitter-disclosure settlement — logged for continuity because the calendar matters. The picture from the May 19 edition stands: the $1.5 million civil settlement, under which a trust in Musk's name (not Musk personally) would pay the fine without any admission of wrongdoing, remains pending approval by U.S. District Judge Sparkle Sooknanan in Washington, D.C. The judge has said she "cannot rubber-stamp" the deal and flagged three specific objections — the trust structure, the penalty being roughly 1% of the ~$150M Musk allegedly cost Twitter shareholders, and the removal of Musk personally as a defendant. Both sides have written answers due June 1. The settlement does not take effect until the judge approves it; the case does not resolve until then. (JD Journal SEC fight, Union Leader settlement terms)

So what: The SEC matter is now the single live legal thread that intersects the IPO calendar directly. June 1 — the written-answer deadline — sits three days before the SpaceX roadshow opens on June 4. If Judge Sooknanan rejects the settlement after the June 1 filings, the SEC matter re-opens during the IPO marketing window: a specific, calendarable downside the SPCX prospectus has to address in its risk-factor language. Courts almost always accept SEC settlements, so outright rejection remains a tail risk — but it is a dated tail risk, and that is what makes June 1 one of the dates to watch hardest in the Muskverse calendar over the next fortnight.
