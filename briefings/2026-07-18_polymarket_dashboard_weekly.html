Iran de-escalation

Geopolitics · 5 underlying markets · The war widened to six countries
trending_downDeteriorating — Brent $88.10, Kuwait plant hit, Trump threatens infrastructure strikes

Three days ago the escalation was bilateral and maritime. It is now regional and infrastructural. Iran says it struck US targets in Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar and Syria in retaliation for the latest American strikes, and on July 17 hit a Kuwaiti power and water desalination plant — a target class that moves the war from military-and-shipping into civilian utilities in Gulf states that are not formally belligerents. A tanker was hit by a projectile off Oman. Trump has said US forces will target Iran's infrastructure next week absent a diplomatic breakthrough; Iran's military command answered that if he does, “all the infrastructure in the region will be crushed.” That is a mutual infrastructure-destruction threat over the world's most important oil chokepoint, delivered in public, with a one-week clock on it. CNBC: oil rises as hostilities threaten Hormuz Al Jazeera: one-month high

The price that matters moved accordingly: Brent +4.6% to ~$88.10 on July 17, roughly $9 above last edition's $79.26 and ~$16 above the pre-war level. The diplomacy book is finally showing live, credible marks rather than fossils: “US-Iran diplomatic meeting by July 31” ~72%, the July-17 leg having settled around ~52%, and — the single most informative instrument in this cluster — “Strait of Hormuz traffic returns to normal by July 31” at ~51%. Read those two together: the crowd thinks a meeting is likely and normal traffic is a coin flip. That is a market pricing talks-without-resolution, which is precisely the stagflationary middle branch. A separate leg puts “Iran charges Hormuz fees by Dec 31” at ~65% — the crowd's quiet verdict that Tehran ends this war with a toll booth, i.e. that the Article 5 dispute the June memorandum never resolved gets settled in Iran's favour. The structural deadlock is unchanged and unchanging: Ukraine wants a ceasefire before a settlement, Russia refuses on the grounds that a pause lets Kyiv regroup. For the first time this year the board is carrying two independent shocks, and they want opposite things from the Fed. Oil says hike. The chip rout says hold. The single most valuable thing on this dashboard is now the instrument that prices both — and it moved one point.

The board's own call from three days ago verified, fast. The July 15 edition argued the post-CPI easing in the hike complex was a rear-view-mirror trade and would snap back on oil.
