Markets & Macro · 10-Day Catch-Up Edition

Weak jobs flip the script: a 57k payroll print sends the Dow to a record 52,900, Europe to all-time highs — while oil falls back to pre-war levels and Bitcoin breaks down.

This edition covers 10 days (26 June – 6 July) — the briefing paused after the 22 June edition, so this is a catch-up. The window contained a holiday-shortened US week (markets closed Friday 3 July for Independence Day, observed). The regime shift: June nonfarm payrolls of just +57,000 halved the market's rate-hike odds, and equities celebrated — Dow record, S&P +1.8% and Nasdaq +2.1% on the week, DAX at its first record since January. Meanwhile Brent slid below $72 as Hormuz traffic recovers and OPEC+ adds barrels, gold reclaimed $4,170, and Bitcoin closed June down ~20% at a 21-month low. Bad news for labor is, for now, good news for stocks.

US equities29 Jun – 2 Jul

Holiday-shortened week: Dow closes at a record 52,900 as weak jobs data kills the hike scare

In the four-day week (markets closed Friday 3 July for the Independence Day observance), the S&P 500 gained 1.8%, the Nasdaq 2.1% and the Dow 2.0%. The catalyst was Thursday's soft June jobs report: the Dow jumped 594.83 points (+1.14%) to a record close of 52,900.07 as traders priced out an imminent Fed hike. The S&P finished the week at 7,483.23 and the Nasdaq at 26,040.03 — the tech index actually slipped on Thursday (−0.7%) as semiconductors fell for a second straight day and Tesla sank. Defensives and rate-sensitives led: Apple +4.8%, Visa and Walmart ~+3%. Note the composition: this record is a Dow story, not a tech story — the rotation from the 22 June edition is intact.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: The week didn't start from strength — it started from a rescue. Monday 29 June snapped a five-day losing streak as the beaten-down Magnificent-Seven names rebounded, and Tuesday 30 June closed the books on the best quarter for US indices since 2020: Dow 52,319.20 (a second straight record, +8.85% YTD), Nasdaq 26,213.72 (+12.79% YTD), S&P 7,449.36 (+9.55% YTD), Russell 2000 3,024.37 (+21.86% YTD, far and away the year's best major index). A structural detail with long legs: Alphabet joined the Dow Jones Industrial Average during the window and jumped ~5% in its first session as a member — that gain mechanically powered the Dow's first-ever close above 52,000. The VIX volatility index settled at 16.45; the 10-year Treasury yield sat around 4.46%.

Die Details: Thursday's record was narrower than the headline suggests. Per TheStreet's closing wrap, the Dow rose 1.14% with 24 of 30 members up — led by Apple (+4.46%) and McDonald's (+3.34%), dragged by Caterpillar (−3.20%) and Cisco (−3.9%) — while the S&P 500 finished the day dead flat (−0.00%) despite 354 of its 500 members advancing (~70% breadth): four declining sectors, above all technology, erased what health care, staples and utilities built. The Nasdaq-100 lost 1.61% (down 2% intraday) as the VanEck Semiconductor ETF fell ~4.5%, and the Russell 2000 slipped 1.08% to 2,980.05 — back below the 3,000 mark it had conquered in June. Weekly initial jobless claims came in at 215,000, still historically low. Northlight's Chris Zaccarelli captured the mood: slowing job growth "could force some of the more hawkish Fed officials to reconsider additional rate hikes."

Einordnung: The composition confirms this is a rotation market, not a melt-up: breadth is genuinely healthy (70% advancers on a flat S&P day), but the index-level prints depend on which names are being sold to fund the buying. Two under-covered institutional stories from the window deserve note: the Supreme Court's 5–4 ruling on 30 June protecting Fed governors from "for cause" removal without proof (the Lisa Cook case) removed a genuine institutional tail risk from US rate markets; and an OMFIF survey of central banks found, for the first time, more reserve managers planning to cut dollar allocations than raise them over the next decade — slow-burn context for the dollar's worst week since April.

Watch-Items: Follow-through was already visible at compile time — TheStreet's Monday live blog reports the Dow pushing above 53,000 for a fresh closing record as tech rebounds. Next tests: FOMC minutes Wednesday 8 July, June CPI 14 July, and whether the S&P can print its own record with tech participating rather than resisting.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: The memory trade entering this window was arguably the most crowded position in equities: Micron up roughly 260% and Sandisk ~750% year-to-date, the chip complex +80%+ in H1, and industry forecasts (Deloitte) calling for a record ~$975B in 2026 global semiconductor sales with roughly half from AI chips. The equipment-maker underperformance (KLA, Teradyne, AMAT worse than the memory names themselves) is the purest capex-cut expression. Sentiment marker: TheStreet Pro ran "Micron is a top-3 stock to short" by Monday.

US labor2 Jul · BLS

June payrolls +57k — the smallest gain in four months, and the market loved it

Nonfarm payrolls rose just 57,000 in June vs 115,000 expected, with April and May revised down a combined −74,000. The oddity: unemployment dipped to 4.2%, its lowest in a year (a shrinking-participation artifact to watch, not strength). Leisure and hospitality shed 61,000 jobs on weak seasonal hiring; professional/business services (+36k) and health care (+22k) carried the gains. Wages rose 3.5% y/y, a tick up. In the current regime the market read this as pure relief: with Warsh's Fed leaning toward hikes, a cooling labor market lowers the hike odds — CME FedWatch hike probability fell from 66% to ~50% on the print, and the Dow rallied 600 points to a record. Bad macro news is currently good equity news.

BLS · Employment Situation CNBC · 57k, 4.2% Yahoo · jobs miss
Mehr lesen▾

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Every labor print this year has been a hike-odds event, because the Warsh Fed has made inflation the priority and treated the labor market as the constraint that isn't binding — May's report had shown "broadening gains," and the June FOMC's Summary of Economic Projections leaned explicitly toward tightening. Capital.com's Daniela Hathorn framed the setup going in: investors wanted to see "whether employment remains strong enough to support the Fed's higher-for-longer stance." It wasn't.

Die Details: The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported +57,000 nonfarm payrolls vs the 115,000 Dow Jones consensus — May itself was revised down to 129,000, and April/May revisions total −74,000. The headline unemployment drop to 4.2% is weaker than it looks: it was driven by the participation rate falling 0.3 points to 61.5% — people leaving the labor force, not finding jobs. Sector detail: leisure & hospitality −61,000 (the weakest seasonal hiring in years), offset by professional/business services +36,000 and health care +22,000. Average hourly earnings +3.5% y/y, a tick up. Same morning, initial claims printed 215,000 — still no layoff wave. Market plumbing: CME FedWatch hike probability collapsed from 66% to ~50%, the Dow rallied 594.83 points to its record, and Northlight's Chris Zaccarelli supplied the regime summary — a weak report "could force some of the more hawkish Fed officials to reconsider additional rate hikes," which "all things being equal, would be better for markets than further tightening."

Einordnung: This is hiring-freeze data, not firing data — claims at 215k with payrolls at 57k means companies have stopped adding, not started cutting. That combination is exactly what lets equities celebrate: it caps the Fed without signaling recession. The uncomfortable read is the participation drop plus 4.2% CPI — a mild stagflation cocktail the market is choosing to price as "no hikes" rather than "no growth." The revision pattern (−74k across two months) says the real-time payroll signal is softer than any single headline; one more print like this and the celebration logic inverts into recession pricing. A structural thread worth tracking: Goldman Sachs published a warning on AI's impact on US hiring the same week — if AI adoption is suppressing entry-level hiring, weak payrolls stop being a clean cyclical signal at all.

Watch-Items: The July jobs report in early August (does the 57k print get revised down too?); participation rate at 61.5% — further decline would flip the "good news" read; June CPI on 14 July as the other half of the stagflation question; wage growth at 3.5% vs the Fed's 2% target arithmetic.

Fed · Warshwindow + 8 Jul minutes

The question is no longer "when do they cut" — it's "do they hike," and jobs just moved the needle
