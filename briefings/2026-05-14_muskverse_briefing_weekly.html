Tesla

Tesla May 12–14 2026

Musk joins Trump's China state visit; FSD approval the unstated agenda; TSLA touches $445

President Trump's state visit to China runs May 13–15 — the first such trip in roughly a decade — and Musk is travelling with the delegation alongside Tim Cook (Apple), Kelly Ortberg (Boeing) and Larry Fink (BlackRock). The unstated Tesla agenda is Full Self-Driving (FSD) regulatory approval in China, which Musk earlier predicted for Q1 2026; Chinese state media publicly contradicted that timeline in January, and Tesla's internal teams have since shifted their working estimate to Q3 2026. Tesla currently charges roughly 62,000 yuan (~$9,100) for FSD in China, but the software remains heavily restricted pending broader approval. The trip gives Musk direct access to Chinese leadership at a moment both governments want to show goodwill. TSLA closed $445.27 on May 13 (+2.7%), capping a roughly 28% one-month rally on the Optimus and robotaxi narratives — though shares were volatile intra-week, dipping ~5% on May 12 on robotaxi-glitch and battery-delay reports before recovering. (EV, Invezz, 24/7 Wall St)

So what: The "China meeting premium" is a recurring TSLA pattern — the stock prices in the optionality of an FSD unlock before anything is signed. Watch for substance, not optics: a concrete approval pathway out of the visit would be a genuine revenue catalyst in Tesla's most compliance-invested market, while a photo-op with no regulatory movement leaves the Q3 internal estimate as the real number. The May 12 dip is the more honest signal of where fundamentals sit.

Tesla May 12–14 2026 · robotaxi

Robotaxi scaling friction surfaces; analysts debate the timeline as the stock outruns delivery

As Tesla's robotaxi service scales beyond Austin into Dallas and Houston, the first concrete operational friction is being reported: long wait times and frequent ride cancellations as the small fleet stretches across more cities. The picture is consistent with the May 11 edition — this is incremental colour, not a new development — but it lands against an analyst debate that sharpened this week. The Motley Fool, MEXC and Piper Sandler all published on whether the rally to $445 is justified, with the bull framing ("Optimus is free at $445") sitting uneasily next to the reminder that Tesla expects no material robotaxi revenue in 2026 and is gating large-scale unsupervised FSD on the v15 software release, presumed early 2027. No change to the underlying fleet size or timeline since May 11. (Motley Fool, TIKR / Piper Sandler)

So what: This is a "watch, don't act" card. The signal-to-noise on robotaxi is poor right now: the stock is pricing a 2027–2028 autonomy story while the 2026 operational reality is wait-time complaints across a ~26-vehicle fleet. The v15 release remains the single gating event — until it ships, every robotaxi headline is sentiment, not fundamentals. Treat the analyst price-target noise accordingly.

xAI / X May 13–14 2026

xAI races to sign Wall Street firms onto Grok ahead of the SpaceX IPO; legacy models retire May 15

Bloomberg reported on May 13 that xAI is pushing hard to get Wall Street firms — many with existing ties to Musk's empire — to adopt the Grok chatbot internally. Apollo Global Management and Morgan Stanley have begun using Grok alongside competing AI software, and Valor Equity Partners is also using it. The connections are not incidental: Apollo has worked with xAI on financing for Nvidia chips, and Morgan Stanley is Musk's long-standing bank, expected to take a senior role in the SpaceX IPO. The push is explicitly framed as a pre-IPO revenue-bolstering exercise — chatbot subscriptions plus compute access — ahead of the offering expected next month. The honest caveat from the reporting: despite signing up, financiers are "rarely using the chatbot for work." Separately, a housekeeping note: on May 15, several legacy Grok models retire from the xAI API, with requests auto-redirecting to grok-4.3. (Bloomberg, Japan Times, xAI Docs)

So what: This is revenue-narrative engineering with the IPO clock running. Signing brand-name financial firms gives the SpaceX S-1 a list of enterprise Grok customers to point at — but the "rarely using it for work" line is the tell. These are relationship adoptions, not product-pull adoptions, and sophisticated IPO buyers will discount the distinction. The interesting question is whether the S-1 discloses Grok enterprise revenue as a hard number or only as a logo wall.

SpaceX May 12 2026 · schedule slip

Starship Flight 12 slips from May 12 to May 19; FAA approves revised southerly Caribbean trajectory

The headline change since the May 11 edition: Flight 12 has slipped from "tomorrow May 12" to May 19. SpaceX confirmed the new target on May 12: a 90-minute window opening 22:30 UTC on May 19 (5:30 p.m. local at Starbase), lifting off from Orbital Launch Pad 2 with Booster 19 + Ship 39 — the maiden flight of the V3 architecture. The FAA has approved a revised, more southerly trajectory: the flight path now threads a narrower corridor south of the Caribbean landmasses, between Jamaica and Cuba, so that any mishap debris (as seen on Ships 33/34) would fall predominantly into open Caribbean water rather than near populated or trafficked areas. The upper stage will deploy 22 Starlink simulators sized like next-generation satellites; the final two will scan Starship's heat shield and downlink imagery to test return-to-launch-site readiness for future flights. V3 introduces Raptor 3 engines, an integrated interstage, and three grid fins instead of four. (Spaceflight Now, NASASpaceflight, Space.com)

So what: A week's slip is routine for Starship and not a red flag in itself — but the timing now matters more. May 19 sits squarely inside the May 18–22 SEC filing window flagged in the May 11 edition, so the launch and the S-1 milestone are now effectively concurrent rather than the launch leading by six days. That removes the buffer: a clean flight and a filing land together as a positive cluster; a failure on the 19th lands directly on top of the filing window. The revised debris-corridor trajectory is also a quiet signal that SpaceX still treats a V3 mishap as a live possibility.

SpaceX May 11–12 2026 · contracts

SpaceX launches NRO intelligence satellites; deepens Golden Dome missile-defense integration

On May 11, SpaceX launched a batch of intelligence-gathering satellites for the NRO (National Reconnaissance Office) — the NROL-172 mission — with deployment confirmed May 12. The launch was flown under the NSSL (National Security Space Launch) Phase 3 Lane 1 contract managed by the U.S. Space Force's Space Systems Command. Separately, SpaceX's role in Golden Dome — the Trump administration's space-based missile-defense architecture, described by analysts as the most expensive U.S. weapons program in history — continues to deepen: the company holds a $57M Pentagon contract to demonstrate space-based satellite crosslinks (Link-182 standard) and has been formally folded into Golden Dome's software development group. The cadence backdrop is unchanged from the May 11 edition: SpaceX passed its 1,000th Starlink satellite of 2026 in April, and Starlink reached ~10 million subscribers in February. (Spaceflight Now, KeepTrack)

So what: The defense-revenue surface keeps widening — NRO launches plus Golden Dome software-group inclusion is exactly the recurring, government-backed cash flow the SpaceX IPO narrative wants to foreground against the loss-making X/xAI drag. Golden Dome is also a political-risk item, though: a program this large and this tied to the current administration carries appropriations and procurement-challenge exposure that a public SpaceX would have to disclose as a risk factor.

Politics May 12 2026 · ruling detail

NEH ruling detail confirmed: DOGE used ChatGPT and keyword filters to cancel grants; administration signals appeal

Follow-on reporting this week filled in the substance behind Judge Colleen McMahon's 143-page ruling (covered in the May 11 edition) that DOGE's cancellation of ~$100M in NEH (National Endowment for the Humanities) grants was unconstitutional. The newly surfaced detail: central to the decision were depositions from two DOGE employees, Justin Fox and Nathan Cavanaugh, who admitted using ChatGPT and diversity-related keyword filters — terms like "DEI, Equity, Inclusion, LGBTQ" — to decide which grants to cancel, without reviewing the applications or supporting materials. McMahon's ruling found the cuts both unconstitutional and discriminatory. The Trump administration has signaled it intends to appeal. No change to the broader DOGE legal posture since May 11: the EFF (Electronic Frontier Foundation) lawsuit keeping Musk personally on the hook proceeds, and the DOJ-appealed discovery pause shielding Musk from sworn testimony remains in place. Musk's special-government-employee window expires at the end of May; the USDS temporary organization still sunsets July 4. (Black America Web, Cuba Headlines, CNN)

So what: The "ChatGPT plus keyword filter, no human review" detail is the part that travels — it's a concrete, quotable factual finding that plaintiffs in the >200 parallel DOGE cases will lift directly into their own briefs. It reframes the constitutional argument from abstract ("no spending authority") to visceral ("a chatbot cancelled federal grants"). The appeal buys the administration time but does not un-ring the bell on the factual record.

Legal May 9–14 2026 · SEC settlement

Judge won't "rubber-stamp" Musk's $1.5M SEC settlement; cites "red flags," orders answers by June 1

The $1.5M settlement Musk reached with the SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission) on May 4 — resolving allegations he disclosed his 2022 Twitter stake 11 days too late, allegedly costing other shareholders ~$150M — hit a wall this week. U.S. District Judge Sparkle Sooknanan said she cannot "rubber-stamp" the deal, cited "red flags" about how it was reached, and ordered attorneys for both Musk and the SEC to answer questions in writing by June 1. The settlement — under which Musk's revocable trust pays the penalty, with no admission of wrongdoing and no clawback of the money he allegedly saved — still needs the judge's sign-off to take effect. The Musk v. Altman trial also moved this week (Nadella and Altman testified May 11–13); per scope, that is covered in the dedicated trial briefing and not duplicated here. (CNBC, Insurance Journal, Bloomberg)

So what: A judge refusing to fast-track a regulator-defendant settlement is unusual and worth watching. The structural objection is obvious: a no-admission deal where a trust (not Musk personally) pays a sum dwarfed by the alleged $150M gain looks less like enforcement than like a closed chapter purchased cheaply. If Sooknanan rejects the deal outright after the June 1 filings, the SEC matter reopens — a low-probability but non-trivial tail risk that had been considered effectively settled.

    The IPO clock is now visibly shaping behaviour across the portfolio. xAI signing Wall Street firms onto Grok "to bolster revenue ahead of the IPO," SpaceX deepening Golden Dome and NRO contract surface, the revised Starship trajectory de-risking the debris case — these are not coincidental. The whole Muskverse is being arranged for the S-1. Watch whether the filing leans on logo walls and combined-entity framing or discloses hard segment numbers; the May 11 edition's structural-risk point still stands.

    The SEC settlement is the week's quiet tail risk. It was treated as a closed chapter on May 4. A judge citing "red flags" and refusing to rubber-stamp it reopens a small but real probability that the matter is not actually over. June 1 is the date to watch. Combined with the DOGE NEH appeal and the EFF case, the legal-overhang line item is not shrinking — it is just moving between venues.
