The week the AI leadership cracked: a record-high open, then a $1 trillion semiconductor wipeout, a hot jobs report that kills the rate-cut hope for good, and a broken Iran ceasefire that put oil back above $97

This was a genuinely big week — the opposite of the holiday-bridge edition two weeks ago. The S&P 500 opened June at a record on Mon 1 June, then reversed hard: Broadcom's earnings beat but guided AI-chip sales below the bar, and the "sell-the-news" reaction tore through the entire semiconductor complex, erasing roughly $1 trillion of market value and dropping the SOX chip index more than 10% — its steepest fall since March 2020. The Nasdaq fell 4.2% Friday alone, its worst day since April 2025. Then Friday's jobs report came in at 172,000 — more than double the ~80k consensus — confirming a labour market too strong to justify any near-term Fed cut and pushing the year-end hike probability back toward 50%. Over the weekend, the fragile April Iran-Israel ceasefire broke: Iran fired missiles at Israel for the first time since the truce, Israel struck back, and an Iranian official said a Trump deal is "no longer feasible." Brent jumped about $4.40 to $97.49 Monday. The thesis the May briefings flagged — markets had "banked the good outcome" on both Iran and AI earnings — got tested this week, and it failed on both fronts.

Earnings & movers

3 stories
Earnings Semis

Broadcom beat — and still triggered a $1 trillion chip selloff on a soft AI guide

This is the week's defining event. Broadcom reported fiscal Q2 (quarter ended 3 May) after the close on Tue 3 June: record revenue of $22.19bn (vs $22.13bn consensus) and non-GAAP EPS of $2.44 (vs $2.39), with AI semiconductor revenue of $10.8bn up 143% year-over-year — on paper, a clean beat. The problem was the guide: Q3 AI-chip sales guidance of $16bn fell short of the $17.2bn analysts expected, and management pointedly did not raise the full-year 2026 AI forecast. After Nvidia's own blowout could not move its stock two weeks ago, that was enough. Broadcom fell 14% on Wed 4 June, and the read-through cascaded: by Fri 6 June the PHLX Semiconductor Index (SOX) had dropped more than 10% — its steepest fall since March 2020 — wiping over $1 trillion in chip-sector market value. Nvidia fell 6%+ (−$300bn). The signal markets took: the AI capex super-cycle may be decelerating at the margin, and the entire complex was priced for acceleration, not deceleration.

AI · Sentiment

Anthropic's "global AI pause" report landed in the middle of the chip rout — and amplified it

Timing matters. On Wed 4 June Anthropic published a report, "When AI Builds Itself" (authored by co-founder Jack Clark and Marina Favaro), arguing that AI systems are approaching the point where they can improve themselves without human oversight — Anthropic disclosed that more than 80% of code merged into its production codebase in May was written by Claude — and calling for a globally coordinated agreement to pause or slow frontier-model development, explicitly compared to nuclear arms control. Whatever the safety merits, the market read was risk-off for the AI trade: a leading lab publicly arguing the technology is moving too fast, dropped into a week when AI-chip guidance was already disappointing, reinforced the "maybe this has run too hot" narrative. European reporting tied it directly to DAX tech weakness. Treat this as a sentiment amplifier on top of the Broadcom fundamentals, not an independent driver — but the two compounding in the same 48 hours is exactly why the selloff was as violent as it was.

Macro & central banks

2 stories
Jobs

172,000 jobs in May — more than double consensus — and the cut hope is now effectively gone

The Friday 5 June payrolls report was the macro hinge of the week. Nonfarm payrolls rose 172,000 in May (similar to April's 179,000) against a Dow Jones consensus of just ~80,000 — a clean upside surprise. The unemployment rate held at 4.3% as expected, and average hourly earnings rose 0.3% on the month, +3.4% year-over-year. Crucially, the prior two months were revised up by a combined 93,000, so the labour market is running materially stronger than the earlier data suggested. The composition was services-led — leisure and hospitality +70k (mostly food service), local government +55k, health care +35k, manufacturing only +7k, and financial activities actually shed 22k — so it is not a broad-based boom, but it is more than enough to remove any case for a near-term Fed cut. Bond yields rose on the print; the equity reaction was negative because a strong labour market plus sticky inflation equals a higher-for-longer rate path, which is precisely what a richly-valued, rate-sensitive AI complex did not want to hear in the same week as the Broadcom guide.

Fed

June 16–17 still a near-certain hold — but the market is now pricing ~50% odds of a HIKE by year-end

The path has hardened. Futures and prediction markets still assign over 95% probability of no change at the 16–17 June FOMC meeting — Kevin Warsh's first full meeting as chair after taking over 15 May — so June itself is not the event. What shifted this week is the tail: with payrolls running hot and inflation re-accelerating (April CPI 3.8% YoY, the highest since 2023, driven by a ~17.9% surge in energy costs from the oil shock; May CPI on Wed expected ~4.2%), the market is now pricing roughly a 50% chance of a rate hike by year-end, with easing effectively off the table. That is a meaningful regime confirmation: the question is no longer "when does the Fed cut" but "does the Fed have to hike." Warsh has historically leaned dovish, but he chairs a committee whose April minutes showed a majority would hike if inflation stays elevated — and energy-driven inflation is doing exactly that. Wednesday's CPI is the next real test of whether the 50% hike-odds tighten further.

Oil

The U-turn: Brent slid to ~$90 on demand fears, then snapped back above $97 as the Iran ceasefire broke

Oil round-tripped this week in a way that captures the whole macro tension. Through Friday, Brent and WTI were soft — WTI slid about 3% to around $90.30 — as the market focused on weaker global demand signals and the lack of any US-Iran breakthrough; that is the disinflationary scenario the May briefing had hoped sub-$100 might extend. Then the weekend reversed it. Iran fired missiles at Israel — its first bombardment since the fragile April ceasefire — Israel launched airstrikes on central and western Iran in response, the Houthis fired at Israel and renewed Red Sea shipping threats, and an Iranian official said a deal with Trump is "no longer feasible at this stage." On Monday 8 June, Brent jumped about $4.40 to $97.49 and WTI surged ~$3.95 to $94.49. The structural backdrop never actually cleared: Iran still asserts its grip on the Strait of Hormuz and the US blockade of Iranian ports remains, so the supply that would push oil durably lower never arrived. The May "Brent below $100 is a vote, not a settlement" call aged exactly right — the vote got revised the moment the missiles flew.

Editor's view

6 takeaways
  1. Both crutches broke in the same week — that is the story. The May briefings repeatedly warned the tape had "banked the good outcome" on two things: a coming Iran deal and an extending AI-earnings cushion. This week both failed simultaneously. Broadcom's soft AI guide cracked the earnings leg (−$1tn in chips, Nasdaq's worst day in over a year), and the broken Iran ceasefire cracked the geopolitical leg (oil back above $97). When a market is priced for two good outcomes and both reverse at once, the air comes out fast. It did.
  2. Broadcom is the more important of the two, because it is structural, not headline. A geopolitical flare can reverse on a single de-escalation headline. A beat that still triggers a $1tn selloff because the guide disappointed is a valuation signal: the AI-chip complex was priced for acceleration, and even 143%-growth AI revenue wasn't enough when the forward number came in light and the full-year forecast wasn't raised. After Nvidia's blowout also failed to move its stock two weeks ago, the pattern is now twice-confirmed — good is no longer good enough. Watch Oracle Wednesday for whether the infrastructure-demand read stabilises or extends the damage.
  3. The jobs report is what makes this more than a one-week scare. 172k vs 80k expected, plus 93k of upward revisions, removes the soft-landing-into-cuts narrative entirely. The market now prices ~50% odds of a hike by year-end. That means the equity drawdown isn't just a tech-positioning unwind that snaps back — it sits on top of a genuinely hawkish rate path. A richly-valued growth complex into higher-for-longer is a structurally harder backdrop than the one that produced eight straight up weeks.
  4. Wednesday's CPI is now the single most important print on the calendar. Expected ~4.2% headline, ~2.9% core — both re-accelerating. A hot number would push the hike tail above 50% and likely extend the selloff; a cool one is the only clean catalyst for a relief rebound, but the bar for "cool" is low given energy is doing the inflation work. This is a risk-management week, not a dip-buying-on-conviction week.
  5. Don't over-read the Dow's relative resilience as breadth strength — it's defensiveness. The Dow fell only 1.35% Friday vs the Nasdaq's 4.2% because it is under-weight the chips that broke. That is a rotation away from the AI trade into lower-beta names (mirrored in the DAX, where Zalando and Beiersdorf rose while Infineon fell 8.6%), not a sign the bull market is broadening healthily. If CPI runs hot, even the defensives have to contend with the rate path.
  6. Pet-food portfolio read. The diesel-and-freight tailwind from sub-$100 Brent is gone — the May read explicitly flagged it as "a tactical tailwind to bank opportunistically, not a regime change," and the broken ceasefire just proved why. With Brent back above $97 and the Hormuz chokepoint premium re-armed, treat the energy line of the DACH/EU cost base as exposed again, not relieved. The hedge discipline set in May holds without change: keep at least two quarters of forward coverage on diesel-linked freight and named-supplier protein/packaging contracts, and do not rebuild the cost model on any assumption that oil stays low — the structural Hormuz risk is unresolved and now actively deteriorating.
