CO-LED ROUNDAGENT SECURITY20 Jul
a16z and Bessemer co-lead $100M into Neo, a control layer that governs what AI agents are allowed to do
Neo, founded by former SentinelOne, Wiz and Palo Alto Networks executives, emerged from stealth on 20 July with a $100 million round co-led by a16z and Bessemer Venture Partners, with Craft Ventures and Merlin Ventures participating. Chief executive Nicholas Warner (ex-SentinelOne chief operating officer) co-founded the company with Shlomi Salem and Eran Shirazi. Neo builds a "real-time control layer" for agentic software: a software inventory called Neoverse, risk intelligence, real-time attribution linking actions back to the responsible user or agent, and policy enforcement that can block risky activity outright. The a16z partner on the deal, Zane Lackey, framed the need plainly: "Organizations need visibility into what this software can do and the ability to govern its behavior in real time." Round stage (seed, Series A, or combined) and valuation were not disclosed.
chevron_rightMehr lesen
Kontext & Vorgeschichte

All three co-founders came out of the same corner of the security industry that has spent a decade building tools for the previous computing paradigm. Warner ran go-to-market at SentinelOne and served as its chief operating officer through its 2021 initial public offering; Salem led detection engineering there for more than a decade and co-ran its in-house threat-research team; Shirazi co-founded customer-experience platform EasySend and led vulnerability research for Unit 8200, the Israeli intelligence corps' cyber unit — a common pedigree among Israeli cybersecurity founders. Zane Lackey, the a16z partner leading the deal, is himself a security-industry veteran: he co-founded and was chief security officer of Signal Sciences (acquired by Fastly in 2020) before joining a16z's enterprise/security practice. The founding insight, per the company's own framing, is that traditional security tooling was built for software that "behaved in a predictable way" — a static application calling a fixed set of application programming interfaces (APIs). Agentic software breaks that assumption: it acts with legitimate credentials, makes autonomous decisions, and can take actions no human explicitly authorized in the moment.

Die Details

Disclosed: $100 million total, a16z and Bessemer co-leading, Craft Ventures and Merlin Ventures participating, Zane Lackey as the named a16z partner. Not disclosed: valuation, whether the round is structured as seed, Series A, or a blended stage, headcount, or named customers — the launch materials describe the product but no reference-customer list circulated with the announcement. Trade coverage (SiliconANGLE, MSSP Alert, SecurityWeek) states the company was built "in stealth" for an unspecified multi-year period before this raise. The market-sizing argument is anchored to a Gartner projection cited across the coverage: agentic features were present in only about 5% of enterprise applications at the end of 2025, a figure Gartner expects to exceed 40% by the end of 2026 — an eightfold jump in twelve months that is the entire commercial case for a governance layer arriving now rather than later.

Einordnung

Read next to last week's Runta seed, this is the second infrastructure layer a16z has bought into for the agent stack in as many weeks: Runta is the execution layer (agents get a whole, stateful computer to run on); Neo is the governance layer sitting on top of it (what that computer is allowed to do, and who is accountable when it doesn't). The two decks were not announced together and use different partners and different language, but the sequencing reads like a firm-level thesis rather than two unrelated cheques. The more interesting tension is external: Security Boulevard's analysis of the deal invokes the "today's product, tomorrow's feature" pattern from the network-access-control era, and notes that roughly $3.6 billion has already flowed into agentic-security startups industry-wide while incumbent platforms — CrowdStrike, Palo Alto Networks, Microsoft — are absorbing similar capabilities natively rather than buying point solutions. That is the standard bear case for any new security category: most entrants get acquired or folded into a platform, and only one or two command an independent, category-defining valuation.

(1) Whether Neo discloses named enterprise customers or a valuation in follow-up coverage. (2) Whether CrowdStrike, Palo Alto or Microsoft ship a comparable native feature within the next two quarters — the fastest test of the "feature not product" thesis. (3) Whether a16z frames Runta and Neo as an explicit combined thesis in future content, the way it did with the Switch–Netris–Runta compute-and-network sequence. (4) The 40%-by-end-2026 Gartner figure against actual enterprise agentic deployment data as the year closes.

THESISAGENT STACK15–20 Jul
The agent infrastructure stack now has two a16z-backed layers: a place to run, and a leash
Two consecutive weekly cheques into the same problem, from different partners, framed in different language, form a pattern worth naming on its own. Martin Casado's Runta (16 July, last week) argued agents need a full, stateful computer to run on. Zane Lackey's Neo (20 July, this week) argues that once agents have that computer, someone needs to see and control what they do with it. Neither announcement references the other, but together they describe a coherent two-layer stack: execution, then governance.
chevron_rightMehr lesen
Kontext & Vorgeschichte

The pattern echoes how enterprise security tooling has historically trailed each computing shift by twelve to twenty-four months: virtualization got hypervisor security years after virtual machines went mainstream; containers got runtime security after Kubernetes was already in production; serverless got function-level monitoring after the first outages. What is different this time is the gap has compressed to weeks rather than years — a16z funded the execution-layer bet and the governance-layer bet in the same month, effectively pre-empting the usual lag rather than reacting to it after an agent-caused incident forces the issue.

Die Details

The two theses, stated by their respective partners: Casado says the problem is that a decade of cloud abstraction (virtual machines, containers, serverless) stripped away the statefulness and control agents actually need, and that this has produced a CPU shortage alongside the known GPU shortage — an unverified, commercially-interested assertion with no supporting pricing data. Lackey says the problem is that agents act with legitimate credentials and make autonomous decisions no human explicitly signed off on in the moment, and that security teams currently lack inventory, attribution or policy control over that behavior — a claim better supported, since Gartner's 5%-to-40%-in-a-year projection is an independent (if still forecast, not measured) data point. Neither company has disclosed round economics, so this thesis is being inferred from two announcements, not stated by a16z as a combined strategy.

Einordnung

If the inference holds, it says a16z's infrastructure practice is trying to own both ends of the agent-deployment problem before enterprises hit the failure mode that would otherwise force the purchase: an agent that runs somewhere insecure, or an agent that runs securely but does something nobody can trace back to a cause. That is a more defensible position than betting on either layer alone, because the two are complementary rather than competing purchases — a chief information security officer buying Neo does not reduce the case for buying Runta-like execution infrastructure, and vice versa. The risk sits entirely on the demand side: both bets assume enterprise agentic deployment reaches meaningful scale on the timeline Gartner and Casado imply. If deployment stalls at pilot stage through 2026, both categories are overbuilt.

Watch-Items

(1) Whether a16z publishes a combined infrastructure-thesis piece connecting Runta, Neo, Switch and Netris explicitly. (2) Independent verification of the Gartner 40%-by-end-2026 figure as actual deployment data arrives later this year. (3) Whether a third layer — observability, cost management, or agent-to-agent payments — gets funded next, completing a four-part stack. (4) Whether either Runta or Neo discloses a customer logo, which would be the first real demand signal beyond the pitch.

Third straight blank deal-week — but a16z crypto publishes hard data on tokenized stocks going non-crypto-native
No new a16z crypto investment landed between 15 and 22 July — the third consecutive blank week on the deal front. What did land was a data-driven research post, "3 Charts on the Tokenized Stocks Boom," published 20 July by Robert Hackett and Ryan Holloway of a16z crypto. The headline number: crypto-linked products' share of the tokenized-stocks market fell from 79% to 21% over the year to June 2026, while artificial-intelligence and semiconductor ("chip") names surged from 0.3% to 15.5% and megacap tech rose from 0.6% to 10.6% of the market. Total tokenized-stock market capitalization reached $1.7 billion, more than five times the $329 million recorded a year earlier, and monthly transfer volume hit $9.22 billion in June 2026 — a more than 170-fold increase from $53 million a year prior.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte

The report sits inside a16z crypto's recurring "State of Crypto" data series, the same franchise that produced the widely cited 2026 trend forecasts (stablecoins, tokenized real-world assets, perpetual futures, prediction markets) that underpinned Chris Dixon's Fund 5 pitch in May. Tokenized stocks — equity exposure issued and settled on a blockchain rather than through a traditional broker-dealer — were, until recently, dominated by crypto-native names (exchange tokens, mining companies, crypto-adjacent equities) because crypto-native traders were the only audience comfortable holding equity exposure on-chain.

Die Details

What changed, per the three charts: (1) market cap growth from $329 million to $1.7 billion year-over-year, with more than half of current value from assets issued within the period — i.e., growth is coming from new issuance, not just price appreciation of existing tokens. (2) Composition flipped: crypto-linked share fell 58 percentage points (79% to 21%) while AI/chip names, megacap tech, and exchange-traded funds (ETFs)/indices collectively absorbed the difference — AI/chips alone went from near-zero to over 15% of the market. (3) Transfer volume — actual on-chain movement of these tokenized shares, including use as collateral in decentralized-finance protocols — rose more than 170-fold to $9.22 billion monthly. What is not in the report, based on available coverage: any breakdown by issuing platform, or confirmation of which specific a16z crypto portfolio companies (if any) benefit directly from this shift.

Einordnung

This is the clearest public evidence yet for the sequencing argument Dixon used to justify raising Fund 5 into a cooling market: that crypto's financial rails need to mature before non-financial use cases take hold, and that "maturity" looks like non-crypto-native assets (AI stocks, megacap tech) migrating onto crypto rails rather than crypto-native tokens dominating them. It is also a research output, not a deployment — a16z crypto is documenting a trend it may or may not have direct financial exposure to via Fund 5. Three consecutive blank deal-weeks alongside an active research cadence reads as a firm still building the analytical case for where its $2.2 billion goes next, rather than one holding capital idle.

Watch-Items

(1) Whether a16z crypto deploys Fund 5 capital into tokenized-equity infrastructure specifically, following its own data. (2) The next disclosed Fund 5 deployment of any kind — four blank weeks would start to look like hesitation rather than sequencing. (3) Whether traditional finance incumbents (exchanges, brokerages) respond to the AI/chip-name migration onto crypto rails with their own tokenization products.
