Headline29 Jun 2026

Andreessen joins the Pentagon's Defense Policy Board — no disclosure rules in place

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth announced the newly reconstituted Defense Policy Board — the panel (established 1985) that advises Pentagon leadership on strategic planning, force structure and national security — with former US Trade Representative Robert Lighthizer as chair, former Senator Norm Coleman as vice chair, and Marc Andreessen among 13 appointed members. The conflict-of-interest angle is the story: a16z's American Dynamism portfolio — Anduril, Skydio, Shield AI, Flock Safety, Hadrian, Saronic, plus stakes in SpaceX, xAI and OpenAI — holds multiple Department of Defense contracts and benefits from the exact regulatory environment the board shapes. Coverage (Yahoo Finance / DroneXL) notes no disclosure rules are yet in place for the board; DroneXL points out a16z-backed drone makers are winning from the ban on DJI (Da-Jiang Innovations, the dominant Chinese drone maker).

This completes a loop the briefing has tracked all year: raise a $1.18B defense fund → broadcast the deregulatory narrative through New Media → now sit inside the building where the policy gets shaped.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. The Defense Policy Board dates to 1985 and has advised every Pentagon leadership since. Hegseth dissolved the previous panel in April 2025 after an internal review, saying it needed realignment with the administration's strategic priorities — the seats then sat empty for nearly a year before this slate was named. a16z's path to the table was built deliberately: the $1.18B American Dynamism fund closed in January, and TechCrunch has reported the firm claims to have raised 18% of all US venture dollars invested in 2025. Andreessen's 2023 essay "Why AI Will Save the World" — arguing against AI regulation explicitly to beat China — is the intellectual scaffolding for the appointment.

Die Details. The board is 15 members and advises three people: Hegseth, Deputy Secretary Stephen Feinberg (himself the billionaire founder of private-equity firm Cerberus) and Under Secretary for Policy Elbridge Colby. Responsible Statecraft's count: at least 8 of 15 members have close ties to the defense industry or foreign governments. Beyond Andreessen: vice-chair Norm Coleman is a registered lobbyist for Saudi Arabia (central to rehabilitating Riyadh after the Khashoggi killing); Theo Wold is a sitting senior counselor at Palantir; Blake Masters is ex-Thiel Capital and remains on the Thiel Foundation board; Mike Garcia is a former Raytheon executive; Michael Pillsbury joined the defense-AI consultancy American Global Strategies a week before appointment; Christopher Williams served on the Iraq-War-era board while his firm held Boeing and Northrop Grumman contracts. Rounding out: Michael Anton, Rachel Bovard, Tom Feddo, Kenneth Jones, Daniel McCarthy, retired Admiral Chas Richard, Francis Sempa. Four members are Claremont Institute fellows or alumni; two come from the America First Policy Institute.

Einordnung. There is precedent for conflicted boards — a 2003 Center for Public Integrity analysis found 9 of 30 Bush-era members tied to companies with $76B+ in defense contracts in the run-up to Iraq. What is new is the Silicon Valley concentration: Andreessen + Wold + Masters form a venture/Palantir bloc, a clear victory for techno-optimists pushing to slash regulation of AI and autonomous weapons. The internal fault line to watch is that bloc versus the America-First restrainers — board member Daniel McCarthy has written there was "no reason" for US involvement in last year's 12-day Iran war. Which faction wins the board's ear on autonomous-weapons policy is the live question.

Watch-Items. (1) Whether disclosure or recusal rules are imposed — none exist yet. (2) New Department of Defense awards to Anduril, Saronic, Skydio or Shield AI in H2 2026, which will now carry the appearance question. (3) Implementation of the DJI ban — the most direct benefit to a16z's drone portfolio. (4) Whether Senate Armed Services Democrats force scrutiny of the board's composition.

Applied AI3 Jul 2026

Probook: $40M for AI dispatch in home services — a16z and Sequoia co-lead

Probook, founded by a former tradesman, raised $40M total including a $34M Series A co-led by a16z and Sequoia to build the AI operating system for America's home-service businesses, starting with dispatch — deciding which technician goes to which job. Textbook "operational drag" thesis: AI sold as labour replacement to a deeply non-tech buyer (plumbing/HVAC — heating, ventilation and air conditioning — contractors), and notable for pulling both a16z and Sequoia onto the same cap table.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Founder George Eliadis grew up working in the trades in upstate New York — six summers pressure-washing houses with his father — and later worked inside a $40M HVAC, plumbing and electrical business, where he watched dispatch decisions get made ad hoc by whoever answered the phone. The incumbent layer he's attacking: ServiceTitan (public since 2024) owns records, scheduling and billing for the trades, but dispatch — which technician goes to which job — is where a contractor's gross margin is actually decided: first-time-fix rate, drive time, matching a high-value job to the right skill level.

Die Details. The $40M splits into a $34M Series A led by a16z and a previously unannounced $6M seed led by Sequoia, with Sequoia also participating in the A. Company release went out 23 June (New York-based); Dealroom frames the prize as the roughly $700B US trades industry. Not public: valuation, revenue, customer count, or the a16z partner leading the deal — the coverage is deal-blog and company-release sourced, and Probook hasn't published operating numbers. That caps the C-Score at 3/5.

Einordnung. The most informative fact is the cap table: a16z and Sequoia rarely sit together this early. Both anchoring a sub-$50M company reads as consensus among the two biggest franchises that the trades vertical supports an AI-native challenger to ServiceTitan rather than a bolt-on feature — and that dispatch, not billing, is the wedge.

Watch-Items. Expansion from dispatch into pricing and procurement; whether ServiceTitan ships a competing AI dispatcher; first disclosed customer metrics.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Four deals in four days, mapping three sleeves of the thesis: workflow AI sold to non-tech buyers (Prosper), picks-and-shovels for the GPU economy (Netris, Ornn), and a frontier-model bet (Mirendil). The Mirendil backstory is the standout: founders Behnam Neyshabur (5.5 years at Alphabet co-leading Gemini's reasoning research, then roughly one year at Anthropic) and Harsh Mehta left Anthropic in December 2025 and closed $200M within about six months — with no shipped product. Die Details — Mirendil. $200M seed at ~$1B, co-led by a16z and Kleiner Perkins, Nvidia participating. Team of ~20 recruited from Anthropic, xAI, Google DeepMind and OpenAI, plus early xAI member Shayan Salehian. The product thesis: train models specialised in AI R&D tasks — experimental design, hyperparameter search (automated tuning of training settings), evaluation, iterative training — and package them so universities, hospitals and enterprises can run frontier-style research without a machine-learning engineering team. The system controls its own GPUs and loops over research problems with progressively less human input — deliberately close to the self-improving-AI end of the map, which the founders defend openly (Gizmodo: "Don't be afraid of self-improving AI").

Die Details — Netris & Ornn. Netris' NAAM platform (Network Automation, Abstraction and Multi-Tenancy) is live at 35+ GPU clusters totalling ~1 million GPUs, claims 800% annual-recurring-revenue growth last year and ~12% of the neocloud market by cluster count; partner Guido Appenzeller (ex-Big Switch Networks co-founder, ex-CTO of VMware's cloud/networking arm) takes the board seat. Notably, Netris uses no AI in its own product — deterministic algorithms. Ornn's $33M seed was led by a16z crypto (partners Ali Yahya and Elizabeth Harkavy), with Galaxy Ventures, Nordstar and SV Angel; founders Kush Bavaria and Wayne Nelms met at MIT. Core asset: the Ornn Compute Price Index (OCPI) — a GPU spot-price benchmark built from cleared trades (H100/H200/B200/RTX 5090), already live on the Bloomberg Terminal — plus new Token Price Indices benchmarking realized inference costs from Anthropic and OpenAI. In May the Intercontinental Exchange announced cash-settled GPU-compute futures referencing OCPI, pending regulatory approval.

Die Details — Prosper AI. $30M Series A led by a16z; Base10 new, Emergence, Y Combinator and Company Ventures returning. Founded 2023 by Xavier de Gracia and Josep Mingot, narrowed to healthcare-only in 2024. Voice agents handle both patient and insurer calls — scheduling, benefits verification, billing, prior-authorization chasing — writing results back into the provider's existing systems. Claims: revenue up 5x since the prior raise, 40+ healthcare organizations, 150,000+ providers reached, and >40% reduction in administrative cost. Company-sourced numbers, not independently verified.

Einordnung. Ornn is really a financialization bet — making compute tradable and hedgeable like oil; if the ICE futures clear regulation, OCPI becomes load-bearing for every neocloud's financing cost. Netris + Ornn together mean a16z holds both the operations layer and the price layer of the GPU economy its application portfolio runs on. One internal wrinkle: Prosper AI overlaps with a16z's existing health-voice position in Hippocratic AI — portfolio-internal competition the firm evidently tolerates.

Watch-Items. ICE regulatory approval for GPU futures; Mirendil's first public demo or paper; whether OCPI/OTPI become industry-standard benchmarks; Prosper vs. Hippocratic positioning.

Controversyviral 29 Jun 2026

"Doctor ChatGPT is a better doctor than 99% of doctors" — the claim, the backlash, the portfolio interest

Andreessen's line from a Joe Rogan podcast appearance in early June went properly viral in this window after Polymarket's X account reshared the clip on 29 June. The medical pushback was immediate and evidence-based: a February 2026 Nature Medicine paper (Mount Sinai) testing ChatGPT Health on 60 clinical scenarios found it failed to send 51.6% of true emergencies to the emergency room. Andreessen offered no data for the 99% figure — and coverage noted the undisclosed interest: a16z holds health-AI positions in Hippocratic AI, Ambience Healthcare and Abridge. A live case study in the cost of the go-direct strategy: when the founder is the brand, an unsupported claim becomes the firm's credibility problem.

Mehr lesen

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. The exact quote from the Rogan appearance: "99% of the time, the answer that I'm getting from the AI is better than I would get from talking to basically almost any expert." It sat quietly for weeks until Polymarket's X account reshared the clip on 29 June and the New York Post ran the video. It also isn't Andreessen's first collision with how these models actually work: in May 2026 he posted a ~300-word "custom super prompt" on X instructing ChatGPT to "never hallucinate or make anything up" — engineers and academics pointed out within hours that hallucination is an architectural property of large language models, not a behaviour a prompt can switch off.

Die Details. The rebuttal evidence is unusually specific. The February 2026 Nature Medicine study, led by Mount Sinai urologist Dr Ashwin Ramaswamy, ran OpenAI's ChatGPT Health through 60 clinical scenarios: it failed to route 51.6% of true emergencies to the emergency room, including one patient going into respiratory failure. NBC framed it as "under-triaged half of medical emergencies"; NPR ran a broader March piece on unreliable medical advice. The honest scientific nuance: models now match or beat physicians at naming a likely diagnosis from a clean vignette — they remain weak at triage, risk-weighing and treatment choice, which is where actual medicine (and malpractice) lives. Andreessen's 99% figure has no published basis on either axis.

Einordnung. The undisclosed-interest layer is what turns a hot take into a firm problem: a16z holds health-AI positions in Hippocratic AI, Ambience Healthcare and Abridge, plus a stake in OpenAI itself — every one of them a beneficiary if "AI beats your doctor" becomes consumer common sense. And structurally this is the go-direct model (next card) operating exactly as designed: no editorial layer between the claim and millions of viewers, so the peer-reviewed correction arrives late, attached to the firm's credibility rather than a reporter's.

Watch-Items. Whether portfolio health-AI marketing leans on the claim; FDA/FTC (US food-and-drug and trade regulators) attention to consumer medical-AI claims; whether Andreessen doubles down in the next podcast cycle — a walk-back would be off-pattern.

Narrative30 Jun 2026

"Going direct" codified: founder-as-brand playbook, plus New Media Fellowship cohort 2 (65 fellows)

Two moves in the window formalise the media operation. First, Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitz laid out the "go direct" thesis at a16z's New Media Summit and in an essay — founders should speak for themselves on channels they own (the firm's own newsletters now live on Substack), making the founder the brand, not the company; TechTimes ran the analysis 30 June. Second, a16z unveiled the second New Media Fellowship cohort: 65 creators and operators including economist Noah Smith, Chris Painter (president of METR — Model Evaluation and Threat Research, the AI-evals nonprofit), and content leads from Stripe, MrBeast and Amazon. The firm is no longer just distributing its worldview — it's training a bench of aligned communicators.

Mehr lesen

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. The New Media operation is a year old and already has real distribution: X, a large daily newsletter (now on Substack), and a podcast with roughly one million monthly downloads — the firm markets the package as "go-direct as a service." The historical frame Andreessen and Horowitz use: before the 1930s, companies were synonymous with founders (Ford, Edison); mass media's narrow channels forced abstract corporate brands; decentralized media — newsletters, multi-hour podcasts — reverses the constraint, so the founder can again be the primary vehicle.

Die Details. Playbook mechanics from the New Media Summit talk and essay: (1) the founder replaces the company as the brand — not merely accelerates it; (2) "outside-in storytelling" — attach yourself to the biggest macro story rather than your product. Palantir's Alex Karp is held up as the "grand wizard": he rarely discusses Palantir software, talking geopolitics and military AI instead, which makes him the default voice when a crisis breaks. The other canonical example is Flexport's Ryan Petersen, whose October 2021 boat tour of the congested Long Beach port produced the viral thread that got the container-stacking limit doubled; (3) radical authenticity — the ability to sit a three-hour unscripted podcast is treated as a core executive skill; (4) hire internet-native storytellers, not legacy-PR veterans. The Fellowship cohort 2 (65 fellows, eight weeks) includes economist Noah Smith, Chris Painter (president of METR — Model Evaluation and Threat Research), and content leads from Stripe, MrBeast and Amazon.

Einordnung. TechTimes' critique is the right frame and worth keeping: going direct removes the journalistic intermediary, which is exactly what makes it powerful and what strips out the external fact-checking layer — the "Doctor ChatGPT" card in this same window is the live demonstration. It also concentrates key-person risk: dissolving the company brand into one founder means one bad claim, scandal or departure hits everything. And the playbook's premise — that legacy journalism has abandoned objectivity for activism — is a contested political claim a founder adopts wholesale by joining the program, not a neutral fact.

Watch-Items. Output and placement of cohort-2 fellows; whether rival funds clone the fellowship; the first go-direct blowup at a portfolio company that a journalist would have caught.

The cheque-book was routine; the Pentagon seat was not

1. The Defense Policy Board seat is the most consequential a16z event since the $15B raise. American Dynamism was always a three-legged strategy — capital, narrative, policy access — and the third leg just landed. Andreessen now advises the institution his portfolio sells to, with no disclosure rules in place. Whatever one thinks of it, it converts a16z from a lobbying force into an inside voice.

2. The deal thesis is broadening from apps to plumbing. Probook and Prosper AI are the familiar "AI removes operational drag" play. But Netris and Ornn mark a new sleeve: infrastructure for the GPU-cloud economy itself — the neocloud provisioning layer and a compute price benchmark heading for exchange-listed futures. Notably, Ornn came out of the crypto fund: a16z is starting to own the rails — operational and financial — under its own application-layer portfolio.

3. Mirendil was not a quiet one — it was a $200M seed at $1B. Two researchers a year out of Anthropic, no shipped product, co-led by a16z and Kleiner Perkins with Nvidia on the cap table. A frontier-model bet aimed at automating AI R&D itself is a different risk class from workflow automation — deliberately at the recursive-improvement end of the map, and the founders defend that openly. This is the buy-the-team playbook (Thinking Machines, Periodic Labs) at its most aggressive yet.

4. The go-direct strategy showed its failure mode. The "Doctor ChatGPT" flap is what happens when founder-as-brand meets an unsupported claim with an undisclosed portfolio interest: the peer-reviewed rebuttal (51.6% of emergencies missed) attaches to the firm, not just the man. Expect more of these as 65 new media fellows scale the volume.
