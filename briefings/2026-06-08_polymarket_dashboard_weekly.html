Iran de-escalation

Geopolitics · 5 underlying markets
trending_downDeteriorating — the May 31 test passed unsigned; the late-May rally is being given back

The cleanest "the test came back" moment on the board. Last edition flagged the late-May surge — before-2027 toward ~80% on Trump's "largely negotiated framework" claim — as direction real, magnitude provisional, with the May 31 deadline as the validator. The deadline passed with no signed text. A 60-day ceasefire-extension memorandum was reportedly agreed on May 28 (reopening the Strait of Hormuz, allowing Iranian oil sales, and committing Iran not to pursue a weapon), but Trump ended a May 29 White House meeting without a "final determination" and the deal still awaits his signature. The market did exactly what the warning predicted: the near-dated deal markets repriced down. June 30 fell 38% → ~24.5% (75.5% No), and a short-dated "deal by June 7" market collapsed from ~72% to ~6% when Trump declined to sign. The structural read holds — a framework exists, de-escalation is real — but the formal nuclear deal is stalled on one signature.

The internal structure still tells the useful story. The crowd is pricing de-escalation without a signed deal: a ceasefire that is holding and being extended in practice, but no comprehensive nuclear agreement on any near-term calendar. The unresolved core is unchanged — enrichment limits, the disposition of Iran's highly enriched uranium (which the proposed MOU would have the US "unearth and destroy" alongside the IAEA), and sanctions sequencing. Adjacent markets corroborate the de-escalation tone without crisis: an enriched-uranium-surrender market and a US-Iran diplomatic-meeting market both still trade actively, and a permanent-peace-deal market dropped on hawkish signals. The honest summary: the level was froth, the direction is now down, and the genuine fact is a ceasefire that survives even as the paperwork does not. Fed monetary path

Macro · 4 underlying markets
trending_downBreaks hawkish — the locked cluster finally moved, and it moved toward a hike

The cluster that had been "locked" for a month broke this fortnight — and it broke in the hawkish direction, which is the single most important repricing on the board. The two catalysts the last edition named both landed hot. April CPI printed 3.8% year-on-year — a three-year high, driven by a ~17.9% surge in energy costs from the oil shock — and the May jobs report added 172k versus an 85k consensus, with unemployment steady at 4.3%. A hot inflation print plus a resilient labour market is the exact combination that kills the cut case: "0 cuts in 2026" firmed from 66% to ~81%, and the June 17 FOMC no-change sits at ~99%. More striking, the market has moved past "no cuts" to actively price a hike — reporting puts the odds of a hike by October around 60% and near-certain by December. The fed funds target holds at 3.50–3.75% for now, but the directional bet has flipped.

Two things make this more than a one-print wobble. First, the personnel: June 17 is Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Fed chair [tool, single-source — treat as 3/5], and Warsh is read by the market as more hawkish than his predecessor, reinforcing the hike tilt. Second, the mechanism connects directly to the Iran cluster above: the 3.8% CPI was driven by the energy spike from the spring conflict, so a signed Iran deal that decisively lowers oil is now the main channel through which the hike bet could unwind — and that signature did not come. Recession eased to ~19%, so this is still "higher-for-longer / no hard landing," but the tail has shifted from "they might cut" to "they might hike into a supply-driven inflation that a stalled Iran deal failed to relieve." The next print — May CPI, due ~June 10, just before the FOMC — is now the highest-stakes macro datapoint on the calendar. insightsThe meta-meta read

The warning paid: the Iran froth gave itself back at the May 31 test. The real news is the Fed cluster breaking hawkish for the first time in a month. The master variable rotates from geopolitics to inflation — with the two linked through the oil channel.

Last edition's central call was the flagged Iran number — and the flag was right. The May 25 edition put the before-2027 deal market's ~80% as "the least-anchored number on the board," with the direction real but the magnitude provisional, and named the May 31 deadline as the test. The deadline passed with no signed text; Trump ended a White House meeting without a final determination; and the near-dated markets did exactly what a deflating froth does — June 30 fell from 38% to ~24.5%, and a short-dated "deal by June 7" market collapsed 72% → 6%. The lesson reinforced: when a single news-mediated number moves 20+ points on one official's unconfirmed claim, price the direction, never the level. A week's discipline was vindicated. What survives is a real ceasefire that holds in practice even as the formal nuclear paperwork stalls — a frozen conflict, not a resolved one.

The genuine new information is the Fed. For weeks this cluster was the board's most predictable surface — "locked," moving only 1–2pp inside a noise band. This fortnight it broke, and it broke hawkish: April CPI at a three-year-high 3.8%, a May jobs report that nearly doubled the consensus, "0 cuts in 2026" firming 66% → ~81%, and — the real tell — the market now actively pricing a hike rather than a cut, into Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chair. This is the single most consequential repricing on the dashboard, because it is the one cluster that touches every other one: it pressures the mega-cap-growth complex (NVDA December eased), it triggered a sharp Bitcoin sell-off (~14% in days on ETF outflows, knocking BTC from #2 to #3 in the asset race as the S&P took the lead), and it deepens the incumbent-party midterm headwind at the margin. A locked cluster that breaks is worth more than five clusters that drift.

The two macro stories are the same story, connected through oil. The 3.8% CPI that broke the Fed cluster was driven by the energy spike from the spring Iran conflict. The channel that would unwind the hawkish break is a signed Iran deal that puts oil decisively lower — and that signature is exactly what did not come at the May 31 test. So the stalled Iran deal and the breaking Fed are not two independent events; they are cause and consequence. Iran de-escalation that stops short of a signed, oil-lowering agreement leaves the inflation impulse in place, which keeps the Fed leaning toward a hike, which is now the live wire across the entire board. The master variable has rotated from "Iran, directly" to "inflation, of which Iran is the upstream driver." The cleanest single datapoint of the next two weeks is May CPI (~June 10), with Trump's signature on the Iran MOU the upstream wildcard that could change the CPI trajectory with a lag.

The architectural read survived another fortnight and got louder. Anthropic shipped Opus 4.8 and firmed the June model market to 85%; it also filed its IPO first, on June 1, ahead of OpenAI — so it now owns the model layer and leads the race to public markets. NVDA holds infrastructure at 90.5% (June) / 68.5% (December); Alphabet is a clear ~14.5% underdog on both. This thesis has now survived I/O, NVDA earnings, a Google frontier update, and an Opus release without taking a blow — it is no longer a thesis but the board's confirmed base case. The only crack worth watching is the macro one: a genuine Fed hike de-rates the whole mega-cap-growth complex, which could ease NVDA's December number on multiple compression even with its operational lead fully intact. For the first time, the risk to the tech read is not a competitor — it is the discount rate.

The quiet clusters are doing real work by staying quiet. Ukraine printed another flat fortnight (peace-by-June-30 at 3%, modal ceasefire date drifting later to December 31 at ~50%) — its silence next to Iran's volatility is the structural proof that both geopolitical clusters now move only on hard diplomatic information. US politics held its Democratic edge unchanged at D+7.2 with no fundamentals moving. The signal of the fortnight is concentrated, not diffuse: one cluster broke (Fed), one test resolved against the froth (Iran), one thesis got reconfirmed (AI/Tech), and the rest stood still. When most of the board is quiet and one locked cluster suddenly moves, that move is the entire story — and the move was toward a rate hike.
