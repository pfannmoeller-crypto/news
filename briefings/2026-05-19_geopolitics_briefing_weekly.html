Weekly · 12 – 19 May 2026

Both wars escalated kinetically while diplomacy stalled. Russia ran the largest 30-hour barrage of the war; Ukraine hit Moscow's oil refinery; Trump came within hours of striking Iran, then paused at Gulf states' request.

The 14 May refresh's read — "the diplomatic envelope narrowed" — converted into kinetic escalation across both theaters this week. Russia launched the most intense single barrage of the war (1,600+ drones and missiles over 30 hours, 13–14 May), and Ukraine answered with one of its largest deep-strike waves on Moscow on 17 May, killing four and damaging an oil refinery near the capital. On the Iran file, Trump was reportedly hours from authorizing strikes on Tuesday 19 May after a National Security Council meeting on 17 May, then held off at the request of Saudi Arabia, Qatar and the UAE who told him they were close to a deal — but he ordered the Pentagon to remain ready "on a moment's notice." Xi Jinping offered diplomatic help (not military) to Trump at their 14–15 May Beijing summit but ruled out aid to Tehran; the summit failed to produce a breakthrough. Sanctions kept moving: the UK added 85 targets 11 May, and Brussels is drafting the 21st package for end-June/early-July, with shadow-fleet tankers and maritime services back in scope after Hungary's new prime minister Péter Magyar broke the prior veto bloc. The honest read: no clean diplomatic exit on either file is visible at the seven-day horizon — and the energy and shipping tails Stefan was hedging against just got fatter.

Ukraine · Russia war and diplomacy
DEVELOPMENT Ukraine 13–14 May

Russia ran the largest single barrage of the war: 1,600+ drones and missiles over 30 hours

Within 36 hours of the ceasefire's midnight 12 May expiry, Russia launched what United24 and Ukrainian air-force tracking call the most intense strike package of the entire war — more than 1,600 drones and missiles over a 30-hour window from 13 to 14 May, eclipsing the prior record of roughly 1,000 weapons. The targeting profile was mixed civilian and critical infrastructure across Ukraine, with at least 15 killed and more than 100 injured, including children. The shape of the operation matters: it was not a single mass raid but a sustained, multi-axis saturation attack designed to exhaust air defence interceptor stocks and crew rest cycles. Air-defence consumption rates at this tempo are not publicly disclosed, but pattern-recognition from prior waves suggests this is the kind of pressure the Ukrainian system absorbs through priority-target triage — civilian neighborhoods take more damage when energy and command targets get the interceptors. The substantive read: the Kremlin used the post-ceasefire moment to demonstrate that the negotiation gap and the kinetic gap are still independent levers, and that the front-line stagnation (45 sq mi net loss) does not constrain the deep-strike campaign.

DEVELOPMENT Ukraine 17 May

Ukraine struck Moscow with one of its largest deep-strike drone waves; oil refinery damaged, four dead

Ukraine answered the 30-hour barrage with one of its largest deep-strike operations of the war. Per NPR and PBS reporting, Russian defences shot down 81 drones aimed at Moscow overnight 16–17 May — among the largest single-night attacks on the capital region since the full-scale invasion began in February 2022. At least four people were killed, three of them near Moscow, and roughly a dozen wounded. The most consequential hit was near a Moscow oil refinery — refining capacity hits, when they land, compound the Russian fuel-distribution stress the bond-default and GDP-cut data are already describing. The retaliation framing is explicit in coverage: the Kyiv-led strike was timed and scaled to demonstrate that the deep-strike asymmetry has narrowed. This matters more than any single damage number: Ukraine's ability to reach Moscow at this scale changes the political-cost calculation inside the Kremlin's ring road in a way the 2024–2025 strikes did not.

Front line 17–18 May

Tempo unchanged on the line; 242 combat engagements on 17 May, 524 drones + 22 missiles overnight 17–18 May

The kinetic exchange has not slowed below the headline waves. Ukrainian General Staff and EMPR's daily tracker logged 242 combat engagements along the contact line on Sunday 17 May, and a further overnight package of 524 attack drones plus 22 missiles into 18 May. Hits were recorded in Kherson (a public transport stop, one civilian wounded) and in Luhansk, where four medical institutions — including the regional clinical hospital — were damaged during the overnight Russian drone wave. No major territorial change in the 7-day window; the Russia Matters report card from the 14 May refresh (~45 sq mi net Russian loss over 14 Apr – 12 May) remains the load-bearing figure for the trajectory. The honest read at this cadence: the line is grinding, deep-strike is escalating in both directions, and the four-week net-loss trajectory for Moscow has not yet been reversed by the post-ceasefire intensification.

Diplomacy 10–18 May

Putin: "war coming to an end" — but still conditioning any Zelensky meeting on a finalized treaty; Trump says talks "getting closer"

The framing on both sides softened verbally even as the kinetic picture hardened. Putin told reporters this week that the war "may be coming to an end" and that a meeting with Zelensky in a third country is "possible" — but only "after a peace treaty aimed at a long-term historic perspective is finalised." That is the same precondition the 14 May edition flagged: the meeting is positioned as the ratification of a deal Moscow has effectively already won, not the venue to negotiate one. Trump, on the US side, said "talks are continuing" and that the parties are "getting closer and closer every day" — characteristic optimism, no announced round, no announced venue. Adding noise rather than signal: a Russian drone hit a Chinese-linked cargo ship near Odesa as Putin transited toward Beijing for the Xi–Trump summit window, an awkward incident given Russia's growing economic dependence on China but unlikely to alter Beijing's calculus. Net: rhetoric softened, structure unchanged.

Russia · internal — economy, dissent, elites
Russia internal 16 May

Fortune: former senior Kremlin official says elites have stopped using "we" when describing Putin — and "can imagine a future without him"

The week's most striking internal-Russia signal is qualitative, not numerical. A Fortune piece (16 May) drawing on a named former senior Kremlin official describes a behavioral shift inside the elite: fellow government peers in Moscow, regional governors and businessmen have stopped using the first-person plural ("we did") when describing Putin's actions, instead saying what "he" does. The same source says Russians — meaning the elite cohort he travels in — "can imagine a future without him." This is not yet a defection event, and the source has obvious reasons to overstate the trajectory; the read should be that this is the soft-power version of what Russia.Post and the EUISS were describing structurally last week (elite distancing without imminent rebellion). The hard backdrop sharpened to match: the state launched a fresh round of high-profile political arrests and raids in recent weeks, internet blackouts continued in major cities, and the Kremlin moved further against VPN use. The arithmetic on Putin's room to manoeuvre is getting narrower from both directions — elite belief and street control — even as no single break point is visible.

Russia internal 12–18 May

The cross-current: IMF lifted its 2026 Russia growth forecast to 1.1% — driven mostly by Iran-war oil revenues

The honest reading of the data this week requires holding two facts in tension. The Russian Economy Ministry officially cut its 2026 GDP forecast to 0.4% from 1.3% on 12 May (last week's hardest number). The IMF, meanwhile, raised its 2026 Russia growth forecast to 1.1%, citing rising oil-export revenues from the US-Iran war as the main driver. Inflation eased to a four-month low of 5.6% in April from 5.9%, and the Bank of Russia cut its key rate by 50 basis points to 14.50% per annum — small moves, but the first easing signals in a while. None of this contradicts the bond-default and Q1 –0.3% trajectory; it just means the Iran-war oil windfall is partially offsetting the structural drag inside the Russian state's own books. The implication for Stefan's read: a Hormuz reopening or any Iran de-escalation would simultaneously remove the offset that is currently making Russia's fiscal position less catastrophic than its own ministry's forecast implies. The Russian and Iranian crises are coupled on the upside for Moscow's revenue line, not just the geopolitical line.

Iran · nuclear, regional, Hormuz
DEVELOPMENT Iran 17–18 May

Trump came within hours of authorizing strikes on Tuesday 19 May, then held off at Gulf-state request — Pentagon kept on "moment's notice" readiness

The highest-stakes single development of the week. Per CNN's 18 May reporting, after a 17 May National Security Council meeting at the White House, Trump signed off on a target list and had plans to strike Iranian targets on Tuesday 19 May — but stood down "for now" after Saudi Arabia, Qatar, the UAE and other Gulf states asked for a few-day pause, telling him they believe a deal is close. Trump ordered the US military to remain ready "on a moment's notice." The target list reportedly includes Iranian energy and infrastructure sites; Trump indicated any resumption of Project Freedom (the original combat phase that ran 28 February – 5 May) would be "much more severe." Public messaging hardened in parallel — Trump's Truth Social post: "For Iran, the Clock is Ticking, and they better get moving, FAST, or there won't be anything left of them." The week's calculus is therefore unusually binary: either Gulf-state-mediated talks close a one-page memorandum framework in days, or US strikes restart from a list already approved. Iran's foreign minister Abbas Araghchi said "we have no trust to Americans" — the latter scenario is more consistent with the rhetoric than the former with the rhetoric, but Gulf state leverage is real because they would absorb the regional second-order damage.

China Iran 14–15 May

Xi-Trump Beijing summit: Xi offered diplomatic help on Iran, ruled out military aid to Tehran — no breakthrough

Trump's roughly 40-hour Beijing trip on 14–15 May produced a clarifying datapoint, not a deliverable. Per Time and Fox News reporting (and Al Jazeera's post-mortem), Xi Jinping told Trump that he would offer diplomatic help to end the Iran war and keep the Strait of Hormuz open, but explicitly ruled out sending military assistance to Iran or its regional allies. Wang Yi separately met Iranian foreign minister Araghchi in Beijing and called for a "comprehensive ceasefire." The summit did not produce an agreement. The value of the Xi position for Stefan's read is calibrational: it confirms China is willing to be a mediator but not an enabler of Iran's military posture — meaning Tehran cannot count on Beijing to underwrite a prolonged war, and Trump cannot count on Beijing to enforce a deal Iran refuses. China's role is now closer to a guarantor than an arbiter: useful in the closing phase, not the negotiating phase. That matches the Gulf-states-as-pause-mechanism pattern (above) — the actors stopping a US strike are not the actors who can deliver an Iranian yes.

Hormuz 12–18 May

Hormuz remains effectively closed; ~1,550 vessels stranded, ~22,500 mariners trapped; UK deployed drones, fighters, warship

The strait did not reopen in this window. The Joint Chiefs of Staff figure as of 8 May (cited in the Hormuz crisis trackers) is roughly 1,550 vessels stranded with around 22,500 mariners trapped — context for what a sustained closure actually means at sea, beyond the price-of-oil framing. New this week on the military side: the UK announced deployment of drones, fighter aircraft and a Royal Navy warship to participate in an international defensive mission for commercial shipping through the strait. This is the formal British contribution to whatever replaces the paused Project Freedom — Tufts Now's 4 May analysis ("Re-Opening the Strait of Hormuz Won't Restore the Status Quo") is the right framing: even if a ceasefire holds, commercial traffic and insurance rates do not snap back. The IRGC Navy's redefinition of the strait into a broader "operational area" extending from Jask to Siri Island makes that point structurally — the Iranian posture is now territorial, not transactional. With Trump's target list approved (above), the asymmetric tail risk is back at the front of the queue, not the back.

Sanctions and trade
Sanctions 11–18 May

UK sanctioned 85 targets on 11 May; Brussels drafting 21st package for end-June/early-July with shadow fleet and maritime services back in scope

The sanctions track stayed productive even as diplomacy did not. The UK Government announced a new package on 11 May targeting 85 individuals and entities tied to forced deportation and indoctrination of Ukrainian children and Kremlin information-warfare operations — narrower in trade impact than EU oil-cap moves, broader in symbolic and prosecutorial range. More structurally important: the European Commission is preparing the 21st package, with Politico-reported scoping pointing at the shadow-fleet tanker network, Russian banks, financial institutions used for circumvention, defence-industry enterprises, and companies trading stolen Ukrainian grain. The maritime-services ban — previously vetoed by Malta and Greece — is back on the table. The political shift that opened this aperture is Hungary: the new prime minister Péter Magyar's election ended Orbán's veto bloc, and Commission Vice President Kaja Kallas said on 24 April that previously blocked "red lines" can now be revisited. Indicative timing: end of June or early July for adoption. The "abandon the oil price cap in favor of a full ban on trading and maritime services for Russian oil" direction of travel signalled in the 20th package becomes more credible as the 21st takes shape.

💡 Editor's view

What actually moved this week

  1. Kinetic escalation outran diplomacy on both files. The 14 May refresh framed the diplomatic envelope as narrowing; this week the envelope didn't move but the kinetic envelope widened — Russia's 1,600-weapon barrage, Ukraine's largest Moscow strike, Trump's pre-authorized Iran target list. Neither file has produced a deal mechanism; both have produced new ceilings on what each side is willing to do. That is the harder read: the cost of waiting is now visibly higher than it was seven days ago.
  2. The Iran file is now binary on a days-to-weeks horizon. Trump signed off on a target list and was talked back only by Gulf states. The Pentagon is on "moment's notice." Either the Saudi/Qatari/UAE-mediated track delivers a one-page memorandum framework, or US strikes resume from a list already approved — with Trump signalling resumption would be "much more severe" than Project Freedom. The asymmetric tail risk in Brent and shipping insurance is no longer a slow-burn — it is event-driven on a calendar measured in days.
  3. The Ukraine front grinds, but the deep-strike asymmetry has flipped meaningfully. The 14 May refresh noted ~45 sq mi net Russian loss over four weeks. This week added the demonstration that Ukraine can reach a Moscow oil refinery at scale (81 drones in a single night, four killed near the capital). The line-of-contact picture remains net-against-Moscow; the deep-strike picture is now genuinely two-way. Kremlin domestic-political costs of war that were abstract in 2024 are concrete inside the ring road in 2026.
  4. The Russia internal story is the same trajectory plus one important offset. Fortune's elite-language read (people in Moscow no longer saying "we" about Putin) sharpens the qualitative picture; the official 0.4% GDP forecast and Q1 –0.3% print confirm the quantitative direction. The cross-current: IMF lifted 2026 Russia growth to 1.1% on the back of Iran-war oil revenues. That means Iran de-escalation removes the offset and tightens Moscow's fiscal box — the two files are now coupled on Moscow's revenue line, not just the geopolitical line.
  5. Sanctions: the political ground for the 21st package is set, the trade-flow shoe drops in late June/early July. Hungary's new prime minister Péter Magyar broke Orbán's veto bloc, the Commission is drafting, the maritime-services ban is back in scope, and the direction of travel away from the oil price cap toward a full trading-and-services ban becomes more credible. This is the discrete event Stefan should watch for the next leg of supply-chain friction on Russian-flag and shadow-fleet tonnage.
  6. Honest call on cadence: the 14 May refresh said the envelope narrowed; this edition says both sides took the narrowing as license to escalate. No new diplomatic mechanism this week. No named-elite defection beyond Fortune's soft-power read. No Hormuz reopening. The framing of the 11–14 May editions — coupled negotiation tracks with unresolved central gaps, Russia internal degrading faster than the front — still holds, but with two harder pieces of evidence: Trump came within hours of striking Iran, and Ukraine reached Moscow at scale.

Wildcard: the Tuesday-19-May Iran strike that Trump paused is the highest-variance item of this 7-day window. If it converts in the next few days, Brent and shipping insurance re-rate before equities can absorb it, the Russia revenue offset widens, and the EU 21st-package politics shift again. The opposite wildcard — a Gulf-state-mediated one-page memorandum landing inside a week — would compress the Brent war premium fast and simultaneously expose the structural weakness of the Russian state budget. Either outcome moves more than any single Ukraine-file development could in the same window.
