FT: SpaceX weighing a standalone US mobile carrier — build ground network beyond T-Mobile deal

The Financial Times reported (2 July) that SpaceX is in early planning to launch a standalone US mobile telecommunications service — meaning building its own ground-network infrastructure rather than continuing to ride solely on the current T-Mobile partnership. The FT frames it as still an "early planning stage" with no launch timeline, but the strategic direction is clear: instead of being a satellite backhaul provider, SpaceX would compete directly with Verizon, AT&T and T-Mobile for retail subscribers. This dovetails with what Shotwell told IPO-roadshow investors: Starlink Mobile is one of the four Muskverse public-market metrics she wants investors to grade the company on, targeting 25 million active users by end of 2026 — a number that requires more distribution than a wholesale T-Mobile deal can deliver.

Kontext — The current T-Mobile Starlink service (launched July 2025) uses SpaceX's direct-to-cell satellite constellation for text messaging where terrestrial coverage is absent, monetised through T-Mobile's plans. It's a wholesale arrangement — T-Mobile owns the customer, SpaceX gets the backhaul fee. A standalone service would flip the economics: SpaceX would own the retail customer relationship and margin, at the cost of building or leasing spectrum, ground-network sites and a billing/support stack. Starlink's residential base crossed 12 million subscribers as of 4 June, with a $65 ARPU (average revenue per user) and ~$15.5B annual run-rate — those subscribers are a warm distribution asset for a mobile upsell.

Die Details — The FT report is single-sourced and early-stage: no spectrum acquisition, no acquisition target named, no timeline. What we know: Shotwell has floated the idea to investors; Starlink already raised residential prices $10/month in May 2026 (a monetisation signal); T-Mobile's five-year exclusivity terms would gate any direct competition until at least 2030 for text-and-satellite services but may not gate a broader terrestrial+satellite hybrid offering. Any US spectrum position would likely involve either an acquisition (Dish's spectrum has repeatedly been floated) or a partnership with EchoStar-adjacent spectrum holders. The regulatory piece — FCC (Federal Communications Commission) approval for a fourth national carrier — is a multi-year path even under a friendly administration.

Einordnung — Read this as SpaceX using its new public-company balance sheet to open a second front: not just internet-from-space, but domestic-carrier-scale telecom. The strategic logic is clean — Starlink's ground segment already exists globally, spectrum is the missing piece, and a listed company can raise the capital for a spectrum bid. The risk: it puts SpaceX into a business with regulated pricing, thin margins and unionised workforces — an operating profile foreign to the current culture. Also worth flagging: it puts SpaceX in direct competition with AT&T (owner of a competing satellite-broadband partner in AST SpaceMobile) and with Verizon — both of which are major DOD and federal government customers Musk's political adversaries in previous cycles.

Watch-Items — (1) any FCC filing or spectrum-lease news from SpaceX; (2) whether T-Mobile publicly confirms or contests the FT framing; (3) Starlink subscriber momentum vs 25M year-end target; (4) response tone from Verizon/AT&T earnings commentary in late July.
