Briefing summary — first edition

Peru's anchovy crisis keeps squeezing the raw-material base while Nestlé commits CHF 520M to Italian wet food and Germany's premium wet-food landscape consolidates further

This inaugural edition of the TAP Pet Food briefing tracks four things: who is building petfood capacity, who supplies the raw materials behind it, how private equity is trading the category, and which brands are moving. The headline capital-investment story is Nestlé Purina's CHF 520 million (~€562M) new wet-food plant in Mantova, Italy, announced 21 July. On the raw-material side, Peru's extended anchovy fishing ban keeps tightening global fishmeal and fish oil supply, with IFFO data showing April 2026 fishmeal output down 21% year-on-year. In M&A, Capstone Partners counts 18 announced or completed pet-sector deals year-to-date versus 8 a year ago — a genuine rebound. And in brand moves, Belgium's United Petfood took a 50% stake in German premium wet-food producer SmartPetPro, one of several signs that DACH wet-food consolidation is accelerating. Fifteen items across four sections, with one honest gap flagged rather than padded.

Nestlé Purina commits CHF 520 million to a new super-premium wet pet food plant in Mantova, Italy
Nestlé Purina PetCare announced on 21 July a CHF 520 million (roughly €562 million) investment in a new integrated wet pet food plant in Mantova, northern Italy, producing super-premium single-serve wet cat and dog food under Purina and other Nestlé petcare labels. The plant, paired with a logistics platform positioned on European transport corridors, will become Purina's 15th production site in Europe and is expected to start operations in 2029. Nestlé cited wet cat food growing at roughly 8% annually in Europe as the underlying demand driver.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte

Petcare is one of Nestlé's four globally designated focus businesses, accounting for roughly 29% of Nestlé sales in Zone Europe and 21% of Group sales worldwide. Rafael Lopez, CEO of Nestlé Purina PetCare Europe, framed the announcement explicitly as continuity of strategy: "this investment is a clear signal of our continued growth ambition." The Mantova commitment follows a CHF 370 million wet pet food factory Nestlé announced in Brazil earlier in 2026, and it lands the same week Purina is preparing the August grand opening of its $550 million Batavia, Ohio dry-food factory (see below) — both data points for a company still expanding capacity broadly rather than concentrating in one region or format.

Die Details

Disclosed: CHF 520 million total investment; Mantova, Italy location; super-premium wet cat and dog food in single-serve formats; a co-located logistics platform; operations targeted for 2029; the site will be Purina's 15th in Europe. Nestlé's own release sizes the addressable European pet food market at roughly CHF 30 billion, serving an estimated 111 million cats and 91 million dogs across the continent. Not disclosed: exact production capacity in tonnes, headcount, or a construction-start date. Nestlé states the facility will include emissions-reduction measures, water-saving systems, energy recovery technology and optimized sustainable transport flows, without quantifying any of those commitments.

Einordnung

A five-year runway to 2029 is a long lead time even by petfood-plant standards (Vafo's smaller Polish plant, by contrast, targets 2027 — see below), which signals Nestlé is building for a demand curve it expects to still be climbing at the end of the decade rather than reacting to a current capacity pinch. The wet-format emphasis is consistent with the category-wide premiumization trend this briefing tracks under Marken-Bewegungen: wet food commands higher margins and is harder for private label to match on formulation complexity, so a leader doubling down on wet capacity is also a soft signal about where it expects premium share to concentrate.

Watch-Items

(1) Whether Nestlé discloses a construction-start date or capacity figure as the project moves from announcement to permitting. (2) Whether competitors (Mars, General Mills/Blue Buffalo, Colgate-Palmolive/Hill's) respond with their own European wet-food capacity announcements. (3) Whether the 2029 timeline holds, given how far out it sits from current demand data.

LEADFISHMEALmost recent: 12 Jun
Peru's open-ended anchovy fishing ban keeps global fishmeal and fish oil supply tightening
Peru — normally responsible for roughly 20% of global fishmeal and fish oil supply — extended its anchovy fishing suspension in the zone north of 16°S on 12 June with no fixed end date, citing Coastal El Niño warm-water conditions and a high proportion of juvenile fish in the biomass. IFFO (the Marine Ingredients Organisation) reports April 2026 global fishmeal production down 21% year-on-year and fish oil down 19%, with cumulative 2026 fishmeal output down 26% versus 2025. No fresher update on the ban's status was found as of 22 July; this remains the most current confirmed position.
chevron_rightMehr lesen
Kontext & Vorgeschichte

Peru's anchovy fishery runs on two seasonal quotas set by IMARPE (the government marine research institute) based on biomass surveys; the Vice-Ministry of Fisheries and Aquaculture can lift, partially lift or gradually lift a suspension as new survey data comes in. This is not a one-off event — Peru's authorities had already been managing a shortened season through the spring, and the 12 June extension pushed what was originally a suspension through 10 June into an open-ended pause. Chile's cumulative anchovy catches are also running well below prior-year levels, and China's own marine-ingredient production is separately constrained by a fishing ban in effect since 1 May, forcing Chinese processors onto frozen reserves and by-products.

Die Details

IFFO's Enrico Bachis (Market Research Director) is the primary quoted source: "Peru accounts for a large share of global fishmeal and fish oil supply, implying that disruptions quickly tighten availability." IFFO's member data — representing roughly 40% of global fishmeal and 50% of global fish oil production — is the basis for the year-on-year figures above. Norway's farmed-salmon sector, a major fishmeal/fish oil buyer, continues to lean on stable salmon by-product supply as a partial offset, though this does not fully substitute for wild-caught anchovy meal in aquafeed and petfood formulations. No article reviewed gave a specific price index number for fishmeal or fish oil in this window — only production-volume figures.

Einordnung

For petfood specifically, fishmeal and marine oils are inputs mainly into premium wet cat food and functional/joint-health formulations, so sustained Peru weakness is a cost and formulation-security issue concentrated in exactly the segment (premium wet) that Nestlé, Vafo and others are simultaneously building new capacity for. A prolonged, open-ended ban — rather than a dated, scheduled reopening — is the more consequential version of this story: it removes the planning certainty that formulators and buyers rely on for forward contracts. This is a raw-material supply-mapping item, not a price-level story (the brief's separate COGS petfood briefing owns pricing), so no cost pass-through claims are made here.

Watch-Items

(1) Whether IMARPE issues a biomass survey update that reopens the fishery, and when. (2) Whether IFFO's next monthly bulletin shows continued or arrested year-on-year decline. (3) Whether petfood formulators publicly disclose any fishmeal substitution moves (e.g., toward insect or algal-oil inputs) tied to this specific supply event.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte

The report's own historical framing: 23 pet-sector M&A transactions in all of 2025 and 26 in 2024, against a 2020–2022 peak of 48–58 annual transactions — so even this "rebound" sits well below the pandemic-era boom. Separately, Cascadia Capital's "Pet Industry Insights: Summer 2026" report (cited in trade coverage but not independently verified here) describes deals as "heating up," consistent with Capstone's count. Capstone frames the Food segment's 6 deals against three concretely named 2026 transactions: Agrolimen's $600M+ acquisition of fresh-food subscription platform Ollie (Feb 2026), Nasta's acquisition of First Mate Pet Foods (Feb 2026, expanding North American manufacturing), and Vet Custom Label's acquisition of supplement brand Myristol (Dec 2025).

Die Details

Segment split: Vet & Health 9 deals, Food 6, Services 3, Products/Retail-Distribution inactive. Buyer mix: 10 strategic-buyer deals (all private) versus 3 a year ago; PE activity comprised 3 new platform deals and 5 add-ons, versus 5 total add-ons in all of 2025. Capstone also flags a regulatory catalyst specific to the Vet & Health segment — FDA Guidance #256 (April 2023) clarifying compounding-pharmacy rules — as improving financeability there, though that is adjacent to, not part of, the core petfood-manufacturing scope this briefing tracks. On consumer resilience: Capstone cites a survey finding only 27% of consumers plan to spend less on pet food and supplies over the next twelve months.

Einordnung

Two deal counts moving in the same direction (Capstone's 18 vs 8, and the wider trade-press narrative of acquisitions "heating up") is a reasonably corroborated signal that the multi-year pet-sector M&A drought is genuinely ending, not just a press-cycle narrative. The buyer-mix shift toward strategics is the more interesting sub-signal: strategic acquirers moved from 3 to 10 deals while PE platform-building stayed comparatively modest (3 platforms), suggesting operating companies with balance-sheet capacity are moving first, with financial sponsors following via smaller add-ons rather than leading with new platforms. Premium, cat-focused and functional/health-adjacent categories are where buyer interest concentrates across nearly every deal named in this section.

Analysis

Five reads from the beat's first week

  1. Wet food is where the capital is concentrating. Nestlé Purina's CHF 520M Mantova plant, Vafo's Polish pouch line, and both DACH consolidation moves (United Petfood/SmartPetPro, AlphaPet's supplement bolt-on adjacent to its food/treat base) all sit inside premium and wet-format petfood. Dry-format news this cycle was almost entirely rationalization (Mars Minden closing) or routine capacity maintenance (Purina Ohio, J.M. Smucker's Topeka upgrade, neither dated fresh enough to lead this cycle). If capital allocation is the tell, wet and premium is where the category believes the growth is.
  2. DACH wet-food consolidation is happening from two directions at once. AlphaPet (Munich, Patria-backed, buy-and-build across food/treats/supplements) and United Petfood (Belgian private-label giant taking a 50% partnership stake in a German premium producer) are structurally different vehicles — platform roll-up versus minority strategic partnership — converging on the same German premium-wet-food landscape in the same month. Worth tracking whether a third structure (a pure financial-sponsor platform bid for a DACH wet-food asset) shows up next cycle.
  3. The raw-material story is a real constraint, not yet visibly priced into the capacity buildout. Peru's open-ended anchovy ban (IFFO: -21% YoY fishmeal output in April) is a genuine supply-security issue for exactly the premium wet-food and functional segments that Nestlé, Vafo and others are simultaneously expanding into. No source reviewed this cycle connected the two stories explicitly — that gap between raw-material tightness and capacity expansion announcements is itself worth watching rather than assuming it will resolve smoothly.
  4. Insect protein is shifting from pilot to industrial scale. FairGene's new BSF egg facility and Innovafeed's €51M "industrialization phase" raise (with R&D headcount cuts) both point the same direction: insect-protein suppliers are now optimizing for production economics rather than research breakthroughs. Neither source confirmed petfood specifically as the end market for FairGene's output — a distinction worth holding onto rather than assuming insect protein's growth automatically flows to petfood formulations.
  5. M&A is genuinely reopening, but strategics are moving first. Capstone's 18-vs-8 deal count, plus a concrete list of named 2026 transactions (Agrolimen/Ollie, Umios/PWI, Pure Treats/Primal, "I and love and you"/Made by Nacho, Years' growth round), reads as a real cyclical recovery rather than a press-cycle narrative. The buyer-mix shift toward strategic acquirers (10 deals vs 3 a year ago) outpacing new PE platform formation (just 3) suggests operating companies are moving ahead of financial sponsors this cycle — watch whether PE catches up as more portfolio companies hit their five-year exit windows.

First-edition note: this is a genuinely thin beat in any single week — several of this cycle's most substantive items (Capstone's data, AlphaPet/Tierliebhaber, Mars Minden, the private-label growth figures) are weeks to months old and are flagged as such rather than presented as fresh. That honesty trade-off is deliberate and will likely persist most weeks; the alternative is padding, which this briefing does not do.
