Weekly · 1 – 8 June 2026

Two weeks after "95% there," the Iran deal is still unsigned — but the blockade quietly came down and the enrichment number converged. Putin used his St Petersburg forum to rule out meeting Zelensky, then his missiles killed 22 in Dnipro.

The 25 May edition called the Iran file binary with the modal outcome flipped to a deal and resolution "slipped to early June." Early June is here, and there is still no signed text — Bloomberg on 5 June still lists Hormuz, Lebanon and enrichment as live sticking points. But two things did move in the de-escalation direction: the US naval blockade of Iranian ports ended on 29 May after six weeks, and the enrichment-moratorium gap that defined the last three editions (US 20 years, Iran 5) has reportedly converged toward a 12–15-year landing zone, with a tentative 60-day ceasefire extension and a new nuclear-talks track agreed. The honest read is incremental de-escalation without closure — the deferral pattern is intact, and "days away" is now into its third week. On the Russia–Ukraine file the cadence stayed where it was on 25 May: another mass strike on the night of 1–2 June (≈656 drones, 73 missiles, ≥22 dead, with a 3-year-old and an 8-year-old among the Dnipro fatalities), a Ukrainian deep strike on the St Petersburg oil terminal on 3 June as Putin's economic forum opened, and Putin using SPIEF to repeat that he sees "no point" meeting Zelensky. De-escalation-leaning on Iran, grinding escalation on Ukraine — the two headline files are still moving in opposite directions.

Iran · nuclear, Hormuz, strike risk
SHIFT Iran 29 May – 5 June

Still unsigned at "early June" — but the blockade ended and the enrichment number converged: incremental de-escalation without closure

The 25 May edition predicted resolution would "slip to early June." It has — without a signature. As of Bloomberg's 5 June read, the US–Iran peace deal remains unsigned, with Hormuz control, Lebanon, and nuclear enrichment still listed as the live sticking points. That validates the deferral thesis the last several editions tracked: "largely negotiated" and "95% there" have now persisted for roughly two weeks without converting into a text. But this was not a static fortnight. Two concrete de-escalatory moves landed: first, the US naval blockade of Iranian ports — in force from 13 April — ended on 29 May, removing the "dual blockade" that had defined the crisis since spring. Second, the enrichment-moratorium duration, the single number that defined three prior briefings (US demanding 20 years, Iran offering 5), has reportedly converged: three sources now put it at "at least 12 years," with 15 cited as a likely landing spot. A tentative deal to extend the ceasefire by 60 days and open a fresh nuclear-talks track has also been reported. Net: the direction is still de-escalatory and the gap is genuinely narrower than on 25 May — but "days away" is into its third week, and Trump has reverted to the strike threat within a news cycle before.

Nuclear · stockpile 1 – 8 June

The first thing the 60-day window has to solve: 440.9 kg of 60%-enriched uranium — a short technical step from weapons-grade

The reported structure of the tentative extension makes the highly enriched uranium (HEU) stockpile the opening item on the negotiating table, ahead of even the moratorium-duration number. Iran is reported to hold 440.9 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60% purity — a level that is only a short technical step from the roughly 90% needed for a weapon. Iran's written commitments under the draft would be to never pursue nuclear weapons and to negotiate both the suspension of enrichment and the removal of that HEU stockpile; Trump's organising principle remains "relief for performance" — sanctions and asset releases sequenced against verified Iranian steps rather than front-loaded. The negotiation is now mediated by Pakistan (a shift in the reported channel from Qatar's 22 May mediation role logged last edition; both have appeared, and the Islamabad track is the one PBS names this week). The substantive read is unchanged in shape from 25 May: the framework converts a single-number dispute into a sequenced negotiation-about-a-negotiation, and the HEU question is the one with the least margin for ambiguity — verified removal or it is not a non-proliferation deal.

Hormuz 1 – 8 June

Blockade down, strait still empty: ~7 transits a day versus ~100 normal — three months of paralysis, and the war premium has not been released

The most important practical point for the energy read: the US lifting its port blockade on 29 May did not restart the strait. More than three months after the February war effectively halted commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, it remains a "ghost route" — CNN/Kpler counts cited only seven transits on a recent Friday and four more departures over a weekend, against the roughly 100 cargo vessels a day that passed in normal times. Shipowners and the largest carriers are still unwilling to return: Iran retains the ability to interdict (the IRGC has forbidden passage, boarded ships, and laid mines), and insurers will not price a route that depends on a still-unsigned deal and an unexecuted demining schedule. Iran has been managing token, supervised convoys out — it claimed to have coordinated 26 vessels out of the strait in 24 hours on 20 May — but that is escort, not reopening. Until there is a certified deal and a credible demining program actually under way, roughly a quarter of seaborne oil and a fifth of seaborne LNG stays bottled. The blockade coming down removes one of two locks; the other — Iranian interdiction risk and uncleared mines — is the binding one.

Ukraine · Russia war and diplomacy
ESCALATION Ukraine 1 – 2 June

A second mass strike in nine days: ~656 drones, 73 missiles, ≥22 killed — a 3-year-old and an 8-year-old among the Dnipro dead

The escalation cadence the 25 May edition flagged did not break. Overnight 1–2 June, Russia launched at least 656 drones and 73 missiles at Ukraine — a near-repeat in scale of the 23–24 May Kyiv barrage — killing at least 22 civilians and wounding around 138. The geographic centre of gravity shifted to Dnipro, where rescue crews recovered the bodies of a 3-year-old child and of a woman and her 8-year-old son; at least 81 were wounded in Kyiv. Russia again used a hypersonic Oreshnik in the campaign. The driver is now explicit and structural, not just retaliatory: reporting frames Putin as escalating the aerial campaign specifically to exploit Ukraine's shortage of US-made air-defense interceptors, with Zelensky conceding that "the current level of supplies for our air defense does not allow us to shoot down a significant share of the missiles." That is the worrying read — the bottleneck is no longer only Russian intent but Ukrainian magazine depth. Two mass strikes in nine days, with child casualties in a second city, is an intensifying pattern, not a one-off.

SPIEF · diplomacy Ukraine 3 – 6 June

Putin's St Petersburg set-piece: rules out meeting Zelensky, says Donbass control and "a deal" don't contradict — as Ukraine torches the St Petersburg oil terminal

Putin used the St Petersburg International Economic Forum (SPIEF, his annual economic-outreach showcase) as the week's main diplomacy signal — and the signal was negative on process. He ruled out direct talks with Zelensky, saying he sees "no point" in a face-to-face meeting and accusing Kyiv of using the offer to stall Russian battlefield progress and rearm. He framed control of Donbass and "a deal on Ukraine" as not contradictory, and said Moscow is "without doubt" ready for a compromise settlement "based on what was discussed with Trump in Anchorage" — but only if Kyiv agrees, which preserves the meeting-as-ratification structure the last three editions described rather than offering any new opening. He also downplayed his own escalation, claiming Russia had not used the Oreshnik "in the full sense" — a notable walk-back given Western confirmation of its use. The forum itself was upstaged: in the early hours of 3 June, as SPIEF opened, Ukrainian forces struck the St Petersburg oil terminal in Leningrad Oblast — a deliberate deep strike timed to Putin's economic showcase, and a continuation of the two-way deep-strike war the briefing has tracked since May. Net: rhetoric of readiness, zero process movement, and a battlefield message delivered to Putin's home region.

Front line 6 – 7 June

Grinding, not breaking: ISW logs Russian gains near Slovyansk and Pokrovsk — but still no operational breakout west of Pokrovsk since December

The territorial ledger moved at the usual slow pace. ISW's 6 June campaign assessment recorded Russian advances in the Slovyansk and Pokrovsk directions, with frontline geometry updated through 7 June. The crucial context is what has not happened: despite capturing Pokrovsk and Myrnohrad in early 2026 — and despite official Russian claims that those captures would unlock major advances across Donetsk — Russia has been unable to convert the seizure into an operational breakthrough, making no operationally significant progress west of Pokrovsk since December 2025. So the read is consistent with the trend the briefing has carried for weeks: continuous tactical pressure and marginal gains, paid for at heavy cost in men and materiel, without the breakout Moscow keeps advertising. The aerial campaign (above) is where Russia is actually generating leverage right now; the ground line is attritional stalemate edging slowly in Russia's favour.

Russia · internal — economy, dissent, elites
Russia internal 5 June

Putin's SPIEF economic pitch: sanctions "help us develop competencies," de-dollarisation — but the IMF's 1.1% and a Q1 contraction tell the quieter story

The Russia-internal story this week is Putin's own framing of the economy at SPIEF, set against the data underneath it. On stage, Putin argued that Western sanctions have "on the contrary helped" Russia develop its own competencies, and that freezing Russian reserves "irreversibly impacted the standing of international currencies, the dollar and the euro" — the now-standard de-dollarisation / new-economic-order pitch aimed at the Global South audience the forum is built to court. The numbers are less triumphal: the IMF estimates roughly 1.1% Russian growth in 2026, on par with 2025's ~1%, while the economy contracted 0.3% in Q1 — its first quarterly contraction since early 2023. That is above the Economy Ministry's own ~0.4% projection but still firmly in the stagnation band the last several editions described. On elites: the consensus that the system has no removal mechanism held — reporting this week reiterates that the Kremlin has used the war and sanctions to strengthen its grip on the elite by rewarding loyalty and seizing assets from anti-war business figures driven abroad. The read is unchanged from 25 May: degradation and stagnation, managed from a position of consolidated control — not a regime under threat. The Iran coupling still holds: a deal that softens the oil price would shrink the very oil revenue propping up the "less-bad" Russia figure.

Sanctions and trade
Sanctions 1 – 8 June

21st EU package still pending for end-June/early-July; US Treasury adds Iran oil-sales sanctions even as it negotiates the ceasefire

Process continuity on the EU track, with one notable cross-current on the US/Iran side. No new EU package was adopted this window — the 21st remains scheduled for presentation at the end of June or the start of July, with scoping unchanged: an expanded shadow-fleet vessel list (with particular attention on Sovcomflot-successor tankers operating under newly incorporated Hong Kong, UAE and Indian shell-owners), more Russian banks and financial-circumvention entities, defence-industry firms, and companies trading stolen Ukrainian grain. The previously vetoed maritime-services ban on Russian crude — blocked by Malta and Greece — remains the headline candidate, with reporting again crediting the "new impetus" to Hungary's change of government under new prime minister Péter Magyar removing Orbán's veto bloc (see flag). The genuinely new item is on the Iran side: even while negotiating the ceasefire extension, the US Treasury imposed additional sanctions on the Iranian military's oil-sales arm — a reminder that "relief for performance" means pressure stays on until Iran performs, and that the maximum-pressure architecture is not being dismantled pre-emptively. The next discrete Russia trade-flow event remains the 21st package, roughly three to four weeks out.

Other · Europe and hybrid warfare
Hybrid warfare Standing read · 2025 – June 2026

Germany alone now logs 321 suspected incidents; Dutch intelligence warns Russia is preparing for a "long standoff" with the West

No single dated hybrid event sits cleanly inside the 1–8 June window, so this remains a standing-read card — but the numbers have hardened since 25 May. Against the EU/NATO-wide tally of more than 150 suspected Russia-linked incidents since 2025, Germany on its own now reports 321 suspected incidents, including escalating drone intrusions into airspace and disinformation campaigns against critical infrastructure. Drones probing Polish airspace, the Baltic, and airports in Denmark and Germany continue; the shadow fleet is increasingly framed as a dual-use instrument — sanctions evasion and a platform for cable-cutting and drone operations over Baltic and North Sea infrastructure. Dutch intelligence's warning that Russia is stepping up hybrid attacks and "preparing for a long standoff" is the key new calibration: this is being read not as tactical harassment but as a structural posture. The analytical thesis the briefing has carried holds — the objective is political and psychological, designed to convince European leaders that deeper support for Ukraine risks direct escalation. That is precisely why a Ukraine "wind-down" narrative and intensifying hybrid pressure are not in tension: under this reading they are two arms of one strategy, and a notional settlement would not necessarily lower the incident rate.
