The repricing that defined the May 11 edition partially undid itself within 72 hours. Nuclear deal by May 31 fell from 21% back to 10% Yes; June 30 from 34% to 28%; before-2027 from 60% to 56% — still above the coin-flip line, but the cleanest evidence that the long-horizon optimism was headline-driven, not structural. The before-2027 market at 56% is the number to hold onto: the crowd still leans toward a 2026 endgame, but the May/June timeline has been repriced as unrealistic. Fed monetary path

Macro · 4 underlying markets
trending_downApril CPI printed hot at 3.8% — "0 cuts" lock re-tightens to 69%

The single highest-information event the last edition flagged — April CPI (Consumer Price Index) on May 12 — resolved decisively hawkish. April CPI came in at 3.8% year-over-year, up from 3.3% in March, a 0.6% monthly gain, a three-year high, driven largely by energy: US gasoline prices have climbed roughly 50% since the Iran war began. This is the opposite of the sub-3.0% core print the last edition said would "break the 0 cuts narrative." "0 cuts in 2026" jumped from 57% to 69%; "1 cut" eased from 21% to 17%. The implied probability of any 2026 cut fell from 43% back to ~31%. June 17 FOMC no-change firmed to 98%.

The right tail (rate cuts later in 2026) that the last edition said was "starting to attract bids again" has been shut. With the geopolitical de-risking arc also reversing (Iran down, Ukraine truce failed), there is no longer a narrative supporting the easing case — only data, and the data went the wrong way. The next CPI is May (released June 9), and the May jobs report lands June 6; the June 16–17 FOMC is now effectively a foregone no-change. Recession sits at 22%, even slightly softer than the 24% baseline — the crowd reads hot inflation as a "higher-for-longer," not a "hard-landing," story. [Axios] [Polymarket: Fed cuts 2026]

insightsThe meta-meta read

The round-trip refresh: last week's de-risking arc reversed in three days. The master variable is volatility itself — and AI is the only thing immune to it.

Most of last edition's story unwound. Three days ago the read was "geopolitics finally moves: Iran reprices up, a Ukraine truce hits 100%, BTC recovers." All three reversed. Iran nuclear deal by May 31 went 21% → 10%, before-2027 60% → 56%. The Ukraine 3-day truce collapsed and the binary ceasefire markets re-resolved — ceasefire-by-2026 back to 25.5%, peace-deal-before-2027 flat at 34.5% (exactly where the last edition said the "genuine signal" sat). BTC's horse-race share faded from 39% to 31%. The lesson is structural: the May 11 edition explicitly flagged the volatility embedded in each of these moves — Trump's "totally unacceptable" hint on Iran, the "read this carefully" asterisk on the Ukraine truce, the CPI test on the Fed. Every one of those flagged risks materialized.

The master variable this refresh isn't Iran — it's volatility. For two months the read was "Iran is the master variable." It still is on substance, but the operational lesson of this three-day window is that the Iran/Ukraine complex is now a high-frequency, headline-whipsawed market that round-trips on a Trump quote. The before-2027 Iran market at 56% — which held above the coin-flip line even through the reversal — is the number that filters out the noise. If you only watch one geopolitical number, watch before-2027 Iran: it is the de-noised signal. Everything shorter-dated is now Trump-rhetoric volatility.

The hot CPI is the consequential, durable move. April CPI at 3.8% (released May 12, three-year high, energy-driven) is the one event in this window that is not a reversible headline — it's printed data. "0 cuts in 2026" went 57% → 69%, and the right tail the last edition saw "attracting bids again" is shut. The geopolitical reversal and the monetary re-tightening reinforce each other: a Hormuz-driven energy shock keeps inflation hot, a hot CPI keeps the Fed locked, and a locked Fed keeps S&P trapped. The cleanest read across the macro wing: this is now a "higher-for-longer" regime, not a "hard-landing" one — recession sits at 22%, even softer than baseline.

Two clusters genuinely didn't move — and that's the honest read. US politics is stuck because the foreign-policy reversal cancelled out the foreign-policy reprice that drove last week's softening — Blue Wave ~74%, House 78.5%, Senate ~47%, a wash. Tech cap-leadership is stuck because NVDA's December lead (58.5%) is too wide for a three-day macro wobble and the real catalyst (NVDA earnings) is still two weeks out. Reporting these as "unchanged since last edition" rather than manufacturing movement is the correct call for a 3-day refresh.

The architectural read — and the one cluster that ignored everything. AI was the lone clean advancer: Anthropic firmed 80.5% → 85.5% for May while every macro theme reversed. The architectural picture is unchanged — Anthropic owns the model layer (~85.5%), NVDA owns infrastructure (~58.5% December), Alphabet is the dual-layer tail (30.5%). The striking cross-theme signal: the AI cluster is now demonstrably uncorrelated with the geopolitical and monetary reversal that hit the other six clusters in the same 72 hours. It is not just "decoupled from macro" — it is the one place on the board where the crowd is pricing a structural trend instead of a headline.
