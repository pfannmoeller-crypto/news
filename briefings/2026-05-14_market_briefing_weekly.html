Macro & central banks

Inflation

April CPI: 3.8% YoY — hottest since May 2023; energy spike now broadening into shelter

The April Consumer Price Index (released Tue 12 May) rose 0.6% month-on-month and 3.8% year-on-year, above the 3.7% consensus and the highest annual reading in nearly three years. Core CPI (all items less food and energy) came in 0.4% MoM and 2.8% YoY, both a tick hot. Energy did the heavy lifting — energy prices jumped 3.8% and accounted for over 40% of the headline gain, with gasoline up 28.4% annually. The flag is shelter: it rose 0.6% after easing in prior months. That is the "secondary transmission" tell — the Iran-war energy pulse is no longer contained to energy line items, it is leaking into the stickier core. This is the print that meaningfully changed the Fed math since Monday.

Inflation

April PPI surges ~6% YoY — biggest wholesale jump since 2022; pipeline pressure still building

The wholesale Producer Price Index (PPI, released Wed 13 May) jumped about 1.4% for the month against a 0.5% expectation — roughly 6% on an annual basis, the largest since 2022. PPI sits upstream of CPI: it measures what producers pay, so a hot PPI says the inflation already in the April CPI is not yet fully passed through to consumers. Read together, the two prints describe an inflation pulse that is still accelerating, not peaking. Equities split the difference Wed — the Dow slid, but the S&P and Nasdaq rose anyway because tech earnings momentum is currently a bigger force than the macro tape.

Fed

Kevin Warsh confirmed Fed chair — 54–45, closest vote in the modern era; first FOMC 16–17 June

The Senate confirmed Kevin Warsh as the next Federal Reserve chair Wed 13 May in a 54–45 vote — the narrowest Fed-chair confirmation in modern history, almost entirely along party lines (only Senator Fetterman crossed over). Warsh, 56, succeeds Jerome Powell and becomes the 11th modern Fed chair; his first Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meeting is 16–17 June. The setup is the awkward part: Trump has been explicit he expects Warsh to cut rates, and Warsh has historically leaned that way — but he is being handed a 3.8% CPI and a 6% PPI on day one. Bank of America's read: "Warsh will push for lower rates, but the data flow precludes cuts for now." Markets are pricing ~98% hold in June and have actually nudged up the December rate-HIKE probability to near 30%.

Energy supply

IEA: market stays "heavily undersupplied until October" even if the war ends next month

The International Energy Agency's May Oil Market Report sharpened the supply warning since Monday: the global oil market is likely to remain heavily undersupplied through October even if the conflict ends as soon as next month. Observed oil stockpiles fell by roughly 4 million barrels per day in March and April, and crude/fuel flows through the Strait of Hormuz dropped by nearly 6 million barrels per day in Q1 (per the US Energy Information Administration). The takeaway for the rates and equity tape: the energy contribution to inflation is not a one-month event that washes out — it is a multi-quarter deficit, which is what makes the shelter leak in the April CPI so concerning.

Editor's view

4 takeaways
  1. The market is now openly ignoring macro because earnings are louder. A 3.8% CPI and a 6% PPI in the same 48 hours is, on paper, a textbook risk-off setup. Instead the S&P and Nasdaq made fresh records and only the Dow obeyed the macro. That tells you the AI-earnings momentum (Cisco +15%, Nebius +17%, AMD last week) is currently a bigger force on the index than the inflation tape — but it also means the indices are leaning entirely on one factor. If the AI print cycle disappoints into Nvidia's August quarter with this inflation backdrop, there is no macro cushion underneath.
  2. Monday's $101 oil "floor" was sentiment, exactly as flagged — and it has already broken. The 11 May edition called the $101 print "a temporary floor, not a stable equilibrium." Three days later Brent is back near $106 because the Hormuz ceasefire wobbled again. The lesson is structural: as long as the negotiation is unresolved, oil is a sentiment instrument and any relief rally in equities or relief in inflation expectations is borrowed, not earned.
  3. Warsh inherits the worst possible welcome gift. The new Fed chair was confirmed into a 3.8% CPI, a 6% PPI and an energy shock still in the pipeline — and a President who has publicly told him to cut. The credibility test arrives immediately: the 16–17 June FOMC and his first public remarks will be read for whether he defends the data or the politics. The market has already answered with its feet, nudging the December HIKE probability toward 30%. Any dovish signal against this data flow would be the genuinely destabilising surprise.
  4. Pet-food portfolio read. The Brent re-spike from $101 back to ~$106 mechanically re-loads the diesel-and-freight pressure into DACH/EU cost-of-goods that Monday's unwind had briefly relieved. Dutch TTF gas should track in sympathy. The April CPI energy component and the IEA's "undersupplied until October" call together say this is not a one-month blip — see the latest pet-food commodity briefing for the Verfügbarkeit and named-supplier detail on rendered proteins, packaging and wet-line steam costs. Plan input hedges around a multi-quarter elevated-energy base case, not a snap-back.
