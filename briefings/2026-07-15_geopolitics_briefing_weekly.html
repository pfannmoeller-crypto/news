Geopolitics · Week of 8–15 July 2026

The Iran "ceasefire over" of last Tuesday became a running war this week — three US strike rounds, more tanker attacks, a moving naval blockade, and oil back at $85 rescuing the Kremlin's budget just as the EU's sanctions machine jams.

Seven days ago the frame was "ceasefire over, but Trump walked it back." That walk-back did not hold. CENTCOM struck Iran again on the night of 8–9 July (~90 targets), then again on 11 July, then a fresh round on 13 July "to hold Iranian forces accountable"; the IRGC answered with more strikes on shipping (two tankers claimed 13 July) and, per Iranian state media, missiles toward Bahrain and Oman — Jordan intercepted four Iranian missiles over its airspace. The US is now blockading Iranian ports and Trump has cast Washington as the "guardian" of the Strait of Hormuz, floating transit fees. Traffic through the strait has collapsed to roughly half of last week's already-reduced level. Brent crude jumped ~9.6% on Monday 13 July and touched $84.91 on 14 July — the master variable has flipped back to Iran's leverage and, incidentally, is refilling Moscow's coffers. Meanwhile the EU's 21st sanctions package failed to pass on 13 July, the oil-price-cap freeze is stuck against a 15 July deadline, and Ukraine's front stayed static while Kyiv's refinery-strike campaign kept grinding (42.47% of Russian refining capacity assessed offline). Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei is still invisible, four-plus months in, prosecuting a war no one with formal authority has publicly claimed.

Iran / Hormuz — the war goes hot

4 items
Open warfare8–14 Jul

Three US strike rounds in a week (~170+ targets), and Washington moves to blockade Iranian ports — the walk-back is dead

Last Tuesday's "I don't think it's going to start again" lasted barely a day. CENTCOM struck Iran on the night of 8–9 July (~90 targets, per CENTCOM), again on 11 July after a fresh ship attack, and ran a new round on 13 July that Trump ordered "to hold Iranian forces accountable." Reported targets: air-defence systems, coastal radar, missile and drone storage, naval infrastructure and small boats — including 30+ fishing boats destroyed at the Panj-Pelleh pier in Bandar Abbas. The US is now blockading Iranian ports, and Trump has recast Washington as the "guardian" of the Strait of Hormuz, floating transit fees on shipping. Iran reported at least 14 dead from the strikes; some rounds reportedly hit bridges and areas near nuclear facilities. This is no longer a signalling exchange — it is a sustained air-and-naval campaign.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The war's opening phase (late February to mid-June) was defined by a US naval blockade plus strikes; the 17 June MoU replaced blockade with toll-free Hormuz transit and a temporary US waiver on Iranian oil sales. Last week's edition documented the collapse: three tanker hits on 7 July, an 80+ target CENTCOM response, the waiver revoked, and Trump's "ceasefire over" from Ankara — followed by a same-day rhetorical walk-back. The base case we carried in was "intermittent kinetic exchange." That base case has been overtaken to the harder end: a repeated, expanding strike campaign plus the return of the blockade instrument.

Die Details — CENTCOM's cumulative tally is roughly 170+ targets over the first two days (8–9 July), with a second-night round hitting ~90 targets and the campaign expanding geographically into southern and northeast Iran — bridges and areas near nuclear facilities were reportedly among targets, though CENTCOM frames the package as degrading the IRGC's ability to attack shipping, not regime decapitation or a deliberate nuclear strike. The Bandar Abbas / Panj-Pelleh small-boat strike (30+ vessels) directly attacks the IRGC's asymmetric harassment fleet. Then came the naval move: the US is blockading Iranian ports and Trump publicly styled the US as "guardian" of the waterway with a transit-fee concept — a claim CENTCOM reinforced by insisting "Iran does not control" the strait. Vice-President J.D. Vance drew the red line bluntly: "That artery has got to remain open... If they try to close it down, there's going to be response from the American military." Iran's toll: at least 14 dead reported from the strikes (Iranian figures, not independently audited). Flagged / de-rated: Iranian state media claims of retaliatory strikes on US assets in Bahrain, Oman and Kuwait are single-source Iranian and not corroborated by US or Gulf Cooperation Council statements; treat as propaganda until confirmed.

Einordnung — Three structural signals. (1) The tempo — three rounds in six days — implies standing rules of engagement, not case-by-case deliberation; Washington has pre-authorised a response ladder. (2) The blockade's return is the strategically bigger move than the strikes: controlling Hormuz transit converts a war of raids into a war over the chokepoint itself, which is what re-prices oil (see Oil card) and drags in every Gulf shipper. (3) The "guardian / transit fee" framing is Trump converting a security problem into a leverage-and-revenue posture — coercive, transactional, and aimed at forcing Iran back to Doha on US terms rather than toppling the regime. Whether Tehran can be coerced while its Supreme Leader is invisible (see Succession card) is the load-bearing unknown.

Watch-Items — Whether the port blockade is formalised and enforced (boardings, seizures) or stays rhetorical; any US strike that clearly hits a nuclear site (a threshold event); confirmation or debunking of the Bahrain/Oman/Kuwait retaliation claims; whether Gulf states (Qatar, Oman) can restart mediation; the transit-fee concept moving from talk to mechanism.

Hormuz shipping11–14 Jul

IRGC keeps hitting tankers (two claimed 13 July), a container-ship crew member is missing, and Hormuz traffic collapses to ~57 transits over a weekend

Iran did not fold under the strikes — it kept attacking shipping. The IRGC hit vessels around 11 July (triggering the second US round) and claimed two more tankers on 13 July. One crew member went missing after an Iranian attack on a container ship. The physical effect on trade is now severe: transits through the Strait of Hormuz fell to about 57 over Friday–Sunday (a 50%+ drop from the prior week), and to a low of 34 ships on 9 July — versus roughly 130 vessels daily before hostilities began in late February. Iran's framing: it accused the US of causing "the return of insecurity in the Strait of Hormuz," and Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf declared "Hormuz will only open with Iranian arrangements, not American threats."

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The MoU's central bargain was toll-free Hormuz transit for 60 days. Its collapse on 7 July returned the strait to the "cheap, deniable IRGC signal" pattern — but this week the signals became sustained attacks: the IRGC is targeting commercial hulls repeatedly, not just issuing VHF warnings. Roughly one-fifth of globally traded oil and a large share of liquefied-natural-gas moves through the strait, so tonnage disruption transmits straight into the oil price (see Oil card) and into Gulf LNG buyers in Asia.

Die Details — Sequence this week: an IRGC ship attack around 11 July prompted the second US strike round; on 13 July Iran said it struck two tankers in the strait, coinciding with the US resuming strikes the same day. A container-ship crew member is missing after one Iranian attack — the first reported personnel loss of this phase. The traffic data is the hard metric: 34 transits on 9 July (lowest since 28 June), then ~57 across the following weekend, both far below the ~130 daily norm pre-war. Rhetoric hardened in lockstep: Ghalibaf's "Hormuz will only open with Iranian arrangements" is a direct rejection of the US "guardian" claim, and Tehran says the latest US strikes "rendered futile" diplomacy. Flagged: exact ship identities and flags for the 11–13 July attacks are not yet consistently named across outlets — the "two tankers on 13 July" is an Iranian claim echoed by CNN's live coverage; treat vessel specifics as provisional.

Einordnung — Iran is demonstrating that it can impose cost on the chokepoint faster than the US can suppress it — small boats and anti-ship missiles are cheap and dispersed, and each attack re-prices global oil regardless of physical damage. That asymmetry is the whole Iranian theory of the case: it cannot win a strike-for-strike exchange with CENTCOM, but it can make the strait ungovernable and let the oil market do its coercion for it. The collapse in transits is the leverage — every tanker that reroutes or waits is a data point for Tehran. The counter-risk for Iran: sustained closure also strangles its own (already sanctioned) exports and antagonises China, its main customer, which needs the strait open.

Watch-Items — Named-vessel confirmation and any fatalities (the missing crew member); whether war-risk insurance premiums force a broader shipping halt; China's posture, given it is the party most hurt by a closed strait; whether Iran attacks a US-flagged or US-Navy vessel (a clear escalation threshold); Gulf-state tanker convoying or naval escort arrangements.

Diplomacy / nuclear9–13 Jul

Tehran says US strikes "rendered futile" diplomacy — but Qatar mediation and the Vance–Kushner–Witkoff channel are still live, and the nuclear file remains frozen

The diplomatic track did not die outright, but it is on life support. Iran said the renewed US strikes had "rendered futile" efforts to negotiate, even as Trump insisted the US would "continue negotiating" while calling the ceasefire "over" ("The Cease Fire is OVER!"). Working the back channel: Vice-President J.D. Vance, Jared Kushner and envoy Steve Witkoff, with Qatar mediating and Pakistan's Prime Minister urging Tehran to preserve "hard-earned peace." The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) file is unmoved — inspectors have had no access to Iranian sites for four months, and Iran continues to bar the bombed enrichment plants (Fordow, Natanz, Isfahan). The US had already agreed a one-week talks pause during Khamenei's funeral; that pause has effectively bled into the current combat.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The MoU set two hard files — unfreezing roughly $25bn in Iranian assets and returning IAEA inspectors — with uranium enrichment as the untouched red line. Last week's edition documented that the Doha working groups (30 June–2 July) had already stalled: Iran offered inspector access only to the Bushehr power plant and the Tehran research reactor, not the bombed sites, and the frozen-asset mechanics were stuck on a $12bn-claim vs $6bn-Qatar-escrow gap. The strikes did not create the diplomatic failure; they landed on top of one that was already underway.

Die Details — The channel structure this week: Trump posted "The Cease Fire is OVER!" while simultaneously saying the US would keep negotiating — the now-familiar coerce-and-talk posture. On the ground the mediators are Qatar (still, despite having a Qatari LNG carrier hit on 7 July) plus Pakistan (PM Shehbaz Sharif urged Iran to preserve the peace); on the US side Vance, Kushner and Witkoff hold the file. Iran's public line is maximalist and defensive at once: strikes "rendered futile" diplomacy, and "Hormuz will only open with Iranian arrangements." The nuclear file is simply frozen — no IAEA access in ~four months, and Ghalibaf's earlier foreclosure of the bombed sites stands. The US had formally agreed to halt talks for a week during the Khamenei funeral (per Fox reporting, early July); that pause has now merged into open combat, so there is no scheduled round to point to. Contradiction to hold: "diplomacy is dead" (Iranian framing) versus "we'll continue negotiating" (Trump) — both are being asserted simultaneously and the truth is a suspended channel, not a severed one.

Einordnung — The read is that neither side has closed the door, because neither has a better option. The US wants the MoU restored on tougher terms (blockade leverage, waiver gone); Iran wants sanctions relief and cannot get it while shooting. But two things now block any quick restart that were not blocking before: (1) Qatar, the indispensable mediator, has been directly attacked, which corrodes its willingness and standing; and (2) there is no visible Iranian principal who can sign (see Succession card). A frozen nuclear file plus a suspended channel plus an invisible ratifier is a recipe for the war continuing by default, not by decision.

Watch-Items — Whether Qatar or Oman convenes any shuttle round by end-July; any IAEA statement on inspector access or a formal non-compliance finding; whether Iran offers a face-saving Bushehr/research-reactor visit; any sign the Vance–Kushner–Witkoff channel produces a text; the 21 August waiver-expiry date as the next automatic pressure point.

Successionongoing

Iran is fighting a two-week war under a Supreme Leader still no one has seen — Mojtaba Khamenei's invisibility is now the central governance fact

The single most striking feature of this war remains a negative: Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei (56) has still not appeared publicly since his 9 March wartime installation — now more than four months, and through his father's 9 July burial. Iran has since prosecuted an expanding conflict with the United States without its constitutional commander-in-chief visibly authorising a single decision. The strikes on shipping, the rejection of US "guardian" claims, the "diplomacy rendered futile" line — all are voiced by the IRGC, Speaker Ghalibaf, President Pezeshkian and the Supreme National Security Council, never by the man who, on paper, holds ultimate authority. Officially his absence is a security measure against Israeli assassination; last week's edition carried de-rated intelligence claims of severe injuries. Either way, the "regency-by-committee" hypothesis is now the operating reality.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The 28 February strike that opened the war killed Ali Khamenei after 36 years of rule, along with much of his household; the Assembly of Experts installed his son Mojtaba on 9 March in an unprecedented wartime succession. The public funeral was deferred four months and staged 4–9 July as a continuity spectacle — at which Mojtaba was, again, absent while three of his brothers attended. This week there is no new sighting to report, which is itself the story: the negative has now persisted through a funeral and into a shooting war.

Die Details — Every authoritative-sounding Iranian statement this week has a name attached that is not the Supreme Leader: Ghalibaf on Hormuz; the Foreign Ministry / state media on "insecurity in the strait" and "futile" diplomacy; the IRGC on the tanker attacks. President Masoud Pezeshkian remains the civilian face; the Supreme National Security Council is the presumed decision node; the IRGC is the executing hand. Nowhere in the record is a decree, audio message, or still image from Mojtaba blessing or restraining the war. The official reason — Israeli assassination risk — is plausible and the war makes it more so. De-rated (carried from last week): Reuters and, in March, US Defense Secretary Hegseth described severe facial and leg injuries, possibly an amputated leg; this is intelligence-grade single-source reporting and remains unverified. What is not speculative is the observable absence.

Einordnung — The governance inference sharpens each week the war runs without him. If the Hormuz escalation and the responses to three US strike rounds were authorised without Mojtaba's public sign-off, then effective wartime power sits with the Supreme National Security Council and the IRGC, in that order — a military-security regency operating under a nominal leader's name. That has two consequences: (1) diplomacy has no obvious counter-signature — the US can strike a deal with Pezeshkian or the Council, but the legitimacy question ("who ratifies for the system?") stays open; and (2) escalation control is diffused across institutions with their own incentives, which raises the risk of an action no single authority intended. An invisible leader is not merely a curiosity; it is a structural source of unpredictability on the receiving end of a US campaign.

Watch-Items — Any appearance in any medium (audio, decree, still image) — the first would be a major event; who is publicly named as chairing war decisions; any Assembly of Experts convening; signs of open IRGC–civilian friction; whether a decision emerges that visibly lacks central authorship (a tell of the vacuum).

EU · 21st package10–15 Jul

The 21st package fails to pass on 13 July — with the oil-price-cap freeze stuck against a 15 July deadline that would let the cap jump from $44.10 to $58

The EU's 21st Russia sanctions package did not get through on 13 July — ambassadors failed to endorse it, pushing another attempt to 15/16 July. The clock matters: the Russian oil-price cap is on a semi-annual dynamic mechanism (15% below the average Urals market price), and without a freeze by 15 July it would automatically revise from $44.10 up to about $58/barrel — a windfall for Moscow precisely as the Iran-driven oil spike lifts Russian revenues anyway. The Commission proposed freezing the review to January 2027; that itself drew objections. The blockers are now a coalition: Bulgaria (Patriarch Kirill listing plus oligarch Vagit Alekperov, tied to a €3bn Lukoil claim against Sofia), with Portugal/Germany (a Russian fish-import ban), France/Italy (a Russian-soldier entry ban) and Malta/Cyprus/Greece (the mechanism postponement) each objecting to different pieces. The EU did clear 250 new rolling listings on banks, crypto operators and tankers.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The 21st package was announced 9 June with a liquefied-natural-gas restriction triad, shadow-fleet vessel blacklisting, and a bunkering-services criterion. Last week's edition had it heading for a 13 July Foreign Affairs Council with Bulgaria + Italy blocking on Patriarch Kirill, and floated decoupling the cap-freeze into a standalone 14 July measure. That decoupling has not cleanly happened — the whole thing jammed on 13 July and slid to mid-week. The cap's current $44.10 level (matching Platts' late-June Urals assessment) is the number that resets 15 July absent action.

Die Details — The core mechanic: the price cap resets every six months to 15% below the average Urals price, so with global oil now spiking on Hormuz risk (see Oil card), the formula would drag the cap up to ~$58 — exactly the wrong direction, gifting Russia headroom to sell legally at higher prices. Brussels' fix is to freeze the review (Commission proposal: defer to January 2027), but unanimity is required and the objections have multiplied beyond the original Kirill fight. Bulgaria is the hard block: PM Rumen Radev has drawn "a red line" on sanctioning Orthodox Church head Patriarch Kirill (religious grounds) and Lukoil-linked oligarch Vagit Alekperov (Lukoil is pursuing a ~€3bn compensation claim against Bulgaria). Around it: Portugal and Germany resist a ban on Russian cod and pollack; France and Italy oppose an ambitious ban on Russian soldiers entering the EU; Malta, Cyprus and Greece (shipping-heavy states) question the automatic-mechanism postponement. EU foreign-policy chief Kaja Kallas after the failed 13 July meeting: "We really need to take this short-term pain for long-term gain." One concrete thing did pass: 250 new rolling listings on banks, crypto operators and tankers facilitating evasion. Diplomats still float splitting the package to push the cap through separately. Contradiction flagged: reporting differs on whether the fallback cap is "$58" (Euronews) or merely "well above $44.10" (worldoil) — the direction is agreed, the exact reset number is not.

Einordnung — This is a small, technical, and genuinely consequential fight, and it is being lost on timing. The substantive measure has always been the cap freeze, not the peripheral listings — and the freeze is now hostage to unrelated national vetoes (a church figure, a fish ban, a soldier-entry ban) at the exact moment the oil market is handing Moscow a revenue reprieve. If 15 July passes without a freeze, the cap floats up toward the market and the EU's flagship Russia-revenue instrument loosens automatically — a self-inflicted own goal driven by the Iran war's effect on the reference price. The 250 listings are real but second-order. Watch whether Brussels can still salvage a standalone freeze after the deadline, or whether the whole package becomes an autumn story.

Watch-Items — The 15/16 July revote and whether the cap-freeze is decoupled and passed alone; whether Bulgaria is bought off (Kirill/Alekperov dropped); the actual reset number if the freeze fails; US secondary-sanctions coordination signals from the Trump administration; whether the Iran oil spike is explicitly cited to justify emergency action.

Oil · master variable13–14 Jul

Brent +9.6% Monday to ~$85 Tuesday — the Hormuz risk premium is fully back, and it is quietly bailing out Russia's budget

Oil made its biggest move of the summer. Brent crude jumped ~9.6% on Monday 13 July and rose another 2% to $84.91 on 14 July — its highest since mid-June — with US benchmark West Texas Intermediate (WTI) around $74.85 after the weekend strikes. Drivers: repeated tanker attacks, three US strike rounds, and Trump's move to blockade Iranian ports. With Hormuz transits halved and the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve buffer thinning, analysts warned of a "violent repricing up" (June Goh, Sparta Commodities) and even that "a move to $100 is quite possible" if physical shortage becomes real (Bart Melek, TD Securities). The second-order effect matters for the whole board: every dollar back on Brent partly reverses Russia's −45% year-on-year oil-and-gas revenue collapse — the Iran war is unintentional Kremlin stimulus, and it is happening in the same week the EU's cap freeze is stuck (see Sanctions card).

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The whiplash is extreme: three weeks ago Brent was above $126 on the war's opening blockade; two weeks ago it had crashed below $72 with Morgan Stanley cutting forecasts on glut fears; last week's edition had it at $78.02 (+5.2%) after the 7 July tanker hits. This week it is back to $85. The 2026 oil market is being driven almost entirely by the Hormuz risk premium — Russia sanctions do not move the price this much, and OPEC+ has been passive.

Die Details — Prices: Brent +9.6% Monday 13 July, then +2% to $84.91 Tuesday 14 July (September futures, highest since 15 June); WTI ~$74.85 (+4.8%) on the 13 July settle. Physical driver: Hormuz transits fell to ~57 over Friday–Sunday, a 50%+ weekly drop, versus ~130 daily pre-war — real tonnage is being withheld, not just risk being priced. Trump's blockade-and-"guardian"-with-transit-fees framing adds a policy premium on top of the security one. Analyst voices: June Goh (Sparta Commodities) — "crude oil is fast losing its strategic petroleum reserve buffer, and a violent repricing up cannot be discounted until the market sees toned-down rhetoric from both parties"; Bart Melek (TD Securities) — "a move to $100 is quite possible, should it become apparent that physical shortage risks are real and increasingly likely." No OPEC+ compensation statement has landed. Cross-reference: this is the market briefing's oil section from the geopolitical side — Stefan's market note should be read together with this card, since the same Hormuz premium drives both.

Einordnung — The master variable has flipped sides again. Two weeks ago cheap oil was "Russia's problem"; now $85 Brent is "Russia's reprieve." The arithmetic: Urals trades at a discount to Brent (recently ~$44 vs a Brent in the $70s), so a higher Brent lifts Urals correspondingly and partly offsets the refinery-strike revenue damage Ukraine has inflicted (see Refinery card). That is the cruel connection running through this whole edition — Ukraine spent the spring degrading Russian refining to cut Moscow's income, and a war in the Gulf that Kyiv had no part in just handed some of that income back. For Iran the price signal is less bullish than it looks: with the US waiver gone, Tehran sells to China at heavy discounts or via the shadow fleet, so a high headline price does not translate cleanly into Iranian cash. At $85, Moscow gains most; at $100, the calculus for everyone changes.

Watch-Items — Whether Brent breaks $90–100 (crisis mode) or fades on any de-escalation; the Urals–Brent spread as the Russia-revenue proxy; OPEC+ compensation posture; any SPR release; whether the price spike forces emergency EU action on the cap freeze (see Sanctions card).

Ukraine / Russia — war & diplomacy

3 items
Front3–4 Jul

No confirmed advances — Putin's staged "133 settlements" claim collapses under ISW's count, and Kostyantynivka is an AI-video propaganda set-piece, not a captured city

The ground war stayed static and the information war got louder. In a staged 3 July meeting with commanders, Putin claimed Russian forces had seized 133 settlements and over 3,000 km² since January (29 settlements / 636 km² in June alone). The Institute for the Study of War (ISW), using geolocated open-source evidence, counts only 64 settlements and 621.7 km² since January — and just 20 settlements / 30.42 km² in June. Russia heavily publicised a claimed 3–4 July seizure of Kostyantynivka, pushing 10+ videos of soldiers with flags that ISW flags as possibly AI-altered; Ukrainian sources put only 100–250 Russian troops in the city — far too few to control it. Around Slovyansk, Russia ran 20–30 assaults daily with no confirmed gains; fuel shortages hampered its logistics near Kupyansk; Ukraine counterattacked near Kopanky and Novoserhiivka.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — Last week's edition documented the summer offensive collapsing at roughly 1,298 casualties per km² captured in June, with Kostyantynivka contested for months as the Donetsk-axis pinch point (its capture would open the approaches to Kramatorsk and Slaviansk). This week's ISW assessment (4 July) extends that picture: the gap between Russian claims and geolocated reality has widened into an explicit propaganda operation, not just optimistic reporting.

Die Details — The claim-vs-reality ledger is stark: Putin's 133 settlements / 3,000+ km² since January versus ISW's 64 settlements / 621.7 km²; June's 29 / 636 versus 20 / 30.42. Distance claims were similarly inflated — commanders put forces 10 km from Sumy City (ISW: 13–17 km) and 8 km from Slovyansk (ISW: 13–19 km). On Kostyantynivka: the Russian Ministry of Defence published at least 10 videos of soldiers holding flags on 3 July; ISW could not confirm whether the footage was AI-altered but noted the volume and rapid-publication pattern match prior disinformation campaigns, and that only 100–250 Russian troops were assessed in the city as of mid-June — insufficient for control. Operationally: Slovyansk direction saw 20–30 assaults per day, no advances; Pokrovsk limited ops, no gains; Kharkiv/Sumy no confirmed advances with fuel shortages near Kupyansk (a direct downstream effect of the refinery campaign — see next card); Ukrainian counterattacks near Kopanky, Novoserhiivka and Hulyaipole. ISW's casualty math: at June's ~1,298 casualties/km² rate, taking the rest of Donetsk would cost Russia ~6.5 million servicemembers — an absurd, unsustainable projection meant to illustrate the exchange ratio.

Einordnung — Two reinforcing signals. First, the shift to AI-video "captures" is a tell of weakness, not strength: when you have to manufacture footage of a flag in a city you don't hold, the ground effort has stalled and the domestic-information front has become the substitute objective. Second, "fuel shortages near Kupyansk" is the war's two campaigns visibly intersecting — Ukraine's strikes on Russian refineries are now showing up as Russian logistical friction at the front, not just as revenue loss. The standing read holds: Russia cannot convert casualties into decisive territory, so it substitutes air pressure on cities and information operations for battlefield movement. The oil-price reprieve (see Oil card) helps Moscow's budget but does not fix its manpower-exchange problem.

Watch-Items — Whether the Kostyantynivka claim is upgraded to an official state "liberation" announcement or quietly dropped; ISW's next territorial map; any Russian mobilisation signal to offset the June cost curve; whether refinery-driven fuel shortages spread to other front sectors; Ukrainian counterattack momentum near Kupyansk.
