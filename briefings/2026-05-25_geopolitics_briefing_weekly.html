Weekly · 18 – 25 May 2026

The Iran binary flipped sign. The Memorial-Day strike never came — by 24 May Trump called the deal "largely negotiated," then deliberately slowed the clock. Russia answered Ukraine's deep-strike campaign with the largest attack on Kyiv of the war.

Last week's edition said the Iran file was binary on a horizon of days, with Trump back at the White House over Memorial Day weekend "seriously weighing new strikes." The strike did not come. Instead the default outcome inverted: by 23–24 May Trump described a deal as "largely negotiated," officials briefed a framework "95% there," and Trump then chose to ease the tempo — "I'm not going to rush into a deal," instinct to "give them 5, 6, 7 days," "time is on our side." The file is still binary, but the modal outcome is now a deal, with resolution pushed into early June. On Ukraine the week moved the opposite way: overnight 23–24 May Russia hit Kyiv with roughly 600 drones and 90 missiles — one of the heaviest bombardments of the war, including the nuclear-capable hypersonic Oreshnik — explicitly ordered by Putin as retaliation for a Ukrainian strike on occupied Starobilsk. The honest read on cadence: a de-escalation-leaning week on Iran, a sharp escalation week on Ukraine, and a genuinely quiet week on the territorial ledger and on sanctions. The two headline files moved in opposite directions at once.

  • Trump paused the planned 19 May Iran strike; by 22 May he was back with his national security team, "seriously weighing new strikes," and had cancelled Memorial Day plans (and reportedly his son's wedding) to stay at the White House.
  • The Iran deal gap had narrowed to one number — the uranium-enrichment-moratorium duration: US demanding 20 years, Iran offering 5, reporting putting the contested floor near 12.
  • Qatar formally assumed a mediator role on 22 May; the Strait of Hormuz dual blockade had persisted since early March, transits near zero since 6 May.

  • Russia Matters / ISW data: Russia lost a net 29 square miles in the 12–19 May week, double the prior week's loss; trailing-year net still +1,585 square miles for Russia.
  • Russia's 12 May cut of its 2026 GDP forecast to 0.4% was being read by CEPA and IISS as an official admission of multi-year structural stagnation.
  • EU 21st sanctions package in drafting for end-June/early-July; the 20th package's crypto sectoral ban set to take effect 24 May.
Iran · nuclear, Hormuz, strike risk
SHIFT Iran 23–25 May

The binary flipped: Trump pivoted from "weighing strikes" to "deal largely negotiated" — then deliberately slowed the clock

The single most consequential move of the window, and a genuine change of sign from the 23 May edition. That edition said the modal outcome was a Memorial-Day-weekend strike. It did not happen. Instead, by 23 May Trump publicly described a deal — including reopening the Strait of Hormuz — as "largely negotiated"; CBS reported negotiators had "agreed to broad principles"; and a framework was briefed as "95% there" on Sunday 24 May. Trump then did something notable: rather than press for an immediate signing, he eased the tempo, saying "I'm not going to rush into a deal," that his instinct is to "give them 5, 6, 7 days" to land it, and that "time is on our side." Read that as leverage, not retreat — a maximalist negotiator signalling he can wait while Iran cannot. The honest framing: the deferral pattern the last three editions described is intact, but the default it defers to has inverted. A week ago the briefing's working assumption was a strike unless a deal intervened; this week it is a deal unless the "language" disputes break it. Resolution has slipped to early June.

Nuclear · framework 24 May

What is actually in the draft: a 60-day ceasefire extension, Hormuz reopening, and "relief for performance"

The framework now has reported shape. Per Axios, CNN and the Washington Post, the draft would extend the existing ceasefire (first announced 7 April) by 60 days — extendable by mutual consent — and, within that window: demine and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, allow Iran to sell oil freely, and open a 30-day negotiating window specifically on a uranium-enrichment moratorium. Iran's written commitments would be to never pursue nuclear weapons and to negotiate the suspension of enrichment plus removal of its highly enriched uranium (HEU) stockpile. Trump's stated organising principle is "relief for performance" — sanctions relief and asset releases sequenced against verified Iranian steps, not front-loaded. The one number that defined last week's briefing is still unresolved: the moratorium duration (US 20 years, Iran 5), and Washington additionally wants a clause that automatically extends the moratorium if Tehran breaches enrichment terms. The substantive read: the framework is real and detailed, but it converts last week's single-number gap into a 30-day negotiation-about-a-negotiation — progress, but not closure.

Hormuz 23–25 May

The hardest knot is sequencing: who controls the strait, and when the blockade actually lifts — both sides have published contradictory texts

The strait, not the centrifuges, is the gap most likely to break this deal. Trump's position is explicit: "The Blockade will remain in full force and effect until an agreement is reached, certified, and signed," and frozen Iranian assets are unfrozen only once Hormuz has reopened. Iran's published position differs on both order and control: Iranian state media — some of it close to the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) — say the strait reopens only if the US lifts its naval blockade within the first 30 days of the deal, and that the strait would remain under Iranian "supervision." Iran's foreign ministry called Trump's Hormuz remarks "inconsistent with reality." Reporting describes negotiators still "haggling over language" on both the strait and Iran's nuclear stockpile. This is a classic deal-on-paper / dispute-in-practice gap: two sides agreeing to reopen the strait while publishing mutually exclusive accounts of how and when. The strait did not reopen this window — open transits have been near zero since 6 May, with roughly 22,500 mariners still on 1,550-plus vessels in and around the Gulf on the most recent counts.

Ukraine · Russia war and diplomacy
ESCALATION Ukraine 23–24 May

Russia's largest strike on Kyiv of the war: ~600 drones, ~90 missiles, the nuclear-capable Oreshnik — ordered by Putin as retaliation

The escalation event of the window. Overnight 23–24 May, Russia hit Kyiv and its surrounding region with roughly 600 strike drones and 90 air-, sea- and ground-launched missiles — one of the heaviest bombardments of the four-year war. The barrage included a hypersonic Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missile (nuclear-capable) fired at Bila Tserkva, south of Kyiv — only the third use of that weapon in the war. At least four people were killed and more than 60–80 wounded; damage was recorded at around 40 locations including residential buildings and schools, with minor damage to Ukraine's cabinet building and Foreign Ministry. Crucially, this was explicitly ordered: Putin directed retaliation after blaming Ukraine for a 22 May drone strike on a college in the Russian-occupied town of Starobilsk, which Russia says killed 21 and injured 42. Ukraine denies hitting a college, saying it struck an elite drone command unit in the area. (Note: last week's edition logged the Starobilsk hit at "six killed, 39 injured" — Russia's figure has since been revised up.) Zelensky called for "consequences." The two-way deep-strike escalation the 23 May edition flagged has now reached the capital itself — this is the clearest single escalation in months.

Front line 18–25 May

Quiet on the territorial ledger: no new ISW war-report card this window — but Russia-Belarus nuclear drills began 18 May and Russia set a drone record

Honesty over completeness here: the Russia Matters War Report Card is a weekly tracker, and the most recent edition (20 May, covering 12–19 May) is the one already logged in last week's briefing — no new card was published inside the 18–25 May window, so the territorial ledger has not formally moved. The standing figures: Russia at a net loss of 29 square miles for 12–19 May, a net loss of 69 over the four weeks to 19 May, against a trailing-year net gain of 1,585 square miles (about 0.7% of Ukraine; roughly 20% of the country occupied). What is new at the margin comes from ISW's own daily assessments: Russia launched more than 8,000 drones last month, the highest monthly total of the war; Russia and Belarus began joint military exercises on 18 May that practised the use of nuclear weapons; and at the line of contact Ukraine liberated Odradne and cleared parts of Stepnohirsk while Russia advanced near Pokrovsk and Rodynske. The trend is unchanged from last week — slow net erosion against Moscow — but the next hard data point is the 27 May war-report card, not anything in this window.

Diplomacy 18–24 May

Putin's "war is ending" rhetoric is now contradicted by his own missiles

The Ukraine diplomatic track produced no movement — and the Kyiv attack is itself the diplomacy story. Putin has continued the framing he used through May: the war "may be coming to an end," and he is open to meeting Zelensky in a third country if a peace deal is "finalised" first — the same conditional structure (meeting as ratification, not negotiation) the last two editions described. But ordering the largest strike on the Ukrainian capital of the entire war, using a nuclear-capable missile, days after repeating that rhetoric, is the cleanest possible illustration of the Chatham House caution carried in the 23 May edition: leader rhetoric is not a process indicator. The earlier three-day ceasefire and 1,000-for-1,000 prisoner swap reported in mid-May belong to a prior window and did not recur. No Zelensky–Putin meeting is scheduled; the named sticking point — Russia's demand that Ukraine withdraw from the parts of Donetsk it still holds — is unchanged. Net: the gap is exactly where it was, and Putin has just made his own wind-down narrative considerably harder to credit.

Russia · internal — economy, dissent, elites
Russia internal 14–24 May

Elite-frustration coverage got louder — but the serious analysts independently agree the system has no removal mechanism

This week the Russia-internal story gained volume without changing shape. On the discontent side: The Guardian reported, citing sources close to the Kremlin, Russian business and Western intelligence, that frustration with Putin is growing among political and business elites over the protracted war and deteriorating economy, with talk of his "isolation" and a "loss of confidence." Fortune (24 May) framed the economy as "much worse than it seems," citing a Swedish analysis of nighttime-luminosity data that suggests the economy actually shrank roughly 8% across 2020–2024 against the official claim of about +13% growth. The essential calibration, though, is the counter-weight — and it is striking that two independent outlets supplied it the same week. The Moscow Times ("Kremlin Elites Have No Way to Remove Putin, Even if They Wanted To") and Foreign Policy ("Don't Believe Rumors of Cracks in Putin's Regime") both argue that elite discontent has no transmission mechanism: the system was built to survive exactly these moments, and its participants cannot act against Putin without forfeiting their own privileged status. The honest read is therefore unchanged from the 23 May edition — degradation without rupture. Economic backdrop: 0.4% projected 2026 GDP, a 0.3% Q1 contraction, no recovery forecast before 2027, and oil-and-gas tax revenue down roughly two-thirds year-on-year in January. The Iran coupling still holds — an Iran deal that calms the oil price would shrink the very oil windfall propping up the IMF's "less bad" Russia figure.

Sanctions and trade
Sanctions 18–25 May

21st EU package still in drafting for end-June/early-July; the 20th package's crypto sectoral ban took effect 24 May

Process continuity, not news — exactly as the 23 May edition projected. No new package was adopted. The 21st remains scheduled for presentation at the end of June or early July, with scoping pointed at additional shadow-fleet tankers, more Russian banks and financial-circumvention entities, defence-industry enterprises, and firms trading stolen Ukrainian grain; a maritime-services ban on Russian crude — previously blocked by Malta and Greece — is the headline candidate. The one new colour this week is on the politics of the unlock: reporting attributes the "new impetus" behind revisiting previously vetoed measures to Hungary's change of government, naming new prime minister Péter Magyar following the early-2026 Hungarian election as the reason Viktor Orbán's veto bloc no longer holds. Meanwhile the 20th package's full sectoral ban on transacting with any Russia-established crypto-asset service provider took effect on schedule on 24 May, closing one of the larger remaining circumvention channels. The next discrete trade-flow event for Russian-flag and shadow-fleet tonnage is still roughly five to six weeks out.

Other · Europe and hybrid warfare
Hybrid warfare Standing read · 2025–May 2026

Russia's hybrid campaign against Europe: a standing escalation read that explains why "war ending" and "more pressure" are the same strategy

No single dated hybrid-warfare event landed inside the 18–25 May window, so this is a standing-read item — but it is worth a card precisely because the Kyiv strike and Putin's "war ending" rhetoric ran side by side this week, and the hybrid lens reconciles them. The established picture: more than 150 suspected Russia-linked hybrid incidents across the EU and NATO since 2025, following a roughly four-fold rise in sabotage operations in 2024 over the prior year; targets concentrated on Europe's expanding defence-production infrastructure and Ukraine-bound supply chains; Polish security services reportedly tracking dozens of attempts daily to probe electric, transport and digital infrastructure. The analytical consensus from GLOBSEC and RUSI is that 2026 brings further escalation, and — importantly — that it is tied directly to the negotiation tracks: the thesis is that Putin uses talks to buy time while escalating pressure on the West below the threshold of direct military confrontation, to sow chaos and erode support for Ukraine. That is the calibration for Stefan's read: a Ukraine "wind-down" narrative and a simultaneous hybrid escalation in Europe are not in tension under this interpretation — they are two arms of one strategy. It also means a notional Ukraine settlement would not necessarily reduce the hybrid-incident rate.
