Briefing summary
A blockbuster week: Google resets the price curve, the AI IPO pipeline goes live, and Anthropic stacks both talent and capital.

Google 19–22 May 2026
Google I/O 2026: a cheap model tier that beats last year's flagship, a 24/7 agent, and smart glasses
Google launched Gemini 3.5 Flash on 19 May. The notable part is positioning: the low-cost Flash tier now outperforms last generation's flagship, Gemini 3.1 Pro, on coding and agentic benchmarks while running roughly 4× faster — at $0.50 / $3.00 per million input/output tokens for the thinking variant. Google paired it with Gemini Spark, a cloud-run “always-on” personal agent, and confirmed Android XR smart glasses shipping this autumn (hardware by Samsung and Qualcomm, frames by Gentle Monster and Warby Parker). It also cut the AI Ultra subscription to about $100/month from $250, and said AI Mode in Search has passed one billion monthly users.

Anthropic 19 May 2026
Andrej Karpathy joins Anthropic's pre-training team
OpenAI co-founder and former Tesla AI lead Andrej Karpathy started at Anthropic this week, joining the pre-training team under Nick Joseph to build a group focused on using Claude to accelerate pre-training research. Pre-training is the most compute-intensive stage of model building and the one where gains compound hardest across capability and efficiency. Set against this week's funding and compute news, it reads as talent, capital and infrastructure all concentrating around one lab at once.

NVIDIA 20 May 2026
NVIDIA prints another blowout quarter — and concedes China to Huawei
NVIDIA reported fiscal Q1 revenue of $81.6 billion, up 85% year-over-year and ahead of the roughly $78.9 billion consensus, with data-center revenue of $75.2 billion (up 92%). It authorised an additional $80 billion in share buybacks and raised its quarterly dividend 25-fold, from $0.01 to $0.25. The candid note came from CEO Jensen Huang, who said NVIDIA has “largely conceded” China's advanced-AI-chip market to Huawei after April's US export-license requirement effectively shut it out — a structural loss the headline numbers mask.

Markets 20–22 May 2026
The AI IPO pipeline goes live: SpaceX files, OpenAI files confidentially, Anthropic targets October
Three of the most valuable private companies in the world moved toward public markets in the same week. SpaceX filed a public S-1 (the SEC registration that precedes an initial public offering) on 20 May, targeting a Nasdaq listing under ticker SPCX at roughly $1.75 trillion, with pricing possible in late June. OpenAI filed a confidential S-1, targeting a September listing at an $852 billion to $1 trillion valuation. Anthropic is reported to be targeting October at around $900 billion. SpaceX is the confirmed leg; the OpenAI and Anthropic timelines are reported and can still slip. Either way, the AI build-out is about to be re-priced in public markets — with the disclosure that entails.

Anthropic xAI 20 May 2026
Anthropic to pay roughly $1.25B a month for xAI's compute — disclosed in SpaceX's IPO filing
Buried in SpaceX's S-1 filing: Anthropic will pay roughly $1.25 billion per month through May 2029 — about $15 billion a year — for full access to xAI's Colossus 1 data center in Memphis (300+ megawatts, 220,000+ NVIDIA GPUs), while xAI shifts its own training to the newer Colossus 2 facility. Two things stand out. First, two direct frontier rivals are now each other's customer and supplier. Second, this is the “neocloud” model out in the open: labs monetising spare infrastructure by renting it to competitors. Either side can terminate on 90 days' notice — so it is large, but not locked in.

Meta 20 May 2026
Meta cuts about 8,000 jobs — roughly 10% of staff — to reorganise around AI
Meta laid off roughly 8,000 employees on 20 May, around 10% of its workforce, and cancelled an estimated 6,000 open roles, with a further ~7,000 staff moving onto AI teams. Management framed it explicitly as a restructuring rather than a performance cull: the company is reorganising into AI “pods” under its Superintelligence Labs division (led by Chief AI Officer Alexandr Wang) and redirecting an estimated $115–135 billion toward AI infrastructure this year. More cuts are signalled for the second half of 2026.

US Policy 21 May 2026
White House shelves its AI executive order hours before signing
President Trump postponed an executive order (EO) that would have created a voluntary pre-release review for frontier AI models — giving agencies up to 90 days to test new systems, with the Treasury, the National Security Agency (NSA) and the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) building a classified benchmark for dangerous cyber capabilities. Trump said he “didn't like certain aspects”; reporting credits a direct intervention by AI adviser David Sacks, with pushback also from Elon Musk and Mark Zuckerberg. The practical effect: no near-term US federal pre-release testing regime. For EU-exposed operations, the EU AI Act's transparency obligations (effective August 2026) remain the binding constraint.

OpenAI 20 May 2026
An OpenAI reasoning model disproved an 80-year-old mathematics conjecture
OpenAI said a general-purpose internal reasoning model disproved the planar unit distance conjecture, an open problem in discrete geometry posed by Paul Erdős in 1946. The model produced an infinite family of point constructions that beats the long-assumed square-grid optimum, and the proof was checked by external mathematicians. The detail that matters: this came from a general reasoning model, not a system trained or scaffolded specifically for mathematics — a concrete data point on how far unspecialised reasoning now reaches.

Anthropic 18 May 2026
Anthropic acquires Stainless, the SDK tooling startup used by its rivals
Anthropic acquired Stainless, a startup whose software generates SDKs (software development kits), command-line tools and MCP (Model Context Protocol) servers — and which powered the official SDKs of OpenAI, Google and Cloudflare as well as Anthropic. The Information had reported a price above $300 million. Anthropic will wind down Stainless's hosted products; existing customers keep the SDKs they have already generated. Small in dollar terms, but pointed: Anthropic now owns a piece of developer infrastructure its competitors were relying on.

Dev tooling 21 May 2026
The Model Context Protocol locks its next-spec release candidate
The Model Context Protocol (MCP) — the now-standard way agents connect to tools and data — locked the release candidate for its next specification on 21 May. The headline change makes the protocol stateless at its core, so remote MCP servers can run behind an ordinary load balancer instead of needing sticky sessions; it also adds an Extensions framework, a Tasks primitive, MCP Apps, authorisation hardening and a formal deprecation policy. The final spec is due 28 July, giving SDK maintainers a ten-week window to validate against real workloads.

💡 Editor's view
1. The cheap tier is now the story. Gemini 3.5 Flash beating last year's flagship at 4× the speed and a fraction of the price means “good enough” is now genuinely cheap. Anything running on a premium model is worth re-testing on the cheap tier before the next invoice — the gap that justified the premium has narrowed sharply.
2. The IPO wave is a regime change, not a headline. SpaceX, OpenAI and Anthropic all moving toward public listings within roughly six months means the financial plumbing of the AI build-out is about to become visible — including the circular deals, like Anthropic renting xAI's compute for about $15B a year. Public disclosure will make the sector's true unit economics much harder to keep off the page.
3. Washington stepped back; Brussels did not. With the US executive order shelved, there is no near-term federal pre-release testing regime. The EU AI Act's transparency rules (August 2026) are now the de facto global floor for anyone with EU exposure — the regulatory variable to watch is European, not American.
The quiet signal of the week is the Karpathy hire. When someone who understands pre-training as well as anyone alive picks joining Anthropic over independence, it is a read on where the people who build the frontier think the frontier is now being built.
