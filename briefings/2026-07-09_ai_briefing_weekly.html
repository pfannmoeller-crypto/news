MCP28 Jul (RC)
MCP 2026-07-28 Release Candidate — the biggest protocol revision since launch
The Model Context Protocol team dropped the release candidate for the 28 July specification: a stateless core that runs behind plain HTTP load balancers (no more sticky sessions or shared session stores), server-rendered UIs via MCP Apps, long-running work via the Tasks extension, OAuth/OpenID-aligned authorization, and a formal deprecation policy so the protocol can evolve without breaking downstream. Stacktree and SecurityWeek both flag genuinely breaking changes for existing integrations.
chevron_rightMehr lesen
Kontext & Vorgeschichte

MCP shipped in late 2024 as Anthropic's proposal for a standard "USB-C for AI" — a wire protocol between models and tools/data. Adoption exploded through 2025 (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, GitHub, Microsoft all shipped support) and 2026 has been the year of enterprise hardening. The 28 July spec is the accumulated response to production complaints: gateway complexity, auth pain, no formal task model for long-running jobs, no versioning discipline.

Die Details

Stateless core: a remote MCP server that previously needed sticky sessions, a shared session store, and deep packet inspection at the gateway can now run behind a plain round-robin load balancer, route traffic on an Mcp-Method header, and let clients cache tools/list responses for as long as the server's ttlMs permits. Extensions: MCP Apps (server-rendered UIs — the tool returns a widget, not just JSON), Tasks (formal long-running-work primitive with progress/cancel/completion). Authorization: alignment with OAuth 2.1 and OpenID Connect deployment patterns. Deprecation policy: versioned, so integrators know how long a call surface stays supported. Microsoft's MCP catalog (60+ ready servers across M365 Copilot, Copilot Studio, Azure AI Foundry, GitHub Copilot) rides on top.

Einordnung

The New Stack's framing ("why MCP won") is broadly right — every major model vendor now speaks MCP, so protocol maturity is the constraint. This release solves the two most-cited production complaints (stateful gateways and auth) and opens the door to a real MCP-based agentic app tier (via MCP Apps + Tasks). Security implication flagged by SecurityWeek: bigger surface area, new attack vectors around server-rendered UIs and long-running tasks — expect vulnerability advisories through Q3.

Watch-Items

Migration pain: Stacktree's "what breaks" list will be the real-world adoption filter. Speed of MCP Apps ecosystem emergence — this is the piece that could reshape "AI app" definition. And any security incidents traceable to the new stateless / server-rendered UI patterns.

EU AI ACTT-24 days
Commission enforcement powers for GPAI providers kick in on 2 August 2026
The critical shift in the AI Act's phased implementation: from 2 August the AI Office gains full enforcement — information requests, model recalls, mitigation orders, fines up to €15M or 3% of global revenue. GPAI obligations themselves have been in force since August 2025; enforcement is now what gets turned on. Council also gave final green light on 29 June to a streamlining regulation that defers high-risk system deadlines (stand-alone high-risk to 2 Dec 2027; embedded to 2 Aug 2028) and adds a ban on non-consensual sexual deepfakes from December.
chevron_rightMehr lesen
Kontext & Vorgeschichte

The EU AI Act entered force in August 2024 with staggered application dates. GPAI provider obligations (transparency, copyright compliance, safety-and-security for systemic-risk models) landed on 2 August 2025. The 2 August 2026 date is when the Commission's enforcement machinery — the AI Office's power to fine, recall, mandate mitigations — becomes operational for those GPAI obligations. In parallel, the Council on 29 June 2026 formally approved a "streamlining" regulation deferring the timelines for high-risk AI systems (the other big obligations bucket) by 12–18 months, and adding a December 2026 prohibition on non-consensual sexual and CSAM-adjacent generated content.

Die Details

GPAI is defined as any model trained with >10²³ FLOPs (floating point operations) capable of generating text/audio/image/video. Fines: up to €15M or 3% of global revenue, whichever higher. The AI Office may request information, order model recalls, mandate mitigations. Signatories of the General-Purpose AI Code of Practice (26 organisations including Amazon, Anthropic, Google, IBM, Microsoft, OpenAI, Cohere, Mistral, Aleph Alpha; SpaceXAI signed only the Safety & Security Chapter; Meta refused) get a presumption of conformity. High-risk deadline deferral: stand-alone high-risk systems now 2 Dec 2027, embedded 2 Aug 2028. December 2026 additions: nude image generation and non-consensual sexual deepfakes prohibited.

Einordnung

Three practical takeaways. First, 2 August is not a "new law" date — obligations are already in force — but it is when non-compliance can be materially expensive for the first time. Second, the streamlining regulation shows the EU institutions blinking on high-risk deadlines under industry pressure while holding the GPAI line. Third, Meta's holdout status on the Code of Practice will be an early test case: no presumption of conformity means it must prove compliance through alternative "adequate means." For any operator selling AI-enabled products into the EU, the immediate compliance surface is documentation (training data summaries, copyright policy, safety testing) and — from December — content marking obligations.

Watch-Items

First AI Office information requests after 2 August. Whether Meta files an alternative compliance framework or takes the fine risk. Legal challenges to the deepfake prohibition (likely from platform intermediaries). And the still-open question of how the €15M / 3% cap is calculated for AI-native businesses without traditional revenue lines.

EU AI ACTT-13 days
Transparency Code of Practice signatories close 22 July, 18:00 CEST
Providers and deployers of generative AI systems have until 22 July, 18:00 CEST to submit signatory forms and appear on the initial published list before 2 August. Signing gives a presumption of conformity with Article 50 (marking/labelling of AI-generated content). The Code is separate from the broader GPAI Code of Practice, and applies to Article 50(2) providers of generative systems for synthetic audio, image, video or text.
chevron_rightMehr lesen
Kontext & Vorgeschichte

The Article 50 obligation — that AI-generated content must be marked and labelled — enters application on 2 August 2026 alongside the broader GPAI enforcement date. The Transparency Code, published June 2026, is the operational voluntary compliance path: sign, implement, get a rebuttable presumption of conformity when the Commission or a national authority comes calling.

Die Details

Signatory deadline: 22 July 2026, 18:00 CEST — this cutoff is specifically to make the initial published list before the Article 50 obligations become operational on 2 August. Scope: providers of generative AI systems capable of producing synthetic audio, image, video or text content, and deployers subject to Article 50(2) or 50(4). Structure: watermarking/provenance signals expected, plus documentation of what the mark looks like and how it can be verified. Signatory list will be public. Note the distinction from the earlier General-Purpose AI Code of Practice (safety, transparency, copyright chapters) — that had 26 signatories including OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft; the Transparency Code is a separate, narrower instrument.

Einordnung

The clean signal to watch is who signs by 22 July. Non-signature doesn't mean non-compliance, but it means the operator carries the burden of proving Article 50 alignment on its own terms — expensive if it goes to a formal enforcement inquiry. Watermarking is the load-bearing technical primitive: expect a scramble of C2PA-style provenance implementations at frontier labs and image/video vendors over the next fortnight.

Watch-Items

The published list on ~23 July. Whether Meta signs the Transparency Code even after refusing the broader GPAI Code. C2PA and provenance vendor traction. And any national-authority guidance clarifying what "sufficient marking" means for text output.
