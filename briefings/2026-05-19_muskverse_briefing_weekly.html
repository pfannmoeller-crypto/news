The OpenAI trial collapses on a calendar technicality. SpaceX moves to file publicly for the IPO. Starship Flight 12 slips a third time.

The SEC settlement red-flag detail sharpened. And Neuralink unveiled a next-generation surgical robot with whole-brain access.

China trip ends without a concrete FSD approval; Musk and Huang flew home on Air Force One

Trump's May 13–15 state visit to China closed without a public Full Self-Driving (FSD) regulatory commitment for Tesla. (CNBC banquet, Yahoo Finance Air Force One, Stocktwits FSD delay)

Markets read it accordingly — the China-meeting premium has now been priced and largely given back. The honest scoreboard: Musk got access and presence, but the Q3 internal estimate remains the operative number on FSD-China. Nvidia got the cleaner win out of the trip.

Tesla May 15 2026 · stock + capex

TSLA gives back ~3.7% to $423; 2026 CapEx hiked above $25B; negative free cash flow expected rest of year

TSLA closed down 3.73% on May 15 to roughly $423, reversing part of the China-trip rally. The pullback coincided with reporting that Tesla's 2026 capital-expenditure plan has been lifted above $25 billion — nearly triple the prior year's $8.53 billion and well above the roughly $20 billion plan discussed earlier in 2026. Management has guided to negative free cash flow for the rest of the year after the Q1 surplus. Separately, the May 14 robotaxi friction story (long wait times, cancellations, navigation issues across Austin/Dallas/Houston) has been picked up more broadly; analysts (Piper Sandler, Motley Fool, MEXC) are coalescing around the view that the rally to $445 priced an early-2027 autonomy story while 2026 operational reality is a ~26-vehicle fleet stretched across multiple cities. Tesla expects no material robotaxi revenue in 2026 and continues to gate scale on the v15 software release, presumed early 2027. (TradingKey May 15, Techi CapEx, Motley Fool ramp)

So what: The fundamental question is becoming visible again. Tesla is shifting capital structure from EV cash machine to AI capex absorber while delivery growth slows. A $25B+ spend year against negative FCF guidance is a different financial profile than the one TSLA traded on at $445. The stock is now adjudicating two stories — the Optimus/robotaxi optionality and the deteriorating near-term cash picture. The bull case still works on a long-dated discount rate; the question is whether the market keeps applying one as the IPO-cycle attention shifts to SpaceX.

Tesla May 2026 · Optimus

Optimus production timeline firms: Fremont line installation underway; V3 reveal slated July–August; start "quite slow"

Fresh detail on the post-Model S/X Fremont conversion: the first-generation Optimus production line is now being installed at Fremont, with the V3 robot reveal targeted late July or August 2026 and production starting "shortly after." Musk has explicitly warned that initial output will be "quite slow" and that predicting the 2026 rate is "literally impossible" given Optimus carries ~10,000 unique parts across an entirely new line. The Fremont line is designed for a long-term capacity of 1 million robots per year; Gigafactory Texas is being prepared for the second-generation line, designed for 10 million per year. Today, Optimus robots are operating within Tesla's own factories but, per the Q4 2025 call, are "primarily for learning and data collection rather than performing productive tasks." (Electrek V3 reveal, Optimus Production Timeline, Greendrive Optimus 3)

So what: The Optimus production story is graduating from rhetorical claim to physical line installation — that is the genuine progress. The "quite slow" framing from Musk himself is the honest disclaimer: the bull thesis at $445 implicitly priced rapid ramp, but the operator-stated rate is "unpredictable, low to start." The valuable signal in the next 90 days is whether the July–August reveal actually happens on time and what it shows on the dexterity/throughput axis — that is the leading indicator on whether the AI-pivot capex earns its cost.

A $1.75T+ asking valuation leaves little margin for disclosure surprises.

A clean V3 flight just before pricing would be a textbook tailwind. OpenAI Trial May 18 2026 · verdict

Jury dismisses all claims in Musk v. Altman in under two hours; statute-of-limitations finding adopted by judge; Musk to appeal

The dominant Muskverse legal event since the May 14 refresh. On Monday May 18, an advisory jury in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California (Oakland) returned a verdict in less than two hours of deliberation dismissing every count in Musk's lawsuit against Sam Altman and OpenAI. Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers immediately adopted the verdict. The basis: the court did not address whether Musk's "breach of charitable trust" claims were substantively valid; it found instead that they fell outside the three-year statute of limitations — meaning Musk waited too long to sue after the alleged breach. Musk had sought $134 billion in disgorged "ill-gotten gains." On the same statute-of-limitations grounds, the jury also rejected Musk's claim against Microsoft for allegedly aiding and abetting Altman and Greg Brockman in breaching their duty to OpenAI. Musk and his attorneys said they will appeal to the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals. In a post on X, Musk called the decision a "calendar technicality". Color note: a federal judge had told Musk earlier in the week that he was not excused from trial; Musk attended the Trump China delegation anyway, and his lawyer apologized for his absence. (NPR verdict, CNBC technicality, NBC News, LNM swift deliberations, NBC China-during-trial)

So what: Read this as a clean legal loss with a soft narrative outcome. The legal outcome is unambiguous: a unanimous-feeling, under-two-hour verdict with the judge stepping in to adopt it instantly is as decisive as it gets. The narrative outcome is softer: because the case died on the calendar, not on the merits, Musk's framing — "OpenAI betrayed its non-profit mission" — survives politically and reputationally, even as the courtroom shut it down. The appeal to the 9th Circuit will likely fail on the same grounds (statute of limitations is rarely reopened on appeal). The bigger consequence: a major piece of legal overhang has now resolved before the SpaceX S-1 publishes, which is plausibly material to how SpaceX risk-factor language can be written. One thread closes; two (SEC, DOGE/EFF) remain open.

SEC May 13–19 2026 · SEC settlement red flags

Judge Sooknanan's three specific red flags come into focus; June 1 written-answer deadline holds

Detail behind last week's "won't rubber-stamp" headline tightened this week. Reporting from Bloomberg, U.S. News, and Insurance Journal makes Judge Sparkle Sooknanan's three specific objections to the $1.5M Musk-SEC Twitter-stake settlement explicit. First: the trust structure. Sooknanan ordered both parties to explain "why the proposed settlement involves a trust tied to Musk instead of the billionaire himself," asking on the record — "Is Mr. Musk getting some kind of special treatment in this case?" Second: the penalty size. The judge noted that $1.5M is approximately 1% of the ~$150M Musk allegedly cost Twitter shareholders by disclosing his stake eleven days late. Third: the structure removing Musk personally as a defendant, which the judge suggested had been crafted "for the sole purpose of Mr. Musk being able to say that no relief was entered." Both sides have written answers due by June 1. The settlement does not take effect until the judge approves it. (US News red flags, Bloomberg, Insurance Journal)

So what: Three named objections, each one a distinct structural defect, is materially worse than a single procedural complaint. The judge is signaling she has read the settlement carefully and finds it unusually defendant-favorable. The probability that Sooknanan outright rejects the deal after the June 1 filings is still likely modest — courts almost always accept SEC settlements — but it has now risen from background to non-trivial tail risk. Critically, June 1 sits squarely inside the SpaceX IPO marketing window (which begins June 4). A rejected settlement re-opening the SEC matter during the IPO roadshow is a specific, calendarable downside scenario the S-1 will have to address.

Neuralink May 2026 · new robot

Neuralink unveils next-generation surgical robot designed for whole-brain access; expands beyond motor cortex

Neuralink has announced a next-generation surgical robot designed to access any region of the brain with high precision, a meaningful expansion from earlier Neuralink systems that focused on the motor cortex and helped users interact with computers via thought. The robot implants ultrathin electrode threads — thinner than a human hair — directly into brain tissue to capture and transmit neural signals. Reporting (YourStory.com, Fierce Biotech, Digital Health News) emphasizes three points: (1) the broader anatomical reach opens research paths to Parkinson's disease, epilepsy, paralysis, and sensory disorders; (2) the robot uses advanced cameras and sensors to navigate brain micro-movement and minimize bleeding/scarring during implantation; (3) Neuralink has not yet provided a commercial timeline for the new robot. Musk's stated framing remains the "generalized neural interface" goal — a brain-computer interface (BCI) that could address neurological conditions broadly rather than serving one indication. (YourStory, Fierce Biotech, Digital Health News)

So what: Whole-brain access is the platform pivot that takes Neuralink from "single-indication motor BCI" to a research tool that competing labs (Synchron, Precision Neuroscience, Paradromics) will need to respond to. The honest disclaimer in the reporting — "no commercial timeline" — matters: this is a capability claim, not a shipped product. For the SpaceX S-1 narrative, Neuralink sits in the "Musk-ecosystem optionality" column rather than the cashflow column, but a credible whole-brain-access platform raises the ecosystem's terminal-value ceiling.
