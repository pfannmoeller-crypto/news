Markets & Macro · Weekly

Wall Street claws back half of last week's chip rout as memory stocks rip — but a tanker hit in the Strait of Hormuz reopened the oil trade, and tonight's Alphabet and Tesla numbers are the real verdict on AI spending.

The week split cleanly into a risk-off Monday and a risk-on Tuesday. Monday, a ninth consecutive night of US strikes on Iran and three American military deaths over the weekend pushed Brent above $90 and dragged the S&P 500 down 0.19%, the Dow down 0.59%. Tuesday reversed nearly all of it: Morgan Stanley's call for a 25% memory-price spike between Q2 and Q3 sent SanDisk up 14.3%, Micron up 12.2% and the VanEck Semiconductor ETF up 4.5%, while AMD gained 8% on Microsoft's adoption of its Helios AI rack system — pushing the S&P 500 to 7,509.20 (+0.89%), the Nasdaq to 25,837.21 (+1.29%) and the Dow to 52,224.64 (+0.74%). Underneath the bounce, Goldman Sachs data show hedge funds have net-sold US tech stocks in six of the last eight weeks, the largest sector retreat in more than a decade, cutting net tech exposure to its lowest level since February. And in the Gulf, mediators are pushing a 10-day ceasefire after a tanker was struck near Hormuz and the Houthis threatened to blockade Saudi shipping — the story now carrying more weight for markets than the Fed. Tonight after the close, Alphabet and Tesla report — the first of the "Magnificent Seven" to face investors since the AI-capex scare, and this week's genuine swing factor.

US equities20–21 Jul

The week's whiplash: Monday's Iran-driven dip gives way to Tuesday's chip-led rebound, clawing back roughly half of last week's semiconductor rout

Monday was a straight risk-off session: nearly two-thirds of US stocks fell as a ninth consecutive night of American strikes on Iran and three confirmed US military deaths over the weekend pushed oil higher, leaving the S&P 500 down 0.19%, Nasdaq down 0.05% and Dow down 0.59%. Tuesday reversed course entirely. Morgan Stanley's call for a 25% memory-price spike ignited a broad chip rally — SanDisk +14.3%, Micron +12.2%, Teradyne +12.1%, AMD +8.1%, Applied Materials +7.4% — lifting the S&P 500 to 7,509.20 (+0.89%), Nasdaq to 25,837.21 (+1.29%) and Dow to 52,224.64 (+0.74%). South Korea's record July export data, showing AI-chip shipments still accelerating, reinforced the bounce. Roughly 88% of S&P 500 reporters have beaten estimates so far this season, with blended earnings growth near 25%.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Last week's edition closed with the PHLX Semiconductor Index in a confirmed bear market — down ~11% on the week and more than 20% off its June high — after Broadcom's soft AI-revenue guide, Meta's "Meta Compute" capacity-resale plan, TSMC's capex raise and Moonshot's Kimi K3 launch combined to reprice the "AI capex has no ceiling" thesis. That briefing flagged Nvidia's next report and "whether Meta Compute publishes pricing" as the watch-items that would confirm or deny a supply glut. Neither had resolved by Monday; instead, a geopolitical shock and a sell-side memory call did the work of moving the tape this week.

Die Details: Monday's session losses were broad but shallow — the S&P's −0.19% and Dow's −0.59% understate how many names were red (roughly two-thirds of the index), with technology, energy and communication services the only pockets of strength as Shell, BP and TotalEnergies gained on the oil spike while Ryanair and Lufthansa fell on fuel-cost fears in Europe. SpaceX (−3.3%), Tesla (−3.0%) and Oracle (−4.0%) led Monday's US decliners. Tuesday's reversal was driven by a specific catalyst rather than a broad re-rating: Morgan Stanley analyst Joseph Moore published a note arguing memory prices would rise at least 25% between Q2 and Q3 2026 on persistent datacenter demand and no signs of easing supply constraints, with shortages potentially extending into 2027–2028. That triggered the sharpest one-day memory-stock move of the year — SanDisk peaked above +14%, Micron above +12%, with Western Digital, Seagate and SK Hynix all up 7–9%. AMD's own 8% gain was a separate, coincident catalyst (see the Helios/Microsoft story below). South Korea's Bloomberg-reported early-July export data — a record pace driven by AI-linked chip demand — landed the same morning and reinforced the "demand is fine, this was a valuation reset" reading that three major banks (Morgan Stanley, and implicitly Goldman and Deutsche via price-target moves) converged on this week.

Einordnung: The clean read is that this week did not resolve last week's question — it deferred it. Nobody produced new evidence that AI infrastructure demand is actually slowing; if anything, South Korean exports and the memory-shortage call argue the opposite. What moved was positioning: a crowded short/underweight in a beaten-down sector met a credible bullish catalyst and covered hard. That is consistent with "healthy reset, not deterioration," the framing several sell-side desks used this week. But the Goldman hedge-fund data covered separately in this edition complicates the bullish read — funds have been net sellers of tech in six of the last eight weeks, and Tuesday's rally does not yet show up as a reversal of that flow. The more important tell is qualitative: the market's institutional plumbing is now visibly two-sided on AI capex for the first time this cycle, where six months ago it was one-way. That raises the stakes on tonight's Alphabet and Tesla numbers far more than a symmetric market would.

Watch-Items: Alphabet and Tesla results after tonight's close — the first read on whether "monetization not spending" (LPL Financial's framing) holds up under scrutiny. Whether the memory rally has legs into Micron's and SK Hynix's next earnings, or fades as a one-note, one-analyst-driven move. The 28–29 July FOMC. And whether South Korea's export strength shows up in Samsung's and SK Hynix's own guidance rather than just customs data.

Alphabet and Tesla report after tonight's close — the first "Magnificent Seven" test since the AI-capex scare began

This is the week's real event, and it lands after this edition is compiled. Alphabet and Tesla headline a genuinely busy S&P 500 earnings week — 86 companies reporting, the third-busiest stretch of the season — alongside 3M, Honeywell, Charles Schwab, Domino's and American Express. Wall Street has raised expectations into the print: Magnificent Seven earnings are tracked to grow 31.1% year-over-year against 24.7% for the broader S&P 500, and excluding Nvidia and Micron, S&P 500 earnings growth would fall to just 16.8% — a reminder of how concentrated this earnings season's strength really is. Investors want proof that Alphabet's roughly $175–185B 2026 capex guide is generating monetizable AI revenue, not just infrastructure.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Last week's briefing closed with Warren Buffett's disclosure that he personally built Berkshire's $31B Alphabet stake, calling Google "more likely to be a winner... than probably 90% or 95% of what gets merchandised through Wall Street" while warning the AI giants are "playing a game they don't want to play" — spending defensively rather than against a clear return hurdle. That framing sets up tonight's report as a direct test: Alphabet guided 2026 capex to $175–185B and raised $80B in equity in June specifically to fund it, an unusual move for a company with Alphabet's free cash flow. Tesla, meanwhile, enters its print already the week's most sold-off Magnificent Seven name — down roughly 3% on Monday alone on Iran-driven risk-off flows, with no chip-rally offset since it isn't a semiconductor name.

Die Details: LPL Financial's Jeff Buchbinder captured the market's framing precisely this week: "AI is entering a new phase. The market is moving from pricing in promise to pricing in execution," adding that investors "should focus less on who is spending the most and more on who is generating measurable returns." That is a materially higher bar than the one Big Tech cleared in its Q1 reports, when a capex raise alone was enough to move a stock higher. The season's aggregate numbers cut both ways for the bulls: 88% of reporters have beaten estimates and blended growth has risen to 24.7–25% from an initial 23.3% estimate, but stripping out Nvidia and Micron collapses growth to 16.8%, meaning most of the "beat" is concentrated in a narrow AI-hardware sliver — precisely the sliver whose valuation is now in question. Alphabet's own Gemini 3.5 Pro model has reportedly been running behind schedule, a data point that featured in coverage of last week's chip selloff and adds a company-specific overhang beyond the sector-wide capex debate.

Einordnung: The stakes are asymmetric. A genuinely strong Alphabet print — cloud growth, ad revenue, and evidence that Gemini is monetizing — would validate the "distribution and monetization layer" thesis that lifted Apple past Nvidia last week and give hedge funds a reason to stop the six-of-eight-weeks selling streak documented elsewhere in this edition. A soft print, or one where capex rises again without a matching revenue story, would confirm the bear case at the worst possible moment — into a market already showing record de-risking and thin breadth. Tesla is a separate but related test: it is not an AI-infrastructure name in the way Alphabet is, but its valuation has long depended on a robotics/FSD narrative that requires the same kind of "spending converts to revenue" leap of faith the market is now demanding evidence for across the board.

Watch-Items: Alphabet's cloud backlog and capex commentary specifically — any upward revision to the $175–185B guide without a matching revenue acceleration would likely be read as bearish, inverting last week's TSMC pattern. Tesla's delivery and margin trajectory. Whether Wednesday/Thursday's market reaction breaks the correlation between good numbers and stock direction that defined last week's regime change, or restores it. And read-through to Meta and Microsoft, which report in the following days.

AMD · MicrosoftMon 20 Jul

AMD's Helios lands Microsoft as a buyer — arguably its biggest AI infrastructure deal yet, and a direct challenge to Nvidia's near-monopoly

AMD unveiled Helios, its first rack-scale AI system, integrating Instinct MI455X GPUs, EPYC Venice CPUs, Pensando networking and ROCm software across 72 interconnected GPUs. Microsoft confirmed it will deploy Helios in Azure for AI inference, joining Meta, OpenAI and Oracle as early adopters. AMD shares rose as much as 8% across the two-day announcement window (+3% Monday, extending Tuesday), with Rosenblatt raising its price target to $655 (from $490) and UBS to $700 (from $670); roughly 82% of covering analysts rate the stock Buy. Nvidia still controls more than 95% of data-centre GPUs against AMD's roughly 4.5%, and responded within days by unveiling its own next-generation "Kyber" rack, doubling GPU density from 72 to 144 per rack.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: AMD has spent the AI cycle as the credible-but-distant number two to Nvidia — its MI300/MI350 series won incremental hyperscaler business, but no customer had treated AMD as a primary rather than supplementary supplier. Microsoft's own AI infrastructure strategy has been the most vendor-diversified of the hyperscalers, and this deal is the clearest expression yet of that "three-vendor" posture (Nvidia, AMD, and its own in-house Maia silicon), explicitly framed by Microsoft around "performance, scale and choice" — corporate language for reducing single-supplier dependency after two years of GPU allocation constraints.

Die Details: Helios racks are estimated by Futurum Group at $5–5.5M per unit versus $3.5–4M for Nvidia's competing Vera Rubin system — AMD is not competing on sticker price but on Microsoft's stated "cost per completed AI task" metric, meaning the pitch is total-cost-of-ownership and power efficiency rather than upfront cost. Meta and Oracle are slated to receive Helios systems later in 2026, alongside earlier-disclosed OpenAI commitments. The stock reaction was immediate and reinforced by sell-side re-rating: Rosenblatt's target jump from $490 to $655 and UBS's from $670 to $700 are both large single-note moves, and the 82.4% Buy-rating consensus (28 buys, 8 holds, zero sells per the cited coverage) is unusually one-sided for a stock already up sharply over the past year. Nvidia's Kyber counter-announcement — doubling rack GPU density to 144 — is the clearest evidence the market leader is responding competitively rather than dismissing AMD's traction.

Einordnung: This is a genuine data point for the "is AI capex demand-driven or fear-driven" debate running through this whole edition, and it points toward demand. Hyperscalers diversifying suppliers is exactly what you'd expect if the underlying compute need is real and growing faster than any single vendor can supply — it is much harder to explain as defensive spending, since qualifying a second GPU architecture is expensive and slow, not the kind of move a buyer makes to hedge a bubble. It also directly supports the memory-rally logic from the index story above: more GPU racks from more vendors means more HBM and DRAM demand regardless of which chip vendor wins share. The Nvidia response (Kyber) is itself informative — a true monopolist facing no competitive pressure would not double rack density on a compressed timeline.

Watch-Items: AMD's Advancing AI conference in San Francisco, where CEO Lisa Su is expected to update the product roadmap and possibly disclose further Helios order commitments. Whether Meta and Oracle confirm delivery timelines. AMD's own next earnings report for whether the order book converts to revenue recognition, not just announcements. And whether other hyperscalers (Amazon, Google) follow Microsoft into multi-vendor GPU commitments.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Last week's briefing tracked July hike odds falling from ~46% to ~16% to ~4% across a single week on back-to-back soft CPI and PPI prints, while noting the Fed faced "falling goods inflation, rising energy prices, elevated consumer inflation expectations, and an equity market repricing its largest sector — four inputs pointing in three directions." Die Details: The current Polymarket distribution (80% hold / 18.9% hike / ~1% cut) is little changed in direction from last week's FedWatch-implied ~4% July-hike reading, though the residual probability sits somewhat higher on this venue — a gap likely reflecting the oil-driven inflation scare rather than new labor or growth data. The structural anchor for the non-trivial hike odds remains June's FOMC minutes, where nine of eighteen officials projected at least one 2026 hike and zero projected a cut — a genuinely hawkish committee posture that the soft CPI/PPI prints have not visibly shifted. May's CPI reading, still being cited in this week's analysis, showed a 4.2% year-over-year headline rise driven by a 23.5% surge in energy costs tied to Middle East tensions — the same mechanism now reloading via the Hormuz tanker attack and the ~$91 Brent print.

Einordnung: The Fed is being asked to hold a line between two genuinely conflicting signals — core disinflation from the goods side and a reloading energy shock from the geopolitical side — for a second consecutive month, and markets are pricing that it will thread the needle with a hold. That is the base case, but the 18.9% hike probability is a meaningfully higher tail than the ~4% priced a week ago on CME FedWatch, which suggests some cross-venue divergence or a modest re-hawkish drift as oil climbed through the week. Combined with a Fed chair (Warsh) whose reaction function markets are still calibrating, and an equity market that — as this edition's other stories show — has stopped treating rate relief as automatically bullish, the July meeting is lower-stakes for stocks than it would have been in a pre-AI-capex-scare regime, but the September meeting's odds (41.5% hike as of last week) remain the more consequential swing factor.

Watch-Items: The 28–29 July FOMC statement and Warsh's press-conference language on the energy-driven inflation reload. Whether Brent's move above $90 shows up in the next CPI print's energy component. September hike odds, last seen at 41.5% and due for their next reading around the July meeting. And whether the Fed explicitly addresses the equity-market AI-capex unwind in its communications, which it has so far avoided doing directly.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: This data point is the missing piece connecting last week's semiconductor bear market to this week's memory-driven rebound. Last week's briefing observed that "leveraged semiconductor ETFs saw forced deleveraging" and that "the marginal holder was weak hands" — evidence of retail capitulation. Die Details: Six net-sell weeks out of the last eight (through 16 July) is the headline, but the magnitude matters more than the frequency: Goldman characterizes it as the largest eight-week aggregate reduction in tech exposure in at least a decade of prime-brokerage tracking, cutting net exposure to roughly 15.5% — nearly 10 percentage points below overall US market exposure, and low enough to erase most of the sector's 2026 gains for the hedge-fund cohort specifically. The stocks named as most affected — Micron, SanDisk, Alphabet — overlap directly with this week's other major stories: Micron and SanDisk are the names that then ripped 12–14% on Tuesday's memory call, meaning some of Tuesday's rally may itself be short-covering or re-risking by funds caught offside on the six-week unwind, not fresh conviction buying. Goldman's own house view — "history, positioning, and lack of a favorable catalyst point to continued near-term challenges" — is explicitly cautious despite the Tuesday bounce, and the firm frames the move as rotation into consumer stocks and out of crypto-adjacent AI infrastructure plays, not a return to cash.

Einordnung: Read together with the Tuesday rebound, this data argues for a specific, less comfortable interpretation than "healthy reset": positioning has become genuinely light in tech, which mechanically makes the sector more volatile in both directions — a good earnings surprise (Alphabet tonight) triggers an outsized squeeze higher because there is little length left to sell, while a disappointment has less far to fall in dollar terms but confirms the institutional flight thesis and could accelerate outflows from the remaining 15.5%. This is the connective tissue between the index-level whiplash story and tonight's earnings: thin institutional positioning is exactly the condition under which single-name earnings reports move the whole tape disproportionately, which is why this week's Alphabet/Tesla prints carry more weight than a typical Wednesday earnings drop.

Watch-Items: Whether Goldman's next weekly prime-brokerage update shows the six-of-eight-week selling streak broken by Tuesday's rally, or whether funds used the bounce to sell into strength. Net tech exposure relative to its historical range — 15.5% is unusually low and mean-reversion in either direction is plausible. The crypto read-through flagged by Coinpedia (AI-infrastructure and compute-adjacent tokens such as Bittensor, Near, Render, and the Artificial Superintelligence Alliance token) as a leading indicator of further de-risking. And whether Alphabet/Tesla earnings become the catalyst Goldman says is currently missing.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Last week's briefing left oil at Brent ~$85–86, up roughly 12% on the week, following a US naval blockade of Iranian ports and thinning Hormuz traffic — with confirmed crude transit through the strait down 62% from pre-conflict levels. That briefing flagged Hormuz traffic as a channel worth watching independent of the equity-market AI story. This week confirms that channel is still live and arguably intensifying: rather than the blockade settling into a stable, priced-in disruption, a direct tanker strike and a second front (the Houthi threat against Saudi shipping, a route separate from Hormuz) both opened this week, which is why oil pushed through $90 rather than merely holding its prior range.

Die Details: The US conducted its ninth consecutive night of strikes on Iranian military targets by Monday, per CENTCOM-sourced reporting, following a weekend in which three American service members were confirmed killed — a casualty count that raises the domestic political stakes of any further escalation. The tanker incident near Hormuz adds to a pattern from earlier in July, when a container ship was attacked and a crew member went missing; Washington continues to dispute Iranian claims of control over the waterway. The Houthi blockade threat against Saudi Arabia is a materially different risk than the Hormuz disruption — it threatens Red Sea/Bab-el-Mandeb-adjacent shipping lanes rather than the Gulf chokepoint, meaning the two threats are not fully substitutable and a resolution of one does not resolve the other. Against this, mediators — reportedly including regional intermediaries — proposed a 10-day ceasefire to revive an interim deal, and a senior Iranian official told Reuters that Tehran had itself secretly submitted a ceasefire proposal while under the intensified strikes, a sign of least one side seeking an off-ramp even as the shooting continues. Oil's net move captures both forces: up sharply on the tanker/blockade risk, but capped by the ceasefire headlines that emerged in parallel — Tuesday's European session saw oil actually ease modestly from its intraday highs on the mediation reports even as US-session data showed a 2% gain.

Einordnung: The market is now pricing a war that neither resolves nor escalates cleanly — each week brings a new tactical development (a tanker, a blockade threat, a ceasefire proposal) without a durable change in the underlying trajectory. That keeps Brent in a wide, headline-driven $85–95 range rather than settling into a stable risk premium, which is harder for equity markets to price efficiently than either a clean resolution or a clean escalation would be. The read-through to this edition's other stories is direct: Monday's equity weakness and the energy sector's outperformance both trace to this story, and the elevated tail on Fed hike odds documented in the Macro section is explicitly linked to energy-driven inflation risk from the same conflict.
