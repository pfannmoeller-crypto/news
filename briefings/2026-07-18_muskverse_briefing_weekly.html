TESLAShareholder revolt16 Jul 2026

Tesla's own retail shareholders spent the week demanding Musk explain three straight quarters of missed robotaxi guidance — and someone spent millions of shares burying the question

Nearly 300 questions have been submitted to Tesla's Q2 2026 earnings Q&A portal ahead of the 22 July call, and Electrek's read of the upvote board is bleak for management. The most-supported substantive question asks flatly: "Tesla has missed short term guidance on robotaxi 3 earnings reports in a row, from 50% coverage of USA by end of 2025 to most recently 7 new cities in 1H26. What is keeping Tesla back?" Variants of the same question — "Why has growth of robotaxi vehicles stalled?", "What are the new milestones considering the prior milestones have missed?" — recur throughout the top 20.

The mechanics are the tell. Tesla runs the Q&A through Say Technologies, where votes are weighted by shares held. The top two questions are promotional softballs about Optimus Gen 3 ramp and robotaxi constraints — each carrying 4.8 million shares, against fewer than 975,000 for the third-place question. A single very large holder appears to have moved late in the week to push the friendly questions to the top of the queue.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Tesla's retail base has historically used the Say portal to lob Musk friendly prompts about Optimus and Mars-scale ambition. The shift this quarter is that the same base is now using it as an accountability instrument. The trigger sequence: at the Q3 2025 call Tesla guided to robotaxi service covering "half the US population by end of 2025"; that slipped. Three consecutive guidance misses on the same metric is what converted enthusiasm into interrogation.

Die Details. Beyond robotaxi, two question clusters matter. First, Hardware 3 (HW3): one of the sharpest questions notes "Elon confirmed HW3 cannot achieve Unsupervised FSD" — Full Self-Driving — and asks what the plan is for owners who paid, whether free hardware upgrades, transfers or refunds, on what timeline, and what liability Tesla has accrued. Tesla sold HW3 cars for years while stating they contained everything needed for full autonomy; on the Q1 2026 call it floated building dedicated micro-factories purely to retrofit those millions of vehicles, a plan few observers believe will materialise. Second, merger anxiety: one question asks Musk to "commit to achieving at least half of the goals outlined in your 2025 compensation plan before considering any offers to acquire or merge Tesla." Another asks about the plan to roll out "Digital Optimus with SpaceXAI," Tesla's capital contribution and its revenue share. The November 2025 pay package ties Musk's payout to an $8.5 trillion market cap plus milestones including 1 million robotaxis and 1 million Optimus units.

Einordnung. The Digital Optimus question is the one with portfolio-wide consequence. Musk previously argued Tesla had the best AI team in the world and did not need xAI to build Optimus's brain. That position has reversed: the robot's intelligence layer now runs through SpaceXAI, an entity Tesla shareholders do not own and in which Musk holds a materially larger economic stake. If the AI value of the humanoid programme accrues to SPCX while the manufacturing cost and warranty exposure sit at TSLA, Tesla holders are financing an option they do not hold. That is the structural reason the merger question reads less as strategy and more as a plea. It also explains why TSLA and SPCX are increasingly traded as a pair rather than as independents.

(2) Any numerical disclosure of HW3 retrofit accrual; Tesla has never quantified it. (3) Whether management gives a hard robotaxi city count for 2H 2026 or retreats further into "preparations underway" language. (4) Any change to the Digital Optimus commercial terms between Tesla and SpaceXAI. (5) Automotive gross margin ex-credits — the number that still funds everything else.

Waymo's approach — fewer cities, deeper coverage, heavier sensor stack — currently produces more rides per city. Tesla's bet is that a camera-only stack generalises faster and therefore wins on cost per mile once it works at all. The 175-vehicle ramp is the first datapoint in a year that supports the bet. SPACEXAIPrivacy incident14 – 16 Jul 2026

Grok Build was uploading entire code repositories — 5.1 GB for a 192 KB task — and SpaceXAI's answer was to open-source the tool and delete everything

The week's biggest own goal. Security researchers at Cereblab found that SpaceXAI's Grok Build command-line coding assistant was packaging and transferring users' complete codebases to Google Cloud — including files it had been explicitly instructed not to open. In one measured test it uploaded 5.1 gigabytes when the task required 192 kilobytes: roughly 26,000 times more data than necessary. The payloads included full Git histories, private source code, and files containing credentials and API keys.

SpaceXAI's response, over 14–16 July, was unusually maximalist. Data retention is now off by default for all users rather than only enterprise plans; the company said "all user data that was uploaded to SpaceXAI before now will be completely and utterly deleted," with Musk adding "Zero anything whatsoever will remain"; and the CLI tool itself is being open-sourced so the transfer behaviour can be independently audited. SpaceXAI's own statement maintains that zero data retention was respected whenever a user had explicitly disabled upload, and frames the excess collection as an early-beta artefact.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Grok Build is SpaceXAI's agentic coding product, the direct output of the Cursor acquisition and the commercial centrepiece of the Grok 4.5 launch on 8 July. Its entire pitch is enterprise coding: Musk positioned 4.5 as a workhorse for engineering teams rather than a consumer chatbot, priced at roughly 60% below Claude Opus 4.8 precisely to win that segment. A data-handling failure in the CLI is therefore not a peripheral bug — it lands on the exact trust dimension the product was built to compete on. Enterprise buyers evaluating coding agents run security review before they run benchmarks.

Die Details. The technical specifics are what make this severe rather than embarrassing. First, the tool ignored explicit exclusion instructions — meaning users who had configured it correctly were still exposed. Second, the uploaded payloads included `.git` histories, which contain deleted secrets that developers believe are gone. Third, the destination was third-party cloud storage, adding a processor to the chain that customers had not evaluated. Fourth, the magnitude — 26,000x over-collection — indicates the CLI was not doing selective context assembly at all but bulk-shipping the working tree. SpaceXAI's remediation has three parts: default-off retention for every tier, a stated global deletion of previously uploaded code, and open-sourcing the client so the packaging logic is inspectable. Open-sourcing is the substantive concession; the deletion promise is unverifiable by outsiders, and the "we always respected explicit opt-out" defence is narrower than the complaint, which was about implicit over-collection.

Einordnung. Read this alongside the Bloomberg reporting in the next card and a pattern emerges: an AI organisation moving fast enough to ship a frontier model in July but not mature enough to have run a data-flow review on its flagship enterprise client. That is a governance signal for SPCX holders, because SpaceXAI is now inside a listed company with public-market disclosure obligations and a defence-adjacent customer base. Grok Build's addressable market — regulated enterprises, financial institutions, anyone with source-code IP — is precisely the population that treats an incident like this as disqualifying for at least one procurement cycle. The competitive timing is brutal: it lands while Grok 4.5 is still blocked in the EU and while Anthropic and OpenAI are actively selling against exactly this risk.

Watch-Items. (1) Whether the open-sourced client confirms or contradicts SpaceXAI's account of the transfer logic — independent audits will land within days. (2) Any EU data-protection authority interest, which would compound the existing EU availability problem. (3) Enterprise churn signals: named logos publicly dropping Grok Build. (4) Whether the deletion claim is backed by any third-party attestation. (5) Whether this delays the EU launch further.

Confidence: very high — multiple outlets plus company confirmation

SPACEXLaunch abort16 – 17 Jul 2026

Starship Flight 13 aborted at T-0 when four Raptor 3 engines failed to ignite — two are being replaced, and NASA's Artemis calendar just got tighter

The countdown reached zero on 16 July and the vehicle did not leave the pad. Four Raptor 3 engines on the Super Heavy booster failed to ignite within the required window, triggering an automatic abort. Musk said on X that engineers will replace two of the booster's Raptors before the next attempt, and that "most probable launch timing is early next week." SpaceX is now targeting 20 July at 22:45 UTC — 17:45 local at Starbase in South Texas.

Flight 13 is the second Starship mission of 2026 and, more importantly, carries the in-space Raptor relight demonstration. That relight is the structural prerequisite for every subsequent milestone NASA requires before Artemis IV can attempt a crewed lunar landing: without a demonstrated in-space restart, SpaceX cannot progress to orbital operations, refuelling or the lunar transfer profile. With NASA's crewed-landing target at early 2028, a four-day slip is small in isolation and painful in aggregate.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte. Flight 13 has been in motion for weeks: Booster 20 completed a static fire on 10 July, which moved the target forward from no-earlier-than 31 July to 16 July. That acceleration is now partly undone. The flight is the first to carry a real operational payload — 20 Starlink V3 satellites — rather than mass simulators, which makes it the transition point from test programme to revenue vehicle. Raptor 3 is the current-generation engine, simplified relative to Raptor 2 with integrated plumbing and no external heat shielding, and it is precisely the kind of hardware where ignition-sequence margins get tested in anger.

Die Details. The failure mode reported is an ignition gap: four engines did not light within the sequence window, and the flight computer aborted before commit. That is the system working — an abort at T-0 is vastly preferable to a partial-thrust liftoff. Musk's diagnosis that only two engines need replacing implies the other two were sequencing issues rather than hardware faults. A four-day turnaround for two booster engine swaps at Starbase is fast and is itself a data point about SpaceX's pad operations maturity. The mission profile beyond ascent: deploy 20 Starlink V3 satellites which will extend solar arrays and antennas and attempt to connect with ground stations in South Africa and with the wider Starlink constellation via high-capacity optical laser links — a first — plus the in-space Raptor relight.

Einordnung. Two consequences. For NASA, the Artemis dependency chain is unusually brittle: relight enables orbital operations, which enable the propellant-transfer demonstration, which enables the Human Landing System profile, all of which must complete before an early-2028 crewed landing. Every slip compresses a calendar that has no reserve. For SPCX shareholders, Flight 13 is the first launch where a scrub has a direct revenue consequence rather than just a schedule one — each full V3 batch on Starship adds roughly 60 Tbps of network capacity, and Starlink is the segment that funds the AI losses described elsewhere in this briefing. The week the Muskverse ran out of things that could be fixed by announcing something

1. Four entities missed in four different ways, and the pattern is the same. Starship aborted because engines did not light. Grok Build shipped without a data-flow review. Optimus did not appear. Robotaxi missed guidance a third time. None of these are demand problems and none are capital problems — every one is an execution problem in an organisation that is simultaneously running a launch programme, a frontier AI lab, a car company, a social network, a political party and two appeals. The scarce resource across the whole portfolio is not money. It is attention, and it is now visibly rationed.

2. The Grok Build incident is worse than its coverage. A coding agent that ignores exclusion rules and ships 5.1 GB — including .git histories with deleted secrets — for a 192 KB task is not a bug, it is an absent design step. And it happened in the one product line SpaceXAI built specifically to compete on enterprise trust at a 60% discount to Anthropic. Open-sourcing the client is genuinely the correct response and deserves credit; the unverifiable global-deletion promise does not. For practical purposes: Grok 4.5 is a reasonable cost option for high-volume, low-sensitivity generation, and is not a candidate for anything touching proprietary code or EU personal data until independent audits of the open-sourced client land.

3. Tesla shareholders have worked out the structural problem before the sell-side has. The most important question in that portal is not about robotaxi cities. It is the one asking about Tesla's capital contribution and revenue share on "Digital Optimus with SpaceXAI." Tesla builds the body and carries the liability; SpaceXAI owns the brain and captures the AI multiple; Musk owns more of SpaceXAI. If that arrangement stands unamended, TSLA is financing an option it does not hold. That is the disclosure to watch on 22 July, ahead of gross margin.

4. Starlink V3 is the most undervalued story in the portfolio. Ten times the downlink and roughly 24 times the uplink per satellite, ~60 Tbps added per full Starship batch, and a direct-to-device architecture advancing in parallel. This is the business that turns SpaceX's $18.67B revenue base into something that can absorb a $5.1B AI research bill. The IPO was narrated as rockets-plus-AI; the actual structure is a satellite ISP subsidising a fourth-place model lab. That is a better business than it sounds and a worse story than it was sold as, which is roughly why SPCX sits 45% below its June high.

5. The two ventures nobody watches are the two hitting their dates. Neuralink went from 21 to 26 patients and has a credible, falsifiable 2026 slate. The Boring Company carried 4 million passengers and just won a unanimous five-year renewal. Both have bounded scope, patient counterparties and no quarterly narrative to feed. It is worth holding that against the assumption that Musk's personal attention is the ingredient that makes these ventures work — the evidence this week points the other way.

6. What would change the read. A clean Flight 13 on 20 July with a successful in-space Raptor relight and 20 healthy V3 satellites would reset the SpaceX narrative in a single evening. A 22 July call where Tesla answers the HW3 question, discloses the SpaceXAI/Optimus terms and gives a hard robotaxi city count would do the same for TSLA. Both are available. Neither is likely, and the base case is that the softballs get answered and the launch slips again — which is precisely why the asymmetry favours watching rather than acting this week.

The Muskverse has always run on the gap between what was promised and what shipped, financed by the belief that the gap eventually closes. This week the gap was measured four separate times by four different constituencies — a flight computer, a security researcher, a shareholder portal and a federal judge — and none of them were persuaded. That is a new kind of week.
