Markets & Macro · Weekly

The AI trade cracked. Chips fell into a bear market, a Chinese model triggered a second DeepSeek shock, Apple took back the crown from Nvidia — and the soft inflation data barely mattered.

This was the week the market stopped trading rates and started trading the AI thesis. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index fell roughly 11% — its worst week since March 2025 — and closed more than 20% below its late-June high, confirming a bear market in the sector that had risen 105% since March. Two catalysts did the damage: TSMC's record quarter came bundled with a capex raise to $60–64B and a further $100B US commitment (spend now, margins later), and on Thursday night Beijing's Moonshot AI released Kimi K3, a 2.8-trillion-parameter open-weight model priced at $15 per million output tokens against Fable 5's $50. Friday closed with the S&P 500 at 7,457.69 (−1.6% on the week), the Nasdaq Composite at 25,520.24 (−2.9%), the Dow at 52,146.42 (−0.9%), the Nikkei down 4.03%, and Apple back above Nvidia as the world's most valuable company at $4.88T. Meanwhile the macro data was unambiguously friendly — June PPI −0.3%, July hike odds down to ~4%, consumer sentiment at a five-month high — and equities sold off anyway. Oil added ~12% on the week as US–Iran strikes cut confirmed Hormuz crude transit 62%.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Last week's briefing left the S&P at 7,548.20 — a fresh high built on the June CPI's −0.4% monthly print — and explicitly flagged the fragility: the rally was "narrow and tech-led, not the broad cyclical/small-cap rotation," meaning it was "borrowing against an energy channel that is already reversing." It was the numerator: investors reassessing whether the AI capital-expenditure supercycle converts into the earnings that chip multiples assume. Die Details: The Friday closes were S&P 7,457.69 (−1.01%), Nasdaq Composite 25,520.24 (−1.4%), Dow 52,146.42 (−406.55 pts, −0.77%). Weekly: −1.6% / −2.9% / −0.9%. Einordnung: The important structural fact is what did not happen. The macro news this week was uniformly good for equities — PPI −0.3% (steepest since April 2020), July hike odds down to ~4%, September odds down from 59% to 41.5%, consumer sentiment at a five-month high — and the index fell anyway. In a discount-rate-driven market, that combination produces a melt-up. It produced a sell-off instead. That is the definition of a regime change: the marginal dollar has stopped asking "what is the rate?" and started asking "what is the return on the $500B-plus of annual AI capex?" Two corollaries follow. First, the September Fed meeting matters less to equity direction than it did a week ago — the rates channel is no longer the binding constraint. Second, breadth becomes the thing to watch: if the money leaving semis rotates into Apple, staples, energy and Europe rather than leaving equities altogether, this is a rotation and the index level holds. Friday's Apple/Nvidia crossover and Europe's positive week are early evidence for rotation rather than de-risking.

Whether S&P 7,400 holds as the first round-number level below Friday's close. The 28–29 July FOMC, which is now near-certain to hold and therefore mostly a communications event. Big Tech Q2 reports landing over the next fortnight — those, not the Fed, are where the AI-capex question gets adjudicated. Chips fall into a bear market: SOX −11% on the week, worst since March 2025, more than 20% below its June high

The single biggest market story of the week. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) dropped roughly 11% over five sessions — its worst week since March 2025 — and is now more than 20% below its late-June record, the technical threshold that confirms a bear market. From March to late June the index had risen 105% on memory-chip and AI-accelerator demand; roughly a fifth of that has now been given back in three weeks. Bloomberg framed it as a trillion-dollar wipeout in Wall Street's most crowded trade. The catalysts were cumulative rather than singular: Broadcom guided Q3 AI-chip revenue to $16B against ~$17.2B expected, Meta announced Meta Compute — reselling surplus AI training and inference capacity to enterprises, which rewrites the supply side of the equation — and then TSMC's capex raise and Kimi K3 landed in the same 48 hours.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: The semiconductor complex has been the engine of the 2026 equity market. Two weeks ago Samsung's Q2 (+1,810% y/y operating profit) had, in this briefing's own words, "buried the HBM-slowdown thesis." Last week SK Hynix's soft US IPO revived a little doubt, and the Monday 13 July session saw chips sell off on AI-capex worries — but the CPI overwhelmed it. The thesis holding the sector up was simple and until recently unfalsifiable: hyperscaler capex is contractually committed, demand exceeds supply, therefore chip revenue is visible for eight-plus quarters. Every element of that thesis got a counter-datapoint in the last three weeks.

Die Details: Reported weekly declines vary by measurement window — sources give the SOX at roughly −8.5% through Thursday and ~−11% including Friday; both agree it is the worst week since either the March 2025 or April 2025 tariff episodes. Peak-to-trough the index is more than 20% off its late-June record. The demand-side catalyst was Broadcom's Q3 AI revenue guide of $16B versus ~$17.2B consensus — the first time a major AI-exposed supplier guided below the trend line rather than above it. The supply-side catalyst was Meta Compute: if the largest buyers of GPUs start reselling spare capacity into the merchant cloud market, the effective supply of AI compute rises without a single additional wafer, which compresses both rental prices and the urgency of incremental orders. The cost-side catalyst was TSMC's capex raise from $52–56B to $60–64B, which reads as "the customers keep ordering" but also as "gross margin dilution and a heavier depreciation base." The final blow was Kimi K3 — the argument that frontier capability now costs a third as much. On Friday the VanEck Semiconductor ETF fell more than 4%, SoftBank (an OpenAI proxy) fell 9.0% in Tokyo, and the Nikkei's 4.03% drop was chip-led. Leveraged semiconductor ETFs saw forced deleveraging.

Einordnung: Distinguish carefully between three claims. (1) "AI demand is falling" — no evidence for this; TSMC just raised full-year revenue guidance to over 40% growth. (2) "AI capital intensity is rising faster than AI revenue" — this is what Broadcom's guide and TSMC's capex raise actually show, and it is a margin story, not a demand story. (3) "The cost of frontier capability is collapsing" — this is the Kimi K3 argument, and it is the genuinely dangerous one for the sector, because it implies the same output can be bought with dramatically less silicon. The market spent this week repricing (2) and (3) simultaneously. Historically, capital-intensity scares in semis have produced 20–30% drawdowns that resolved upward when the earnings arrived; the difference this cycle is that the equity was priced for the resolution in advance. A 105% five-month rally leaves no cushion for a repricing of the terminal margin.

Watch-Items: Nvidia's next report as the definitive datapoint on whether order books are actually softening. Whether Meta Compute publishes pricing — cheap resale capacity would be the clearest confirmation of the supply-glut thesis. Memory specifically (Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron), which has the most operating leverage in both directions and where at least one commentator is already calling the sell-off a buying opportunity. The Kimi K3 open weights release scheduled for 27 July, which will let independent parties verify the efficiency claims. And whether the SOX's 20%-below-high line holds on a weekly close.

China AI · catalyst16–17 Jul

The second DeepSeek shock: Moonshot's Kimi K3 claims Fable-5-class performance at a third the price

Beijing-based Moonshot AI unveiled Kimi K3 on Thursday 16 July — at 2.8 trillion parameters, the largest open-weight model ever released. Moonshot's benchmarks put it competitive with Anthropic's Fable 5 and "substantially outperforming" Opus 4.8 and GPT-5.6 Sol; one independent Arena.AI leaderboard placed it first outright. The number that moved markets was the price: $15 per million output tokens versus $50 for Fable 5. Most observers — including Anthropic's Dario Amodei — had expected a Chinese lab to reach this level roughly six months later; Elon Musk had guessed Q1 2027. On Friday TSMC fell 7% despite a 77% jump in quarterly operating profit, SoftBank fell 9%, Nvidia fell 1.2% and briefly lost the market-cap crown, and Meta fell over 2.4%. Full model weights are due for public release on 27 July.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: This is the third Chinese frontier-model shock in eighteen months and the second to move Western equity markets. DeepSeek's January 2025 release triggered the original repricing; DeepSeek V4 in April 2026 offered frontier performance at $0.87 per million output tokens but did not shock markets, partly because it was expected. z.ai's GLM-5.2 followed in mid-June, released days after US officials briefly cut off access to Anthropic's Fable and Mythos models for some non-US users — z.ai's launch post explicitly framed itself as a response ("Frontier intelligence should not belong to only a few people"). Moonshot itself was already known to Silicon Valley: its K2.5 and K2.6 models gained traction on coding tasks, and in March the US coding-assistant maker Cursor admitted its Composer 2 agent was built on top of Kimi 2.5. Moonshot is backed by Alibaba, Tencent, Meituan and HSG (formerly Sequoia China) and is reportedly weighing a Hong Kong IPO. The company is named after Pink Floyd's The Dark Side of the Moon, founder Yang Zhilin's favourite album.

Die Details: K3 is 2.8T parameters, open-weight, and priced at $15/M output tokens. Moonshot's own benchmarks rank it consistently in the global top three; the Arena.AI independent leaderboard put it first. The equity reaction was immediate and global: TSMC −7% on Friday (on a day it had just reported +77% operating profit), SoftBank −9.0% in Tokyo, z.ai — a listed Chinese competitor — down almost 30% in Hong Kong, Nvidia −1.2%, Meta −2.4%, Nasdaq-100 −1.0% by 2pm ET. The structural cost story behind Chinese model pricing has several independent legs: cheaper Chinese electricity and faster data-centre buildout versus US permitting resistance; willingness to sacrifice margin for standard-setting; open-source release, which lets third-party hosts run the model for the marginal cost of GPUs and energy without amortising R&D; and — ironically — US export controls, which forced Chinese labs to extract more performance per unit of compute. As analyst George Chen put it, for the price of one Nvidia chip a Chinese lab can buy ten domestic Huawei chips. Meituan's LongCat 2.0, a 1.6T-parameter model, was trained entirely on Chinese-made semiconductors. As of this week, all five most-used models on the OpenRouter marketplace are Chinese. On 17 July at the World AI Conference in Shanghai, Xi Jinping committed China to continued open-source release and criticised "overstretching the concept of national security in the field of AI."

Einordnung: The investable claim is not "China is winning AI." It is narrower and more corrosive: the price of a unit of frontier intelligence is falling faster than the cost of the infrastructure that produces it. That squeezes the middle of the value chain — model providers charging premium API prices — and it questions whether the marginal GPU purchase earns its cost of capital. It does not obviously hurt the application layer or the distribution layer, which is precisely why capital rotated into Apple on the same day. The rebuttal, which desks will make next week, is that Moonshot's benchmarks are self-reported, that inference at scale is not the same as benchmark performance, and that open weights invite exactly the kind of scrutiny that has deflated previous claims — the 27 July weights release is the falsification test. Note also the second-order regulatory read: a Chinese open-weight model at genuine frontier capability makes US export controls look less effective and raises the probability of a policy response, which is its own tail risk for the chip names.

Watch-Items: The 27 July open-weights release and the first wave of independent benchmarking. Whether US labs cut API prices in response — that would be the clearest confirmation the pricing pressure is real. Enterprise adoption signals: DoorDash's CTO already reports pushing "lower-level work" to Kimi for "better quality, cheaper cost." Washington's response — export-control tightening or model-release restrictions. And Moonshot's rumoured Hong Kong IPO, which would put a public valuation on the thesis.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Nvidia took the top spot from Apple in 2025 and held it for roughly a year — the entire period during which "AI infrastructure" was the market's organising thesis. Apple spent that year as the designated laggard of the Magnificent Seven, criticised for a late and underwhelming AI product story and for having no data-centre exposure. That criticism was correct on the technology and, this week, wrong on the stock. The reversal is not an Apple story; it is a repricing of what part of the AI stack captures the economics.

Die Details: Apple reached $4.88T on Friday against Nvidia's $4.86T, a gap of roughly $20B — under half a percent, and, as several outlets noted, a lead narrow enough to vanish in after-hours trade. Apple last held the title in April 2025. Nvidia fell 1.2% on Friday on the Kimi K3 news, having already been dragged by the week's broader semiconductor rout. Contextually, the PHLX Semiconductor index is down almost 19% from its all-time high and chip stocks were heading for their worst weekly performance in over a year. The framing several analysts used: investors began pricing the thesis that companies which monetise AI at scale through distribution, services and installed-base consumer relationships may generate more durable earnings than the infrastructure layer alone. Apple's installed base and services annuity are the archetype of that argument; so, in a different way, is Alphabet — which is the same week Buffett disclosed he personally drove Berkshire's $31B Alphabet position.

Einordnung: Treat the crown itself as noise and the direction as signal. What matters is that the market is now willing to pay up for the layer furthest from the capex — the opposite of the trade that worked for eighteen months. Historically these leadership handoffs at the very top of the market-cap table are meaningful but slow: they mark the point where the prior narrative stops attracting incremental capital, not the point where it stops working. Two cautions. First, Apple's own AI position is not obviously strong on the merits — this is a rotation into a business model, not a verdict on product. Second, if the AI-capex scare turns out to be a margin timing issue rather than a demand issue, the infrastructure names have far more upside beta on the way back. The crossover is best read as evidence for the rotation-not-de-risking interpretation of the week: money left semis and stayed in equities.

Watch-Items: Whether Apple holds the lead through next week or hands it straight back — a single Nvidia bounce reverses it. Apple's approach to $5T as a psychological marker. Big Tech Q2 earnings, where the "monetisation layer" thesis gets tested against actual AI-attributable revenue. And whether the rotation broadens beyond Apple into other distribution-layer names (Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft) or stays a single-stock crowding trade.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte: Last week's edition observed that "Europe's rally has quietly become less German and more broad" — the DAX churning in a range as the continent's purest oil-sensitivity trade while the STOXX 600 hugged its record on financials, energy majors and defensives. This week that observation became the dominant fact. Europe's index composition, long treated as a structural handicap, turned into a structural hedge: the STOXX 600 has minimal semiconductor weight relative to the S&P 500 (ASML is the notable exception), meaningful energy weight that benefits directly from Brent's ~12% weekly gain, and heavy financials exposure that benefits from the same higher-for-longer rate environment producing record trading revenue at the US banks.

Die Details: The STOXX 600 clocked its best week in over a month specifically because the rally broadened — Reuters' framing was rotation into a wider set of sectors rather than a narrow leadership bid, which is the exact opposite of what happened in the US. The sell-side capitulated upward in the same week: UBS's revised target implies roughly 8% STOXX 600 upside by year-end, with BofA, Deutsche Bank and Kepler Cheuvreux also raising. Germany was the laggard — the DAX closed Friday fractionally below 25,000 (having been indicated around 24,675 intraday, roughly 1% lower), pressured by AI-linked weakness and the Middle East oil impulse feeding into German industrial input costs. On monetary policy, market pricing collapsed to just 23bp of total ECB tightening for the year, and Lagarde has publicly conceded a less aggressive path may suffice — a meaningful dovish shift that supports European multiples independently of the US rate path. Note the reference point: earlier in July the STOXX 600 had printed 652.35 and the DAX an all-time high, so Europe enters the back half of July near records while the US is retracing.

Einordnung: This is the first genuine test of the "European equities as an AI hedge" argument, and Europe passed it. If the AI-capex repricing has further to run, the composition advantage compounds — a market with no hyperscalers and no US-scale semiconductor weight simply has less to give back. That said, be precise about what Europe is long instead: energy (levered to a geopolitical premium that can vanish on a ceasefire headline), financials (levered to rates staying high, which the ECB pricing now argues against), and industrials (levered to a global growth cycle that an AI-capex slowdown would itself damage with a lag). The hedge is real but it is not free. For Germany specifically, sub-25,000 is the level to watch — with Brent above $85 and the ECB less likely to tighten, the DAX's problem is input costs and AI-adjacent exposure, not the discount rate.

Watch-Items: The ECB decision on Thursday 23 July, where the 23bp-for-the-year pricing gets its first reality check. Whether the DAX reclaims 25,000 or extends below it. ASML as Europe's single largest AI-exposure vector — it is the transmission channel for any US chip contagion. European energy majors (BP, Shell, TotalEnergies) as the offsetting bid. And whether the sell-side target raises mark a genuine flow shift or the usual top-tick of a consensus trade.

Second, Japan carries a compounding second exposure: as an energy importer with essentially no domestic crude, the ~12% weekly oil move is a direct terms-of-trade hit on top of the equity story, and it pressures the yen, which in turn complicates the Bank of Japan's path. Watch for the correction to either stabilise on domestic institutional buying — the intraday recovery is a hint — or extend if US semis keep falling.

Watch-Items: Whether 64,000 holds as support on Monday's open, and whether the intraday 62,700 low gets retested. SoftBank specifically as the highest-beta expression of the frontier-model repricing. The yen and the Bank of Japan's response to an oil-driven terms-of-trade shock. Japanese semi-equipment earnings, which report over the coming weeks and will show whether fab order books have actually moved or only sentiment has.
