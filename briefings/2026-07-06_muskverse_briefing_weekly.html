Q2 deliveriesStock −7.5%02 Jul 2026

480,126 deliveries, +25% — and the worst TSLA day in nearly a year

Tesla delivered 480,126 vehicles in Q2 (+25% year over year) against consensus of ~406k — an ~18% beat — on production of 451,758, with Europe sales improving. The energy business deployed 13.5 gigawatt-hours (GWh) of storage vs 9.6 GWh a year earlier. The market's answer: TSLA fell ~7.5%, its worst single day in almost a year. The consensus read is that the beat was already priced in after weeks of channel checks, and that a delivery-pull-forward (buyers front-running expiring incentives and the robotaxi-era repositioning) makes Q3 comparisons harder. Note the whiplash vs Q1: deliveries missed then and the stock held; deliveries smashed now and it sold off — the tape is trading the AI narrative, not the car count. Q2 financials land 22 July.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — This is Tesla's first year-over-year delivery growth after two straight years of decline; sales peaked in 2023 and had been shrinking since. The immediate backdrop matters: in Q1 2026 Tesla built roughly 50,000 more cars than it sold, stacking inventory, and the delivery miss (358,023) was forgiven by a market focused on the AI story. Q2 reversed both dynamics — deliveries exceeded production by ~28,000 units, meaning Tesla drained most of that excess inventory instead of building more. Electrek's read on the demand driver is unglamorous: the war-driven spike in gasoline prices earlier this year triggered a surge in EV (electric vehicle) demand exactly when Tesla was sitting on 50k unsold cars, alongside newly introduced lower-cost Model 3/Y variants and the rollout of FSD (Supervised) in European markets, which helped Europe recover while US sales declined.

Die Details — The mix: Model 3/Y accounted for 442,936 produced and 467,762 delivered; "Other Models" (Model S, X, Cybertruck, Semi) just 8,822 produced and 12,364 delivered — the non-3/Y lineup remains a rounding error at ~2.6% of volume. Consensus stood at 406,024, with the most bullish desks (Goldman Sachs, Barclays) at 418–420k; Tesla beat even those by 60,000+. It is the best Q2 in company history (previous: 466,140 in Q2 2023) but still below the all-time record of 497,099 from Q3 2025, which was inflated by US buyers front-running the 30 September 2025 expiry of the $7,500 federal EV tax credit. Energy storage deployments of 13.5 GWh actually came in slightly below the ~13.8 GWh analyst expectation despite growing 40% — one under-reported soft spot. The competitive gap: BYD delivered 557,090 battery-electric vehicles in Q2 (−8% year over year), so Tesla now trails by ~77,000 units, down from a gap of 220,000+ a year ago — the trajectories crossed directions even if the ranking hasn't flipped yet.

Einordnung — The −7.5% day despite an 18% beat is best read through the valuation: at a ~204x forward price-to-earnings ratio, TSLA discounts essentially none of its value from cars. A gas-price-driven, incentive-flavored volume quarter doesn't move the robotaxi/Optimus thesis, and the pull-forward reading means Q3 faces a hard comparison against both this quarter's momentum and the 497k record from a year ago. There is also a margin question buried in the beat: lower-cost Model 3/Y variants plus European FSD promotion suggest the volume was bought with mix and price — which is exactly what the 22 July income statement will reveal, on top of the guided $25B+ capital expenditure ramp.

Watch-Items — (1) 22 July Q2 earnings: automotive gross margin ex-credits vs the volume beat; (2) whether Q3 orders show a demand air-pocket after the pull-forward; (3) European monthly registrations (UK June already +42% for Tesla) to see if the recovery holds without gas-price tailwind; (4) energy storage re-acceleration after the slight Q2 miss.

Robotaxi / FSD29 Jun – 03 Jul 2026

Robotaxi lands in Miami — first expansion beyond Texas — plus an "FSD v14 Lite" for older hardware

Alongside the delivery print, Tesla launched unsupervised robotaxi service in Miami on 3 July — the first market outside Texas running the fully driverless commercial tier, with a ~20-square-mile geofence and no safety personnel in the vehicle from day one. Musk reiterated the plan to expand the service rapidly through 2026. In parallel, Tesla began rolling out "FSD v14 Lite" (software 2026.20.5.1, from 29 June) — a build that brings v14-generation Full Self-Driving behavior to older Hardware 3 (HW3) vehicles, commercially relevant because it widens the paid-software base beyond new cars. Analyst framing (Cantor Fitzgerald) is now openly "the deliveries don't matter": AI, robotics and chips are cast as the drivers of a "transformational" 2026.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — Miami arrives almost exactly one year after the original Austin pilot (June 2025) and follows the April 2026 launch of fully unsupervised rides in Dallas and Houston. The network now spans five operational territories with three distinct supervision regimes: Miami, Dallas and Houston run fully unsupervised Model Ys; Austin runs a mix of unsupervised cars and vehicles with safety monitors; and the San Francisco Bay Area remains restricted to monitored rides only. (A correction to earlier trade-press framing echoed in this morning's edition: Miami is the fourth unsupervised market overall, not the second — but the first outside Texas.)

Die Details — The Miami geofence covers roughly 20 square miles of the core commercial and transit hubs; Miami International Airport sits inside the zone, but Tesla is not yet legally authorized for terminal pick-ups or drop-offs — a meaningful revenue exclusion in a tourism market. Rides are hailed via the standalone Robotaxi app (iOS/Android). First-ride footage (via @SawyerMerritt and early riders on X) confirms empty front seats in regular traffic, and Florida's subtropical rain is widely flagged as the hardest weather test yet for Tesla's camera-only (no lidar/radar) approach. On the software side: FSD v14 Lite is a supervised, Level 2 driver-assistance build for HW3 cars — Musk has conceded HW3 hardware will never achieve unsupervised operation, so "Lite" is about keeping ~millions of older cars inside the paid-FSD funnel, not about extending the robotaxi fleet; Tesla has confirmed an international v14 Lite release will follow. Expansion mechanics beyond Miami: a Phoenix, Arizona plan is already filed, Tesla holds a permit application for 5,000 ride-hailing vehicles in Nevada, and steering-wheel-less Cybercabs began public-road testing in Austin in late June. Tesla has said truly large-scale expansion waits for FSD v15 — touted at ~10x the parameter count of current builds, expected late 2026/early 2027.

Einordnung — The strategically important detail is the supervision gradient: Tesla is now confident enough to launch new markets unsupervised on day one (Miami) while its oldest non-Texas market (Bay Area) still requires monitors — regulatory posture, not technical capability, is increasingly the binding constraint. For the TSLA thesis, Miami is the first datapoint on whether the Texas playbook transfers to a dense, wet, tourist-heavy market; airport exclusion and weather performance are the two variables that will show up in utilization data first. The v14-Lite move is quietly defensive — it manages the legal and reputational exposure from HW3 owners who paid for "Full Self-Driving" that their hardware cannot deliver unsupervised.

Watch-Items — (1) Miami airport authorization and any rain-related incident reports; (2) Phoenix/Nevada launches and the 5,000-vehicle permit; (3) Cybercab testing milestones in Austin; (4) FSD v15 timing — it gates both scale-up and, per Musk, material robotaxi revenue in 2027.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — SPCX only qualifies this fast because Nasdaq wrote new rules for it: the exchange introduced "fast track" eligibility criteria for mega-IPOs, under which a new listing can join the Nasdaq-100 on its 15th trading day (the S&P 500, by contrast, requires a year of trading plus profitability tests). Nasdaq announced the inclusion on 26 June, effective before market open on 7 July. There was early speculation the position might be phased in given its size; the index methodology settles it — additions happen as a single event, no provision for piecewise entry.

Die Details — The two Invesco index vehicles alone (QQQ and its cheaper twin QQQM) manage a combined ~$570B; the total pool of Nasdaq-100-tracking assets exceeds $800B globally. JPMorgan's estimate of ~$4.3B of forced buying refers to that Nasdaq-100 tracking pool; TradingKey cites a broader figure of up to ~$27B if subsequent Russell and other index-family inclusions are counted — treat the larger number as an upper bound with fuzzy timing. Despite a ~$2.3T market capitalization comparable to Amazon (weighted ~4%), SPCX enters at only ~1% index weight, because Nasdaq weights on free-float market cap and only 3–5% of SpaceX shares trade publicly. That weight mechanically rises as lock-ups expire and float grows — meaning passive funds will be forced buyers again at each float expansion, but active sellers gain supply at the same moments. The historical pattern for high-profile inclusions (Coindesk, Motley Fool both flag it) is a post-inclusion fade: forced buying pulls demand forward and the stock often underperforms in the following months.

Einordnung — For the portfolio read, the inclusion is plumbing, not verdict: nothing about Starship cadence, Starlink subscriber economics or Grok changed this week, yet billions must transact. The more consequential precedent is the fast-track rule itself — Motley Fool notes it paves the way for Anthropic and OpenAI to enter the index within weeks of their eventual IPOs (2026–27 per current chatter). That means the "Muskverse vs OpenAI" rivalry is on course to be fought inside the same index, with the same passive flows owning both sides. Also worth logging: every QQQ holder — tens of millions of retail accounts — becomes an indirect SpaceX/xAI/X owner on 7 July, which subtly changes the political economy of regulating any of those businesses.

Watch-Items — (1) 7 July close vs the pre-inclusion run-up (fade test); (2) first lock-up expiry date and size — the float mechanics cut both ways; (3) S&P 500 eligibility clock (mid-2027 at the earliest); (4) sell-side initiation wave and where consensus valuation lands vs the $1.75T IPO mark.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — "Trump Accounts" were enacted in last year's Republican tax bill: every US child born between 1 January 2025 and end-2028 is eligible for $1,000 in federal seed money in a tax-advantaged investment account. More than 6 million children had enrolled before the 4 July launch. The program has become a corporate-philanthropy magnet: Michael and Susan Dell donated $6.25B in cash, and BlackRock and Bank of America agreed to match employee donations. The SpaceX angle emerged 29 June via Semafor's Eleanor Mueller and Liz Hoffman; the report knocked SPCX slightly in overnight trading — the market's first instinct was dilution/entanglement, not halo.

Die Details — Shotwell's gift: one Class A share per account for 2M+ children, worth ~$165 per share on Monday, totaling ~$320M — about one-fifth of the Class A shares she owns or has options on (per her SEC Form 3; Class A carries one vote vs the high-vote Class B that she, Musk and other executives also hold, so the donation does not move control). The legal wrinkle is real: the statute requires account investments to track indexes — the design goal was protecting kids from single-stock swings — and Treasury has so far approved four exchange-traded funds (ETFs), at least two of which hold every publicly traded US company. Treasury said last week that direct stock donations "could go into the accounts" but published no mechanism, so how 2 million single-share SPCX positions square with the index-only rule is genuinely unresolved (Yahoo's headline: "the rules don't allow it"). Eligibility criteria for which children receive Shotwell's shares are also not yet defined. Trump, asked Monday whether he'd solicited other CEOs: "I speak to everybody… I'm like a cheerleader for geniuses."

Einordnung — The story upgraded materially during the window: from single-source talks to an on-record, SEC-filing-backed personal donation by the No. 2 of the listed company — while the company-level donation (which would be the real precedent: corporate treasury shares flowing into a presidential program) is still only at the talks stage. Second-order effects: (1) it seeds a generation of households as SPCX shareholders with a Republican-branded program as custodian; (2) it deepens the alignment-risk noted in the short read — SpaceX's regulatory counterparties (FAA licensing, FCC spectrum, NASA and Department of War contracts) all report to the administration the company is now visibly funding a signature program of; (3) the timing, three days before forced index buying, is at minimum excellent PR sequencing. Note the same week's other data point: Musk himself remained conspicuously absent from the announcement — this ran through Shotwell, the system's most trusted operator.

Colossus backlash30 Jun 2026

SpaceX halves Starlink prices in Memphis to quell Colossus data-center backlash

SpaceX is offering half-price Starlink plans to Memphis residents (normally $55–130/month, cut to ~$27.50–65) as opposition to the Colossus data centers — the xAI supercomputer complex in Memphis and neighboring Mississippi that trains the Grok models — hardens into community protest and Clean Air Act litigation over pollution, centered on the gas turbines powering the sites. The company also committed to resume building a wastewater-recycling plant that would supply the data centers and local utilities. This is the operational cost of the merged structure becoming visible: the environmental liabilities of xAI's compute build-out now sit inside the same listed entity as Starlink, and the mitigation is being paid for in Starlink revenue. Worth watching as Colossus 2 scales for Grok 5 training.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — Colossus 1 went up in South Memphis in 2024–25 at record speed, powered in part by on-site methane gas turbines rather than waiting for grid interconnection; the build-out has since expanded to Colossus 2 in Southaven, Mississippi, just across the state line. The neighborhoods around the Memphis site — historically Black, already carrying a heavy industrial pollution burden — became the center of organized opposition through 2025. In April, the NAACP (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People) and its Mississippi State Conference sued xAI under the Clean Air Act, alleging the company operated 27 unpermitted methane gas turbines at Colossus 2; Earthjustice and the Southern Environmental Law Center are litigating parallel claims that the turbines constitute an illegal unlicensed power plant, citing repeated breaches of permit limits for nitrogen oxides (NOx) and particulate matter.

Die Details — The Starlink offer: standard $55–130/month residential plans cut ~50% to roughly $27.50–65 for eligible addresses in defined communities around the sites, with upfront hardware costs waived; eligibility is checked by address, referrals to neighbors are enabled, and the discount lapses if the customer moves away — i.e., it is precisely targeted at the protest-relevant zip codes (boosters call it a "data center dividend"). SpaceX simultaneously committed to restart construction of the recycled-wastewater plant, halted since late 2025, which would cool the data centers and supply local utilities. On the other side of the docket, the federal government has intervened in the NAACP case — the Justice Department argues that shutting the generators down would threaten national security, given xAI's Department of War and government AI contracts. NAACP environmental-justice director Abre Connor's framing: "Our homes, churches, and playgrounds will not be sacrifice zones for Big Tech's convenience." Community reception of the discount is mixed at best — one widely shared Reddit line called it "a discount asking people not to stop the operation of illegal natural gas turbines."

Einordnung — Structurally, this is the first clear case where the SPCX merger makes one division pay for another's externalities: Starlink margin is being spent to buy social license for xAI's compute. The DOJ national-security intervention is the more consequential development — it converts a local pollution suit into a test of whether "frontier AI training" can shield energy-permitting violations, a precedent every hyperscaler is watching. For the Grok roadmap the linkage is direct: Grok 5 training capacity at Colossus 2 depends on exactly the turbines under challenge. A court-ordered shutdown or retrofit is the tail risk; a settlement with permitting-plus-community-fund terms is the likelier path.

Watch-Items — (1) rulings on the DOJ's intervention/dismissal motion in the NAACP case; (2) turbine permitting decisions in Shelby County (TN) and DeSoto County (MS); (3) wastewater plant construction actually restarting; (4) whether the half-price model gets replicated at other xAI/data-center sites — that would signal a standing playbook.

DOGE officially ended on 4 July — agencies are rehiring, and Musk says he "wouldn't do it again"

The Department of Government Efficiency formally expired on 4 July 2026, the sunset written into the January 2025 executive order. The obituaries are unflattering: the organization terminated without a final report (the White House budget office confirmed none is planned); agencies gutted under the 2025 cuts are actively rehiring; and its claimed $215B in savings — about a tenth of the original $2T target — remains only ~30% documented by published receipts. Musk's own retrospective: DOGE was "somewhat successful," but he wouldn't do it again. This closes the DOGE chapter of this briefing for good — residual relevance is only the Musk–Trump relationship, which has migrated to the SpaceX/Trump-Accounts track above.

Nextgov · closeout audit E&E/Politico · post-mortem NewsNation · Musk retrospective
Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The "US DOGE Service Temporary Organization" was created by executive order on 20 January 2025 with a hard-coded termination date of 4 July 2026 — pitched by Trump as a gift for the US semiquincentennial. The original ambition, announced in November 2024 with Musk and Vivek Ramaswamy as co-leads, was $2 trillion in savings; Ramaswamy left within months, Musk exited in spring 2025 after the spectacular falling-out with Trump, and the organization spent its final year as a diminished technocratic remnant embedded in the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) orbit.

Die Details — The final claimed tally on doge.gov: $215B saved, or "$1,335.40 per taxpayer," from contract/lease cancellations, grant terminations, regulatory changes, asset sales and workforce reductions. Nextgov's closeout review found the published receipts cover only ~30% of that claim, with wildly uneven documentation — some entries are specific (a $3.9B Air Force contract-ceiling reduction; an $877M canceled Texas health-department epidemiology grant; a $1.2M CDC Atlanta lease), while others (notably USAID items) state only that "details are unavailable for legal reasons." OMB Director Russell Vought confirmed on 1 July that no final report will be produced. The human ledger: more than 260,000 federal employees left government in 2025 under DOGE-driven measures; agencies including the Nuclear Regulatory Commission and the Office of Personnel Management (OPM, the government's HR agency) have since rehired for lost expertise. Two legal loose ends survive the sunset: the executive order explicitly says termination of the temporary organization does not terminate other authorities in the order — so pieces of the apparatus persist inside agencies — and Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) litigation over DOGE's records, paused by the Supreme Court in May 2025, continues.

Einordnung — The honest scorecard: real, large, chaotic cuts (260k departures is not nothing) against a savings claim that is 10% of target, 30% documented, and contradicted by rehiring costs and an estimated ~$11B budget hole (single-analysis figure — keep the caveat). For the Muskverse specifically, DOGE's legacy is that it burned Musk's political capital with half the country, nearly destroyed the Trump relationship, and yielded — per Musk himself — an experience he wouldn't repeat. The relationship repair is now running through a far more Musk-native channel: equity, contracts and index funds rather than org charts. Historians will note the sequencing: DOGE self-deleted on 4 July, the same day Trump Accounts launched — the personnel-cutting era replaced by the asset-distributing era in a single news cycle.

Watch-Items — (1) which DOGE-created authorities quietly persist inside agencies under the surviving EO language; (2) the FOIA cases — the eventual document record could reopen the story; (3) Government Accountability Office (GAO) / Inspector-General audits of the $215B claim; (4) whether doge.gov's data and API stay online now that the organization is gone.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The case (N.D. Cal., No. 4:24-cv-04722) was Musk's attempt to unwind OpenAI's conversion from nonprofit to commercial juggernaut, packaging fraud, breach-of-charitable-trust and unfair-competition theories around a $150B damages headline. It never reached those merits: the trial was decided on the threshold question of when Musk knew or should have known of the alleged betrayal. The jury answered: by 2021 at the latest (the falling-out era), more than three years before filing — hence time-barred. Musk's appellate theory, per lead counsel Steven Molo's statements in May, is that the clock should start at OpenAI's 2023–24 commercial restructuring, when the alleged harm crystallized, not at the early friction.

Die Details — What this window's checking actually established: (1) the CourtListener/RECAP mirror of the district docket shows "Date of Last Known Filing: May 4, 2026" and was last updated 5 May — i.e., the free public mirror predates even the verdict, because RECAP only updates when someone pulls documents from PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records, the paywalled federal docket system). Absence of appeal evidence there is therefore weak evidence of absence. (2) No wire service, legal-trade outlet, or aggregator has reported either a notice of appeal or a waiver of appeal since the May coverage burst. (3) The procedural nuance the "missed deadline" read ignores: under Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4, the 30-day clock runs from entry of judgment — but timely post-trial motions (renewed judgment as a matter of law, or a new-trial motion under Rules 50/59) reset that clock until the motions are decided. If Musk's team filed such motions in late May/early June, the appeal window could still be comfortably open with zero Ninth Circuit docket activity to show for it — and nobody has reported either way.

Einordnung — Three readings, in rough order of likelihood: (a) post-trial motions are pending below and the fight is simply between news cycles; (b) the appeal was noticed but is too procedurally boring for coverage in a week dominated by SPCX index flows; (c) Musk is letting the litigation die quietly because the rivalry now runs through markets — SPCX vs the coming OpenAI IPO — where he has better weapons than a long-shot statute-of-limitations reversal.
