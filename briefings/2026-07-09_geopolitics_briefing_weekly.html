Geopolitics · Week of 3–9 July 2026

The Doha MoU collapsed the day Trump flew to Ankara — Iran hit three tankers, the US struck 80+ targets, and the NATO summit ended up funding Ukraine while re-opening a war with Iran.

Three days ago the base case was "implementation with friction." As of 7 July it is "ceasefire over": on the twentieth day of a sixty-day memorandum, Iran's IRGC (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps) struck a Qatari LNG carrier, a Saudi crude tanker, and a third vessel; hours later, live from the Ankara summit's opening dinner, President Trump ordered CENTCOM to hit dozens of Iranian air defense, radar, missile and small-boat sites. The Treasury pulled the sanctions waiver on Iranian oil. Brent settled +5.2% at $78.02. NATO's 32 leaders spent the second day of their summit pledging €70 billion for Ukraine in 2026 plus at least the same in 2027 (€140bn combined), all from Europe and Canada — the US neither pledged nor blocked. Trump then held separate calls with Putin (4 July, 85 minutes) and Zelensky, told the press "both sides want to settle" the Ukraine war, and said he would allow Ukraine to produce Patriot interceptors. Meanwhile the invisible new Iranian Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei buried his father in Mashhad today (9 July) still without a single public appearance — Iran is fighting a restarted war under a leader nobody has seen.

Iran / Hormuz — the ceasefire breaks

4 items
Ceasefire broken7 Jul

IRGC hits three tankers in Hormuz on Day 20 of the MoU — Qatari LNG carrier, Saudi crude tanker, and a third vessel struck within 24 hours

On the night of 6–7 July, hours before Trump's plane touched down in Ankara for the NATO summit, Iran's IRGC Navy launched what US officials describe as at least two missiles at commercial shipping in the strait, and a third commercial ship was attacked the following morning. Named targets: the Qatari-owned LNG (liquefied natural gas) carrier Al Rekayat — evacuated after an engine-room fire — and the Saudi-flagged crude supertanker Wedyan, confirmed by Riyadh's Foreign Ministry. Both ships damaged, crews reported safe. Coming on Day 20 of the 60-day MoU signed in Islamabad on 17 June, the strikes chose the two most politically loaded flags in the region — a Gulf mediator (Qatar) and Iran's regional rival (Saudi Arabia) — which is not accidental targeting.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The 17 June memorandum promised toll-free Hormuz transit for 60 days while a permanent deal was negotiated; the last Monday edition described the pattern as "cheap, deniable IRGC signals (a VHF warning costs nothing) that move insurance premiums instantly." Between 4 and 6 July the signals had been escalating — an IRGC statement re-declared the strait "closed" citing Israeli strikes in southern Lebanon as an MoU violation, and tanker U-turns on the Oman-coast route were already re-appearing. The 7 July strikes are the escalation the signals were pricing.

Die Details — Al Jazeera and CNN corroborate the sequence: Al Rekayat, an LNG carrier owned by Qatar's national shipping company Nakilat, was struck by projectiles in the strait, its engine room caught fire and it was evacuated at risk of exploding; the Saudi crude carrier Wedyan was hit in a separate strike Riyadh's Foreign Ministry confirmed publicly; a third commercial vessel was hit on the morning of 7 July, per US officials cited by Axios. Choosing a Qatari flag is a specifically hostile choice — Qatar is the MoU's principal mediator and has been the "adult in the room" between Tehran and Washington. Choosing a Saudi flag re-opens the Gulf axis Iran had spent five years de-freezing (2023 Beijing reconciliation). The IRGC's dial has moved from "signaling" to "attack". Iranian state media (IRIB, Tasnim) initially framed the strikes as responses to Israeli MoU violations in Lebanon; those framings are propagandistic and de-rated.

Einordnung — Two readings, both viable. (1) IRGC autonomy: with Mojtaba Khamenei invisible during the funeral week, the IRGC used the succession opacity to escalate on its own institutional agenda — proving no US–Iran channel can bypass it. (2) Regime-directed collapse: the negotiation was going nowhere on the nuclear file, the $12B assets dispute was hardening, and Tehran chose a controlled break before the 21 August waiver expiry could be used against it. Either way, targeting Qatar burns the mediator and targeting Saudi burns the Gulf detente — this is not a signal aimed at Washington, it is one aimed at re-drawing the regional map.

Watch-Items — Whether Qatar publicly walks away from mediation; Saudi retaliation posture (Riyadh publicly ruled out air action but is running IRGC-adjacent options with the UAE); insurance war-risk premium reset; whether IRGC ops continue during Mashhad burial rites (9 July).

US retaliation7–8 Jul

Trump declares the ceasefire "over" from Ankara — CENTCOM hits 80+ Iranian targets, Treasury pulls the oil waiver, naval blockade "under consideration"

Speaking from the NATO summit opening dinner in Ankara, Trump said the Iran ceasefire was "over" and ordered a retaliatory strike package. US Central Command hit "over 80 targets with precision munitions" over approximately four hours the night of 7 July — air defenses, radar sites, anti-ship missile positions, and IRGC fast-boat pens near the strait. In parallel: the Treasury revoked the temporary Iran oil-sale waiver that had been granted under the MoU (reversing what had been a major Iranian revenue lifeline), and Trump publicly floated "considering" reimposing the naval blockade that had defined the war's opening phase. By day-end 8 July, Trump had walked the ledge back marginally — "I don't think it's going to start again" — but the MoU architecture is unrecognizable from Monday's version.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The war's opening phase (late Feb – mid-June) was defined by naval blockade + strikes; the MoU replaced blockade with toll-free transit and a temporary US oil waiver. In a late-June cycle Washington had already hit Iranian targets over two nights for a drone attack on a commercial vessel; the difference now is scale (80+ targets vs targeted strikes) and Trump's public declaration that the ceasefire is over.

Die Details — CENTCOM's statement listed the target categories: air defenses, radar, anti-ship missile capabilities, and small-boat pens the IRGC uses for asymmetric harassment — a package designed to degrade the IRGC's ability to repeat the 7 July attacks rather than a regime-decapitation strike. Iranian state media (IRIB and pro-regime Telegram) claimed retaliatory strikes on US military sites in Bahrain and Kuwait; those claims are single-source Iranian, not corroborated by US or GCC (Gulf Cooperation Council) statements as of writing, and are de-rated. The financial retaliation was substantive: Treasury reimposed sanctions on Iranian oil exports that had been lifted under the MoU — a material revenue hit given that the waiver was worth several hundred thousand barrels per day of legalized flows to Chinese buyers. Trump publicly floated reimposing the naval blockade but has not yet ordered it. Oil markets: Brent settled +5.2% at $78.02 on 8 July after Trump's "second day" bombing threat; prices then eased on Trump's "I don't think it's going to start again" clarification. NATO Secretary-General Rutte and European leaders were caught mid-summit having to speak on a war they had not been consulted on.

Einordnung — Three signals in one 36-hour cycle. (1) Trump personally owns the escalation ladder — CENTCOM struck within hours of the tanker hits, not days; that turn-around implies pre-authorization, not deliberation. (2) The oil-waiver revocation is the substantive punishment; the strikes are the choreography. (3) Trump's later "not going to start again" walk-back tells you he wants the MoU restored on tougher terms, not a full re-war — the theatre is aimed at forcing Iran back to Doha with the IRGC contained. Whether Tehran reads it that way is the actual open question.

Watch-Items — Any second-round US strikes; whether the naval blockade order actually issues; formal Iranian response after Mashhad burial (9 July); whether Qatar/Oman restart mediation shuttle by weekend; oil price behavior next 72 hours.

Succession4–9 Jul

Khamenei's funeral ends in Mashhad today with Mojtaba still invisible — Iran fought a restarted war under a leader nobody has seen

The multi-city funeral of Ali Khamenei ended today 9 July with burial in Mashhad — and the new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei (56) has still not appeared publicly since his 9 March installation, four months on. On 5 July three of Ali's sons — Mostafa, Meysam and Masoud — prayed at the rites; his fourth son and named successor did not. Then, on 7 July, Iran resumed a war under that same absent leader. Officially Mojtaba's non-appearance is due to Israeli assassination threats; Reuters and US Defense Secretary Hegseth (in March) described severe facial and leg injuries, possibly an amputated leg — intelligence-grade single-source reporting, de-rated. The governance question is now acute: the IRGC's Hormuz escalation ran during funeral week without a visible Supreme Leader to bless or block it.

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The 28 February strike that opened the war killed Ali Khamenei after 36 years of rule plus much of his household — his daughter, a son-in-law, a daughter-in-law, a 14-month-old granddaughter, and Mojtaba's wife Zahra Haddad-Adel. The Assembly of Experts installed Mojtaba on 9 March in wartime succession; the public funeral was deferred over four months and staged now as a show of continuity.

Die Details — The six-day choreography ran 4–9 July: private lying-in-state with foreign dignitaries; public display at Tehran's Grand Mosalla; processions through Karbala, Najaf (Iraq) and Qom (Iran); burial in Mashhad on 9 July. Attendees included President Masoud Pezeshkian, Speaker Ghalibaf, Quds Force commander Esmail Qaani, and Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif. Crowds carried red flags of revenge and chants of "Death to America"; TIME summarized the official message as "continuity and revenge". Then came the 7 July tanker attacks — a wartime escalation staged during the country's official mourning week, without the Supreme Leader visibly present to authorize it. That is not a coincidence, and it is not a normal ordering of events in the Islamic Republic's constitutional architecture. Al Jazeera's 5 July piece put it plainly: "Iran's new leader has yet to appear."

Einordnung — The regency-by-committee hypothesis from Monday's edition just delivered its first observable output — a war restart nobody with authority has publicly claimed. Two structural inferences: (1) whoever authorized the Hormuz strikes did not need Mojtaba's public sign-off, which tells you where power now sits — Supreme National Security Council + IRGC, in that order; (2) the "invisible leader" cost is now non-hypothetical — a US retaliation package is landing on a country whose head of state cannot address it publicly. Watch whether Mojtaba appears in any form (audio, decree, still image) in the 72 hours after burial. If not, the succession is de facto unresolved and the war has a leadership vacuum on the receiving end.

MoU status2–7 Jul

Doha working groups had already stalled on IAEA access and $6B Qatar-escrow mechanics — the ceasefire broke on the same faults it was built on

The last confirmed diplomatic activity before the collapse: Iran's Parliament Speaker Ghalibaf stated 2 July that inspectors of the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA — the UN nuclear watchdog) would receive access only to the Bushehr power plant and the Tehran research reactor, not to bombed sites Fordow, Natanz or Isfahan — barred by a parliamentary law and a Supreme National Security Council resolution. On frozen assets: Ghalibaf claimed $12B of ~$24B would be released, but Doha working groups only actually agreed a $6B initial tranche administered via Qatar's Central Bank as procurement-in-kind (not cash) — the exact mechanism used in the 2023 Qatar arrangement and re-frozen after 7 October 2023. The IAEA has had no access to Iranian sites for 121 days. Neither side signed off on either file — and then Hormuz overtook the negotiation.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The MoU's 60-day clock started 17 June. It set two hard files: unfreezing ~$25B in Iranian assets and returning IAEA inspectors — with enrichment as the untouched red line. The Doha rounds (30 June – 2 July) were meant to produce working groups on both files. Neither delivered anything binding, per Qatar's Foreign Ministry readout.

Die Details — On assets: the MoU's operative language is that the US "undertakes to make fully available for use the frozen or restricted funds and assets" upon implementation — a conditional promise, not a disbursement mechanism. The Doha working group agreed only that "procedures related to the release of these funds" would be agreed during negotiations. The $6B Qatar-escrow figure that surfaced from Iranian negotiator Kazem Gharibabadi mirrors the 2023 arrangement — but that pot was re-frozen by joint US-Qatar agreement after 7 October 2023 and Iran has accessed none of it. Ghalibaf's public "$12B now" claim is domestic messaging, not the working-group text. On IAEA: Grossi (IAEA chief) insists inspectors "have to have access" under the MoU; Ghalibaf publicly foreclosed access to the exact sites the world wants monitored. Timing note before 7 July: the MoU was at Day 14 of 60, with the 21 August US-waiver expiry roughly at Day 65 — meaning the sanctions relief runs out days after the 60-day clock, giving Tehran a structural incentive to run the clock while the IRGC toggled the strait. That is the architecture the tanker attacks blew up.

Einordnung — Read forward: any restart of Doha will not skip these blockages — it will need cash disbursement of a real assets tranche (not procurement-in-kind), and it will need at least one bombed-site IAEA visit, before the US or Iran can politically defend a deal. Neither is easier now that shots have been fired again. Read backward: the working groups were quietly failing all week before the strikes; the strikes may have been Tehran preempting a US declaration of MoU non-compliance rather than the opening move it looked like.

Watch-Items — Whether Grossi issues a formal non-compliance finding; whether Qatar's Central Bank $6B mechanism survives (or a new mediator takes it); any Iranian offer of an IAEA visit to Bushehr or the research reactor as a face-saver; the 21 August waiver deadline as the next automatic pressure point.

NATO Ankara Summit — 7–8 July

Summit outcome7–8 Jul

Ankara Summit Declaration: €70bn for Ukraine 2026 + at least the same in 2027 (€140bn combined) — all from Europe and Canada, US neither pledges nor blocks

The Ankara Summit Declaration, signed 8 July by all 32 heads of state and government, committed €70 billion ($80bn) in military equipment, training and assistance for Ukraine in 2026, with a formal sovereign commitment to at least equivalent levels in 2027 — a combined €140bn two-year pledge. Kyiv Post and Ukrainska Pravda flagged the structural point: the funding is expected to come from the 31 non-US allies (all European members plus Canada) — Washington neither contributed to nor blocked the pledge, an implicit ratification of Europe's takeover of Ukraine security assistance. NATO's Article 5 was re-affirmed ("attack on one is attack on all"). Average European + Canadian core defense spending: 2.53% of GDP in 2026 YTD, up from 2.3% in 2025. Rutte identified the summit's three priorities as increased defense investment, transatlantic industrial production, and Ukraine support.

Mehr lesenexpand_more

Kontext & Vorgeschichte — The Ankara summit was the 36th NATO Heads of State and Government summit and the first hosted by Turkey. Erdogan's welcome to Trump was a set-piece moment. The summit arrived after three months of the US–Israel–Iran war and after Trump's habitual public denigration of allies — European anxiety over Russia's capability, US commitment, and hybrid pressure was the operating baseline.

Die Details — The pledge structure is what matters: €70bn for 2026 as a hard number, "at least equivalent levels in 2027" as a sovereign commitment (each ally responsible for its share) — Kyiv Post totals €140bn combined. It excludes US contributions, which are now expected to come through commercial channels: Trump publicly said he would allow Ukraine to produce Patriot missiles (i.e., US licensing of Patriot manufacture on Ukrainian soil), which if implemented would be a genuinely major shift from consumable to productive support. The declaration also welcomed the European Union's multi-year Ukraine Support Loan — the fiscal side of the same architecture. Article 5 language was reaffirmed against Russia, and Russia was again named "the most significant and direct threat to allied security." Trump's presence caused turbulence but not collapse — CSMonitor's read was "healthy partnership, even as new Iran tensions flare." The Kremlin's response was Peskov calling the summit "confrontational" — the reflexive dismissal of a package aimed exactly at Moscow.

Einordnung — Structurally the summit ratified two shifts. (1) Europe now owns Ukraine security assistance — €140bn over two years from 31 non-US allies makes explicit what has been implicit since spring: the US shifted from lead to permissive, and Europe accepted the bill. That is either the new NATO model working or the end of NATO-as-alliance depending on your priors; there is no third reading. (2) Patriot licensing is the tell: Washington is willing to arm Ukraine's industrial base but not its treasury — a Reagan-era Contra-style architecture rather than the Biden-era grant flow. The €140bn number is a nominal-strong / real-fragile figure: sovereign commitments in NATO summit declarations have been under-delivered often, and 2027 is a long way away.

Watch-Items — Whether the Patriot licensing actually materializes (announcement vs contracts); whether the 2027 pledge survives the first NATO budget cycle; delivery-vs-pledge ratios by December; Turkey's own posture (host effect passes fast); Hungary/Slovakia carve-outs on the EU side of the funding.

Ukraine's 40-day campaign hits Russia's largest western-Siberian refinery — Kyiv claims 42.74% of refining capacity now disabled

Ukraine's Security Service (SBU) 40-day strategic strike campaign reached its deepest target this week: on 4 July, drones struck oil and military facilities near St Petersburg; on 6 July, Ukrainian drones hit Russia's largest oil refinery in the western Siberian region of Omsk, marking one of the deepest strikes inside Russian territory since 2022. Ukraine's General Staff claims 42.74% of Russia's oil refining capacity disabled as of early July. Caveat: these are Ukrainian-sourced figures with the usual incentive to overstate — but Russian domestic fuel rationing, the extended diesel export ban, and reported 400,000 t/month emergency imports from India corroborate the direction.

Einordnung — Ukraine's strategy for 2026 is quietly explicit: if you cannot win on the ground (see next card) and the diplomacy is stalled, damage Russia's revenue pipeline until autumn Duma politics changes the equation. The 40-day window's end (~early August) is designed to land next to the September Duma elections and the ISW-documented Russian offensive exhaustion. Whether that timing works or not, the campaign is one of the few decisive strategic instruments in the war right now — and it costs Ukraine less per unit of Russian damage than any other option.
