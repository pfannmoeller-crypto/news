Weekly · 16 – 23 May 2026

The Iran strike Trump paused on 19 May is back on the table. He is at the White House this weekend, weighing fresh strikes "unless there is an eleventh-hour breakthrough." Ukraine widened its deep-strike campaign; Russia's net territorial loss grew to 29 square miles in a week.

Last week's edition called the Iran file "binary on a days-to-weeks horizon." Seven days later the clock has run most of the way down. Trump postponed the Tuesday-19-May strike at Gulf-state request to allow "serious negotiations" — but by Friday 22 May he was meeting his national security team again, reportedly skipping his son's wedding to stay at the White House, with US military and intelligence personnel cancelling Memorial Day plans. The negotiating gap is unchanged and concrete: the enrichment-moratorium duration. The US is demanding 20 years, Iran has offered 5, and reporting puts the current floor at roughly 12 — no agreed number. Qatar formally assumed a mediator role on 22 May despite having absorbed the March strike on its Ras Laffan LNG complex. On the Ukraine file the kinetic picture moved the way the front line did not: ISW data via Russia Matters shows Russia losing a net 29 square miles in the 12–19 May week (up from 12 the prior week), and Ukraine ran its widest deep-strike week of the war — a drone-pilot training camp in occupied Snizhne (65+ cadets killed), the Syzran oil refinery, and roughly 19,200 Russian personnel struck in 19 days per Kyiv's Unmanned Systems Forces. The honest read: no diplomatic exit closed on either file, and the Iran tail is now event-driven on a horizon measured in days, not weeks.

Iran · nuclear, strike risk, Hormuz
DEVELOPMENT Iran 22–23 May

The paused 19 May strike is back: Trump met his security team Friday, is "seriously weighing" new strikes, and is staying at the White House this weekend

The single highest-stakes item of the window. The 19 May edition flagged that Trump had a target list approved and was talked back only by Gulf states. That pause held for three days. By Friday 22 May, per Times of Israel and CNN reporting, Trump met top members of his national security team again and is "seriously weighing new strikes unless there is an eleventh-hour breakthrough in the talks." The signalling underneath the rhetoric is the part that matters: Trump is reportedly skipping his son's wedding to remain at the White House, and sources with direct knowledge of planning say some US military personnel and intelligence officers cancelled Memorial Day weekend plans. That is operational-tempo evidence, not posturing — it is consistent with a decision window inside days. No final decision has been made, and the administration has paused before; the honest read is that the binary call last week's edition described has not resolved, it has been deferred by one cycle and is now arriving on the Memorial-Day-weekend horizon. The asymmetric tail in Brent and shipping insurance is at the front of the queue.

Nuclear talks 16–22 May

The deal gap is one number: enrichment-moratorium duration — US wants 20 years, Iran offered 5, reporting puts the floor near 12

The one-page MOU has not collapsed and has not closed. The framework Iranian officials are reviewing — negotiated by envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner directly and through mediators — would declare an end to the war and open a 30-day window for a detailed agreement covering Iran's nuclear program, US sanctions relief, release of frozen Iranian funds, and reopening the Strait of Hormuz against lifting of the US naval blockade. The single unresolved variable is the duration of the uranium-enrichment moratorium. The US has demanded 20 years; Iran has proposed 5; sources describe the contested floor as "at least 12." That is the whole negotiation in one parameter — a gap measured in years, not principles, which is why both a deal and a strike remain genuinely live. Trump's public posture stayed maximalist: "If they don't agree, the bombing starts, and it will be, sadly, at a much higher level and intensity than it was before." The substantive read: this is now a bargaining-range problem, and bargaining-range problems either split the difference fast or break — the absence of a middle is what makes the week binary.

Hormuz 22 May

Qatar formally took the mediator role on 22 May — despite having absorbed the March strike that knocked out 17% of its LNG capacity

The mediation structure firmed up rather than the strait. On 22 May Qatar formally assumed a mediator role in the war, and Pakistan's army chief Asim Munir met Iranian foreign minister Abbas Araghchi in Tehran in talks running late into the night on the diplomatic initiatives aimed at preventing escalation. Qatar's willingness to mediate is notable because it is not a neutral bystander: the March strike on its Ras Laffan complex damaged two of fourteen LNG trains and one gas-to-liquids facility, sidelining an estimated 12.8 million tonnes of annual LNG output for three to five years and roughly 17% of Qatar's export capacity. A mediator with that much skin in a renewed war has strong incentives to land a deal — but the same Gulf-state dynamic the 19 May edition identified still holds: the actors who can pause a US strike are not the actors who can deliver an Iranian yes. The strait itself did not reopen in this window; the dual blockade persists. For the energy tail, the relevant fact is that the Ras Laffan capacity loss is structural and multi-year regardless of how the negotiation resolves.

Ukraine · Russia war and diplomacy
DEVELOPMENT Ukraine 20–21 May

Ukraine ran its widest deep-strike week of the war: drone-pilot training camp in occupied Snizhne hit, 65+ cadets killed; Syzran refinery struck

The deep-strike campaign the 19 May edition described as "now genuinely two-way" widened sharply. Per CNN, a Ukrainian strike hit a Russian drone-pilot training camp in the occupied town of Snizhne overnight Wednesday, killing at least 65 cadets and an instructor — a targeting choice aimed at the human capital behind Russia's own drone program, not just hardware. A separate strike hit a Russian security headquarters and air-defence system, with Zelensky claiming nearly 100 Russians killed or wounded; Russia acknowledged a third mass-casualty event when drones hit a college dormitory in Starobilsk (six killed, 39 injured). Ukraine also struck the Syzran oil refinery in western Russia, with Zelensky releasing footage of the burning facility — a continuation of the Moscow-refinery hit pattern from last week. Kyiv's Unmanned Systems Forces commander put cumulative figures at roughly 19,200 Russian personnel struck over the first 19 days of May. These are Ukrainian claims and not Russia-confirmed except where Moscow acknowledged specific hits; the standard wartime discount applies. The substantive read: Ukraine is now targeting the inputs to Russia's drone war — trained pilots and refining throughput — not just front-line formations.

Front line 12–19 May

Russia Matters: Russia lost a net 29 square miles in the 12–19 May week — the loss is accelerating, not flattening

The front-line trajectory the 19 May edition described as "net-against-Moscow" steepened. The Russia Matters War Report Card of 20 May, using ISW data, puts Russia's net territorial change at a loss of 29 square miles over 12–19 May, following a 12-square-mile loss the prior week (5–12 May) — i.e. the rate of Russian loss roughly doubled week-on-week. Over the four weeks 21 April – 19 May the net figure is a 69-square-mile Russian loss. Ukraine's foothold in Kursk and Belgorod held steady at 4 square miles. The longer arc remains in Russia's favour — net +1,585 square miles (about 0.7% of Ukraine) over the trailing year, 20% of Ukraine still occupied — but the recent direction is unambiguous: Ukrainian counteroffensive pressure is producing measurable reversal at the line of contact even as the deep-strike campaign escalates. The honest read: the front is not stalemated in Russia's favour the way it appeared through 2025; it is now slowly moving against Moscow, and that complicates Putin's "war is ending" framing on his own terms.

Diplomacy 16–22 May

Donetsk withdrawal is the new named sticking point; Putin's "war ending" rhetoric continues, structure unchanged

The Ukraine diplomatic track produced clarification, not movement. Reporting now identifies the concrete deadlock: Russia is demanding Ukraine withdraw troops from the parts of Donetsk region that Russian forces have failed to capture — roughly a quarter of the oblast remains under Ukrainian control — and Kyiv has refused to cede territory it still holds. That is a more specific articulation of the same structural problem the 19 May edition flagged: Putin positions a Zelensky meeting as the ratification of a deal Moscow has effectively won, not the venue to negotiate one, and still conditions it on a treaty being "finalised" first. A Chatham House analyst's caution is the right calibration — there have been "plenty of promises over the last 18 months that the end of the war was imminent," and Putin's recent comments should not be read as a reliable indicator the conflict is genuinely near resolution. The Vatican remains an offered venue (Pope Leo XIV), but no meeting is scheduled. Net: the sticking point got a name this week; the gap did not get smaller.

Russia · internal — economy, dissent, elites
Russia internal 16–22 May

The 12 May forecast cut now reads as an official admission of structural, multi-year stagnation — analysts call it the end of the "rebound" assumption

The number itself was last week's story; this week the framing around it hardened. The Economy Ministry's 12 May projection — 2026 GDP growth of 0.4%, down from 1.3% — is now being described by analysts as the first official Russian document to concede at least two more years of stagnation with no rebound waiting on the other side. CEPA's read ("Russia Settles for Stagnation") and the IISS research paper "The Coming Crisis in Russia's Political Economy" both treat the revision as structural rather than cyclical: the constraints follow directly from the war, with military spending up from 3% of GDP in 2021 to 8% in 2025, and the economy already having contracted 0.3% in Q1 even as Putin pressed officials to accelerate growth. The ministry's own outlook does not forecast recovery until 2027 at the earliest. This does not contradict last week's cross-current — the IMF's 1.1% figure, lifted by Iran-war oil revenues — it sharpens the dependency: the only thing making 2026 less bad than the ministry's own number is the Iran-war oil windfall, which is exactly the variable an Iran de-escalation would remove. No named-elite defection or break point surfaced this week; the trajectory is degradation without rupture, the same read as the 19 May edition.

Russia Iran Standing — late April–May

Russia–Iran: a partnership of rhetoric and trade re-routing, not military rescue — the Hormuz war exposed the ceiling

No major new Russia–Iran agreement landed in the 16–23 May window, so this is a standing-read item. The structure is set: the comprehensive strategic partnership treaty signed January 2026 and ratified by both parliaments commits Moscow and Tehran to political, economic and military-technical cooperation but contains no mutual-defence clause — and the Iran war has demonstrated that limit in practice. Iranian foreign minister Araghchi's late-April St Petersburg visit produced praise for Moscow's "firm and unshaken" support, but Iranian critics openly asked "where Russia was" during the fighting. The substantive cooperation is economic re-routing: with Gulf shipping disrupted, Tehran is leaning on railways, Caspian ports and sanctions-era trade networks linking it to Russia, plus dollar-bypass financial mechanisms. There is also a December 2025 arms-procurement deal (around EUR 495m, launcher units and missiles) — but delivery runs 2027–2029, i.e. it does nothing for the current war. The calibration for Stefan's read: Russia is Iran's economic backstop and diplomatic ally, not its military guarantor; Tehran cannot count on Moscow to change the war's kinetic outcome, which is part of why the US negotiating leverage on enrichment is real.

Sanctions and trade
Sanctions 16–24 May

21st EU package still in drafting for end-June/early-July; the 20th package's crypto sectoral ban takes effect 24 May

No new package was adopted this week — the relevant moves are a scheduled entry-into-force and the continued drafting flagged last week. The EU's 20th package (adopted 23 April) includes a full sectoral ban on transacting with any crypto-asset service provider established in Russia, and that measure takes effect 24 May — closing one of the larger remaining circumvention channels for Russian-linked flows. The 20th package also brought the shadow-fleet listing total to 632 vessels and 70 sanctioned Russian banks, and established the legal basis for a future maritime-services ban on Russian crude. The 21st package remains in preparation for end-June or early-July, with scoping pointing at additional shadow-fleet tankers, more banks and financial-circumvention entities, defence-industry enterprises and firms trading stolen Ukrainian grain — the maritime-services ban itself is the headline candidate. This is process continuity, not news: the political ground was already cleared (Hungary's veto bloc broken), and the next discrete trade-flow event for Russian-flag and shadow-fleet tonnage is still six to seven weeks out.

What actually moved this week

  1. The Iran binary did not resolve — it rolled forward one cycle onto the Memorial-Day-weekend horizon. The 19 May edition said the file was "binary on a days-to-weeks horizon." Seven days later that is still true, only the days count is lower. Trump paused the 19 May strike, then by 22 May was back with his security team and reportedly skipping his son's wedding to stay at the White House while military and intelligence staff cancel holiday plans. That operational-tempo evidence is the real signal — it points to a decision window inside days, not a slow burn.
  2. The whole Iran deal is now one contested number. Not principles, not sequencing — the duration of the enrichment moratorium. US 20 years, Iran 5, floor near 12. Bargaining-range problems of this shape either split the difference quickly or break, and the absence of a natural middle is exactly what makes a deal and a strike both genuinely live. This is the cleanest, most trackable variable in the entire briefing: watch for any reported convergence on a year-number.
  3. Ukraine has shifted from striking Russian formations to striking the inputs of Russia's war. The Snizhne drone-pilot training camp (65+ cadets) and the Syzran refinery are not battlefield targets — they are the human capital and the refining throughput behind Russia's own drone campaign and fuel logistics. The 19 May edition said deep-strike had gone "two-way." This week it went up a level: Ukraine is now attacking Russia's capacity to regenerate the war, not just its forces in the field.
  4. The front line is now measurably moving against Moscow, and the loss rate is accelerating. Russia Matters/ISW: net −29 square miles in the 12–19 May week, double the −12 of the prior week, −69 over four weeks. The trailing-year picture still favours Russia (+1,585 square miles, 20% occupied), but the recent direction is unambiguous. That quietly undercuts Putin's "war is ending" framing — he is narrating a wind-down while losing ground.
  5. Russia's economy: the story is no longer the number, it is the official concession. The 12 May 0.4% forecast is now read by CEPA and IISS as Moscow's first formal admission of multi-year structural stagnation with no rebound assumption. The only thing keeping 2026 from being worse is the Iran-war oil windfall — which means the two crises are coupled on Moscow's revenue line. An Iran deal that calms the oil price is, paradoxically, fiscal bad news for the Kremlin.
  6. Honest call on cadence: a deferral week, not a resolution week. No deal closed, no strike launched, no diplomatic mechanism created, no named-elite defection. The 19 May framing — coupled negotiation tracks with one unresolved central gap, Russia internal degrading without rupture, no Hormuz reopening — still holds intact. What changed is that the Iran decision window compressed from "days-to-weeks" to "this weekend or next."

Wildcard: the Memorial-Day-weekend Iran decision is the highest-variance item of the next seven days by a wide margin. A strike order converts the Brent and shipping-insurance tail into a spot event, widens Russia's oil-revenue offset, and re-energises the EU 21st-package politics — all at once. The opposite wildcard — Witkoff/Kushner and Qatari mediation landing a year-number compromise on enrichment inside the window — would compress the Brent war premium fast and simultaneously expose how much of Russia's "less catastrophic than forecast" fiscal position was borrowed from the Iran war. Either outcome moves the macro picture more than any Ukraine-file development could in the same window.
