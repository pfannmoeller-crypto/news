"The Singularity has had a price since March, but no major exchange to trade it on, until now."

Compute gets its first major-exchange futures contract

Two months after Ornn — a company Wissner-Gross advises and helped form, backed by 021T Capital — began publishing the Ornn Compute Price Index (OCPI) on the Bloomberg Terminal, the index has found its keystone: Ornn plans to launch exchange-listed GPU compute futures through Intercontinental Exchange, parent of the NYSE. "OCPI settles against cleared GPU prices, not rate cards or surveys. That was the credentialing step. But credentialing is not clearing." The contracts will be US-dollar-denominated and cash-settled, referencing OCPI series for H100, H200, B200, RTX 5090 and more — pending regulatory approval.

The ICE precedent — dragging opaque markets into transparent clearing

"The parallel to GPU compute today is exact." Jeff Sprecher founded ICE in 2000, three years after buying Continental Power Exchange for $1,000, with the vision of hauling opaque OTC energy markets into transparent, electronically cleared trading. ICE inherited Brent crude in 2001 and grew it into the reference price for roughly three-quarters of the world's traded oil — "the same plumbing that runs Brent crude, TTF natural gas, EU carbon allowances, and the world's benchmark sugar, coffee, and cotton contracts is now being extended to compute."

Who can finally hedge the $7 trillion buildout

Once OCPI-referenced futures clear at ICE, lenders financing GPU buildouts can hedge exposure on the same infrastructure that clears oil and gas; insurers can underwrite residual-value risk against a regulated curve; and hyperscaler treasury desks can lock in forward compute costs "the way airlines have locked in jet fuel since the 1980s." Crucially, sovereign infrastructure funds and pension capital — pools that cannot touch unregulated venues at scale — can finally participate in the $7 trillion compute buildout.

The historical arc — every civilizational commodity ends on the same plumbing

Echoing the Solve Everything argument that the Intelligence Revolution turns scarce domains abundant "but only after the financial infrastructure catches up": Edison built the generators, Insull made them financeable; Carnegie made the steel, J.P. Morgan made it bankable through the 1901 merger that formed U.S. Steel. Oil took over a century to reach a futures contract (1859 → 1983); gas took even longer. "Compute took only years because oil and gas had already built the machinery. Those years end now."

The frontier where the record does not exist

"Everything AI has ever learned came with a record already written." Text, code, protein, images: humans had documented each domain, and "AI progressed in the order things were written down." Non-human animal cognition is the first frontier "where the record does not exist, because no other species ever wrote one down." Synthetic data cannot help, "because they only interpolate distributions a model has already seen." The non-human data, Wissner-Gross argues, have to be "measured into existence."

Sarama — the first consumer-scale interspecies foundation model

Sarama, an interspecies foundation model lab founded by Praful Mathur that Wissner-Gross helped form and advises, backed by 021T Capital, is announcing "the world's first consumer-scale interspecies foundation model, beginning with a dog collar." Prior art exists — Earth Species Project's NatureLM-audio, Google's DolphinGemma, Project CETI's sperm-whale codas — but all learn from researcher-collected archives. "Sarama's bet is the opposite: data generated and self-labeled by an instrument in the home."

open_in_newSarama open_in_newNatureLM-audio open_in_newDolphinGemma
Why dogs first, and why a per-dog model

"Dogs had to be first. They are the only non-human mind that co-evolved to be legible to us," sharing our naturally labeled home and existing at consumer scale. "And this is not a novelty bark translator. A universal bark dictionary fails for the same reason a universal human dictionary would: meaning is individual." Sarama builds a separate model of each dog, trained on that dog's own life — reaching the home as a two-mode app: a third-person AR mode overlaying a dog's attempted communications onto the animal, and a first-person collar mode streaming the dog's-eye view "with a newsfeed of what it is trying to say and needs."

open_in_newWhy dogs open_in_newAR mode open_in_newCollar mode
The instrument — a self-labeling collar and the team behind it

The team — Mathur, "a three-time consumer-supply-chain founder," and chief scientist Peter Bermant, a bioacoustics researcher whose sperm-whale-coda work was early proof-of-concept for Project CETI — "spent years decoding whales and dolphins, and yet could not understand their own dogs." The collar is a custom pod with camera, microphone and motion sensors that transmits feature vectors, not raw media, "so collection stays private." Crucially, "the sensors label themselves": camera frames the bowl, motion logs the lunge, microphone the bark — "three readings labeling one another, no annotator in the loop." It already reads core needs and identifies dogs by voice "at roughly 93% accuracy on emotion and intent."
